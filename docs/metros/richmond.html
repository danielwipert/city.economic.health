## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Richmond’s labour market shows a mixed but generally stable picture, with underlying strengths tempered by emerging headwinds. The unemployment rate stands at 3.3%—within a healthy range—though it has risen by 0.7 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), suggesting some softening. This is reinforced by a shrinking civilian labour force, down 1.22% over the past year (102A_clf_yoy), which may reflect demographic trends or waning worker attraction rather than immediate job losses. However, demand for labour remains relatively firm, as indicated by a strong labour demand composite score (107E_ldc_composite: 5.99) and a 0.29% year-on-year increase in nonfarm employment (107E_employment_growth_yoy). Average hourly earnings are growing robustly at 6.81% (103B_earnings_yoy), outpacing inflation and supporting household resilience.

Housing and business activity, however, point to cooling momentum. Residential construction has slowed sharply, with building permits down 20.27% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), among the weakest performances nationally. The housing market is also accelerating: homes are selling quickly, with a median of just 39 days on market (204A_dom_level_days), down 11.36% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), a sign of tight supply rather than surging demand. The cost of living is moderate (104C_col: 2.28), while office activity is slightly above average (105C_owr: 2.98), though not indicative of strong commercial revitalisation.

**Outlook:** Richmond’s economy is navigating a transition, supported by wage growth and steady employment, but constrained by a retreating construction sector and a stagnant labour supply. Absent an influx of workers or renewed business investment, growth is likely to remain subdued in the near term, with housing affordability pressures persisting despite broader economic stability.

Economic Analysis

The Richmond metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 30.7th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier at 12th percentile, and its above-average wage growth, at 62nd percentile with a 5.36% year-over-year increase. These metrics suggest a complex environment where businesses may face challenges in finding talent, but also benefit from a skilled workforce with rising earnings.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Richmond is -0.36% year-over-year, and weekly hours are 0.591% above the city's own trend. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, as hours are above trend during a period of job losses, indicating a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This suggests that businesses may face challenges in finding talent, as the labor market is not expanding.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Richmond is 3.30%, which corresponds to a near-median percentile rank of 49th. This indicates a relatively balanced labor market with neither significant slack nor tightness. For a business trying to hire in this city, this means that they may not face extreme competition for talent, but still need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract workers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Richmond is 5.36%, which is above average, ranking 62nd percentile. This fast wage growth signals rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing worker purchasing power, which can benefit local businesses that rely on consumer spending. As a result, businesses may need to adjust their pricing strategies to account for higher labor costs.

Cost of Living

Richmond has a cost of living ratio of 3.78, which ranks it 24th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent, as the high cost of living may deter workers from relocating to the area without sufficient compensation. As a result, businesses may face higher labor costs to attract and retain talent.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Richmond is contracting at a rate of -1.22% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This contraction suggests that businesses may face structural headwinds in hiring, as the supply of workers is decreasing. As a result, businesses may need to invest in training and development programs to upskill existing workers.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Richmond has decreased by 20.27% year-over-year, indicating a tightening of housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the limited housing supply may drive up costs and deter workers from relocating to the area. As a result, businesses may face challenges in attracting talent due to the limited availability of affordable housing.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Richmond is 39 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 11.4%. This suggests a competitive market where homes are selling relatively quickly, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. As a result, businesses may need to offer relocation assistance or other incentives to help workers navigate the competitive housing market.

Office Economy

Richmond has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy percentile rank of 74th. This suggests that the city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a skilled and knowledgeable workforce. However, the city may be less suitable for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which require a different type of workforce.

The Richmond metro area offers businesses a skilled workforce with rising earnings, but also presents challenges in terms of labor demand and cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the limited availability of affordable housing, which may deter workers from relocating to the area and drive up labor costs. As a result, businesses need to carefully consider these factors when making location decisions and develop strategies to mitigate these risks.

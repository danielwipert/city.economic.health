Economic Analysis

The Richmond metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 33rd out of 50 US metros, with a composite score defined largely by its weak labor demand and declining building permits. The labor demand composite score of 2.22, combining a -0.54% employment growth rate and a +1.323% weekly hours deviation, signals a contraction in job opportunities. This is further exacerbated by a significant decline in building permits, down 24.31% year-over-year.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Richmond is -0.54% year-over-year, while weekly hours are running 1.323% above the city's own 12-month trend. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 2.22 falls in the bottom tier, at the 8th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Richmond is 3.70%, ranking at the 58th percentile, indicating a near-median level of slack in the labor market. This suggests that while the market is not extremely tight, it is also not overly loose, with some competition for jobs but not extreme wage pressure. For a business trying to hire, this means a moderate level of difficulty in finding qualified candidates.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Richmond is +3.03% year-over-year, ranking at the 40th percentile, indicating a moderate pace of wage increases. This implies that labor costs for employers are rising, but not extremely rapidly, while worker purchasing power is increasing at a steady rate. The wage growth rate is neither extremely fast nor stagnant, suggesting a relatively stable labor cost environment.

Cost of Living

Richmond has a cost of living score of 29th percentile, with a PSF of $233/sqft and average hourly earnings of $36.20, resulting in a ratio of 6.44. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive, with a rising PSF trend, making it less attractive for talent without wage premiums. The below-average affordability score suggests that businesses may need to offer higher wages to compensate for the cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in Richmond is shrinking at a rate of -0.33% year-over-year, ranking at the 42nd percentile. This contraction in the labor force supply implies a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find qualified candidates. The declining labor force growth rate suggests that the city's workforce is not expanding to meet demand.

Building Permits

Building permits in Richmond are down 24.31% year-over-year, indicating a significant tightening of the housing supply. This decline in permits signals a potential future affordability crisis and workforce accommodation challenges, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. The sharp decline in permits suggests that the city's housing market may become increasingly competitive.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Richmond is 38 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.6%, ranking at the 70th percentile. This suggests a relatively slow market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold, making it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. The rising days on market indicates a normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand-driven surge.

Office Economy

Richmond's office economy is relatively weak, with a professional and office worker share ranking at the 30th percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool is not particularly deep in specialized roles, making it less suited for tech, finance, or consulting businesses. However, the city may be more attractive for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Richmond metro area offers a business a relatively stable labor cost environment and a moderate level of difficulty in hiring, but the single biggest risk or constraint is the declining labor force and tightening housing supply, which may lead to future affordability and workforce accommodation challenges. The city's weak labor demand and declining building permits are significant concerns for businesses considering relocation or expansion. Overall, Richmond's economic character is defined by its contraction in job opportunities and declining housing supply, making it a challenging location for businesses to thrive.

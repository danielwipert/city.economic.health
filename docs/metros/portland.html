## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Portland’s labour market shows signs of softening, with unemployment at 4.9%—0.8 percentage points higher than a year ago (101A_unemp_yoy_pp)—and minimal workforce expansion, as the civilian labour force grew just 0.04% year-on-year (102A_clf_yoy). Employment trends are notably weak: nonfarm payrolls declined 0.69% over the past year (107E_employment_growth_yoy), while the labour demand composite score of 3.33 places Portland in the 14th percentile nationally, reflecting subdued hiring and a 0.58% shortfall in average weekly hours relative to its 12-month baseline (107E_wh_trend_deviation_pct). Wage growth at 3.94% (103B_earnings_yoy) remains modest but aligns with national norms, offering limited offset to broader labour market stagnation.

Housing market indicators point to persistent imbalance. With median days on market at 49 days—down 3.9% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct)—and a composite score in the 16th percentile (204A_dom_composite), turnover remains sluggish despite some improvement in listing velocity. New construction has sharply decelerated, with residential permits down 18.14% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), suggesting constrained supply growth. The cost of living, particularly housing affordability, remains a structural challenge, as reflected in a cost-of-living composite score of 2.02 (104C_col), which ranks in the 67th percentile—indicating above-average pressure relative to income. Office demand remains depressed, underscored by a low office worker ratio score of 1.3 (105C_owr), placing Portland in the 12th percentile and highlighting incomplete recovery in commercial real estate.

**Outlook:** Portland faces near-term headwinds in employment and housing supply, with limited labour demand and construction activity constraining growth. Without a rebound in business investment or meaningful supply-side housing reforms, the city risks prolonged subpar economic performance relative to peer metros.

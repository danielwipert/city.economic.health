Economic Analysis

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area has received an overall grade of D, ranking 22.8th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its struggling labor demand and high unemployment rate. The city's labor demand composite score of 1.72, combining a -1.84% employment growth rate and a -1.485% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a clear contraction in job market activity. This is further exacerbated by an unemployment rate of 4.90%, indicating a labor market with significant slack.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro stands at -1.84%, indicating a decline in the number of jobs available, while the weekly hours deviation from trend is -1.485%, suggesting that existing workers are seeing their hours reduced. This combination signals a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze, as both employment and hours worked are decreasing. This contraction is a significant concern for businesses looking to expand or establish operations in the area.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 4.90%, which, although not extremely high, is still indicative of a labor market with some slack. This rate suggests that there are available workers, making it somewhat easier for businesses to hire, but it also implies weaker local consumer demand. For a business trying to hire in this market, the relatively high unemployment rate could be seen as an advantage in terms of finding candidates, but it also reflects underlying economic challenges.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is +4.98%, placing it near the median in terms of wage growth among the 50 US metros. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are increasing, but at a pace that is neither exceptionally high nor stagnant. For workers, this wage growth is positive, as it indicates an increase in purchasing power, which can be beneficial for the local economy.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 1.96 (price per square foot divided by hourly earnings) and ranking in the 61st percentile, Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is more affordable than many of its peers. This affordability is a talent attraction advantage, as businesses can attract workers without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset high living costs. The relatively low cost of living can be a draw for companies looking to relocate or establish themselves in a more affordable urban environment.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has grown by +0.04% year-over-year, indicating a very slow expansion of the workforce supply. This slow growth implies that the hiring capacity for businesses may be constrained, as the pool of potential workers is not increasing significantly. For companies looking to hire, this slow labor force growth could pose challenges in finding sufficient talent.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -18.14%, indicating a significant tightening in the housing supply. This decline in building permits suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with potential demand. This could lead to increased housing costs and reduced attractiveness for workers considering relocation.

Days on Market

The current median days on market for homes in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 49 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -3.9%. This relatively fast market, as indicated by the low days on market, suggests that homes are selling quickly, which can make it challenging for relocating workers to find and secure housing. The fast pace of the housing market can be a disadvantage for businesses trying to attract talent from other areas.

Office Economy

Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has a professional and office worker share that places it in the bottom tier, with a percentile rank of 15th. This indicates a relatively shallow talent pool in professional and office sectors, making the city less suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, or those looking to establish headquarters. The city's economy is more geared towards industrial or logistics-dominant sectors.

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area offers a mixed bag for businesses, with affordability and moderate wage growth being positives, but labor demand contraction, slow labor force growth, and a tightening housing supply posing significant challenges. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker considering this location is the struggling labor market, which could hinder hiring and expansion plans, making it crucial to carefully weigh these factors in any location decision.

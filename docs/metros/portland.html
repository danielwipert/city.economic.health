Economic Analysis

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its poor labor demand and labor force growth metrics, which are both in the bottom tier. The labor demand composite score of 1.50, combining a -2.85% employment growth rate and a -0.220% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. This, coupled with a labor force growth rate of -1.57%, defines the city's current economic character as one of shrinking workforce supply and diminishing job opportunities.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is -2.85% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.220% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a clear contraction in labor demand rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing a reduction in job openings and hours worked, which can lead to a surplus of labor. The labor demand composite score of 1.50 is in the bottom tier, reinforcing this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 4.80%, which, although not extremely high, indicates some slack in the labor market, ranking in the bottom tier at the 12th percentile. This suggests that while it may not be extremely difficult to hire, the market does have some available labor, potentially mitigating some wage pressure. However, for businesses looking to hire, this rate implies a slightly easier staffing environment compared to tighter labor markets.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is +6.21%, placing it in the above-average category at the 78th percentile. This rate of wage growth is relatively fast, implying rising labor costs for employers but also increased purchasing power for workers. Businesses should anticipate higher compensation costs, but the local workforce will have more disposable income, potentially benefiting consumer-facing industries.

Cost of Living

Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has a cost of living score that ranks in the above-average percentile (65th percentile), with a PSF to earnings ratio of $310/sqft to $42.24/hr, or 7.34. Given that PSF is falling by -2.5% year-over-year, this indicates a relatively affordable environment compared to peer cities. This affordability could serve as a talent attraction advantage, as workers may not require significant wage premiums to maintain their standard of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is shrinking at a rate of -1.57% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor supply. This negative growth rate suggests that the pool of potential workers is decreasing, which could pose a structural headwind for businesses looking to hire and expand in the area.

Building Permits

The city is experiencing a +23.17% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, which is above average, ranking in the 76th percentile. This significant increase in permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which could improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce in the future, mitigating potential housing-related challenges for relocating workers.

Days on Market

Homes in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro are currently sitting on the market for a median of 47 days, with a year-over-year increase of +6.8%, ranking in the above-average percentile (74th percentile). This indicates a slightly slower market, which could make it more accessible for workers relocating to the city, as they may have more time to find suitable housing without facing extreme competition.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is relatively low, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool, while present, is not as deep in professional and office skills as in other metros, making it less suited for businesses heavily reliant on tech, finance, consulting, or HQ operations, but potentially more favorable for industries with different skill requirements.

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area offers a mix of affordability and rising wages, which could attract certain types of businesses, especially those not heavily dependent on a deep professional talent pool. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker considering this location is the shrinking labor force and poor labor demand, which could significantly hinder hiring and expansion plans, making it crucial to carefully weigh these factors against the city's advantages.

Economic Analysis

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area has an overall grade of D, ranking 22.8th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its poor labor demand and labor force growth metrics, which are both in the bottom tier. The labor demand composite score is 1.50, and the labor force growth rate is -1.04% YoY. These numbers signal significant challenges for businesses looking to expand or relocate in this area.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is -2.45% YoY, indicating a contraction in the job market. Without data on weekly hours deviation from the trend, it's difficult to assess whether the remaining workers are experiencing a survivor squeeze. However, the combination of negative employment growth and lack of information on hours worked suggests a lack of genuine demand expansion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in this metro area is 5.50%, which is relatively high, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but it also implies weaker local consumer demand. Businesses may find it relatively easier to staff their operations, but they may also face less disposable income among consumers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is +5.15%, which is above average, ranking in the 67th percentile. This indicates that labor costs for employers are rising, but it also means that workers have more purchasing power. Businesses should be prepared for increasing labor costs, but they can also expect a more affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in this metro area is near the median, with a percentile rank of 50th. This suggests that the city is neither particularly affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. As a result, businesses may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to cost of living, and they may need to offer competitive wages to attract and retain workers.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is shrinking, with a year-over-year growth rate of -1.04%. This contraction in the labor force supply poses a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the workers they need. Businesses should be prepared to compete aggressively for talent or consider alternative locations with more favorable labor market dynamics.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in this metro area has decreased by -18.14% YoY, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, potentially exacerbating the challenges of attracting and retaining workers. Businesses should consider the potential long-term implications of a constrained housing market on their operations.

Days on Market

Data on the current median days on market is not available, making it difficult to assess the state of the housing market for relocating workers. However, without this information, it's challenging to determine whether the market is competitive or accessible for workers looking to move to the area.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is near the median, with a percentile rank of 50th. This suggests that the city has a moderately deep talent pool, making it suitable for a range of businesses, but not necessarily those requiring highly specialized knowledge-economy workers. The city may be more suited for businesses with diverse workforce needs rather than those requiring a high concentration of professional or office workers.

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area presents a mixed bag for businesses, with above-average wage growth and a moderately deep talent pool, but also significant challenges related to labor demand, labor force growth, and housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force and tightening housing market, which may limit the area's attractiveness for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

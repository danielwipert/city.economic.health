Economic Analysis

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score largely defined by its weak labor demand and low office economy share. The labor demand composite score of 1.50, combining a -2.85% employment growth rate and a -0.220% weekly hours deviation, signals a contraction in labor demand. Additionally, the low office economy share of 0.68, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile, highlights the city's limited professional talent pool.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is -2.85% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.220% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city experiencing a decline in both job creation and working hours. As a result, businesses may face a less competitive environment for talent, but also fewer pressures to rapidly expand their workforce.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 4.80%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 12th percentile, indicating a relatively slack labor market. This means that businesses may find it easier to hire workers, as there is a larger pool of available talent, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. However, the relatively high unemployment rate compared to other metros may limit wage pressure, making it a more attractive location for businesses looking to manage labor costs.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is +6.21%, ranking above average at the 78th percentile, indicating relatively fast wage growth. This implies that employer labor costs are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing, which can be beneficial for businesses that rely on a strong local consumer base. However, rapid wage growth may also lead to increased labor costs for businesses, potentially affecting their bottom line.

Cost of Living

Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has a cost of living score ranking above average at the 65th percentile, with a PSF of $310/sqft and average hourly earnings of $42.24/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.34. The city's cost of living is relatively affordable, with the PSF falling -2.5% year-over-year, making it an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums. This can be a major advantage for businesses looking to attract and retain workers without breaking the bank.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is contracting at a rate of -1.57% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This contraction in labor supply may create structural headwinds for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the talent they need to expand or maintain their operations. As a result, businesses may need to consider alternative locations or strategies to attract workers.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is increasing by +23.17% year-over-year, indicating an expansion in housing supply. This growth in building permits signals that developer confidence is rising, and future housing supply is likely to increase, which can improve affordability and make the city more attractive to relocating workers. However, the current pace of growth may not be sufficient to meet demand, and businesses should monitor the situation closely.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is currently 47 days, with a year-over-year increase of +6.8%, indicating a slowing market. This means that workers relocating to the city may find a more accessible and less competitive housing market, which can be beneficial for businesses looking to attract talent. However, the slowing market may also indicate weakening demand, which could have implications for local consumer spending and economic growth.

Office Economy

The office economy in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is relatively underdeveloped, with a professional and office worker share of 0.68, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile. This indicates a limited talent pool for businesses that require specialized office or professional workers, making the city less suited for tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in industrial or logistics sectors, which can capitalize on the available workforce and relatively affordable cost of living.

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a slowing housing market, making it an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums. However, the city's weak labor demand, contracting labor force, and limited office economy share pose significant risks and constraints for businesses, particularly those that rely on a strong professional talent pool. As a result, decision-makers should carefully weigh these factors when considering the city as a potential location for their business.

Economic Analysis

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area has an overall grade of D, ranking 24.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its struggling labor demand and high unemployment rate. The city's labor demand composite score of 1.50 and unemployment rate of 5.50% are key metrics defining its current economic character, with the -2.45% employment growth YoY and -1.727% weekly hours deviation from trend being particularly concerning. These numbers indicate a city facing significant economic headwinds.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is -2.45% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating -1.727% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city instead experiencing a decline in job creation and hours worked. The result is a labor market characterized by reduced opportunities and increased competition for existing roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro stands at 5.50%, which, although not extremely high, still indicates some slack in the labor market. This rate corresponds to a bottom-tier percentile rank of 8th, suggesting that while hiring may be somewhat easier than in tighter markets, local consumer demand may be weaker. For a business trying to hire in this city, the relatively higher unemployment rate could provide a larger pool of potential candidates, but may also reflect underlying economic challenges.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is +5.15% YoY, placing it in the 66th percentile, which is above average. This rate of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but it also indicates good news for worker purchasing power. As wages increase, the local workforce may have more disposable income, potentially benefiting businesses that rely on consumer spending.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $312/sqft to $41.82/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.46, and a percentile rank of 63rd, Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is more affordable than many of its peers. The fact that PSF is falling -2.5% YoY relative to wages further enhances the city's affordability. This means that the city has a talent attraction advantage, as workers may not require significant wage premiums to afford the cost of living, making it easier for businesses to attract and retain staff without incurring excessive labor costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is growing at a rate of -1.04% YoY, indicating a contraction in the labor force supply. This negative growth rate suggests that the workforce is shrinking, presenting a structural headwind for hiring and potentially limiting the city's ability to support business expansion.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -18.14%, signaling a tightening in housing supply. This decline in permits suggests that developer confidence may be waning, and the future housing supply may not keep pace with demand, potentially leading to affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The current median days on market for homes in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 49 days, with a YoY direction of -3.9%. This relatively fast pace of home sales, combined with the decreasing trend, indicates a competitive market that may be challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, the pace is not excessively fast, suggesting some balance between demand and supply.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share composite score of 0.79, ranking in the 11th percentile, Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses requiring deep knowledge-economy talent, such as tech, finance, consulting, or HQ operations, and may be more appropriate for industries with different workforce requirements.

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area presents a mixed bag for businesses, with some advantages in terms of affordability and wage growth, but significant challenges related to labor demand, labor force growth, and housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker considering this location is the city's struggling labor market, characterized by declining employment growth and a shrinking labor force, which could limit the ability to hire and expand operations.

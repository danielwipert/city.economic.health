Economic Analysis

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 32.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score largely defined by its weak labor demand and high wage growth. The labor demand composite score of 1.50, combining a -2.85% employment growth rate and a -0.220% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. This, coupled with a strong 6.21% year-over-year wage growth, suggests a complex economic environment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is -2.85%, indicating a decline in job additions, while the weekly hours deviation from trend is -0.220%, signaling hours are running below the 12-month baseline. This combination suggests a labor demand contraction rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The negative employment growth rate is a significant concern for businesses looking to expand or establish operations in the area.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 5.20%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile, indicating a relatively high level of unemployment compared to other metros. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but it also implies weaker local consumer demand. Businesses may find it relatively easier to staff their operations, but the local economy's overall health could be a concern.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 6.21%, placing it in the 78th percentile, indicating fast wage growth. This rapid increase in wages suggests rising labor costs for employers but also implies strong worker purchasing power. Businesses should be prepared for higher labor costs, but the local workforce will have increased disposable income, potentially benefiting consumer-facing industries.

Cost of Living

Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has a cost of living score that ranks in the 67th percentile, with a PSF of $311/sqft and average hourly earnings of $42.24/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.36. The PSF is falling by 2.8% year-over-year, contributing to the city's relative affordability. This makes the city more attractive for talent without requiring significant wage premiums, as the cost of living is more manageable compared to peer cities.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is growing at a rate of -1.39% year-over-year, indicating a contraction in the labor force supply. This negative growth rate suggests that the workforce is shrinking, posing a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion. Businesses may face challenges in finding and retaining talent due to the declining labor pool.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has increased by 18.23% year-over-year, signaling an expansion in housing supply. This growth in permits suggests that developer confidence is strong, and future housing supply is expected to increase, which could improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. This is a positive sign for businesses concerned about the ability of their employees to find affordable housing.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro is 47 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.8%. This indicates a slower market, where homes are taking longer to sell. For workers relocating to the city, this could mean a more accessible and less competitive housing market, allowing them more time to find suitable housing.

Office Economy

Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro has an office economy score ranking in the 8th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers at 0.68. This suggests a relatively shallow talent pool in professional and office sectors, making the city less suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, or HQ operations that rely heavily on such talent. However, it might be more suitable for industries with different workforce requirements.

The Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro metro area offers a complex mix of high wage growth and a contracting labor force, making it challenging for businesses to navigate. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor pool, which could significantly impact hiring capacity and business expansion plans. Despite some positive indicators like increasing building permits and a relatively affordable cost of living, the overall economic character of the city, defined by its weak labor demand and labor force contraction, poses significant challenges for businesses considering location decisions.

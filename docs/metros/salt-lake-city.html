Economic Analysis

Salt Lake City-Murray has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 63.7th percentile among 50 US metros, with a strong labor demand and a deep professional talent pool being the two metrics that most define its current economic character, scoring 86th and 94th percentiles respectively. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.43 and office/professional worker share of 4.40 out of 5 indicate a robust economy. With employment growth of 0.94% and weekly hours 0.187% below trend, the city is experiencing genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 0.94% and weekly hours deviation of -0.187% signal genuine demand expansion, as the city is adding jobs and hours are running slightly below trend, indicating a healthy labor market. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing a period of growth, with employers hiring and workers having a moderate workload. The labor demand composite score of 5.43 further supports this, ranking in the top tier at 86th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 3.90% indicates a near-median labor market, ranking in the 56th percentile, meaning that there is some slack in the market, but not excessively so. This rate suggests that businesses may face some competition for talent, but it is still relatively accessible. The practical implication for a business trying to hire here is that they may need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of 4.34% is above average, ranking in the 60th percentile, indicating moderate to fast wage growth. This rate suggests that employer labor costs are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing. As a result, businesses may need to budget for higher labor costs, but they can also expect a more affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 6.69, where PSF is $263/sqft and hourly earnings are $39.31, and a percentile rank of 65th, Salt Lake City-Murray is relatively affordable compared to its peers. The fact that PSF is falling by 0.8% year-over-year further supports this. This affordability means that the city has a talent attraction advantage, as workers can maintain a high standard of living without requiring excessive wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The year-over-year growth rate of the civilian labor force is -0.50%, ranking in the 31st percentile, indicating a contracting labor pool. This contraction implies that businesses may face structural headwinds when trying to hire, as the supply of workers is shrinking. As a result, companies may need to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is 14.73%, ranking in the 70th percentile, indicating an expansion of housing supply. This growth suggests that developer confidence is high, and future affordability and workforce accommodation are likely to improve. As a result, businesses can expect a more stable and affordable housing market for their employees.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 45 days, with a year-over-year direction of 0.0%, ranking in the 24th percentile. This indicates a relatively fast market, where homes are selling quickly. For a worker relocating to this city, this means that they may face a competitive housing market, and may need to act quickly to secure a home.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share of 4.40 out of 5, ranking in the 94th percentile, Salt Lake City-Murray has a deep and specialized talent pool, making it well-suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This city is less suited for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses, but its strong professional sector makes it an attractive location for knowledge-economy companies.

In conclusion, Salt Lake City-Murray offers businesses a strong labor market, a deep professional talent pool, and a relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for companies looking to expand or relocate. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the contracting labor pool, which may require businesses to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent.

Economic Analysis

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 56.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score at the 78th percentile, and its top-tier office economy, ranking at the 95th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a combination of employment growth and hours worked above trend, signaling genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has an employment growth rate of 1.08% year-over-year and a weekly hours deviation of 0.047% above its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. The labor demand composite score of 5.86 is above average, ranking at the 78th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.10%, ranking at the 46th percentile, indicating a near-median level of slack in the labor market. This means that while the market is not extremely tight, it is still relatively competitive for businesses trying to hire, with some wage pressure. However, it is not as challenging as in cities with lower unemployment rates.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.93%, ranking above average at the 65th percentile. This indicates moderate to fast wage growth, which will lead to rising labor costs for employers but also increase worker purchasing power. As a result, businesses may need to adjust their compensation packages to remain competitive.

Cost of Living

Salt Lake City-Murray has a cost of living ratio of $265/sqft to $39.31/hr, with a near-median percentile rank of 59th. This means that the city is neither extremely affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. However, with a stable PSF-to-earnings ratio, the city may still attract talent without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Salt Lake City-Murray is contracting at a rate of -0.73% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 20th percentile. This indicates that the labor pool is shrinking, which may create structural headwinds for hiring and limit the city's ability to support rapid business growth.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Salt Lake City-Murray has decreased by 3.28% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 48th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is not expanding rapidly, which may lead to future affordability concerns and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Salt Lake City-Murray is 44 days, with no year-over-year change. This indicates a relatively fast-paced market, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, the stable days on market suggests that the market is not becoming more competitive.

Office Economy

The office economy in Salt Lake City-Murray is deep, with a professional and office worker share ranking at the 95th percentile. This makes the city an attractive location for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a highly skilled and specialized workforce. However, it may be less suited for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand and a deep professional talent pool, making it an attractive location for knowledge-economy companies. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force, which may limit the city's ability to support rapid business growth and create challenges in hiring and retaining talent.

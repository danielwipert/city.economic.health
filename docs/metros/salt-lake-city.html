Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Salt Lake City's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.4%, which, although relatively low, has increased by 0.4 percentage points year-over-year. This uptick is somewhat mitigated by a modest growth in the civilian labour force, with a 0.62% year-over-year change. Average hourly earnings have risen by 3.58% over the same period, suggesting that workers are experiencing some wage growth. However, the labour demand composite score of 7.43, coupled with a nonfarm payroll employment growth rate of 1.83% year-over-year, indicates a relatively strong labour market.

In terms of housing and business activity, Salt Lake City is experiencing a surge in residential construction, with a 14.73% year-over-year increase in building permits. This could be a response to the city's relatively high cost of living composite score of 2.43, which may be driving up demand for new housing. The office worker ratio composite score of 4.28 is also noteworthy, suggesting a strong presence of office workers in the city. However, the median days on market for homes has remained steady at 45 days, with no year-over-year change, which could indicate a balanced housing market. The labour market's weekly hours trend deviation of 0.564% from its 12-month baseline is a minor concern, but overall, the city's labour demand composite score and employment growth rate suggest a robust economy.

The city's overall economic strength is reflected in its high office worker ratio composite score and labour demand composite score, both of which are in the 96th percentile. While the labour market is experiencing some stress, as evidenced by the rising unemployment rate, the growth in average hourly earnings and the strong labour demand suggest that the city's economy is resilient. The residential construction boom is also a positive sign, as it indicates confidence in the city's future growth prospects. In conclusion, Salt Lake City's economy is characterized by a mix of positive and negative signals, but its overall strength and resilience are likely to support continued growth in the medium term.

Closing outlook: Salt Lake City's economy is expected to continue growing, driven by its strong labour market and residential construction activity. However, the city's policymakers should monitor the rising unemployment rate and take steps to address any potential labour market imbalances. With its high office worker ratio and labour demand composite score, Salt Lake City is well-positioned to attract new businesses and investment, which should support its long-term economic growth prospects.

Economic Analysis

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Salt Lake City’s economy shows a mixed but broadly resilient profile, anchored by strong labor demand and solid business activity, though tempered by modest workforce expansion and elevated housing market tightness. The labor market remains tight, with an unemployment rate of 3.4% (101A_unemployment), up 0.4 percentage points from a year ago (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), suggesting some softening in labor market balance. Nevertheless, labor demand remains robust, as evidenced by a 1.83% year-over-year increase in nonfarm employment (107E_employment_growth_yoy) and a labor demand composite score in the 96th percentile nationally (107E_ldc_composite). Weekly hours worked are slightly above the city’s 12-month trend (107E_wh_trend_deviation_pct: +0.564%), and wage growth is steady at 3.58% (103B_earnings_yoy), though not outpacing inflation meaningfully. The civilian labor force has grown only 0.62% over the past year (102A_clf_yoy), indicating constrained labor supply despite healthy job creation.

Housing market dynamics present a notable stress point. While residential building permits are up 14.73% year-over-year (200B_permits_yoy), signaling responsive supply growth, the median home remains on the market for 45 days (204A_dom_level_days), with no year-over-year change in days on market (204A_dom_yoy_pct: 0.0%), reflecting persistent demand pressure. The housing market’s composite score ranks in the 26th percentile (204A_dom_composite), indicating relative tightness. Meanwhile, the cost of living, measured as a price-to-income ratio, is near median nationally (104C_col: 2.43; 56th percentile), suggesting affordability is not yet severely strained. Office occupancy trends are strong, with an office worker ratio composite score in the 96th percentile (105C_owr), underscoring sustained demand for commercial space relative to peers.

**Outlook:** Salt Lake City’s economy is likely to maintain moderate momentum, supported by resilient labor demand and construction activity. However, constrained labor force growth and housing market tightness could limit upside potential in the medium term. Absent a pickup in workforce participation or accelerated housing supply, wage pressures and affordability constraints may persist.

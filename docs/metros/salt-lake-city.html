Economic Analysis

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 63.8th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and deep office economy talent pool. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.71, which combines a 0.74% year-over-year employment growth rate and a 0.375% deviation in weekly hours above its own trend, signals a genuine demand expansion. This is further supported by its top-tier percentile ranks in both employment growth and weekly hours deviation, at 86th percentile.

Labor Demand

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has seen a 0.74% year-over-year employment growth rate, combined with a 0.375% deviation in weekly hours above its own trend, indicating a genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing a period of job growth, with hours worked above the trend, suggesting that businesses are actively seeking to hire and expand their operations. The labor demand composite score of 5.71 further reinforces this notion, placing the city in the top tier of labor demand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Salt Lake City-Murray stands at 3.40%, ranking in the 60th percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to cities with tighter labor markets. However, this also implies that local consumer demand may not be as strong as in areas with lower unemployment rates.

Wage Growth

The city has experienced a 4.04% year-over-year wage growth rate, ranking in the 49th percentile, which is near the median. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit at a moderate pace. This balance could be beneficial for businesses looking to manage labor costs while still attracting and retaining talent.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 1.83, ranking in the 67th percentile, Salt Lake City-Murray is more affordable than many of its peers. This affordability advantage means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset high living costs, potentially reducing their labor costs and improving their competitiveness.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Salt Lake City-Murray has grown by 0.62% year-over-year, ranking in the 40th percentile, indicating a near-median expansion of the workforce supply. This positive growth rate suggests that the labor pool is expanding, which could support hiring capacity and business growth, although at a moderate pace.

Building Permits

The city has seen a 14.73% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking in the 68th percentile, indicating an expansion in housing supply. This rise in building permits signals developer confidence in the market and suggests that future housing affordability and workforce accommodation may improve, supporting the attraction and retention of talent.

Days on Market

Homes in Salt Lake City-Murray currently sit on the market for a median of 45 days, with a 0% year-over-year change, ranking in the 26th percentile. This relatively fast market, especially considering the context of strong job growth, may pose challenges for relocating workers, as they may face competitive pressure in finding and securing housing.

Office Economy

With an office and professional worker share composite score of 4.54, ranking in the 94th percentile, Salt Lake City-Murray boasts a deep and talented professional pool. This makes the city particularly well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less ideal for those in industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand, a deep office economy talent pool, and relative affordability, making it an attractive location for certain types of businesses. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers to factor in is the competitive housing market, which could pose challenges for relocating workers and may require businesses to offer competitive compensation packages to attract and retain talent.

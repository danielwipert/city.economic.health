Economic Analysis

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 58.8th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a top-tier composite score of 86th percentile, and its deep professional talent pool, ranking at the 94th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a 0.94% year-over-year employment growth rate and a -0.187% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend.

Labor Demand

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has a labor demand composite score of 5.43, driven by a 0.94% year-over-year employment growth rate and a -0.187% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are slightly below trend during a period of job growth. The strong labor demand indicates a competitive job market with a high level of economic activity.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.10%, ranking at the 43rd percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This rate suggests that the market has some slack, but it is not overly loose, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. The relatively low unemployment rate implies that businesses may face some wage pressure when trying to attract talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.34%, ranking at the 59th percentile, indicating a moderate level of wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and that workers have some bargaining power. The moderate wage growth rate implies that businesses can expect to pay somewhat higher wages to attract and retain talent.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Salt Lake City-Murray is near median, with a ratio of $265 per square foot to $39.31 per hour, ranking at the 59th percentile. This means that the city is neither extremely affordable nor extremely expensive relative to its peers. The near-median cost of living implies that businesses may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to affordability, but they also do not need to offer large wage premiums to compensate for high costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Salt Lake City-Murray is contracting at a rate of -0.73% year-over-year, ranking at the 20th percentile. This indicates that the labor force supply is shrinking, which may create a structural headwind for hiring. The declining labor force growth rate implies that businesses may face challenges in finding qualified talent, particularly in the long term.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Salt Lake City-Murray is increasing at a rate of 14.73% year-over-year, ranking at the 68th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The rising building permits imply that the city is likely to have a more stable and affordable housing market in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Salt Lake City-Murray is 44 days, with a year-over-year change of 0.0%, ranking at the 24th percentile. This indicates that the housing market is relatively fast-paced, making it challenging for relocating workers to find homes. The low days on market imply that businesses may need to provide relocation assistance or other incentives to help employees navigate the competitive housing market.

Office Economy

The professional and office worker share in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.42, ranking at the 94th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool. This city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. The strong office economy implies that businesses can tap into a highly skilled and knowledgeable workforce.

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand, a deep professional talent pool, and a moderately growing economy. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses is the shrinking labor force supply, which may create challenges in finding qualified talent and drive up labor costs.

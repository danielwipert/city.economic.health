Economic Analysis

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has an overall grade of A- with a composite score ranking at the 62.6th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a top-tier composite score of 84th percentile, and its deep professional talent pool, ranking at the 94th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a 1.33% year-over-year employment growth rate and a -0.047% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend.

Labor Demand

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has a labor demand composite score of 6.12, driven by a 1.33% year-over-year employment growth rate and a -0.047% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are not significantly above trend despite job growth. The strong labor demand indicates a competitive job market with many opportunities for workers.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Salt Lake City-Murray is 3.60%, ranking at the 57th percentile, indicating a near-median level of slack in the labor market. This rate suggests that the market is not extremely tight, but still relatively competitive, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. As a result, businesses may face some wage pressure, but it is not as intense as in cities with lower unemployment rates.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.31%, ranking at the 58th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not extremely rapidly, and worker purchasing power is increasing. The moderate wage growth provides a relatively stable environment for businesses to operate, without extreme pressure on labor costs.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Salt Lake City-Murray is relatively high, with a percentile rank of 39th, and a price-to-salary ratio of $266/sqft to $39.12/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.80. This means that the city is less affordable compared to its peers, which may require businesses to offer wage premiums to attract talent. However, the relatively high cost of living may also indicate a strong local economy with high demand for housing.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Salt Lake City-Murray is contracting at a rate of -1.05% year-over-year, ranking at the 37th percentile. This contraction indicates that the labor pool is shrinking, which may pose a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion. As a result, businesses may need to invest in talent development and retention strategies to overcome the limited labor supply.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Salt Lake City-Murray has increased by 23.54% year-over-year, ranking at the 78th percentile. This significant increase in building permits signals that housing supply is expanding, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The rising permits also indicate developer confidence in the local market, which may lead to increased economic activity.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Salt Lake City-Murray is 45 days, with a year-over-year increase of 2.3%, ranking at the 50th percentile. This relatively fast pace of home sales indicates a competitive market, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, the rising days on market may also signal a healthy normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand erosion.

Office Economy

The professional and office worker share in Salt Lake City-Murray is 4.32, ranking at the 94th percentile, indicating a deep and specialized talent pool. This city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of professional workers. However, it may be less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand, a deep professional talent pool, and a growing housing supply, making it an attractive location for companies in the tech and finance sectors. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this city is the shrinking labor pool, which may pose challenges for hiring and expansion, and require investments in talent development and retention strategies.

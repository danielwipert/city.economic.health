Economic Analysis

Salt Lake City's economy exhibits a mixed yet resilient profile, driven by robust labor demand and solid business activity. However, this is tempered by modest workforce expansion and a tight housing market. The labor market remains tight, with an unemployment rate of 3.4%, up 0.4 percentage points from a year ago, indicating some softening. Despite this, labor demand remains strong, as evidenced by a 1.83% year-over-year increase in nonfarm employment and a labor demand composite score in the 96th percentile nationally. Weekly hours worked are slightly above the city's 12-month trend, with a 0.564% deviation, and wage growth is steady at 3.58%, although it is not outpacing inflation significantly.

The civilian labor force has grown only 0.62% over the past year, suggesting constrained labor supply despite healthy job creation. In contrast, housing market dynamics present a notable challenge. While residential building permits have increased by 14.73% year-over-year, indicating responsive supply growth, the median home remains on the market for 45 days, with no year-over-year change. This reflects persistent demand pressure, and the housing market's composite score ranks in the 26th percentile, indicating relative tightness. The cost of living, measured as a price-to-income ratio of 2.43, is near the national median, ranking in the 56th percentile, which suggests that affordability is not yet severely strained.

Office occupancy trends are strong, with an office worker ratio composite score in the 96th percentile, underscoring sustained demand for commercial space. Looking ahead, Salt Lake City's economy is likely to maintain moderate momentum, supported by resilient labor demand and construction activity. However, constrained labor force growth and housing market tightness may limit upside potential in the medium term. Unless there is a pickup in workforce participation or an acceleration in housing supply, wage pressures and affordability constraints may persist.

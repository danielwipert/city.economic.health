## Original analysis draft (for reference)

St. Louis exhibits a mixed economic profile, with a relatively stable labour market offset by sluggish broader employment trends. The city’s unemployment rate stands at 3.5% (101A_unemployment), slightly below the national average and unchanged meaningfully from a year ago (101A_unemp_yoy_pp: +0.2 pp), while the civilian labour force has grown modestly at 1.01% year-on-year (102A_clf_yoy). However, labour demand remains soft, as indicated by flat nonfarm employment growth (107E_employment_growth_yoy: -0.01%) and a composite labour demand score (107E_ldc_composite: 4.29) that reflects below-average performance. Average hourly earnings growth is notably weak at 0.71% (103B_earnings_yoy), placing St. Louis in the 8th percentile nationally, suggesting limited wage pressure and potentially subdued consumer income growth.

Housing market indicators point to a decelerating sector. Residential building permits have declined by 7.0% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), and homes are taking longer to sell—median days on market has risen 17.95% year-on-year to 46 days (204A_dom_level_days), a trend that ranks among the slowest in the peer group (204A_dom_composite: 8.68, 90th percentile). The cost of living remains moderate relative to incomes (104C_col: 3.38), though this advantage is tempered by limited office occupancy momentum (105C_owr: 2.14) and compressed working hours, with weekly hours 1.8% below their 12-month trend (107E_wh_trend_deviation_pct). Together, these signals suggest constrained business activity and housing turnover.

**Outlook:** St. Louis is likely to experience continued modest labour force expansion but faces headwinds from stagnant employment growth, weak earnings momentum, and a cooling housing market. Without stronger private sector job creation or wage acceleration, the city’s economic trajectory is likely to remain below potential.

Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The St. Louis economy presents a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals emerging from its key indicators. On the labor market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.5%, which, although relatively low, has seen a slight increase of 0.2 percentage points year-over-year. This modest rise in unemployment is accompanied by a sluggish growth in the civilian labor force, with a year-over-year change of 1.01%. Average hourly earnings have also grown at a modest pace, with a year-over-year increase of 0.71%. These labor market indicators suggest a degree of stability, but also point to potential challenges in terms of workforce supply growth and wage pressures.

In terms of business activity, the office worker ratio composite score of 2.14 indicates a relatively low level of office-based employment, which may be a concern for the city's commercial real estate sector. The labor demand composite score of 4.29, while not particularly strong, does suggest some underlying momentum in employment and weekly hours worked. However, the year-over-year change in nonfarm payroll employment is essentially flat, at -0.01%, which may indicate a lack of dynamism in the city's job market. The weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is also negative, at -1.805%, which could be a sign of slowing economic activity.

The housing market in St. Louis appears to be experiencing some stress, with residential building permits declining by 6.99% year-over-year. The median days on market has increased by 17.95% year-over-year, to a current level of 46 days, which may indicate a degree of softness in the market. The cost of living composite score of 3.38 is relatively low, which could be a positive factor for attracting businesses and talent to the city. Overall, while St. Louis has some strengths, such as its relatively low cost of living, its economy appears to be facing some challenges, particularly in terms of labor market growth and business activity.

Closing outlook: The St. Louis economy is likely to continue to face challenges in the near term, particularly in terms of labor market growth and business activity. However, its relatively low cost of living and stable labor market fundamentals may help to support economic stability and attract new investment to the city. Policymakers and business leaders may need to focus on initiatives to boost workforce supply growth, support small businesses and entrepreneurship, and enhance the city's commercial real estate sector in order to drive more dynamic economic growth.

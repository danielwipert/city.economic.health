Economic Analysis

St. Louis has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 39th out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand, which is below average, and its wage growth, which is in the bottom tier at 12th percentile with a YoY growth rate of +0.64%. The combination of a -0.58% employment growth rate and a -1.143% weekly hours deviation from trend suggests a contraction in labor demand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in St. Louis is -0.58% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating -1.143% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not adding jobs at a significant rate and hours are below trend. This suggests that businesses may not face intense competition for labor, but it also indicates a slower economy.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in St. Louis is 4.40%, ranking it near the median at the 51st percentile. This suggests that the market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire. However, the relatively moderate unemployment rate also means that local consumer demand may not be extremely strong.

Wage Growth

The YoY wage growth rate in St. Louis is +0.64%, which is stagnant and ranks in the bottom tier at the 12th percentile. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising quickly, but it also implies that workers have weak bargaining power and limited purchasing power.

Cost of Living

St. Louis has a cost of living score that ranks it below average at the 31st percentile, with a PSF of $167/sqft and average hourly earnings of $36.95/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.52. The fact that PSF is rising +1.2% YoY relative to wages means that the city is becoming less affordable. This makes it more challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in St. Louis is growing at a rate of -0.23% YoY, indicating that the labor pool is contracting. This contraction implies that businesses may face structural headwinds when trying to hire, as the supply of workers is shrinking.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in St. Louis is increasing by +9.19% YoY, which is above average and ranks at the 64th percentile. This expansion in housing supply suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may improve, making it easier for businesses to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market in St. Louis is 42 days, with a YoY increase of +7.7%, ranking it in the top tier at the 80th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively long time, indicating a slower and more accessible market for workers relocating to the city.

Office Economy

The professional and office worker share in St. Louis is relatively low, ranking in the bottom tier at the 16th percentile. This suggests that the city has a limited talent pool for businesses requiring specialized office or professional skills, making it less suited for tech, finance, or consulting companies, but potentially more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

In conclusion, St. Louis offers businesses a relatively slow economy with limited labor demand and stagnant wage growth, but also a slower and more accessible housing market. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's contracting labor force, which may pose structural challenges for hiring and talent attraction.

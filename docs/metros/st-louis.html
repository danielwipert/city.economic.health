Economic Analysis

St. Louis earns a B- grade, ranking 46.7th among 50 US metros, with a single most important takeaway being its stagnant wage growth of +0.66% YoY, a bottom-tier ranking that raises concerns about consumer spending power and market health. The city's 3.50% unemployment rate and 65.27% labor force participation rate suggest a constructive labor market, but wage stagnation is a significant concern.

The housing market presents a notable tension, with homes taking 46 days to sell and a 17.9% year-over-year increase, indicating a slowing market. This slowdown, combined with a below-average cost of living composite score of 3.50, suggests that the city's affordability advantage may be a double-edged sword. The low cost of living is attractive, but the slowing housing market may indicate weakening demand or a normalization of the market after rapid growth.

St. Louis offers a relatively stable labor market and an affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for companies seeking to minimize costs. However, the primary risk is the potential for weakening consumer demand, driven by stagnant wage growth and a slowing housing market, which could impact the city's long-term economic vitality.

Economic Analysis

St. Louis has an overall grade of C, ranking 36.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 3.26, which is below average at the 29th percentile. The city's current economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand and stagnant wage growth, with employment growth at -0.74% year-over-year and wage growth at +0.79% year-over-year. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in St. Louis is -0.74% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -1.534% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city experiencing a decline in both jobs and hours worked. As a result, businesses may face a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in St. Louis is 4.40%, which is near the median at the 48th percentile. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also means that businesses may still face some competition for talent, particularly in certain industries.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in St. Louis is +0.79%, which is in the bottom tier at the 11th percentile. This stagnant wage growth signals a flat cost environment for employers, but also weak bargaining power for workers. As a result, businesses may not face significant pressure to increase wages, but workers may also have limited purchasing power.

Cost of Living

St. Louis has a cost of living ratio of $167/sqft to $37.25/hr, with a percentile rank of 43, indicating that the city is near the median in terms of affordability. However, with the PSF increasing by 1.2% year-over-year, the city is becoming slightly less affordable. This means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent, particularly if they are relocating workers from more affordable areas.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in St. Louis is growing at a rate of -0.23% year-over-year, indicating a contraction in the labor pool. This signals a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of workers is shrinking. Businesses may need to be more competitive in their hiring practices or consider alternative locations with more robust labor force growth.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in St. Louis is decreasing by -6.99% year-over-year, which is near the median at the 51st percentile. This suggests that housing supply is not expanding significantly, which could lead to a supply squeeze and decreased affordability in the future. As a result, businesses may face challenges in accommodating a growing workforce, particularly if they are relocating workers to the area.

Days on Market

The median days on market in St. Louis is 42 days, with a year-over-year increase of 7.7%, which is above average at the 78th percentile. This signals a slower market, where homes are taking longer to sell. For workers relocating to the city, this means that they may have more time to find a home and negotiate a purchase, making the city more accessible.

Office Economy

St. Louis has a professional and office worker share of 1.28, which is in the bottom tier at the 17th percentile. This indicates that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-oriented economies.

In conclusion, St. Louis offers businesses a relatively low-cost environment with a flat wage growth landscape, but also presents challenges in terms of labor demand and labor force growth. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering relocation to St. Louis is the shrinking labor pool and limited talent pool in professional and office sectors, which may require more competitive hiring practices or alternative location considerations.

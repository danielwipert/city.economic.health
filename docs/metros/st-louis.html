Economic Analysis

St. Louis has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 47.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its below-average labor demand, with a composite score of 3.86 ranking it at the 37th percentile, and its relatively slow days on market, with homes selling in 42 days, a 7.7% year-over-year increase. The labor demand and office economy metrics are particularly noteworthy, with the latter ranking at the 21st percentile.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate is not available, but the weekly hours deviation from trend is -1.143%, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, suggesting that the city's labor market is not experiencing significant growth. The below-average labor demand composite score of 3.86 further reinforces this notion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in St. Louis is 4.40%, ranking it at the 50th percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This suggests that the market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to cities with tighter labor markets. However, this also implies that local consumer demand may be weaker due to the availability of labor.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate is not available, but the city's overall ranking suggests that wage growth is likely near median. If wage growth is indeed moderate, this would imply that labor costs for employers are rising at a steady pace, while worker purchasing power is increasing, but not rapidly. This could have implications for businesses looking to manage labor costs while also attracting and retaining talent.

Cost of Living

St. Louis has a cost of living ranking near the median, suggesting that it is neither particularly affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. With a percentile rank near the median, the city may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to affordability, and businesses may need to consider wage premiums to attract top talent.

Labor Force Growth

The year-over-year growth rate of the civilian labor force is not available, but the city's overall ranking suggests that labor force growth is likely near median. If labor force growth is indeed moderate, this would imply that the workforce supply is expanding, but not rapidly, which could have implications for hiring capacity and the ability of businesses to find skilled workers.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is not available, but the city's overall ranking suggests that housing supply is likely expanding at a moderate pace. If building permits are indeed rising, this would signal that housing supply is expanding, which could improve affordability and make it easier for workers to relocate to the city.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 42 days, with a 7.7% year-over-year increase, ranking it at the 80th percentile. This suggests that the housing market is relatively slow, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this could also indicate that the market is normalizing after a period of rapid growth.

Office Economy

The office economy in St. Louis ranks at the 21st percentile, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, and may be more suitable for industries with fewer specialized roles.

In conclusion, St. Louis offers a relatively stable labor market with some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but also implies weaker local consumer demand. However, the city's below-average labor demand and shallow office economy talent pool are significant risks that decision-makers should factor in when considering a location in St. Louis.

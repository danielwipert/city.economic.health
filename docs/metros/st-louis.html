Economic Analysis

St. Louis has an overall grade of C, ranking 36.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 3.26, which is below average at the 30th percentile. The city's current economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand and stagnant wage growth, with employment growth at -0.74% year-over-year and wage growth at +0.79% year-over-year. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in St. Louis is -0.74% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -1.534% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city's labor market experiencing a decline in both jobs and hours worked. As a result, businesses may face a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in St. Louis is 4.60%, which is below average at the 38th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses trying to hire in the city may face challenges in finding available talent, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract workers. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also suggests that the local consumer demand may be weaker than in other cities.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in St. Louis is +0.79%, which is in the bottom tier at the 13th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that employer labor costs are not rising rapidly, but it also implies that worker purchasing power is not increasing significantly. As a result, businesses may not need to worry about rapidly rising labor costs, but they may also not benefit from a highly productive and motivated workforce.

Cost of Living

St. Louis has a cost of living percentile rank of 42, with a PSF of $166/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.25/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.46. This means that the city is near the median in terms of affordability, neither extremely expensive nor highly attractive for talent without wage premiums. The fact that PSF is rising 1.8% year-over-year relative to wages may indicate that the city's affordability is decreasing over time.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in St. Louis is growing at a rate of -0.14% year-over-year, indicating a slight contraction in the labor force supply. This means that the hiring capacity for businesses in the city may be limited, and they may face challenges in finding available talent. The shrinking labor pool may also signal a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in St. Louis is decreasing by -6.99% year-over-year, which is near the median at the 54th percentile. This decline in building permits suggests that the housing supply in the city may be tightening, which could lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for housing in the future. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact on their workforce's ability to find affordable housing.

Days on Market

The median days on market in St. Louis is 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of 17.9%, which puts the city in the top tier at the 90th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively long time, indicating a slower and more buyer-friendly market. For workers relocating to the city, this may mean a more accessible and less competitive housing market, but it also signals a potential demand erosion in the local real estate market.

Office Economy

St. Louis has a professional and office worker share of 1.26, which is in the bottom tier at the 20th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This means that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

In conclusion, St. Louis offers a relatively low-cost environment for businesses, but its weak labor demand, stagnant wage growth, and shrinking labor force supply pose significant challenges for companies looking to expand or relocate. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's limited hiring capacity and potential affordability issues in the future, which may impact their ability to attract and retain talent.

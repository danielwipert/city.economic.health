Economic Analysis

The St. Louis metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 38.8th out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier, and its relatively low unemployment rate, which ranks in the 70th percentile. The labor demand composite score of 3.04, combined with an employment growth rate of +0.17% and a weekly hours deviation of -1.702%, signals a contraction in labor demand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in St. Louis is +0.17%, which is a slow pace, and the weekly hours deviation is -1.702%, indicating that hours are running below trend. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The low labor demand composite score of 3.04, ranking in the 10th percentile, further supports this conclusion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in St. Louis is 3.50%, which ranks in the 70th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that there is less slack in the market, making it harder for businesses to hire and potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. As a result, businesses trying to hire in this city may face challenges in finding available talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in St. Louis is +1.41%, which is relatively slow and ranks in the 13th percentile. This stagnant wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also implies that workers have limited bargaining power and purchasing power is not increasing quickly. As a result, businesses may not face significant pressure to increase wages, but workers may not have the disposable income to drive local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

St. Louis has a cost of living ratio of 3.29, which ranks in the 33rd percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that the city is not particularly attractive for talent without offering wage premiums, as the cost of living is higher than in many other cities. Businesses may need to consider offering higher wages to attract and retain talent in this city.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in St. Louis is growing at a rate of +1.01% year-over-year, which ranks in the 62nd percentile, indicating a moderate expansion of the labor force. This means that the supply of workers is increasing, which could make it easier for businesses to hire and find talent. However, the growth rate is not extremely high, so businesses should not expect a large influx of new workers.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in St. Louis is decreasing at a rate of -6.99% year-over-year, which ranks in the 51st percentile, indicating a slight tightening of the housing supply. This decrease in building permits signals that the housing supply may not be expanding quickly enough to accommodate a growing workforce, which could lead to affordability issues and challenges in attracting talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market in St. Louis is 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of +17.9%, which ranks in the 90th percentile, indicating a relatively slow market. This means that homes are sitting on the market for longer, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this could also be a sign of a weakening housing market, which could have implications for the local economy.

Office Economy

St. Louis has a professional and office worker share of 1.32, which ranks in the 19th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics companies. On the other hand, businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting sectors may find it challenging to attract and retain talent in this city.

The St. Louis metro area offers a relatively stable labor market with low unemployment, but it also presents challenges such as slow labor demand growth and a relatively expensive cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the potential difficulty in attracting and retaining talent due to the high cost of living and limited professional and office worker talent pool.

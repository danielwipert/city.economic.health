Economic Analysis

The Phoenix-Mesa-Chandler metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 42.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its below-average labor demand, with a composite score of 3.46, and its above-average cost of living affordability, ranking 61st percentile. The labor demand and cost of living metrics are crucial in understanding the city's current economic situation, with labor demand being driven by employment growth of 0.22% and weekly hours deviation of -1.153%.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 0.22% and weekly hours deviation of -1.153% signal a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not experiencing genuine demand expansion. This combination suggests that the city's job market is not strong, with hours worked being below the 12-month trend. The labor demand composite score of 3.46 ranks 31st percentile, further emphasizing the city's below-average labor demand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.20% ranks 43rd percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This suggests that the job market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also means that businesses may face some wage pressure when competing for talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of 4.00% ranks 56th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so. At the same time, workers are experiencing an increase in purchasing power, which can have a positive impact on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

The city's cost of living, with a PSF of $272/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.34/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.28, ranks 61st percentile in terms of affordability. This means that the city is relatively more affordable compared to its peers, making it an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums. The fact that PSF is falling by 1.8% YoY further enhances the city's affordability.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is contracting at a rate of -1.44% year-over-year, ranking 11th percentile. This indicates that the labor pool is shrinking, creating a structural headwind for hiring and potentially limiting the city's ability to support business growth.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is 16.86%, ranking 72nd percentile. This significant increase in permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which can improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. This trend is a positive signal for the city's future affordability and workforce attraction.

Days on Market

Unfortunately, the current median days on market is not available, but the city ranks 4th percentile in this metric. This low ranking suggests that the market is highly competitive, making it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, without the actual days on market data, it is difficult to assess the exact impact on worker relocation.

Office Economy

The city's office economy, with a professional worker share of 2.81, ranks 61st percentile. This indicates a deep talent pool in professional and office sectors, making the city well-suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. However, it may not be as suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented economies.

The Phoenix-Mesa-Chandler metro area offers a relatively affordable cost of living and a deep professional talent pool, making it an attractive location for certain types of businesses. However, the city's below-average labor demand and shrinking labor force are significant concerns that decision-makers should factor in, as they may limit the city's ability to support business growth and create challenges for hiring and talent attraction.

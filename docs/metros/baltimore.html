Economic Analysis

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 32.9th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 2.03, indicating a struggling job market. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a -1.62% employment growth rate and -0.871% weekly hours deviation from its own trend, as well as its high cost of living, with a PSF to earnings ratio of 5.89. These metrics suggest a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is -1.62%, and weekly hours are deviating -0.871% from the city's own trend, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with payrolls contracting and hours below trend. The city's labor market is experiencing a decline, making it a less attractive location for businesses looking to hire.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is 4.50%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 15th percentile, indicating a relatively slack labor market. This means that businesses may find it easier to hire workers, but the local consumer demand may be weaker. However, the relatively high unemployment rate also suggests that the city may not be experiencing significant wage pressure.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is +3.85%, ranking near the median at the 54th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a moderate pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing. While this may be beneficial for workers, it may also lead to higher labor costs for businesses.

Cost of Living

Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has a cost of living percentile rank of 78th, with a PSF to earnings ratio of 5.89, indicating that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. However, the fact that the PSF is not increasing significantly (+0.0% YoY) suggests that the city may still be attractive to talent without requiring large wage premiums. The high cost of living percentile rank means that the city is more affordable than most of its peers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is growing at a rate of -0.62% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This decline in labor force supply may create a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find qualified workers. The shrinking labor pool may limit the city's ability to support business growth.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is decreasing by -11.14% year-over-year, indicating a tightening housing supply. This decline in building permits may lead to a supply squeeze, making it more difficult for workers to find affordable housing and potentially eroding the city's future affordability and workforce attraction. The decrease in building permits is a concern for businesses looking to relocate or expand in the city.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is 37 days, with a year-over-year increase of +27.6%, ranking in the top tier at the 96th percentile. This suggests a slower market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold, making it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. The rising days on market may indicate a normalization of the housing market, but it also suggests that the city's housing market is not as competitive as it once was.

Office Economy

Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has an office economy percentile rank of 23rd, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. The city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. The city's office economy is not as strong as some of its peers, which may limit its appeal to certain types of businesses.

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro area offers a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate wage growth rate, but its low labor demand, contracting labor force, and tightening housing supply are significant concerns for businesses looking to relocate or expand. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's struggling job market, which may limit its ability to support business growth and attract top talent.

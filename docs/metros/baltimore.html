Economic Analysis

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking at the 29.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand, with a labor demand composite score in the bottom tier at the 2nd percentile, and its relatively affordable cost of living, ranked at the 63rd percentile. The combination of a -1.46% employment growth rate and a -0.423% weekly hours deviation from trend signals a genuine demand contraction.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is -1.46% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.423% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a genuine demand contraction, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job creation and a decrease in working hours. As a result, businesses may face a challenging environment for expansion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is 3.60%, ranking at the 30th percentile, indicating a below-average level of unemployment. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the market is not overly loose, and businesses may still face some competition for top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is +3.05%, ranking at the 30th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a pace that is not excessively rapid. Workers in the city are experiencing some improvement in their purchasing power, which could have a positive impact on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has a cost of living ratio of 1.85, ranking at the 63rd percentile, indicating that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. This affordability advantage can be a talent attraction benefit for businesses, as they may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for high living costs. As a result, businesses can attract and retain workers without incurring excessive labor costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is contracting at a rate of -0.62% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This decline in labor force growth poses a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find and recruit workers. As a result, businesses may need to invest more in training and development programs to build the skills they need.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has decreased by -11.14% year-over-year, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This decline in building permits signals that the city's housing market may become less affordable in the future, potentially making it more difficult for workers to relocate to the area. As a result, businesses may face challenges in attracting and retaining workers due to limited housing options.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is currently 37 days, with a year-over-year increase of +27.6%, ranking at the 96th percentile. This indicates a slower market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this slower market may also signal a decrease in demand for housing, which could have implications for the local economy.

Office Economy

Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has a professional and office worker share of 1.55, ranking at the 26th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies. However, it may not be the most attractive location for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on specialized office workers.

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate pace of wage growth, but its weak labor demand and shrinking labor force pose significant challenges. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's limited labor pool and declining labor force growth, which may hinder businesses' ability to find and recruit the workers they need to expand and thrive.

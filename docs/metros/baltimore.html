Economic Analysis

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking in the 33.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 2.03, placing it in the bottom tier. The city's economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand, with employment growth of -1.62% YoY, and its relatively high cost of living, with a PSF to earnings ratio of 5.89. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is -1.62% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating -0.871% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze, as both employment and hours are declining. This suggests that businesses may face a shrinking pool of potential employees.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is 4.50%, which is relatively high, placing it in the bottom tier of the 15th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but also suggesting weaker local consumer demand. As a result, businesses may find it relatively easier to staff their operations, but may also face less robust consumer spending.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is +3.85%, which is near the median, ranking in the 53rd percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a manageable pace, while workers are seeing some improvement in their purchasing power. This could be a positive sign for businesses looking to attract and retain talent.

Cost of Living

Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has a cost of living percentile rank of 79th, indicating that it is relatively expensive, with a PSF to earnings ratio of 5.89. This means that the city may struggle to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may offset the moderate wage growth. Businesses may need to consider this when determining compensation packages for their employees.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is growing at a rate of -0.62% YoY, indicating a contracting labor pool. This suggests that the supply of potential employees is shrinking, which could create a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion. Businesses may need to be more aggressive in their recruitment efforts or consider alternative locations.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is declining at a rate of -11.14% YoY, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This could lead to future affordability challenges and make it more difficult for workers to relocate to the area, potentially constraining workforce growth. Businesses may need to consider the potential impact on their ability to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Baltimore-Columbia-Towson is 37 days, with a year-over-year increase of +27.6%, placing it in the top tier of the 96th percentile. This suggests a slower market, which could make it more accessible for workers relocating to the city, but may also indicate a weakening demand for housing. Businesses may find it easier to attract talent, but may also face a slower pace of economic growth.

Office Economy

Baltimore-Columbia-Towson has an office economy percentile rank of 26th, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This suggests that the city may be less suited for businesses that require a deep pool of specialized office workers, such as tech or finance companies, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. Businesses should carefully consider their talent needs before locating in this city.

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro area offers a relatively low-cost business environment, but its weak labor demand and high cost of living may pose significant challenges for businesses looking to expand or relocate. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's shrinking labor pool, which could create a structural headwind for hiring and business growth, making it essential for businesses to carefully weigh their options before making a location decision.

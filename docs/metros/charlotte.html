Economic Analysis

The Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and top-tier labor force growth. The city's economic character is defined by its 74th percentile labor demand composite score, which combines a 0.85% year-over-year employment growth rate with a -0.248% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend. Additionally, the city's labor force is growing at a rate of 2.28% year-over-year, ranking in the 92nd percentile.

Labor Demand

The Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metro area has a labor demand composite score of 5.24, driven by a 0.85% year-over-year employment growth rate and a -0.248% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are not excessively above trend despite job growth. The labor market is experiencing a moderate expansion, with employers adding jobs at a steady pace.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia is 3.60%, ranking in the 60th percentile, indicating a near-median level of slack in the labor market. This rate suggests that the market is not overly tight, but still competitive, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. As a result, businesses may face some wage pressure, but it is not extreme.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia is -1.28%, ranking in the 0th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth environment means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing. As a result, businesses may enjoy a flat cost environment, but workers may have limited bargaining power.

Cost of Living

Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia has a cost of living ratio of 3.26, ranking in the 35th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that the city may not have a significant talent attraction advantage without offering wage premiums, as the cost of living is higher than in many other metros. Businesses may need to consider offering higher salaries to attract and retain talent.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia is growing at a rate of 2.28% year-over-year, ranking in the 92nd percentile. This rapid labor force growth indicates that the supply of workers is expanding, making it easier for businesses to hire and find talent. The growing labor pool is a significant advantage for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia has decreased by 39.50% year-over-year, ranking in the 2nd percentile. This sharp decline in building permits signals that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and limitations on workforce accommodation. As a result, businesses may face increasing competition for talent and rising housing costs.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia is 49 days, with a year-over-year increase of 13.9%, ranking in the 82nd percentile. This indicates a slower market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. The rising days on market suggests a healthy normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand erosion.

Office Economy

Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia has a deep professional talent pool, ranking in the 83rd percentile, with a composite score of 3.58. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of specialized roles. However, the city may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses, which require a different type of workforce.

The Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metro area offers businesses a growing labor force and a deep professional talent pool, making it an attractive location for companies in the knowledge economy. However, the city's stagnant wage growth and tightening housing supply are significant risks that decision-makers should factor in, as they may lead to increasing competition for talent and rising labor costs.

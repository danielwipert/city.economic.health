Economic Analysis

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 58.6th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score at the 69th percentile, and its relatively slow wage growth, at +3.13% year-over-year. The labor demand and unemployment metrics, in particular, signal a city with genuine job market expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate and hours deviation in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington both contribute to a labor demand composite score of 5.00, ranking at the 69th percentile. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This indicates that the city is experiencing a strong job market with increasing labor needs.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 4.10%, ranking at the 68th percentile, which suggests a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. As a result, wage pressure is likely to be higher in this city.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is +3.13%, ranking at the 40th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit slowly. This moderate wage growth may help balance the needs of both employers and employees.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank at 50th, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is near the median in terms of affordability compared to its peers. This means that the city is neither particularly expensive nor unusually affordable, and businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent from other areas. The cost of living is relatively balanced, with no strong advantage or disadvantage in terms of talent attraction.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is growing at a rate of +0.73% year-over-year, ranking at the 54th percentile, indicating a slowly expanding workforce supply. This moderate growth rate suggests that the hiring capacity in the city is increasing, but not rapidly, which may help support business expansion plans. However, the growth rate is not exceptionally high, which may limit the city's ability to support very rapid business growth.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is not available, but the percentile rank is at 50th, suggesting a stable or moderately growing housing supply. This stability in housing supply may help maintain affordability and support workforce accommodation in the city, but it does not indicate a significant expansion of housing options.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.0%, ranking at the 74th percentile. This suggests a relatively slow market, which may make it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, the rising days on market may also indicate a normalization of the housing market, rather than a significant downturn in demand.

Office Economy

With an office economy percentile rank at 50th, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington has a moderately deep professional talent pool, making it suitable for a variety of businesses, including tech, finance, and consulting. However, the city may not be as well-suited for businesses that require an exceptionally high concentration of specialized office workers, as its talent pool is not exceptionally deep.

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area offers businesses a strong job market with genuine demand expansion, a relatively tight labor market, and moderate wage growth. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this city is the potential for wage pressure and challenges in hiring due to the tight labor market, which may require businesses to offer competitive wages to attract and retain talent.

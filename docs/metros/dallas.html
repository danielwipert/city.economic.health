Economic Analysis

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 54.9th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and cost of living. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.49, which combines employment growth and weekly hours deviation, is in the 68th percentile, while its cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 5.36, is in the 77th percentile. These two metrics define the city's current economic character, signaling a strong job market with relatively affordable living costs.

Labor Demand

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has an employment growth rate that is above average, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -0.510% from its own trend, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in hours that are slightly below the trend, but still indicative of a strong labor market. The labor demand composite score of 4.49 is in the 68th percentile, confirming the city's above-average labor market performance.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 4.20%, which is near the median, ranking in the 56th percentile. This rate indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to cities with tighter labor markets. However, the unemployment rate is not so low that it would drive up wages significantly, providing a balanced environment for employers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is +3.24%, which is near the median, ranking in the 40th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are increasing, but not at an alarming rate, while also providing workers with some purchasing power. The wage growth is not stagnant, indicating a relatively healthy labor market, but it is also not excessively high, which could be a concern for businesses looking to control labor costs.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington, with a PSF to wages ratio of 5.36, is in the 77th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. The PSF is $201/sqft, which has decreased by 1.9% year-over-year, making the city even more attractive in terms of affordability. This affordability advantage can be a significant talent attraction factor for businesses, as they may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is growing at a rate of +0.31% year-over-year, which is near the median, ranking in the 47th percentile. This slow but positive growth rate indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, albeit at a modest pace, providing businesses with a slightly increasing pool of potential employees. However, the growth rate is not strong enough to significantly alleviate hiring challenges.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is -20.96%, which is in the bottom tier, ranking in the 19th percentile. This sharp decline in building permits signals that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. This trend is a concern for businesses that rely on a steady supply of housing for their employees.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 48 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.7%. This indicates a relatively balanced market, neither extremely competitive nor excessively slow. For workers relocating to this city, the market is somewhat accessible, but the rising days on market could signal a normalization of the market, making it slightly easier for buyers to find homes.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is in the top tier, ranking in the 85th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This city is well-suited for businesses that require specialized knowledge-economy workers, but may not be as attractive for industries that are more industrial or logistics-dominant.

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with relatively affordable living costs, making it an attractive location for talent attraction and retention. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tightening housing supply, signaled by the sharp decline in building permits, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

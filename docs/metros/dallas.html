Economic Analysis

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 58.6th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score at the 64th percentile, and its highly affordable cost of living, ranked at the 84th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a combination of employment growth and hours worked above trend, signaling genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has seen employment growth of 0.51% year-over-year and weekly hours 0.186% above its own 12-month trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as both jobs and hours worked are increasing, indicating a strong and growing labor market. The labor demand composite score of 5.20 is at the 64th percentile, further reinforcing the notion of a robust labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 3.80%, ranking at the 46th percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, this also means that local consumer demand may not be as strong as in areas with lower unemployment rates.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 2.44%, which is below average, ranking at the 34th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate implies that labor costs for employers are increasing, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is growing, albeit not as quickly as in other metros. This could be seen as a relatively stable cost environment for businesses.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $203/sqft to $37.61/hr, and a year-over-year decrease of 1.9% in PSF, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is highly affordable, ranking at the 84th percentile. This means that the city has a significant talent attraction advantage, as workers can afford a higher quality of life without requiring wage premiums. The falling PSF relative to wages is a key driver of this high affordability score.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington has grown by 0.41% year-over-year, ranking at the 76th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This positive growth rate suggests that the labor pool is increasing, providing a favorable environment for hiring and business expansion.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -4.95%, ranking at the 44th percentile, indicating a tightening housing supply. This decline in permits suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 48 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.7%, ranking at the 68th percentile. This indicates a relatively slower market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city, as they have more time to find a home. However, this also suggests a normalization of the market, rather than a highly competitive environment.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is ranked at the 96th percentile, indicating a very deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This city is well-suited for businesses requiring specialized knowledge-economy roles, but may be less ideal for those with more industrial or logistics-dominant needs.

In conclusion, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, a highly affordable cost of living, and a deep professional talent pool. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tightening housing supply, which may impact future affordability and workforce accommodation, potentially limiting the city's ability to attract and retain talent in the long term.

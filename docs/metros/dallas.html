Economic Analysis

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has an overall grade of A, ranking in the 64.3th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and cost of living. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.00, combining employment growth and weekly hours deviation, both above the 80th percentile, defines its current economic character. Additionally, its cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 5.41, ranks above average at the 75th percentile.

Labor Demand

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area exhibits strong labor demand, with employment growth and weekly hours deviation both above average, signaling genuine demand expansion. This combination indicates that the city is adding jobs at a rate above its trend, and workers are putting in more hours, suggesting a healthy and growing economy. The labor demand composite score of 5.00, at the 80th percentile, reinforces this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington stands at 4.10%, ranking above average at the 68th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This tightness implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as well as upward pressure on wages. However, it also suggests a strong local economy with a high level of labor utilization.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is +3.24%, which ranks below average at the 39th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This moderate growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a manageable pace, while workers are experiencing some improvement in their purchasing power. However, the relatively slow wage growth may limit the attractiveness of the city to workers seeking higher pay.

Cost of Living

Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington has a cost of living that is more affordable than many of its peers, with a PSF to wages ratio of 5.41, ranking above average at the 75th percentile. The city's PSF is $203/sqft, which has decreased by 1.5% year-over-year, making it an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums. This affordability advantage can be a key factor in attracting and retaining workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is growing at a year-over-year rate of +0.73%, ranking near the median at the 57th percentile. This positive growth rate indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which can facilitate hiring and support business growth. However, the moderate growth rate may not keep pace with rapid demand expansion.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is not available, but the city's ranking at the 50th percentile suggests a stable, near-median level of activity. A stable or rising level of building permits would signal an expanding housing supply, which can improve affordability and support workforce growth. However, a decline in permits could indicate a tightening housing market, posing risks to future affordability and workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.0%, ranking above average at the 73rd percentile. This suggests a relatively slow market, which can be beneficial for workers relocating to the city, as they have more time to find a suitable home. However, this slower market may also indicate some softening in demand.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington ranks near the median at the 50th percentile, indicating a moderate depth of talent in these sectors. While the city may not be as strongly suited for businesses requiring a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance, it can still support a range of industries, including those with more balanced workforce requirements.

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area offers businesses a strong labor market, affordable cost of living, and expanding workforce supply, making it an attractive location for investment and growth. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for a tightening labor market, which could drive up wages and make hiring more challenging, highlighting the need for strategic workforce planning and management.

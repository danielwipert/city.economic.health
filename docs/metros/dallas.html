Economic Analysis

Dallas's labour market is showing signs of moderation, with the unemployment rate standing at 3.6%, a 0.1 percentage point increase year-over-year. This reflects tightening conditions amid modest workforce expansion, with civilian labour force growth slowing to 0.64% year-over-year - below the average of its peer metros. Employment growth has been tepid, with nonfarm payrolls rising just 0.33% year-over-year, while the labour demand composite score of 4.62 indicates stable but unremarkable hiring momentum. However, average hourly earnings have grown 4.52% year-over-year, outpacing inflation and supporting consumer strength.

In contrast, the housing market is softening, with residential building permits contracting sharply by 20.96% year-over-year - the weakest performance among major indicators. This suggests developer caution, and homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market rising to 48 days, up 6.7% year-over-year. Despite this, the cost of living in Dallas remains relatively favourable, with a composite score of 1.33, placing it in the top 17% of most affordable large metros. This affordability, combined with labour cost competitiveness, underpins the city's appeal for in-migration.

However, the decline in new construction permits poses a structural risk to long-term housing supply. Looking ahead, Dallas's underlying economic strengths are likely to be offset by near-term headwinds in construction and employment growth, suggesting a cyclical slowdown. Without a recovery in business investment or the office market, expansion is likely to remain below trend.

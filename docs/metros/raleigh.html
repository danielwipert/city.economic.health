Economic Analysis

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has earned an overall grade of A+ with a composite score of 72.4th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. This city's economic character is defined by its exceptional labor demand, with a composite score of 92nd percentile, and its highly developed office economy, ranking in the 98th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a 1.70% year-over-year employment growth rate and a weekly hours deviation of -0.025% from its own trend.

Labor Demand

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has a labor demand composite score of 6.66, driven by a 1.70% year-over-year employment growth rate and a weekly hours deviation of -0.025% from its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running slightly below trend during a period of job growth. This indicates that the labor market is experiencing real growth, rather than just a survivor squeeze where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Raleigh-Cary is 3.00%, ranking in the 81st percentile, indicating a tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the low unemployment rate puts upward pressure on wages. However, this also suggests that the local consumer demand is strong, driven by a workforce with stable employment.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Raleigh-Cary is 3.97%, ranking in the 43rd percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate is neither exceptionally high nor stagnant, suggesting that employer labor costs are rising, but at a manageable pace. Meanwhile, workers in the area are experiencing an increase in purchasing power, which can contribute to a stable and growing local economy.

Cost of Living

Raleigh-Cary has a cost of living ratio of 1.42, ranking in the 80th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. This means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset the cost of living. The affordability of the city can be a major advantage in talent attraction and retention.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Raleigh-Cary is growing at a rate of 0.53% year-over-year, ranking below average in the 36th percentile. This indicates that the labor force supply is expanding, but at a slower pace than many other metros. While this growth is positive, it may not be sufficient to meet the demands of a rapidly growing economy, potentially leading to labor shortages in certain sectors.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Raleigh-Cary has increased by 38.82% year-over-year, ranking in the 85th percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This suggests that developer confidence is high, and the future housing supply is expected to increase, which can improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Raleigh-Cary is 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of 4.5%. This indicates a relatively competitive market, but one that is not excessively heated. For workers relocating to the city, this means that they may face some competition in the housing market, but it is still accessible.

Office Economy

Raleigh-Cary has a highly developed office economy, with a professional and office worker share ranking in the 98th percentile. This indicates a deep talent pool suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors. However, this city may be less suited for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which require a different type of workforce.

The Raleigh-Cary metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand, a highly developed office economy, and relatively affordable cost of living. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for labor shortages due to the slower-than-average labor force growth, which may require businesses to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract and retain top talent.

Economic Analysis

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has earned an overall grade of A+ with a composite score of 76.9th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. This city's economic character is largely defined by its exceptional labor demand, with a composite score of 8.63, and its strong office economy, with a professional worker share of 4.48. The labor demand is driven by a 2.02% year-over-year employment growth rate and a 1.762% deviation in weekly hours above its own trend.

Labor Demand

The combination of a 2.02% employment growth rate and a 1.762% increase in weekly hours signals a genuine demand expansion in the Raleigh-Cary metro area. This indicates that the city is experiencing a surge in job creation, with hours worked above the trend, suggesting a strong and growing economy. The labor demand composite score of 8.63 further reinforces this notion, placing the city in the top tier.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Raleigh-Cary stands at 3.00%, which is relatively low and indicates a tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The 80th percentile rank for unemployment suggests that the market has some slack, but it is still relatively tight.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Raleigh-Cary is 4.09%, which is moderate and near the median. This suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively, and worker purchasing power is increasing. The moderate wage growth rate may help businesses balance their labor costs with the need to attract and retain talent.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank of 80th, Raleigh-Cary is relatively affordable compared to its peers, particularly given the PSF of $220/sqft and average hourly earnings of $38.03/hr. The city's affordability, driven by a -1.4% year-over-year decrease in PSF, makes it an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums. This can be a major advantage for businesses looking to attract workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Raleigh-Cary is growing at a rate of 0.90% year-over-year, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This growth suggests that the city has a structural advantage in terms of hiring capacity, as the labor pool is increasing. Businesses can tap into this growing labor force to meet their staffing needs.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Raleigh-Cary has increased by 5.85% year-over-year, signaling an expansion in housing supply. This growth in permits suggests that the city is likely to see an improvement in affordability and an increase in the availability of housing for workers, making it easier for businesses to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Raleigh-Cary is 46 days, with a 2.2% year-over-year increase. This suggests a relatively competitive market, but one that is still accessible for workers relocating to the city. The moderate days on market indicate a balanced market, neither extremely hot nor cold, which can help businesses plan their relocation and hiring strategies.

Office Economy

With a professional worker share of 4.48, Raleigh-Cary has a deep and talented pool of office workers, placing it in the top tier. This makes the city an ideal location for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require specialized knowledge-economy talent. However, it may be less suited for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Raleigh-Cary metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand, a growing labor force, and a relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for investment. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for a tight labor market to drive up wages and make hiring more competitive, which could impact business costs and profitability.

Economic Analysis

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has earned an overall grade of A+ with a composite score ranking in the 78.6th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is defined by its strong labor demand, with a top-tier composite score of 94th percentile, and its high share of office and professional workers, ranking in the 98th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a 1.77% year-over-year employment growth rate and a weekly hours deviation of -0.200% from its own trend.

Labor Demand

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has a labor demand composite score of 6.58, ranking in the 94th percentile, driven by a 1.77% year-over-year employment growth rate and a weekly hours deviation of -0.200% from its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are not excessively above trend during job growth. The strong labor demand indicates a healthy job market with increasing opportunities.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Raleigh-Cary is 3.30%, ranking in the 80th percentile, indicating a relatively tight market with some slack. This means that businesses may face moderate competition for talent, but it is still possible to hire qualified workers. The relatively low unemployment rate implies that businesses may need to offer competitive wages to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Raleigh-Cary is 3.63%, ranking in the 50th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This means that employer labor costs are rising at a moderate pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing. The moderate wage growth suggests that businesses can expect to pay higher wages to attract and retain talent, but the increase is not excessively rapid.

Cost of Living

Raleigh-Cary has a cost of living percentile rank of 84th, with a price-to-salary ratio of $218/sqft to $37.93/hr, or 5.75, and a year-over-year decrease in PSF of -2.2%. This indicates that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. The high affordability score means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset the cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Raleigh-Cary is growing at a rate of 1.76% year-over-year, ranking in the 82nd percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This means that businesses can expect to have a growing pool of potential workers to hire from, making it easier to find qualified talent. The positive labor force growth rate suggests that the city has a strong foundation for future economic growth.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Raleigh-Cary has increased by 38.82% year-over-year, ranking in the 85th percentile, indicating an expansion of housing supply. This suggests that the city is likely to experience improving affordability and a more accommodating environment for workers in the future. The rapid growth in building permits signals that the city is investing in its infrastructure to support population growth.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Raleigh-Cary is 44 days, with a year-over-year increase of 2.3%, ranking in the 40th percentile. This indicates a relatively competitive market for workers relocating to the city, but the rising days on market suggests a healthy normalization of the market. The moderate days on market means that workers may have a relatively easy time finding a home, but the market is not excessively slow.

Office Economy

Raleigh-Cary has a deep professional talent pool, with an office and professional worker share ranking in the 98th percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant economies. The high ranking indicates that businesses can tap into a highly skilled and specialized workforce, making it an attractive location for knowledge-economy companies.

The Raleigh-Cary metro area offers businesses a strong labor market, a highly skilled workforce, and a relatively affordable cost of living. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses is the potential for rising labor costs due to moderate wage growth, which may impact profit margins and require businesses to adjust their compensation strategies to remain competitive.

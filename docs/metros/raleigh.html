Economic Analysis

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has earned an overall grade of A+ with a composite score ranking in the 79.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its exceptional labor demand, with a composite score in the 98th percentile, and its low unemployment rate of 3.00%, ranking in the 90th percentile. The combination of strong employment growth and low unemployment signals a highly competitive job market.

Labor Demand

The Raleigh-Cary metro area has seen employment growth of +2.02% year-over-year, combined with a +1.762% deviation in weekly hours above its own 12-month trend. This signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth, indicating a strong and vibrant labor market. The labor demand composite score of 8.63 further reinforces this assessment, ranking in the top tier at the 98th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Raleigh-Cary stands at 3.00%, ranking in the 90th percentile, which indicates a very tight labor market with little slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this area will face significant competition for talent, potentially driving up wage costs. With such a low unemployment rate, employers may need to offer competitive salaries and benefits to attract and retain top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Raleigh-Cary is +4.09%, ranking near the median at the 50th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are seeing some improvement in their purchasing power. However, the relatively modest wage growth may not be sufficient to keep pace with the cost of living in the area, potentially affecting worker affordability.

Cost of Living

Raleigh-Cary has a cost of living percentile rank of 84th, indicating that it is relatively more affordable compared to its peers, with a PSF of $218/sqft and average hourly earnings of $38.03/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.73. The fact that PSF is falling by -2.2% year-over-year further enhances the city's affordability. This makes the area an attractive destination for talent, as workers can enjoy a relatively lower cost of living without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Raleigh-Cary is growing at a rate of +1.72% year-over-year, ranking in the 90th percentile, which indicates that the workforce supply is expanding. This positive growth rate suggests that businesses will have access to a growing pool of potential employees, making it easier to hire and staff their operations.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Raleigh-Cary has decreased by -2.03% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile. This decline in permits suggests that the housing supply may be tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Raleigh-Cary is 44 days, with a year-over-year increase of +2.3%. This indicates a relatively competitive market, but one that is still accessible for workers relocating to the area. The rising days on market may signal a healthy normalization of the housing market, but it is essential to monitor this metric to ensure it does not become a barrier to workforce attraction.

Office Economy

Raleigh-Cary has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy percentile rank of 98th, indicating a strong presence of jobs in professional and office sectors. This makes the area well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but potentially less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented economies.

The Raleigh-Cary metro area offers businesses a highly competitive and vibrant labor market, with a strong talent pool and relatively affordable cost of living. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers to factor in is the potential tightening of the housing supply, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce, making it essential to monitor the building permits and days on market metrics closely.

Economic Analysis

The Philadelphia-Camden-Wilmington metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 53.5th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and labor force growth, which are in the top tier at the 80th and 86th percentiles, respectively. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.92, combining a +0.12% employment growth rate and a +1.451% weekly hours deviation from its own trend, signals genuine demand expansion. This is further supported by a labor force growth rate of +1.02% YoY.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.12% and weekly hours deviation of +1.451% indicate a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing an increase in economic activity, leading to more jobs being added. The labor demand composite score of 5.92 is in the top tier, at the 80th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 3.90% is near the median, ranking at the 55th percentile, indicating a relatively balanced labor market with neither significant tightness nor slack. For a business trying to hire in this city, the moderate unemployment rate suggests that finding talent may not be overly challenging, but it also may not be extremely easy, as the market is not particularly loose. This rate implies a stable environment for hiring.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.37% is in the bottom tier, at the 4th percentile, indicating stagnant or even slightly decreasing wages. This slow wage growth implies a flat cost environment for employers but weak bargaining power for workers, potentially affecting their purchasing power. For businesses, this could mean lower labor costs, but it also suggests workers may have less disposable income.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $233/sqft to $32.82/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.10, and a percentile rank of 20th, the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. The fact that the PSF is not falling YoY (with a 0.0% change) does not help improve affordability. This means that attracting talent without offering wage premiums could be challenging due to the higher cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +1.02% YoY, which is in the top tier, at the 86th percentile. This positive growth rate indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, suggesting a favorable environment for hiring, as there is an increasing pool of potential employees. This growth supports the city's ability to accommodate business expansion.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is +12.30%, ranking above average at the 68th percentile. This increase in building permits signals that housing supply is expanding, which could lead to improved affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce in the future. It suggests developer confidence in the market.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 36 days, with a year-over-year increase of +2.9%. This indicates a near median market speed, ranked at the 58th percentile. For a worker relocating to this city, the relatively moderate days on market suggests a competitive but not overly challenging housing market, making it accessible for newcomers.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors, with a composite score of 2.38, places the city near the median, at the 40th percentile, in terms of its talent pool depth. This suggests that while the city has some professional and office workers, it is not particularly specialized in these areas, making it less suited for businesses requiring a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms.

The Philadelphia-Camden-Wilmington metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand and expanding labor force, which are significant advantages for companies looking to grow. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker to factor in is the relatively high cost of living, which could necessitate wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially increasing labor costs.

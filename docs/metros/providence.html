Economic Analysis

The Providence-Warwick metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 54.6th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and top-tier labor force growth. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.86, combining a +0.60% employment growth rate and a -0.276% weekly hours deviation, signals genuine demand expansion. Additionally, the city's labor force growth rate of +1.69% YoY indicates an expanding workforce supply.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.60% and weekly hours deviation of -0.276% suggest that while jobs are being added, hours are slightly below trend, indicating a moderate demand expansion. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, rather than a survivor squeeze, as hours are not significantly above trend during job growth. The labor demand composite score of 4.86 is above average, ranking in the 76th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.30% is below average, ranking in the 27th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight market implies upward wage pressure, making it essential for businesses to consider this factor in their hiring strategies.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.42% is near the median, ranking in the 47th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively, and worker purchasing power is increasing. The moderate wage growth implies a relatively stable labor cost environment, which can be beneficial for businesses looking to manage their expenses.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Providence-Warwick, with a PSF of $358/sqft and an earnings ratio of $36.74/hr, resulting in a ratio of 9.74, is ranked in the 0th percentile, indicating a very expensive city. The 9.8% YoY increase in PSF further exacerbates the affordability issue. This means that the city is less attractive for talent without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may deter potential employees.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +1.69% YoY, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This growth rate, ranking in the 81st percentile, suggests that the city has a strong hiring capacity, with a increasing pool of available workers. The expanding labor force supply implies a favorable environment for businesses looking to hire and grow their operations.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is +23.50%, ranking in the 85th percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This growth suggests that the city is experiencing an increase in developer confidence, which may lead to improved affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce. The rising permits signal a positive outlook for future affordability and workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 48 days, with a +33.3% YoY increase, ranking in the 94th percentile, indicating a slower market. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a longer period, making it a more accessible and less competitive environment for workers relocating to the city. The rising DOM suggests a healthy normalization of the market, rather than demand erosion.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers is 3.08, ranking in the 63rd percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. The city's office economy is above average, making it an attractive location for businesses in these sectors. However, it may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses, which may require a different type of workforce.

The Providence-Warwick metro area offers businesses a unique combination of above-average labor demand and top-tier labor force growth, making it an attractive location for companies looking to expand their operations. However, the city's high cost of living and tight labor market pose significant risks, as businesses may need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract and retain talent, which can increase labor costs and impact profitability.

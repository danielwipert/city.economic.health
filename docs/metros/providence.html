Economic Analysis

The Providence-Warwick metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 48.1th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score ranking at the 71st percentile, and its extremely low affordability, with a cost of living ratio ranking at the 0th percentile. Specifically, the city's employment growth rate of +0.60% and weekly hours deviation of +0.101% signal genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.60% and weekly hours deviation of +0.101% indicate that the city is experiencing genuine demand expansion, with more jobs being added and hours running above trend. This combination suggests that businesses are hiring and workers are putting in extra hours to meet growing demand. The labor demand composite score of 5.24, ranking at the 71st percentile, further reinforces this trend.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.30% ranks at the 15th percentile, indicating a relatively high level of unemployment compared to other cities. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, this also implies weaker local consumer demand, which could impact businesses that rely on local spending.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +2.78% ranks at the 26th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This means that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. Workers in Providence-Warwick will see some increase in their purchasing power, but it may not be as strong as in other areas.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio ranking at the 0th percentile, Providence-Warwick is one of the most expensive cities relative to local wages. This means that the city is at a significant disadvantage when it comes to attracting talent without offering wage premiums. Businesses may need to pay higher salaries to compensate for the high cost of living, which could impact their bottom line.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +1.69% year-over-year, ranking at the 83rd percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire. The growing labor force provides a larger pool of potential workers, making it easier for companies to find the talent they need.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is +23.50%, ranking at the 83rd percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which could lead to improved affordability and a more attractive environment for workers. As more housing units become available, the city may become more competitive in terms of talent attraction and retention.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 48 days, with a year-over-year increase of +33.3%, ranking at the 94th percentile. This indicates a slower market, where homes are taking longer to sell. For workers relocating to Providence-Warwick, this could be a positive sign, as it may provide more time to find a suitable home and negotiate a better price.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers is 3.08, ranking at the 70th percentile. This suggests that Providence-Warwick has a relatively deep talent pool in these sectors, making it well-suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ operations. However, it may be less suitable for industries that require a large workforce in industrial or logistics roles.

The Providence-Warwick metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand and an expanding workforce, but its extremely high cost of living is a significant constraint. Decision-makers should carefully consider the need to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent in this expensive city, which could impact their labor costs and overall competitiveness.

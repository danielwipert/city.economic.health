Economic Analysis

The Providence-Warwick metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 50.5th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its labor demand and labor force growth metrics, which both rank above average at 64th and 80th percentiles, respectively. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.69, combining a +0.60% employment growth rate and a -0.451% weekly hours deviation, signals a genuine demand expansion. The labor force growth rate of +1.69% YoY also indicates an expanding workforce supply.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.60% and weekly hours deviation of -0.451% suggest that while jobs are being added, hours are not running significantly above trend, indicating a moderate demand expansion. This combination signals genuine demand, rather than a survivor squeeze, as hours are not excessively high during a period of job growth. The labor demand composite score of 4.69 reinforces this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.30% is below the national average, ranking in the 35th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This tightness implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as well as upward pressure on wages. As a result, companies may need to offer competitive salaries to attract top talent in this market.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.77% is near the median, ranking in the 50th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are experiencing some improvement in their purchasing power. As a result, businesses should factor in moderate wage increases when planning for labor costs.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Providence-Warwick, with a PSF of $359/sqft and an earnings ratio of $36.75/hr, resulting in a ratio of 9.77, ranks in the 2nd percentile, indicating a relatively expensive city. This low affordability score means that the city may struggle to attract talent without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living. As a result, businesses may need to consider offering higher salaries to compensate for the expensive housing market.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +1.69% YoY, ranking in the 80th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This growth suggests that the city has a strong pipeline of potential employees, making it easier for businesses to hire and expand their operations. As a result, companies can expect a relatively favorable hiring environment.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is +132.32%, ranking in the 98th percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This rapid growth in permits suggests that the city is experiencing a surge in developer confidence, which should lead to improved affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce in the future. As a result, businesses can expect a more stable and affordable housing market for their employees.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 27 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -6.9%, ranking in the 2nd percentile, indicating a very competitive market. This fast-paced market means that workers relocating to the city may face challenges in finding and securing housing, as homes are selling quickly. As a result, businesses may need to consider offering relocation assistance or other incentives to help employees navigate the competitive housing market.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in the city ranks in the 64th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. The city's strong office economy makes it an attractive location for businesses in these sectors, but may be less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces. As a result, companies in the knowledge economy may find the city's talent pool particularly appealing.

The Providence-Warwick metro area offers businesses a unique combination of moderate labor demand, a tight labor market, and a rapidly expanding housing supply, making it an attractive location for companies in the knowledge economy. However, the city's high cost of living and competitive housing market pose significant challenges for talent attraction and retention, and businesses should carefully consider these factors when making location decisions.

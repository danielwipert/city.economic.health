Economic Analysis

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking at the 54.5th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and labor demand. The city's economic character is defined by its top-tier wage growth of 6.85% year-over-year and its above-average labor demand composite score of 5.50, which signals genuine demand expansion. These metrics indicate a city with a strong and growing economy.

Labor Demand

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area has an employment growth rate of 0.64% year-over-year and a weekly hours deviation of 0.303% above its own trend, combining to form an above-average labor demand composite score. This signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. The labor market is characterized by an increase in jobs and working hours, indicating a strong demand for labor.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 4.10%, ranking near the median at the 49th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced labor market, neither extremely tight nor slack. For a business trying to hire in this city, the moderate unemployment rate suggests that finding talent may be manageable, but not overly easy, and may require competitive offers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 6.85%, placing it in the top tier at the 90th percentile. This fast wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers, but also strong worker purchasing power. As wages continue to rise, businesses may face increasing costs, but the local workforce will have more disposable income to spend.

Cost of Living

San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad has a cost of living percentile rank of 12, indicating that it is relatively expensive compared to its peers, with a PSF to earnings ratio of $604/sqft to $43.04/hr, or 14.03. Although PSF is falling by 4.0% year-over-year, the city remains expensive. This means that attracting talent without offering wage premiums may be challenging due to the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is shrinking at a rate of -1.52% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 22nd percentile. This contraction in labor force supply poses a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of available workers is decreasing. Businesses may face challenges in finding and recruiting new talent in this environment.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is +0.17%, ranking near the median at the 50th percentile. This slight increase in permits suggests that housing supply is slowly expanding, which may help improve affordability and accommodate the workforce in the future. However, the modest growth rate may not be enough to keep pace with demand.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of 2.6%. This indicates a near-median market pace, where homes are selling at a moderate speed. For a worker relocating to this city, the housing market is neither extremely competitive nor overly accessible, offering a relatively balanced environment for homebuyers.

Office Economy

San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad has an office economy percentile rank of 74, indicating a deep talent pool in professional and office sectors. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ operations that require specialized knowledge workers. However, it may be less ideal for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area offers businesses a strong and growing economy with top-tier wage growth, but also poses challenges due to its expensive cost of living and shrinking labor force. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker to factor in is the high cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially offsetting the benefits of the city's strong labor demand and wage growth.

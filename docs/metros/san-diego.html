Economic Analysis

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its labor demand and labor force growth metrics, which stand at 69th percentile and 81st percentile, respectively. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.95, combining a 0.70% employment growth rate with a -0.329% deviation in weekly hours, suggests a moderate expansion in job opportunities. The unemployment rate and cost of living metrics also play significant roles in defining the city's economic character, with an unemployment rate of 4.40% and a cost of living ratio of 4.90.

Labor Demand

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area has an employment growth rate of 0.70% and a weekly hours deviation of -0.329%, indicating a genuine demand expansion, albeit at a moderate pace. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs, but the hours worked are slightly below the 12-month trend, suggesting some efficiency in labor utilization. The labor demand composite score of 4.95, ranking at the 69th percentile, further supports the notion of a growing job market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad stands at 4.40%, ranking at the 28th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This level of unemployment suggests that there may be some competition for talent, potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. For a business trying to hire in this market, the tight labor conditions imply a need for competitive compensation packages to attract and retain top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 4.58%, placing it near the median at the 55th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are increasing, but at a manageable pace. For workers, this wage growth supports purchasing power, contributing to a stable local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 4.90, ranking at the 13th percentile, San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is considered one of the more expensive cities relative to its peers. This low percentile rank indicates that the city's housing costs are high compared to local wages, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The high cost of living may deter some businesses from locating in the area, especially those with tight labor budgets.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is growing at a rate of 1.66% year-over-year, ranking at the 81st percentile. This significant growth in the labor force supply suggests that the city has an expanding pool of potential workers, which can support business growth and hiring needs. The positive labor force growth is a favorable indicator for companies looking to expand their workforce in the area.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is -0.37%, ranking near the median at the 55th percentile. This slight decline in building permits may indicate a tightening in future housing supply, potentially affecting affordability and the ability to accommodate a growing workforce. However, the current moderate pace of permit issuance suggests that the impact on future affordability may not be immediate.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 38 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.6%. This relatively fast pace of home sales, ranking near the median at the 49th percentile, indicates a competitive housing market. For workers relocating to the city, the fast-moving market may present challenges in finding and securing housing, potentially affecting relocation decisions.

Office Economy

San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad has an office economy percentile rank of 49th, indicating a moderate depth of professional and office sector talent. The city's composite score of 2.58 suggests that it is suited for businesses that require a mix of professional services and other sectors, but may not be as competitive for tech, finance, or consulting firms that demand a very deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area offers businesses a growing labor force and moderate labor demand, making it an attractive location for companies looking to expand. However, the city's high cost of living and relatively tight labor market present significant challenges, particularly in terms of talent attraction and retention, which decision-makers must carefully consider when evaluating this location for their business operations.

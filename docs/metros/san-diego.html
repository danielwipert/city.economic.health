Economic Analysis

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 54.7th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and high wage growth, at 70th and 90th percentiles, respectively. The city's economic character is defined by these two metrics, with employment growth at 0.64% year-over-year and weekly hours 0.303% above trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, with jobs being added and hours increasing.

Labor Demand

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area has an employment growth rate of 0.64% year-over-year and weekly hours 0.303% above trend, indicating a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing an increase in jobs and working hours, which is a positive indicator for businesses looking to expand or relocate. The labor demand composite score of 5.50 places the city in the 70th percentile, above average.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 4.10%, which is near the median, ranking in the 50th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced labor market, neither too tight nor too slack, which means businesses may face moderate competition for talent. With an unemployment rate at this level, businesses can expect to find qualified candidates, but may still need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 6.85%, placing the city in the 90th percentile, indicating fast and rising labor costs for employers. This strong wage growth is good for worker purchasing power, as employees will have more disposable income to spend on goods and services. However, for businesses, this means that labor costs will be increasing, which could impact profitability.

Cost of Living

San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad has a cost of living ratio of $604/sqft to $43.04/hr, with a percentile rank of 12th, indicating that the city is relatively expensive. The PSF has decreased by 4.0% year-over-year, but the city still ranks as one of the more expensive places to live. This means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent, as the high cost of living could be a deterrent for some employees.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad has contracted by 1.52% year-over-year, ranking in the 22nd percentile, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This contraction in the labor force supply means that businesses may face structural headwinds when trying to hire, as there may be fewer qualified candidates available. This could lead to increased competition for talent and upward pressure on wages.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 0.17%, ranking in the 50th percentile, indicating a relatively stable housing market. This slow growth in building permits suggests that the housing supply is not expanding rapidly, which could lead to future affordability issues and constraints on workforce accommodation. However, the current pace of permit issuance is not indicative of a severe supply squeeze.

Days on Market

The median days on market in San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of 2.6%, ranking in the 56th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced housing market, neither too hot nor too cold, which means that workers relocating to the city may face a moderately competitive market. The rising days on market suggests a healthy normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand erosion.

Office Economy

San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad has a deep professional talent pool, with an office/professional worker share of 3.28, ranking in the 74th percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of specialized knowledge workers. However, the city may be less suitable for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which require a different type of workforce.

The San Diego-Chula Vista-Carlsbad metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion and high wage growth, making it an attractive location for companies in the professional services sector. However, the city's high cost of living and shrinking labor force supply are significant constraints that businesses should factor into their decision-making, as they may need to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent.

Economic Analysis

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area has an overall grade of A- with a composite score ranking it at the 60.1th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 3.60% and its high cost of living affordability score, ranking at the 91st percentile. The combination of these metrics suggests a tight labor market with a high demand for talent.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Atlanta is -0.18% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating 0.360% above the city's own 12-month trend. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 3.69 ranks below average at the 35th percentile.

Unemployment

The actual unemployment rate in Atlanta is 3.60%, which is relatively low and indicates a tight labor market. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city may face challenges in finding available talent, leading to potential wage pressure. With an unemployment rate ranking at the 80th percentile, Atlanta's labor market has relatively little slack.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Atlanta is +5.44%, which is above average and indicates rising labor costs for employers. This level of wage growth is beneficial for worker purchasing power, as it allows them to keep up with the cost of living. The 72nd percentile rank for wage growth suggests that Atlanta's labor market is experiencing moderate to fast wage growth.

Cost of Living

Atlanta is relatively affordable, with a cost of living score ranking at the 91st percentile. The city's PSF of $198/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.98/hr result in a ratio of 5.21, which is relatively low. This affordability makes it easier for businesses to attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Atlanta is growing at a rate of +1.26% year-over-year, which indicates an expanding workforce supply. This growth suggests that businesses will have a relatively easier time finding talent in the future. The 70th percentile rank for labor force growth indicates that Atlanta's workforce is expanding at a moderate rate.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Atlanta is -30.90%, which indicates a tightening housing supply. This decline in permits suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of housing is not keeping up with demand. The 11th percentile rank for building permits is a concern for businesses looking to relocate or expand in the city.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Atlanta is 49 days, with a year-over-year increase of +6.5%. This suggests a relatively accessible market for workers relocating to the city, as homes are not selling extremely quickly. The 67th percentile rank for days on market indicates a moderate pace of home sales.

Office Economy

Atlanta has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy score ranking at the 80th percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors. However, it may be less suitable for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors.

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a tight labor market, moderate wage growth, and relatively high affordability. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the decline in residential building permits, which may lead to future affordability and workforce accommodation challenges. This risk should be carefully considered when evaluating the city as a potential location for business expansion or relocation.

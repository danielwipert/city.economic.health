Economic Analysis

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 54.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and wage growth. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.00, which is in the 71st percentile, and its wage growth of +6.47% year-over-year, ranking in the 79th percentile, are the two metrics that most define its current economic character. These numbers indicate a city with a strong job market and increasing labor costs.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate and hours deviation in Atlanta are both above average, with the labor demand composite score in the 71st percentile. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This is a positive sign for businesses looking to expand or locate in the area, as it indicates a strong and growing labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Atlanta is in the bottom tier, ranking in the 19th percentile, which translates to a tight labor market with low unemployment. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer higher wages to attract talent. The practical implication is that companies may need to be more competitive in their hiring practices to attract and retain workers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Atlanta is +6.47%, ranking in the 79th percentile, which is considered fast. This rapid wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing purchasing power for workers. As a result, businesses may need to budget for higher labor costs, but can also expect a more affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

Atlanta has a cost of living ratio of 1.52, ranking in the 74th percentile, which means it is more affordable than many of its peer cities. This affordability advantage can be a talent attraction magnet, as workers can maintain a higher standard of living without requiring wage premiums. As a result, businesses may find it easier to attract and retain workers in Atlanta compared to more expensive cities.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Atlanta is growing at a rate of +0.51% year-over-year, ranking in the 34th percentile, which is below average. This slow growth rate means that the labor force supply is expanding, but at a slower pace than many other cities. The implication for hiring capacity is that businesses may need to be more strategic in their recruitment efforts, as the pool of available workers is not growing as quickly as in other areas.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits in Atlanta is near the median, ranking in the 50th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is neither rapidly expanding nor tightening, which has neutral implications for future affordability and workforce accommodation. As a result, businesses can expect a relatively stable housing market, but may not see significant improvements in affordability or availability.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Atlanta is 51 days, with a year-over-year increase of +8.5%, ranking in the 45th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively average amount of time, and the market is not extremely competitive or accessible for relocating workers. As a result, businesses may need to provide relocation assistance or support to help workers navigate the housing market.

Office Economy

Atlanta has an office economy composite score of 2.47, ranking in the 40th percentile, which indicates a moderately deep professional talent pool. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting sectors, but less ideal for those in industrial or logistics-dominant industries. The city's office economy is neither a major strength nor a weakness, but rather a neutral factor in its overall economic profile.

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area offers businesses a strong labor market, rapid wage growth, and an affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for companies looking to expand or relocate. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tight labor market, which may require businesses to be more competitive in their hiring practices and offer higher wages to attract and retain talent.

Economic Analysis

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area has an overall grade of A- with a composite score ranking at the 62.6th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its above-average labor demand, with a composite score at the 67th percentile, and its tight unemployment market, ranking at the 77th percentile with an unemployment rate of 3.60%. The labor demand and unemployment metrics suggest a city with genuine job growth and a competitive labor market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Atlanta is +0.36% year-over-year, and while the weekly hours deviation from trend is not available, the labor demand composite score indicates above-average demand at the 67th percentile. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, with jobs being added and hours potentially running above trend. This is a positive sign for businesses looking to expand or establish operations in the city.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Atlanta is 3.60%, which is relatively low and indicates a tight labor market, ranking at the 77th percentile. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight market also suggests that workers may have more bargaining power.

Wage Growth

While the exact year-over-year wage growth rate is not available, Atlanta's wage growth is near the median at the 50th percentile. This suggests moderate wage growth, which may not put significant pressure on employer labor costs but still supports worker purchasing power. However, the exact impact on labor costs and worker purchasing power cannot be determined without more specific data.

Cost of Living

Atlanta's cost of living is near the median at the 50th percentile, indicating a relatively average affordability level compared to peer cities. This means that the city may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to affordability, and businesses may need to consider wage premiums to attract workers from other areas.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Atlanta is growing at a rate of +1.26% year-over-year, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This is a positive sign for businesses, as it suggests that the city has a growing pool of potential workers, making it easier to hire and expand operations.

Building Permits

While the exact year-over-year change in building permits is not available, Atlanta's building permit growth is near the median at the 50th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is likely expanding at a moderate rate, which could help maintain affordability and support workforce growth.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Atlanta is 49 days, with a year-over-year increase of +6.5%, ranking at the 66th percentile. This indicates a relatively competitive market, but the rising days on market suggests a healthy normalization, making it slightly more accessible for workers relocating to the city.

Office Economy

Atlanta's professional and office worker share is near the median at the 50th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This suggests that the city is suited for a variety of businesses, including those in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, but may not have a significant advantage in attracting headquarters or high-end office operations.

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area offers businesses a competitive labor market with genuine job growth and a growing workforce supply. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tight labor market, which may lead to challenges in hiring and require competitive wages to attract talent.

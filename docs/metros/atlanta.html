## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Atlanta’s labour market shows resilience amid mixed signals in broader economic activity. The city’s unemployment rate stands at 3.3%, unchanged from a year earlier (101A_unemp_yoy_pp = 0.0), reflecting a stable but tightening job market. Wage growth remains robust, with average hourly earnings up 8.28% year-on-year (103B_earnings_yoy), placing Atlanta in the 94th percentile nationally for income momentum. However, employment growth has stalled, with nonfarm payrolls effectively flat (107E_employment_growth_yoy = –0.01%) and labour demand weak (107E_ldc_composite = 3.75, 24th percentile). The civilian labour force expanded modestly (102A_clf_yoy = 0.51%), suggesting limited supply-side constraints. Weekly hours worked are slightly above trend (107E_wh_trend_deviation_pct = +0.531%), indicating employers are leaning on existing staff rather than hiring.

Housing and business investment point to cooling momentum. Residential construction has sharply decelerated, with building permits down 30.9% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), among the weakest rates nationally (10th percentile). This contraction likely reflects higher financing costs and waning investor appetite. The housing market is softening, with homes taking a median 51 days to sell—up 8.5% from last year (204A_dom_yoy_pct)—though inventory conditions remain relatively balanced (204A_dom_composite = 7.51, 44th percentile). Atlanta maintains strong fundamentals in cost competitiveness (104C_col = 0.93, 96th percentile) and office occupancy (105C_owr = 3.68, 90th percentile), supporting business location decisions.

**Outlook:** Atlanta’s economy is navigating a transition from high growth to moderation. Near-term headwinds in construction and employment growth contrast with durable strengths in wages and affordability. Absent external shocks, the city is likely to maintain modest expansion, anchored by its diversified corporate base and continued in-migration.

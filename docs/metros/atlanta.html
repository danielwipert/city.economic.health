Economic Analysis

Atlanta's labour market has demonstrated resilience, despite mixed signals in the broader economy. The city's unemployment rate remains at 3.3%, unchanged from a year ago, indicating a stable yet tightening job market. Average hourly earnings have risen by 8.28% year-on-year, placing Atlanta in the 94th percentile nationally for income momentum. However, employment growth has stalled, with nonfarm payrolls virtually flat at -0.01% and labour demand weak, ranking in the 24th percentile. The civilian labour force has expanded modestly, at 0.51% year-on-year, suggesting limited supply-side constraints. Employers appear to be relying on existing staff, with weekly hours worked slightly above trend at +0.531%.

In contrast, housing and business investment are showing signs of cooling. Residential construction has slowed sharply, with building permits down 30.9% year-on-year, one of the weakest rates nationally. This decline likely reflects higher financing costs and decreased investor appetite. The housing market is softening, with homes taking a median 51 days to sell, up 8.5% from last year. Although inventory conditions remain relatively balanced, the market is losing momentum. Nevertheless, Atlanta maintains strong fundamentals, including cost competitiveness and office occupancy, which support business location decisions.

Looking ahead, Atlanta's economy is transitioning from a period of high growth to more moderate expansion. Near-term challenges in construction and employment growth are offset by the city's durable strengths in wages and affordability. Assuming no external shocks, Atlanta is likely to sustain modest growth, underpinned by its diversified corporate base and continued in-migration.

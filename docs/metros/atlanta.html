Economic Analysis

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 52.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its near-median labor demand and above-average unemployment rate of 3.60%. The city's economic character is defined by its modest employment growth and relatively low unemployment, with a labor demand composite score of 3.69, placing it near the median at the 40th percentile. The combination of a 3.60% unemployment rate and 5.44% wage growth suggests a relatively tight labor market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Atlanta is -0.18% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating 0.360% above the city's own 12-month trend, indicating a modest level of labor demand. This combination signals a slight contraction in job growth, but with hours running above trend, it may also indicate a degree of survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. The labor demand composite score of 3.69, at the 40th percentile, reinforces this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Atlanta is 3.60%, placing it at the 77th percentile, which means the market is relatively tight. This tightness implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, with more wage pressure, as the low unemployment rate suggests a competitive labor market. With a tight market, employers may need to offer higher wages to attract talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Atlanta is 5.44%, ranking at the 72nd percentile, indicating relatively fast wage growth. This rapid wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, while worker purchasing power is increasing. As a result, businesses may need to factor in higher labor costs, but workers will have more disposable income to spend.

Cost of Living

Atlanta has a cost of living percentile rank of 50th, indicating that it is near the median in terms of affordability. This means that the city is neither particularly expensive nor unusually affordable compared to its peers. As a result, businesses may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to cost of living, and may need to consider wage premiums to attract workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Atlanta is growing at a rate of 0.25% year-over-year, placing it at the 45th percentile, which indicates a modest expansion of the workforce supply. This slow growth suggests that hiring capacity may be limited, and businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers. However, the positive growth rate indicates that the labor pool is not shrinking.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Atlanta has decreased by 30.90% year-over-year, ranking at the 10th percentile, which signals a significant tightening of housing supply. This sharp decline in permits suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. This could lead to increased housing costs and reduced attractiveness for relocating workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Atlanta is 51 days, with a year-over-year increase of 8.5%, placing it at the 44th percentile. This indicates a relatively slow market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold. For workers relocating to Atlanta, this slower market may make it easier to find a home, as there is less competition and more time to make a decision.

Office Economy

Atlanta has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy percentile rank of 83rd, indicating a strong presence of jobs in professional and office sectors. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a highly skilled and knowledgeable workforce. However, it may be less suitable for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors.

The Atlanta-Sandy Springs-Roswell metro area offers businesses a relatively tight labor market with fast wage growth, which can be both a benefit and a challenge. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the significant decline in residential building permits, which may lead to future affordability and workforce accommodation issues, making it essential to factor in the potential impact on housing costs and talent attraction. Overall, businesses must carefully weigh the benefits and challenges of locating in Atlanta to make an informed decision.

Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Cleveland's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.4%, with a year-over-year increase of 0.4 percentage points. However, the civilian labor force has grown by 1.8% over the same period, suggesting a moderate expansion in workforce supply. Average hourly earnings, on the other hand, have declined by 1.59% year-over-year, which may be a cause for concern. The labour demand composite score of 5.27, coupled with a nonfarm payroll employment growth rate of 0.57% year-over-year, indicates a relatively stable employment market.

In terms of business activity, Cleveland's office worker ratio composite score of 2.98 suggests a moderate level of office-based employment. The city's residential building permits have increased by 11.14% year-over-year, indicating a growing construction sector. However, the median days on market for residential properties has risen to 55 days, with a year-over-year increase of 7.84%, which may indicate a slowing housing market. The cost of living composite score is not available, but the overall economic grade of "B" suggests that Cleveland's economy is average compared to other cities.

The city's economic strengths lie in its moderate labour force growth and stable employment market. However, the decline in average hourly earnings and the slowing housing market are obvious stress points. The increase in residential building permits is a positive sign, but it remains to be seen whether this will lead to a sustained increase in construction activity. Overall, Cleveland's economy appears to be navigating a period of moderate growth, with some challenges to be addressed in the labour and housing markets.

Closing outlook: Cleveland's economic prospects are likely to be shaped by its ability to address the decline in average hourly earnings and the slowing housing market. If the city can attract new businesses and investments, it may be able to leverage its moderate labour force growth and stable employment market to drive economic expansion. However, this will require careful policy decisions and strategic investments to support key sectors such as construction and office-based employment.

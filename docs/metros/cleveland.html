Economic Analysis

Cleveland's labour market is experiencing modest expansion, with nonfarm employment increasing 0.57% year-on-year and an unemployment rate of 3.4%, below the national average. However, a 0.4 percentage point rise in the unemployment rate suggests some softening. The 1.8% growth in the civilian labour force over the past year indicates strong workforce supply growth, ranking in the 82nd percentile, which may reflect underlying confidence or demographic inflows.

In contrast, average hourly earnings have fallen 1.59% in real terms year-on-year, ranking in the 0th percentile, potentially due to weak wage pressures or sectoral composition. Labour demand, as measured by the composite index, remains stable at 5.27, with a 58th percentile ranking, supported by a slight 0.168% increase in weekly hours worked above the 12-month baseline.

The housing market is showing signs of a gradual slowdown, with median days on market increasing 7.8% year-on-year to 55 days, indicating softer demand and a 38th percentile ranking. Nevertheless, residential building permits have risen 11.14% year-on-year, with a 68th percentile ranking, suggesting that builders remain confident in medium-term occupancy. The office market is relatively resilient, with an office worker ratio composite of 2.98, ranking in the 64th percentile, although this does not indicate strong growth.

Looking ahead, Cleveland's economy is expected to maintain a stable but unremarkable trajectory. While labour supply growth and construction activity provide a solid foundation, wage stagnation and slowing housing turnover pose constraints. Without productivity gains or sectoral upgrading, the city is likely to experience modest growth, leaving it vulnerable to broader manufacturing or interest rate shocks.

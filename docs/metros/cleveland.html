Economic Analysis

Cleveland earns an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 49.0th percentile among 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its stagnant wage growth and strong labor force expansion, with a year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.55% and a labor force growth rate of +1.80%. These metrics suggest a complex labor market with both opportunities and challenges for businesses.

Labor Demand

Cleveland's employment growth rate is +0.57% year-over-year, while weekly hours are -0.363% below trend, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.73, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile. This combination signals a moderate labor market with some genuine demand expansion, but not enough to drive significant hours growth. The labor demand is neither strongly expanding nor contracting.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Cleveland is 3.40%, ranking above average at the 68th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, with more wage pressure due to the limited availability of workers. However, the current rate still suggests some slack in the market, allowing for selective hiring.

Wage Growth

With a year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.55%, Cleveland experiences stagnant wage growth, ranking at the bottom tier of the 2nd percentile. This implies that labor costs for employers are not increasing significantly, but worker purchasing power is also not growing. The stagnant wage environment may attract businesses looking for a flat cost structure but could limit worker bargaining power.

Cost of Living

Cleveland's cost of living, with a PSF of $143/sqft and average hourly earnings of $33.75, results in a ratio of 4.24, ranking near the median at the 50th percentile. Given the 13.5% year-over-year increase in PSF, the city is relatively average in terms of affordability. This means that Cleveland does not offer a significant talent attraction advantage without wage premiums, as its cost of living is neither particularly high nor low compared to peers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Cleveland is growing at a rate of +1.80% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier at the 92nd percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, providing a positive environment for hiring, as businesses can tap into a growing pool of potential employees.

Building Permits

Residential building permits in Cleveland have decreased by -18.19% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 24th percentile. This decline suggests that housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

Homes in Cleveland currently sit on the market for 55 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.8%. This ranks near the median at the 46th percentile, indicating a moderate market. For relocating workers, this means that the housing market is relatively accessible, with homes not selling extremely quickly, allowing for some flexibility in finding a place to live.

Office Economy

Cleveland's professional and office worker share is above average, ranking at the 74th percentile. This suggests a deep talent pool suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, or headquarters operations. However, it may be less ideal for industries dominated by industrial or logistics roles.

In conclusion, Cleveland offers businesses a unique combination of a growing labor force and a relatively tight labor market, but with stagnant wage growth and tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for future affordability issues and workforce accommodation challenges due to the decline in residential building permits, which could offset the benefits of the city's expanding labor pool.

Economic Analysis

Cleveland earns an overall grade of B, ranking 53.7th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its labor force growth and office economy metrics, which stand at 85th percentile and 74th percentile, respectively. The city's labor force is expanding at a rate of 1.80% year-over-year, and its share of professional and office workers is significant. These factors suggest a city with a growing and skilled workforce.

Labor Demand

Cleveland's employment growth rate is 0.57% year-over-year, combined with a -1.207% deviation in weekly hours from its own 12-month baseline, indicating a labor demand composite score of 3.89, which ranks near the median at 48th percentile. This combination signals a modest expansion in job numbers but with hours worked below trend, suggesting some level of survivor squeeze where remaining workers may be absorbing the workload of eliminated roles. This scenario does not indicate strong, genuine demand expansion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Cleveland is 3.40%, which places it at the 77th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This tightness implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring due to the lower availability of workers, potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. For a business trying to hire in Cleveland, this means a more competitive hiring environment.

Wage Growth

Cleveland experiences a year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.47%, ranking it in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. This stagnant wage growth environment suggests that labor costs for employers are not increasing rapidly, but it also indicates weak bargaining power for workers and potentially slower growth in worker purchasing power. This could be beneficial for businesses looking to control labor costs but may not be as attractive for workers seeking wage increases.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $143/sqft to $33.79/hr, Cleveland ranks near the median at the 50th percentile in terms of affordability. The city's PSF has increased by 13.5% year-over-year, which does not contribute to an improvement in affordability. This level of affordability means that Cleveland does not particularly stand out as a talent attraction hub without the need for wage premiums, as its cost of living is average compared to its peers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Cleveland is growing at a rate of 1.80% year-over-year, placing it in the top tier at the 85th percentile. This expansion in the labor force supply is a positive indicator for businesses, as it suggests a growing pool of potential workers, making it easier to find and hire talent. This growth is a significant advantage for companies looking to expand their workforce.

Building Permits

Cleveland has seen an 11.14% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking it above average at the 71st percentile. This rise in building permits indicates that the housing supply is expected to expand, which should improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. This is a positive signal for future workforce attraction and retention, as it suggests that the city is preparing for and can support population growth.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Cleveland is 55 days, with a year-over-year increase of 7.8%, ranking it below average at the 38th percentile. This indicates a relatively fast-moving market, which can be challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, the rising days on market could also signal a healthy normalization of the housing market, depending on the context of job market strength.

Office Economy

Cleveland's share of professional and office workers places it at the 74th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ operations. This makes the city more attractive for knowledge-economy businesses but less suited for industries that are more industrial or logistics-dominant.

In conclusion, Cleveland offers businesses a growing and skilled workforce, with a significant advantage in labor force expansion and a deep professional talent pool. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker to factor in is the tight labor market and its potential to drive up wages, alongside the average cost of living that does not offer a significant advantage in talent attraction without wage premiums.

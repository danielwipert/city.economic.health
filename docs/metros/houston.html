## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Houston’s labour market shows moderate expansionary pressure, with nonfarm employment growing 0.43% year-on-year—below the national average but stable—while the unemployment rate stands at 4.2%, up 0.2 percentage points from a year earlier. The civilian labour force expanded 0.93% YoY, indicating modest workforce supply growth, and average hourly earnings rose 4.45%, suggesting persistent wage pressure. Labour demand, as reflected in the composite score (70th percentile), is supported by solid employment growth and slightly above-trend weekly hours (0.64% above 12-month baseline), pointing to resilient underlying demand for workers despite a marginal uptick in unemployment.

Housing market dynamics reveal emerging imbalances. Residential building permits have declined 12.89% YoY (30th percentile), signaling a contraction in new supply at a time when affordability remains relatively strong: the cost of living composite score ranks favourably at 92nd percentile (1.08 PSF/earnings ratio). However, housing market liquidity is softening, with median days on market at 50 days, up 11.1% YoY, though not yet indicating oversupply. Office absorption remains weak, as shown by the low office worker ratio composite score (8th percentile), reflecting structural challenges tied to hybrid work trends and elevated vacancy rates.

**Outlook**: Houston’s economy remains resilient, supported by steady labour demand and strong affordability, but near-term headwinds include constrained housing construction and soft office fundamentals. The modest rise in unemployment amid slowing job growth warrants monitoring, though wage growth and labour force expansion suggest underlying stability. Over the longer term, demographic inflows and energy sector investment could reinforce growth, provided housing supply responds.

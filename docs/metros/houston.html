Economic Analysis

The Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking at the 54.9th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its near-median labor demand and cost of living. The city's economic character is defined by its 96th percentile cost of living ratio, indicating a highly affordable environment, and its 59th percentile labor demand composite score, which suggests a moderate pace of job growth. With an unemployment rate of 4.20% and wage growth of 4.13%, this city presents a mixed picture for businesses considering relocation.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands is 0.71% year-over-year, while weekly hours are running 0.571% below the city's own 12-month trend. This combination signals a moderate expansion of labor demand, but with some signs of hours contraction, which may indicate a slight survivor squeeze. The labor demand composite score of 4.72, at the 59th percentile, reinforces this interpretation of a near-median labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in this metro area is 4.20%, ranking at the 36th percentile, which indicates a below-average level of slack in the labor market. This suggests that the market is relatively tight, making it harder for businesses to hire, with potential upward pressure on wages. For a business trying to hire in this market, the relatively low unemployment rate may require offering competitive salaries to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands is 4.13%, placing it at the 51st percentile. This moderate pace of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are seeing some improvement in their purchasing power. The wage growth rate is neither exceptionally high nor stagnant, indicating a relatively stable labor cost environment.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 1.01, ranking at the 96th percentile, Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands is one of the most affordable cities relative to its peers. This high affordability means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset the cost of living, providing a talent attraction advantage. The low cost of living also contributes to a lower real-wage floor, making it easier for businesses to operate.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in this metro area is growing at a rate of 0.93% year-over-year, placing it at the 57th percentile. This positive growth rate indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a favorable condition for businesses looking to hire. The growing labor force provides a structural tailwind for hiring capacity, making it easier for companies to find the talent they need.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -12.89%, ranking at the 30th percentile, indicating a significant tightening of the housing supply. This sharp decline in building permits signals a potential future affordability challenge and workforce accommodation risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. The falling permits may lead to increased competition for available housing, potentially driving up costs.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 50 days, with a year-over-year increase of 11.1%, placing it at the 61st percentile. This indicates a slowing market, where homes are taking longer to sell. For a worker relocating to this city, the relatively slower market may make it easier to find a home, as there is less competition and pressure to act quickly.

Office Economy

With an office and professional worker share composite score of 3.03, ranking at the 64th percentile, Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands has a deep talent pool suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors. This city is well-suited for knowledge-economy businesses but may be less ideal for companies in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which require different types of specialized labor.

The Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands metro area offers businesses a unique combination of affordability, moderate labor demand, and a deep professional talent pool, making it an attractive location for certain types of businesses. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers to factor in is the tightening housing supply, signaled by the sharp decline in building permits, which may lead to future affordability challenges and workforce accommodation risks.

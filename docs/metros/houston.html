Economic Analysis

The Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 52.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and highly affordable cost of living, as evidenced by a 96th percentile cost of living score. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.18, combining a +0.22% employment growth rate and a +0.569% weekly hours deviation from trend, suggests genuine demand expansion. Additionally, the cost of living, with a PSF of $173/sqft and a -2.3% YoY change, versus $36.90/hr, presents a favorable ratio of 4.69.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.22% and weekly hours deviation of +0.569% from the city's own trend indicate genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This combination signals a healthy labor market where businesses are adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, suggesting strong economic activity. The labor demand composite score of 5.18 further reinforces this interpretation, placing the city in the 62nd percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.30% is below average, ranking in the 30th percentile, which means the labor market has some slack but is not overly loose. This rate implies that while it may not be extremely difficult to hire, there is still a manageable pool of potential employees, and businesses may face some competition for talent. However, the relatively low unemployment rate suggests that wage pressure might be moderate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +1.85% is below average, ranking in the 26th percentile, indicating moderate to slow wage growth. This suggests that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, which could be beneficial for businesses looking to control expenses. However, for workers, this means their purchasing power is not increasing as quickly as in other metros.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living score in the 96th percentile, Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands is highly affordable relative to its peers, thanks to a PSF of $173/sqft that is decreasing by -2.3% YoY, compared to hourly earnings of $36.90. This affordability is a significant talent attraction advantage, as businesses can recruit without needing to offer substantial wage premiums to offset high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of -0.05% year-over-year, which is near the median, ranking in the 58th percentile. This slight contraction in labor force supply suggests a potential structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of available workers is not expanding. Businesses may need to compete more intensely for a smaller pool of new entrants into the workforce.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -17.22%, indicating a tightening in housing supply. This sharp decline suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation might be at risk, as the supply of new housing units is not keeping pace with demand. This could lead to increased housing costs and reduced attractiveness for relocating workers.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 49 days, with an 11.4% year-over-year increase, placing it in the 88th percentile. This slower market means that homes are sitting longer before being sold, which could be beneficial for workers relocating to the area, as they have more time to find a suitable home. However, this also indicates a less competitive housing market.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share composite score ranking in the 80th percentile, Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands has a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This makes the city an attractive location for businesses in these sectors but less ideal for those in industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Houston-Pasadena-The Woodlands metro offers businesses a unique combination of affordable living, a deep professional talent pool, and genuine labor demand expansion, making it an attractive location for certain types of businesses. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tightening housing supply, signaled by a sharp decline in building permits, which could erode the city's affordability advantage and make it less attractive for relocating workers over time.

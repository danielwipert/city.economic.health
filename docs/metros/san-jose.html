Economic Analysis

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 52.8th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its exceptional labor demand and deep office economy. The city's labor demand composite score of 7.35, combining a 1.32% employment growth rate and a 1.205% weekly hours deviation above trend, signals a genuine demand expansion. This, coupled with its high share of professional and office workers, defines the city's current economic character.

Labor Demand

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area exhibits a strong labor demand, with a 1.32% employment growth rate and weekly hours 1.205% above its own 12-month trend. This combination signals a genuine demand expansion, indicating that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, a sign of a thriving economy. The labor demand composite score of 7.35 places the city in the top tier, at the 96th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara stands at 3.70%, which is relatively low and places the city at the 74th percentile. This tight labor market implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. However, it also suggests a strong local economy with high demand for labor.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in the San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area is 0.00%, indicating stagnant wages. This slow wage growth environment means that labor costs for employers are not increasing, but it also signifies weak bargaining power for workers and potentially less purchasing power. The 6th percentile rank in wage growth highlights the city's challenge in this area.

Cost of Living

San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara has a cost of living score that places it in the bottom tier, at the 2nd percentile, due to its high price per square foot of $844 and a ratio of PSF to hourly earnings of 15.59. Although the PSF is decreasing by 3.0% year-over-year, the city remains expensive relative to wages. This expense could hinder talent attraction, as businesses may need to offer wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara is shrinking at a rate of -1.19% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor supply. This decline suggests that the city may face structural headwinds in hiring, as the pool of potential workers is decreasing. The 28th percentile rank in labor force growth underscores the challenge of finding and retaining talent in this market.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara has increased by 9.92% year-over-year, signaling an expansion in housing supply. This growth in permits suggests that developer confidence is strong, and the future housing supply is likely to improve, which could enhance affordability and make the city more attractive for workforce accommodation. The 62nd percentile rank in building permits growth indicates a positive trend for future affordability.

Days on Market

Homes in San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara are currently sitting on the market for a median of 29 days, with a year-over-year increase of 7.4%. This indicates a slightly slower market, which could make it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, the 24th percentile rank suggests that the market remains competitive, and workers may still face challenges in finding suitable housing quickly.

Office Economy

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area has a deep professional and office worker talent pool, ranking at the 90th percentile. This makes the city highly suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors that require specialized knowledge-economy talent. Conversely, it may be less ideal for industries dominated by industrial or logistics roles.

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand and a deep talent pool in professional and office sectors, making it an attractive location for certain industries. However, the city's high cost of living and stagnant wage growth pose significant challenges, particularly in terms of talent attraction and retention, which decision-makers must carefully consider when evaluating this location for their business operations.

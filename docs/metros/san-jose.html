Economic Analysis

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 51.7th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and office economy. The city's labor demand composite score of 6.38, which combines a 1.69% employment growth rate and a -0.289% weekly hours deviation, signals genuine demand expansion. The unemployment rate and cost of living ratio are also key metrics, with the city's 4.00% unemployment rate and 5.74 ratio of price per square foot to hourly earnings indicating a mix of tight labor market conditions and high living costs.

Labor Demand

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area has a labor demand composite score of 6.38, driven by a 1.69% employment growth rate and a -0.289% weekly hours deviation, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs at a rate above the national average, with hours worked also increasing, albeit slightly below trend. The strong labor demand suggests a competitive market for workers, with businesses likely to face challenges in attracting and retaining talent.

Unemployment

The city's unemployment rate of 4.00% ranks at the 49th percentile, indicating a near-median level of labor market slack. This rate suggests that the market is relatively tight, with some competition for workers, making it potentially challenging for businesses to hire and staff their operations. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also indicates a stable and experienced workforce, which can be beneficial for businesses looking to establish a strong team.

Wage Growth

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area has a year-over-year wage growth rate of 0.65%, ranking at the 11th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are not increasing rapidly, but it also implies that workers may have limited bargaining power and purchasing power. The slow wage growth may make it easier for businesses to manage labor costs, but it could also limit the attractiveness of the city to workers seeking better compensation.

Cost of Living

The city's cost of living ratio of 5.74, ranking at the 2nd percentile, indicates that the city is extremely expensive relative to peers. This low percentile rank means that the city is one of the most expensive in the country, making it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering significant wage premiums. The high cost of living may limit the city's appeal to workers and businesses alike, as it can erode the purchasing power of workers and increase the cost of living for businesses.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in the San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area is growing at a rate of 0.78% year-over-year, ranking at the 51st percentile, indicating a near-median expansion of the workforce. This growth rate suggests that the supply of workers is increasing, but at a moderate pace, which may help businesses meet their hiring needs. However, the relatively slow growth rate may also indicate that the city's workforce is not expanding rapidly enough to meet the demands of a rapidly growing economy.

Building Permits

The city has seen a 195.09% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking at the 98th percentile, indicating a rapid expansion of housing supply. This surge in building permits suggests that developers are confident in the city's future growth prospects, and that the housing supply is likely to increase in the coming years. The rapid growth in building permits may help alleviate some of the pressure on the city's housing market, making it easier for workers to find affordable housing and for businesses to attract talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in the San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area is 24 days, with a 9.1% year-over-year increase, ranking at the 2nd percentile, indicating a very competitive market. This rapid pace of home sales suggests that the city's housing market is highly competitive, making it challenging for workers to find and purchase homes. The fast-paced market may make it difficult for businesses to attract and retain talent, as workers may struggle to find affordable housing options.

Office Economy

The city's office economy is deep, with a professional and office worker share of 3.74, ranking at the 87th percentile, indicating a highly specialized and knowledge-intensive workforce. This suggests that the city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, which require a highly skilled and educated workforce. However, the city may be less suitable for businesses in industries that require a more general or manual workforce, such as manufacturing or logistics.

The San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metro area offers businesses a highly skilled and educated workforce, a strong office economy, and a rapidly expanding housing supply, making it an attractive location for businesses in the tech and finance sectors. However, the city's high cost of living, stagnant wage growth, and competitive housing market may pose significant challenges for businesses looking to attract and retain talent, making it essential for decision-makers to carefully consider these factors when evaluating the city as a potential location.

Economic Analysis

Kansas City earns an overall grade of A+ with a composite score of 75.2th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. The city's economic character is defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score of 5.47, and its top-tier wage growth of 6.36% year-over-year. These metrics signal a city with a thriving job market and rising earnings.

Labor Demand

Kansas City's employment growth rate is 0.57% year-over-year, combined with a 0.372% deviation in weekly hours above its own trend, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, a sign of a strong and growing economy. The labor demand composite score of 5.47 is in the top tier, at the 88th percentile.

Unemployment

The city's unemployment rate is 4.20%, which is above average, ranking at the 68th percentile. This means the market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter labor markets. However, the relatively low unemployment rate still suggests that businesses may face some competition for top talent.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Kansas City is 6.36% year-over-year, which is in the top tier, ranking at the 85th percentile. This fast wage growth signals rising labor costs for employers, but it also means workers have increasing purchasing power, which can benefit local businesses. The strong wage growth is a double-edged sword, offering benefits for workers but potential challenges for businesses looking to control labor costs.

Cost of Living

Kansas City has a cost of living score that ranks at the 86th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $199/sqft to $35.51/hr, or 5.60. This high percentile rank means the city is more affordable relative to its peers. The affordability of Kansas City gives businesses a talent attraction advantage, as they can offer competitive wages without needing large premiums to offset high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Kansas City is growing at a rate of 1.87% year-over-year, which is in the top tier, ranking at the 89th percentile. This expanding labor force supply means that businesses have a growing pool of potential workers to hire from, making it easier to find talent. The positive labor force growth is a significant advantage for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Kansas City is increasing by 5.41% year-over-year, which is above average, ranking at the 62nd percentile. This expansion in housing supply signals that the city is likely to see improving affordability and a more accommodating environment for its workforce in the future. The growth in building permits is a positive sign for businesses concerned about the long-term viability of their workforce's housing needs.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Kansas City is 41 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 12.8%. This fast-paced market, ranking at the 9th percentile, means that homes are selling quickly, which can make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. The competitive housing market may require businesses to offer relocation assistance or other incentives to attract top talent.

Office Economy

Kansas City's professional and office worker share ranks at the 37th percentile, indicating a less deep talent pool in these sectors compared to other cities. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses that require a large number of specialized office or tech workers, but may be more appropriate for industries with different workforce needs.

In conclusion, Kansas City offers businesses a strong labor market with growing demand, rising wages, and an affordable cost of living. However, the city's competitive housing market, with homes selling quickly, may pose a challenge for relocating workers, and businesses should factor this into their decision-making process, potentially considering relocation assistance or other incentives to attract top talent.

Economic Analysis

Kansas City earns an overall grade of A+ with a composite score of 69.2th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. The city's economic character is largely defined by its strong wage growth, with a year-over-year increase of 6.99%, and its highly affordable cost of living, with a PSF to earnings ratio of $199/sqft to $35.79/hr, resulting in a top-tier percentile rank of 90th. These metrics suggest a city with a competitive labor market and attractive living conditions.

Labor Demand

Kansas City's labor demand composite score is 4.58, with employment growth of 0.04% and weekly hours deviation of 0.223% above its own trend. This combination signals a genuine demand expansion, albeit at a moderate pace. The city's labor market is growing, but not at a breakneck speed, indicating a stable and sustainable economic environment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Kansas City is 4.20%, which corresponds to an above-average percentile rank of 68th. This indicates a relatively tight labor market with some slack, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire new talent. However, the unemployment rate is not excessively low, suggesting that businesses can still find qualified workers without excessive wage pressure.

Wage Growth

With a year-over-year wage growth rate of 6.99%, Kansas City is experiencing fast and rising labor costs. This strong wage growth is beneficial for worker purchasing power, but it may pose challenges for employers looking to maintain a stable cost environment. As a result, businesses may need to adjust their compensation packages to remain competitive in the local labor market.

Cost of Living

Kansas City's cost of living is highly affordable, with a top-tier percentile rank of 90th. The city's PSF to earnings ratio of $199/sqft to $35.79/hr indicates that housing costs are relatively low compared to local wages. This affordability advantage can be a significant talent attraction factor, allowing businesses to recruit and retain top talent without needing to offer excessive wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Kansas City is growing at a rate of 1.87% year-over-year, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This positive growth rate suggests that the city's hiring capacity is increasing, making it an attractive location for businesses looking to scale their operations.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Kansas City has increased by 34.49% year-over-year, signaling a significant expansion of the city's housing supply. This growth in building permits is a positive indicator for future affordability and workforce accommodation, suggesting that the city is well-positioned to support a growing population and labor force.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Kansas City is 41 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 12.8%. This relatively fast pace of home sales indicates a competitive housing market, which may pose challenges for relocating workers. However, the city's overall affordability and growing housing supply may help mitigate these challenges.

Office Economy

Kansas City's professional talent pool is relatively shallow, with a below-average percentile rank of 26th. The city's office economy is not as deep as some of its peers, making it less suited for businesses that require a large pool of specialized office workers, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may still be an attractive location for businesses in other industries, such as logistics or manufacturing.

In conclusion, Kansas City offers businesses a unique combination of strong wage growth, affordable living conditions, and a growing labor force. However, the city's relatively shallow professional talent pool may pose challenges for businesses that require specialized office workers, making it essential for decision-makers to carefully consider their specific needs and requirements when evaluating Kansas City as a potential location.

Economic Analysis

Kansas City earns an overall grade of A+ with a composite score of 75.5th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. The city's economic character is defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score of 88th percentile, and its top-tier wage growth of 83rd percentile, with hourly earnings increasing by 6.36% year-over-year. These metrics signal a city with a thriving job market and rising labor costs.

Labor Demand

Kansas City's employment growth rate is 0.57% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating 0.372% above the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, indicating that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, a sign of a healthy and growing economy. The labor demand composite score of 88th percentile further reinforces this trend.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Kansas City is 4.10%, which corresponds to an above-average percentile rank of 75th. This indicates a relatively tight labor market with some slack, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire, but not excessively so. As a result, businesses may face some wage pressure, but it is manageable.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Kansas City is 6.36%, ranking it in the 83rd percentile. This is a relatively fast pace of wage growth, which implies rising labor costs for employers. However, it also means that workers have increasing purchasing power, which can benefit local businesses that rely on consumer spending.

Cost of Living

Kansas City has a cost of living percentile rank of 85th, with a price-to-salary ratio of $200/sqft to $35.51/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.63. This indicates that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. The stable year-over-year change in PSF of 0.0% suggests that affordability is not deteriorating. As a result, Kansas City has a talent attraction advantage, as it can offer a relatively low cost of living without needing to provide excessive wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Kansas City is growing at a rate of 1.76% year-over-year, ranking it in the 83rd percentile. This indicates that the labor force supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire. The growing labor force provides a structural tailwind for hiring capacity.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Kansas City is increasing by 5.41% year-over-year, ranking it in the 63rd percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which should improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The rising permits signal a positive outlook for future workforce attraction and accommodation.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Kansas City is 45 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 11.8%. This corresponds to a bottom-tier percentile rank of 2nd, indicating a highly competitive market. The fast-selling homes make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing, which may be a concern for businesses looking to attract talent from outside the city.

Office Economy

Kansas City's professional and office worker share is below average, ranking in the 35th percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool is not as deep in professional services as some of its peers. As a result, Kansas City is better suited for businesses that are not heavily reliant on tech, finance, or consulting talent, but may be a good fit for industries like logistics or manufacturing.

In conclusion, Kansas City offers businesses a strong labor market with rising wages, a relatively affordable cost of living, and an expanding labor force. However, the city's highly competitive housing market, with homes selling quickly, may pose a challenge for relocating workers, and businesses should factor this into their decision-making process. Overall, Kansas City is an attractive location for businesses that can navigate its fast-paced housing market.

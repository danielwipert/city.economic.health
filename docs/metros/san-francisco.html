## Original analysis draft (for reference)

San Francisco’s labour market shows modest resilience amid broader economic headwinds. The metro area’s unemployment rate stands at 4.1% (101A_unemployment), up slightly by 0.1 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), reflecting a tightening but not deteriorating job market. Labour demand, as captured by the composite index (107E_ldc_composite: 3.18), remains weak—evidenced by a 0.52% year-on-year contraction in nonfarm payrolls (107E_employment_growth_yoy)—suggesting that job creation lags national trends. Average hourly earnings rose 2.33% (103B_earnings_yoy), below inflation-moderated wage growth seen in leading tech hubs, while the civilian labour force expanded by just 0.43% (102A_clf_yoy), indicating limited workforce supply growth. Weekly hours worked are marginally above the 12-month baseline (0.396% deviation), hinting at stable utilisation despite softer employment trends.

Housing market dynamics present a mixed but cautiously improving picture. Residential construction has surged, with building permits up 86% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), the strongest growth among peer metros and a sign of sustained supply-side response to affordability pressures. However, affordability remains deeply strained, with a cost-of-living composite score of 5.77 (104C_col), placing severe pressure on middle- and lower-income households. The office sector continues to underperform, reflected in a low office worker ratio score of 2.22 (105C_owr), consistent with persistent hybrid work adoption. Meanwhile, housing market velocity has accelerated sharply: homes sell in a median 29 days (204A_dom_level_days), down 6.45% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), though this reflects both improved demand dynamics and historically thin inventory.

**Outlook:** San Francisco’s economy remains in transition, balancing structural challenges in employment and office use against a rebound in housing construction. Near-term labour demand is subdued, but rising permits may alleviate long-term housing constraints. The city’s fiscal and policy resilience will be tested as it navigates a prolonged adjustment in commercial real estate and workforce composition.

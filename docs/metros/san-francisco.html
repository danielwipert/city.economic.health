Economic Analysis

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 54.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and high wage growth, at 74th and 84th percentiles, respectively. The city's economic character is defined by these two metrics, with employment growth at +0.34% year-over-year and weekly hours 1.065% above trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, with jobs being added and hours worked above the city's own 12-month baseline.

Labor Demand

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has an employment growth rate of +0.34% year-over-year and weekly hours 1.065% above trend, indicating genuine demand expansion. This signals that the city is experiencing an increase in jobs and hours worked, which is a positive indicator for businesses looking to locate in the area. The combination of these two metrics suggests that the city's labor market is strong and growing.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in the San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area is 3.90%, which is relatively low and indicates a tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring and retaining workers, as the pool of available labor is limited. As a result, businesses may need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract and retain top talent.

Wage Growth

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has experienced wage growth of +6.70% year-over-year, which is relatively high and indicates a strong labor market. This rapid wage growth may lead to increased labor costs for employers, but it also means that workers have more purchasing power and are likely to be more productive. As a result, businesses may need to factor in higher labor costs when considering locating in the area.

Cost of Living

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has a cost of living score that ranks in the 16th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $664/sqft to $50.65/hr, or 13.11. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive, which may make it difficult for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums. However, the fact that the price per square foot is falling by 3.5% year-over-year may help to improve affordability in the future.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in the San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has contracted by -2.44% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This may create structural headwinds for hiring, as the supply of available workers is decreasing. As a result, businesses may need to consider alternative locations or strategies for attracting and retaining talent.

Building Permits

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has experienced a +0.83% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, which is near the median. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, but at a moderate pace. As a result, the city's affordability and workforce accommodation may improve in the future, but it is unlikely to have a significant impact in the short term.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in the San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area is currently 32 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -8.6%. This indicates a relatively fast-paced market, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, the fact that the market is slowing down slightly may help to create a more balanced environment for buyers and sellers.

Office Economy

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy score that ranks in the 66th percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, which require a high concentration of specialized workers. However, the city may be less suitable for businesses in industries that require a large workforce or have significant logistics requirements.

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with high demand and rapid wage growth, but it also presents challenges such as a tight labor market and high cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering locating in the area is the shrinking labor pool, which may create structural headwinds for hiring and require alternative strategies for attracting and retaining talent. Overall, businesses must carefully weigh the benefits and drawbacks of locating in the San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area and consider factors such as labor costs, talent attraction, and housing affordability when making their decision.

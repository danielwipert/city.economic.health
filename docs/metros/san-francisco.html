Economic Analysis

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 55.0th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score at the 76th percentile, and its extremely low affordability, ranking at the 15th percentile in terms of cost of living. The labor demand is driven by a combination of employment growth and hours worked above trend, signaling genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has an employment growth rate of +0.65% year-over-year and weekly hours deviation of +0.074% from its own trend, indicating a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing an increase in jobs and hours worked, which is a positive indicator for businesses looking to expand or relocate. The labor demand composite score of 5.28 further reinforces this trend, ranking at the 76th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont is 4.10%, ranking at the 47th percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, but it is not overly loose, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. However, this also means that there is some room for wage growth and talent attraction without excessive pressure.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont is +5.54%, ranking at the 70th percentile, indicating above-average wage growth. This suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but it also means that workers have increasing purchasing power, which can be beneficial for businesses that rely on local consumer demand. The strong wage growth is a positive indicator for businesses that can afford to pay competitive wages.

Cost of Living

San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont has a cost of living ratio of 4.52, ranking at the 15th percentile, indicating that it is one of the most expensive cities in the US relative to local wages. This means that the city is at a significant disadvantage in terms of talent attraction, as businesses may need to offer wage premiums to compensate for the high cost of living. The low affordability ranking makes it challenging for businesses to attract and retain talent without incurring significant costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont is growing at a rate of +0.43% year-over-year, ranking at the 32nd percentile, indicating below-average labor force growth. This suggests that the supply of workers is expanding, but at a slower rate than many other cities, which may pose a challenge for businesses looking to hire in the long term. The slow labor force growth may lead to increased competition for talent and upward pressure on wages.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont has increased by +86.01% year-over-year, ranking at the 96th percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This suggests that the city is experiencing a surge in developer confidence, which may lead to improved affordability and increased housing options for workers in the future. The strong growth in building permits is a positive indicator for businesses concerned about workforce accommodation and affordability.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont is 29 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -6.5%, ranking at the 0th percentile, indicating an extremely competitive market. This suggests that homes are selling very quickly, making it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. The fast-paced market may pose a challenge for businesses trying to attract talent from other areas, as workers may struggle to find affordable and available housing.

Office Economy

San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont has an office economy percentile rank of 55th, indicating a near-median depth of professional talent pool. This suggests that the city is suited for a variety of businesses, including tech, finance, and consulting, but may not have the same level of specialization as other cities. The city's office economy is likely to support businesses that require a diverse range of professional skills, but may not be the best fit for businesses that require highly specialized talent.

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, above-average wage growth, and a deep professional talent pool. However, the city's extremely low affordability and competitive housing market pose significant challenges for talent attraction and retention, making it essential for businesses to factor in the high cost of living and potential wage premiums when making location decisions.

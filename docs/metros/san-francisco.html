Economic Analysis

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 54.1th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and high wage growth, scoring 74th and 84th percentiles respectively. The city's economic character is defined by these two metrics, with employment growth at 0.34% year-over-year and weekly hours 1.065% above its own 12-month trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, with more jobs being added and hours running above trend.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 0.34% year-over-year, combined with weekly hours 1.065% above trend, indicates a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion. This suggests that the city is experiencing an increase in economic activity, with businesses hiring more workers and existing employees working more hours. The labor demand composite score of 5.84, ranking in the 74th percentile, further supports this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont is 3.90%, ranking in the 63rd percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight market also implies upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate is 6.70%, ranking in the 84th percentile, indicating fast wage growth. This suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing. The high wage growth rate may attract top talent to the area, but also increases the cost of doing business.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont is relatively high, with a percentile rank of 16, and a price-to-salary ratio of $664/sqft to $50.65/hr, or 13.11. Although the price per square foot is falling by 3.5% year-over-year, the city remains expensive. This may make it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may offset the benefits of working in the area.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is contracting at a rate of -2.44% year-over-year, ranking in the 10th percentile, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This contraction may create a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of available workers is decreasing. Businesses may need to adapt their hiring strategies to attract talent from other areas or invest in training and development programs.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is 0.83%, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile, indicating a modest expansion of housing supply. This suggests that developer confidence is stable, and future housing supply may be expanding, which could improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market is 32 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 8.6%, ranking in the 4th percentile. This indicates a highly competitive market, where homes are selling quickly. For workers relocating to the city, this may mean a challenging and competitive housing market, with limited options and rapid sales.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers is 3.06, ranking in the 66th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This city is well-suited for businesses that require specialized knowledge-economy talent, but may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The San Francisco-Oakland-Fremont metro area offers a strong labor market with high wage growth, making it an attractive location for businesses that require specialized talent. However, the high cost of living and competitive housing market may pose significant challenges for attracting and retaining workers, and the shrinking labor pool may create hiring difficulties, making it essential for businesses to carefully consider these factors when making location decisions.

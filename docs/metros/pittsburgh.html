Economic Analysis

Pittsburgh earns an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 46.9th percentile among 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its strong wage growth, with a year-over-year increase of 6.43%, and its below-average labor demand, signaled by a composite score of 3.45. These metrics suggest a complex economic environment, with rising labor costs but limited job expansion.

Labor Demand

Pittsburgh's employment growth rate is -0.61% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.767% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The city is experiencing a decline in job creation and hours worked, indicating a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Pittsburgh is 4.70%, ranking it below average at the 32nd percentile. This suggests a moderate level of slack in the labor market, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, the unemployment rate is not low enough to indicate a highly competitive market for workers.

Wage Growth

Pittsburgh's year-over-year wage growth is 6.43%, ranking it in the top tier at the 81st percentile. This rapid wage growth signals rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing purchasing power for workers. Businesses should be prepared for higher labor expenses, but can also expect a more affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

Pittsburgh's cost of living is above average, with a percentile rank of 62, and a price-to-salary ratio of $171/sqft to $33.29/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.14. The city's cost of living is increasing, with a 3.0% year-over-year rise in PSF. This makes it relatively less affordable for talent, potentially requiring wage premiums to attract and retain workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Pittsburgh is growing at a rate of 0.51% year-over-year, ranking it near the median at the 50th percentile. This modest expansion in labor supply suggests a relatively stable hiring environment, with some capacity for businesses to attract new workers.

Building Permits

Pittsburgh's residential building permits are declining at a rate of -4.03% year-over-year, ranking it near the median at the 45th percentile. This slowdown in housing development signals a potential tightening of the housing supply, which could impact future affordability and workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Pittsburgh is 50 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.4%. This rising DOM suggests a slowing market, making it relatively more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, the absolute level of 50 days still indicates a moderately competitive market.

Office Economy

Pittsburgh's professional and office worker share is ranked near the median at the 42nd percentile, indicating a moderate depth of talent in these sectors. The city is relatively well-suited for businesses that can thrive in a mixed economy, but may not offer the same level of specialized talent as more knowledge-economy dominant cities.

In conclusion, Pittsburgh offers a complex economic environment with strong wage growth, but limited job expansion and a relatively high cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the potential for rising labor costs and limited affordability, which could impact talent attraction and retention.

Economic Analysis

Pittsburgh earns an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 48.4th percentile among 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its strong wage growth, with a year-over-year increase of 6.43%, and its below-average labor demand, signaled by a composite score of 3.45. These metrics suggest a complex economic environment, with rising labor costs but also a shrinking labor pool.

Labor Demand

Pittsburgh's employment growth rate is -0.61% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.767% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job creation and hours worked. This suggests that businesses may face less competition for workers, but also a less dynamic labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Pittsburgh is 4.70%, ranking it 31st percentile among US metros. This indicates a relatively slack labor market, with some availability of workers for hire. For a business trying to hire in Pittsburgh, this means that there may be less wage pressure and easier staffing, but also potentially weaker local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

Pittsburgh's year-over-year wage growth rate is 6.43%, ranking it in the top tier at the 84th percentile. This signals fast-rising labor costs for employers, but also strong worker purchasing power. As a result, businesses may face increasing costs, but workers will have more disposable income to spend in the local economy.

Cost of Living

Pittsburgh's cost of living is near median, with a percentile rank of 57th, and a price-to-salary ratio of $171/sqft to $33.29/hr, or 5.14. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage without requiring wage premiums. However, with PSF rising 3.0% year-over-year, affordability may be decreasing over time.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Pittsburgh is growing at a rate of 0.51% year-over-year, ranking it near median at the 52nd percentile. This indicates a slowly expanding labor pool, which could support modest hiring capacity. However, this growth rate is relatively slow, which may limit the city's ability to accommodate large-scale workforce expansion.

Building Permits

Pittsburgh's residential building permits are declining at a rate of -4.03% year-over-year, ranking it near median at the 48th percentile. This signals a tightening housing supply, which could lead to decreasing affordability and increasing competition for workforce accommodation in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Pittsburgh is 50 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.4%, ranking it above average at the 60th percentile. This suggests a slowing market, which could make it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this also indicates a potential decrease in demand for housing, which could have implications for the local economy.

Office Economy

Pittsburgh's professional and office worker share is ranked near median at the 44th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This makes the city suited for businesses that require a mix of professional and industrial skills, but potentially less competitive for tech, finance, or consulting firms that require a highly specialized workforce.

In conclusion, Pittsburgh offers a complex economic environment with strong wage growth, but also a shrinking labor pool and decreasing affordability. The single biggest risk or constraint for a business considering location in Pittsburgh is the potential for decreasing labor market dynamism and increasing labor costs, which could limit the city's ability to support long-term growth and expansion.

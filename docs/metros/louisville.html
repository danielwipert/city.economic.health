Economic Analysis

The Louisville-Jefferson County metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 30.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score of 1.91 ranking in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile, and its below-average wage growth of 2.34% year-over-year, ranking at the 32nd percentile. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Louisville-Jefferson County is -0.80% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +1.270%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job creation and hours worked. The positive deviation in weekly hours may suggest a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Louisville-Jefferson County is 4.00%, ranking at the 65th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight market also implies upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Louisville-Jefferson County is 2.34%, ranking at the 32nd percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. Workers in the area will see some improvement in their purchasing power, but it may not be as significant as in cities with faster wage growth.

Cost of Living

Louisville-Jefferson County has a cost of living score ranking at the 37th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $180/sqft to $31.28/hr, or 5.75. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. The lack of affordability may make it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the cost of living is a significant consideration for workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Louisville-Jefferson County is contracting at a rate of -1.41% year-over-year, ranking at the 26th percentile. This decline in labor force supply signals a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of available workers is shrinking. Businesses may need to adapt their recruitment strategies to attract talent from other areas or invest in training and development programs.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Louisville-Jefferson County has decreased by 18.58% year-over-year, ranking at the 24th percentile. This decline in building permits suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which may lead to increased competition for available housing and upward pressure on prices. This could exacerbate affordability concerns and make it more challenging for workers to relocate to the area.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Louisville-Jefferson County is 38 days, with no year-over-year change. This relatively slow pace of home sales indicates a buyer-friendly market, which may be beneficial for workers relocating to the area. However, the lack of change in days on market may also suggest that the housing market is not experiencing significant pressure, which could be a sign of a relatively stable but unremarkable market.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Louisville-Jefferson County is 0.44, ranking at the 4th percentile. This indicates a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making the city less suited for businesses that rely heavily on professional or office workers, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. The city may be more suitable for industries with different workforce requirements, such as logistics or manufacturing.

The Louisville-Jefferson County metro area offers businesses a relatively stable but unremarkable economic environment, with some challenges in labor demand, wage growth, and affordability. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the declining labor force supply, which may limit the city's ability to support business expansion or relocation. Businesses will need to carefully consider these factors when evaluating Louisville-Jefferson County as a potential location.

Economic Analysis

The Louisville-Jefferson County metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its labor demand and unemployment metrics. The labor demand composite score of 2.76, combining a -1.10% employment growth rate and a -0.245% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. The unemployment rate of 4.60% also plays a significant role in defining the city's economic character.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Louisville-Jefferson County is -1.10%, indicating a decline in job additions, while the weekly hours deviation from trend is -0.245%, signaling that hours are running below trend. This combination suggests a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The labor demand composite score of 2.76 falls in the bottom tier, at the 14th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Louisville-Jefferson County is 4.60%, which is above average, ranking at the 73rd percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire. However, the relatively low unemployment rate still suggests that wage pressure may be present, albeit not as intense as in tighter labor markets.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Louisville-Jefferson County is +2.35%, which is below average, ranking at the 28th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit not rapidly. The wage growth rate is not stagnant, but it is not fast enough to drive significant economic expansion.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Louisville-Jefferson County, with a PSF of $180/sqft and average hourly earnings of $31.52/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.71, is near the median, ranking at the 41st percentile. This means that the city is neither extremely affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. The modest affordability level may not provide a significant talent attraction advantage without wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The year-over-year growth rate of the civilian labor force in Louisville-Jefferson County is -1.24%, indicating a contraction in the labor force supply. This decline suggests that the hiring capacity may be limited, posing a structural headwind for businesses looking to expand or relocate to the area.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is not available, but the metric is ranked at the 50th percentile, indicating a neutral trend. Without specific data, it is challenging to determine whether housing supply is expanding or tightening, but the median ranking suggests that the city's housing market may be relatively stable.

Days on Market

The days on market metric is not available, but it is ranked at the 4th percentile, indicating a very competitive market. However, without the actual days on market and year-over-year direction, it is difficult to assess the implications for relocating workers. A low percentile rank typically suggests a fast-paced market, which can be challenging for workers to find housing.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Louisville-Jefferson County is ranked at the 50th percentile, indicating a moderate depth of professional talent pool. This suggests that the city may be suited for a variety of businesses, but it may not have a significant advantage in attracting tech, finance, or consulting firms that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The Louisville-Jefferson County metro area offers a moderate cost of living and a relatively slack labor market, which may be attractive to businesses looking to hire. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the contraction in labor demand and labor force supply, which may limit the city's ability to support significant business expansion or relocation.

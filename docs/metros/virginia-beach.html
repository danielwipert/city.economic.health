Economic Analysis

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 46.0th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier, and its high wage growth, with a year-over-year change of +6.78% ranking it in the top tier. The combination of these metrics suggests a complex economic environment with both challenges and opportunities.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in this metro area is -0.73% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +2.049%. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 1.78 is in the bottom tier, indicating a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in this metro area is 3.50%, ranking it near the median at the 53rd percentile. This suggests a relatively balanced market with neither significant slack nor extreme tightness. For a business trying to hire in this area, the moderate unemployment rate implies a manageable but not extremely favorable hiring environment.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in this metro area is +6.78%, ranking it in the top tier at the 88th percentile. This indicates fast wage growth, which implies rising labor costs for employers but strong purchasing power for workers. Businesses operating in this area should be prepared for increasing labor costs.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in this metro area is relatively high, with a PSF of $224/sqft and an earnings ratio of 6.32, ranking it at the 61st percentile. This means the city is less affordable than many of its peers, which could make it harder to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The fact that PSF is increasing by +1.8% year-over-year further exacerbates the affordability challenge.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in this metro area is shrinking at a rate of -1.08% year-over-year, ranking it below average at the 35th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply implies a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the workers they need.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in this metro area is increasing by +69.04% year-over-year, ranking it in the top tier at the 94th percentile. This significant expansion in housing supply signals improving affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce in the future.

Days on Market

The current median days on market for homes in this area is 33 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -8.3%. This fast-paced market, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile, indicates a competitive environment for workers relocating to this city, potentially making it tougher for them to find housing.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in this metro area is below average, ranking at the 20th percentile. This suggests a less deep talent pool for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, or HQ operations, making this city less suited for such industries.

In conclusion, the Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area offers a complex mix of high wage growth and challenging labor demand, alongside an expanding housing supply. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the shrinking labor force, which could significantly impede hiring capacity and growth plans.

Economic Analysis

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 43.7th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand, with a labor demand composite score ranking in the bottom tier at 4th percentile, and strong wage growth, with a year-over-year change in average hourly earnings of +6.78%, ranking in the top tier at 88th percentile. These two metrics signal a complex economic environment with both challenges and opportunities.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in this metro area is -0.73% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +2.049%. However, the combination of these two metrics signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion, as the hours increase is likely due to a survivor squeeze where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles. This suggests that the labor market is not expanding, and businesses may face challenges in finding new talent.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in this metro area is 3.90%, which is near the median, ranking at the 44th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, but it is not extremely loose, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. The practical implication is that businesses may need to offer competitive wages to attract top talent, but they are not likely to face extreme wage pressure.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in this metro area is +6.78%, which is relatively fast, ranking in the top tier at 88th percentile. This signals that labor costs for employers are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing. As a result, businesses may need to budget for higher labor costs, but they can also expect to benefit from a more affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in this metro area is near the median, with a percentile rank of 55th, and a price-to-salary ratio of 6.29, based on $223/sqft and $35.44/hr. This means that the city is moderately affordable, but not extremely attractive for talent without wage premiums. The fact that the PSF is rising by +2.3% year-over-year relative to wages may indicate that affordability is decreasing over time.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in this metro area is contracting at a rate of -0.41% year-over-year, which is below average, ranking at the 36th percentile. This implies that the supply of workers is shrinking, creating a structural headwind for hiring. Businesses may need to consider recruiting from outside the area or investing in training and development programs to build the skills they need.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in this metro area is increasing by +38.19% year-over-year, which is a top-tier growth rate, ranking at the 86th percentile. This signals that housing supply is expanding, which should improve affordability and make it easier for workers to relocate to the area. As a result, businesses may benefit from a more accessible and affordable housing market for their employees.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in this metro area is 35 days, with no year-over-year change. This is a relatively fast market, ranking below average at the 26th percentile. For a worker relocating to this city, this means that the housing market is competitive, and they may need to act quickly to secure a home.

Office Economy

The professional talent pool in this metro area is relatively shallow, ranking below average at the 20th percentile. This means that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, it may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-oriented operations.

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area offers businesses a complex economic environment with both challenges and opportunities. While it has strong wage growth and an expanding housing supply, its weak labor demand and shrinking labor force create significant risks for businesses looking to hire and grow. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the limited availability of skilled talent, which may require businesses to invest in recruitment and training programs to build the skills they need.

Economic Analysis

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 45.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand, with a labor demand composite score in the bottom tier at 4th percentile, and strong wage growth, with a year-over-year change in average hourly earnings of +7.51%, ranking it in the top tier at 90th percentile. These two metrics suggest a complex economic environment with both challenges and opportunities.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in this metro area is -0.66% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +1.552%. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 1.88 is in the bottom tier, indicating a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in this metro area is 4.00%, ranking it near the median at 59th percentile. This suggests a relatively balanced labor market with some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire compared to extremely tight markets. However, the practical implication for a business trying to hire here is that it may not face extreme wage pressure, but still needs to compete for talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in this metro area is +7.51%, ranking it in the top tier at 90th percentile. This indicates fast wage growth, which implies rising labor costs for employers but also strong worker purchasing power. Businesses operating in this area should be prepared for increasing labor costs, but also benefit from a workforce with growing disposable income.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in this metro area is near the median, with a percentile rank of 59th, and a PSF to earnings ratio of $223/sqft vs $35.86/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.22. The PSF is increasing by +2.3% year-over-year, which may impact affordability. This suggests that the city is neither extremely affordable nor expensive relative to peers, and businesses may not have a significant talent attraction advantage without offering wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in this metro area is shrinking at a rate of -1.18% year-over-year, ranking it in the bottom tier at 16th percentile. This indicates a contracting labor pool, which poses a structural headwind for hiring and may limit businesses' ability to find and recruit talent.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in this metro area is increasing by +38.19% year-over-year, ranking it in the top tier at 86th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. This is a positive signal for businesses considering relocation, as it indicates a more favorable environment for their employees to find housing.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in this metro area is 35 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%, ranking it below average at 26th percentile. This indicates a relatively competitive market, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find and secure housing quickly. However, the stable days on market suggests a balanced market without extreme pressure.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in this metro area is below average, with a percentile rank of 22nd. This suggests a relatively shallow talent pool for businesses requiring specialized office or professional skills, making it less suited for tech, finance, or consulting businesses. However, it may be more suitable for industries with different skill requirements, such as logistics or manufacturing.

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area offers businesses a complex environment with strong wage growth but weak labor demand and a shrinking labor force. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the limited hiring capacity due to the contracting labor pool, which may require businesses to adapt their recruitment strategies or consider alternative locations with more favorable labor market conditions.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Virginia Beach’s labour market shows mixed signals, with strong wage growth offset by weakening employment and workforce contraction. Average hourly earnings rose 11.8% year-on-year—among the fastest in the peer group—indicating tightness in certain sectors or repricing following prior imbalances. However, the unemployment rate increased to 3.6% (up 0.8 percentage points YoY), and nonfarm payroll employment declined by 1.34% over the same period, suggesting moderating demand for labour. The civilian labour force shrank by 2.4% annually, one of the weakest performances nationally, pointing to longer-term demographic or structural outflows that could constrain growth. Weekly hours worked remain slightly above the 12-month baseline (deviation: +0.7%), but the broader labour demand composite sits at its lowest percentile, reflecting broad-based softness in hiring momentum.

Housing market activity has slowed sharply, with residential building permits down 46.8% year-on-year—the weakest rate in the dataset—indicating a significant near-term contraction in construction. Homes are selling relatively quickly, at a median of 36 days on market (down 5.3% YoY), but this reflects subdued inventory rather than robust demand, given the broader downturn in permits and economic momentum. The cost of living, measured as a composite of housing and earnings, is moderately high (score: 1.44), placing affordability pressure on middle-income households despite strong nominal wage gains. Office demand, as proxied by the office worker ratio, is average (score: 2.4), with no clear trend toward expansion or contraction.

**Outlook:** Near-term headwinds dominate, particularly in labour supply and construction. While elevated wage growth may support consumer spending, structural workforce decline and housing underbuilding pose risks to long-term resilience. Economic momentum is likely to remain subdued without offsetting inflows or policy adjustments.

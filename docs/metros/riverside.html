Economic Analysis

The Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking 44.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong wage growth, with a year-over-year increase of 6.68%, and its high unemployment rate of 5.10%, which ranks in the bottom tier at the 13th percentile. These two metrics suggest a complex labor market with both positive and negative trends.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is 0.88% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating -1.883% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a labor demand contraction, as the modest job growth is accompanied by a decrease in working hours, indicating that the labor market is not experiencing genuine demand expansion. This contraction may lead to a surplus of workers, making it easier for businesses to hire.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is 5.10%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 13th percentile. This indicates a labor market with significant slack, making it easier for businesses to find and hire workers. However, this also suggests weaker local consumer demand, which may impact businesses that rely on local spending.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is 6.68%, ranking in the top tier at the 83rd percentile. This fast wage growth rate implies rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing worker purchasing power, which can benefit businesses that rely on local consumer spending. As wages continue to rise, businesses may need to adjust their pricing strategies to remain competitive.

Cost of Living

Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario has a cost of living ratio of 3.12, ranking below average at the 39th percentile. This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. Businesses may need to consider offering higher salaries or benefits to compensate for the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is growing at a rate of 1.15% year-over-year, ranking above average at the 68th percentile. This expanding labor force supply indicates a positive trend for hiring capacity, as more workers are available to fill job openings. This growth can support business expansion and relocation plans.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario has increased by 18.04% year-over-year, ranking above average at the 74th percentile. This significant increase in building permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which can improve affordability and support workforce accommodation. As new housing developments come online, the city may become more attractive to workers and businesses alike.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is 54 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.9%. This indicates a slowing market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, the relatively low percentile rank of 33rd suggests that the market is still relatively competitive, and businesses may need to consider this when planning relocation packages or talent attraction strategies.

Office Economy

Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario has a professional and office worker share of 0.30, ranking in the bottom tier at the 0th percentile. This indicates a relatively shallow talent pool for businesses that require specialized office or professional skills. The city is best suited for businesses that do not rely heavily on these skills, such as industrial or logistics companies, but may be less attractive for tech, finance, or consulting firms.

The Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong wage growth and a relatively large labor force, but also presents challenges such as a high unemployment rate and a relatively expensive cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's limited professional and office talent pool, which may limit the attractiveness of the city for certain types of businesses.

Economic Analysis

The Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 30.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its below-average labor demand, with a composite score of 3.03 ranking it in the 24th percentile, and its above-average wage growth of 5.40% year-over-year. The combination of low employment growth and declining weekly hours suggests a labor market with limited expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is a mere 0.01% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating -1.281% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a labor market that is not experiencing genuine demand expansion, but rather a potential survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. The labor demand composite score of 3.03, ranking in the 24th percentile, further supports this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is 4.90%, ranking in the 22nd percentile, indicating a labor market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it relatively easier to hire in this market, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. The unemployment rate suggests that the market is not overly tight, giving employers some flexibility in their hiring decisions.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is 5.40%, ranking in the 68th percentile, indicating a relatively fast pace of wage growth. This implies that employer labor costs are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing. The strong wage growth may attract workers to the area, but also poses a challenge for businesses looking to keep labor costs under control.

Cost of Living

Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario has a cost of living score ranking it in the 33rd percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $337/sqft to $35.23/hr, or 9.57. The fact that the PSF is falling -2.3% year-over-year is a key driver of the city's relatively affordable ranking. This means that the city may have a talent attraction advantage, as workers can afford to live in the area without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is shrinking at a rate of -2.20% year-over-year, ranking in the 14th percentile. This indicates that the labor force supply is contracting, posing a structural headwind for hiring in the area. Businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers, as the pool of available labor is decreasing.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is declining at a rate of -18.08% year-over-year, ranking in the 26th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and make it harder for workers to relocate to the area. The decline in building permits is a concern for businesses that rely on a steady supply of new housing to attract and retain workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario is currently 56 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.7%, ranking in the 46th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced market, where homes are not selling extremely quickly, but also not sitting on the market for too long. For workers relocating to the area, this suggests a moderately competitive market, where they may have some time to find a suitable home.

Office Economy

Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario has a very shallow professional talent pool, ranking in the 0th percentile. This makes the city less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-oriented economies.

The Riverside-San Bernardino-Ontario metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a labor market with some slack, but also poses significant challenges, including a shrinking labor force and tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the limited labor force growth, which may make it difficult for businesses to find and retain qualified workers in the area.

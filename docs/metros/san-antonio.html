Economic Analysis

The San Antonio-New Braunfels metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.7th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its below-average labor demand and tight unemployment market. The city's labor demand composite score of 3.29 and unemployment rate percentile of 39th define its current economic character, signaling a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire and expand. With employment growth of +0.35% and weekly hours deviation of -2.620%, the city's labor market is characterized by slow job growth and decreasing work hours.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.35% and hours deviation of -2.620% indicate a labor market that is not experiencing genuine demand expansion, but rather a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals that the city's job market is not adding jobs at a rapid pace, and the decrease in weekly hours worked suggests that businesses may be reducing their labor needs. As a result, the labor market is experiencing a contraction, which may lead to a surplus of workers and downward pressure on wages.

Unemployment

Although the exact unemployment rate is not available, the city's unemployment rate percentile of 39th suggests that the market has some slack, but is still relatively tight. This means that businesses may face some challenges in hiring, but the market is not as competitive as those with lower unemployment rates. The practical implication for a business trying to hire in this city is that they may need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.05% is below average, indicating moderate wage growth. This means that employer labor costs are rising, but at a slower pace than in other cities. As a result, workers in this city have some bargaining power, but it is limited, and businesses may not face significant pressure to increase wages.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank of 50th, the city is near the median in terms of affordability, suggesting that it is neither particularly expensive nor affordable compared to its peers. This means that businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent, but the city's affordability is not a major advantage in attracting workers either.

Labor Force Growth

The year-over-year growth rate of the civilian labor force is -0.11%, indicating that the labor force is contracting. This means that the supply of workers is shrinking, which may create structural headwinds for hiring and business expansion. As a result, businesses may need to be more competitive in their hiring practices and offer attractive wages and benefits to attract top talent.

Building Permits

Although the exact year-over-year change in building permits is not available, the city's building permits percentile rank of 50th suggests that housing supply is neither expanding nor tightening significantly. This means that the city's affordability and workforce accommodation are not likely to be significantly impacted by changes in housing supply.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 53 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -8.6%, indicating a relatively fast-paced market. This means that homes are selling quickly, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. As a result, businesses may need to offer relocation assistance or other incentives to attract talent.

Office Economy

With an office economy percentile rank of 50th, the city has a moderate professional talent pool, suggesting that it is suited for a variety of businesses, but may not have the deep knowledge-economy talent pool that tech, finance, or consulting firms require. The city is likely best suited for businesses that require a general workforce, rather than specialized professional or office workers.

The San Antonio-New Braunfels metro area offers businesses a relatively stable labor market with moderate wage growth, but its below-average labor demand and contracting labor force pose significant risks to hiring and expansion. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's shrinking labor force, which may create challenges in attracting and retaining top talent, and businesses should factor this into their location decision. Overall, the city's economic character is defined by its slow job growth and decreasing work hours, making it a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand.

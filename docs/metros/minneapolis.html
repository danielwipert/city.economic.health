Economic Analysis

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 30.4th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score of 3.68, and its stagnant wage growth, at +0.51% year-over-year. The labor demand and wage growth metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is -0.44% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.099% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not adding jobs at a significant rate and hours are not increasing, suggesting a lack of genuine demand expansion. This could lead to a surplus of workers, making it easier for businesses to hire, but also potentially indicating a weaker local economy.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 3.80%, which is below average, ranking 39th percentile. This indicates a relatively tight labor market, making it harder for businesses to hire and potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. However, it also suggests that the local consumer demand may be stronger due to a lower number of unemployed individuals.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is +0.51%, which is stagnant and ranks in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile. This slow wage growth implies a flat cost environment for employers, but it also means that workers have weak bargaining power, potentially limiting their purchasing power and overall economic well-being.

Cost of Living

Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington has a cost of living score that ranks 22nd percentile, with a PSF of $214/sqft and average hourly earnings of $39.19/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.46. The PSF is falling by -0.5% year-over-year, making the city more affordable relative to peers. This affordability advantage can be a talent attraction benefit for businesses, as they may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for a high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is growing at a rate of -0.90% year-over-year, which is near the median but still indicates a contracting labor pool. This contraction implies a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of workers is shrinking, making it potentially more challenging for businesses to find the talent they need.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is -11.97%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This sharp decline in building permits signals a potential future affordability crisis and workforce accommodation challenges, as the supply of housing is not expanding to meet demand, potentially limiting the city's ability to attract and retain workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 34 days, with a year-over-year increase of +3.0%. This indicates a relatively accessible market for workers relocating to the city, as homes are not selling extremely quickly, giving buyers some time to make decisions and find suitable housing.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington ranks in the bottom tier at the 12th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these areas. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

In conclusion, Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a accessible housing market, but it also presents challenges such as low labor demand, stagnant wage growth, and a tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's limited talent pool in professional and office sectors, which may hinder the growth and expansion of businesses that rely on these skills.

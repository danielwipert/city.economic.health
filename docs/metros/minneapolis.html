Economic Analysis

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 34.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 4.16 and an unemployment rate of 4.80%. The city's economic character is most defined by its near-median labor demand and bottom-tier unemployment rate, with the latter being a significant concern for businesses looking to hire. Specifically, the combination of -0.10% employment growth and -0.766% weekly hours deviation signals a contraction in labor demand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is -0.10%, and weekly hours are deviating -0.766% from the city's own trend. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The near-median labor demand composite score of 4.16 and 56th percentile rank reflect this lukewarm labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 4.80%, which is relatively high and corresponds to a bottom-tier percentile rank of 12th. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but also suggesting weaker local consumer demand. As a result, businesses may face less wage pressure when hiring, but may also need to consider the potential impact of a weaker local economy.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is +1.64%, which is below average and corresponds to a 20th percentile rank. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. As a result, worker purchasing power is increasing, but at a relatively slow rate.

Cost of Living

Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington has a cost of living score corresponding to a near-median 41st percentile rank, with a PSF of $215/sqft and average hourly earnings of $39.84/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.40. The fact that PSF is falling -0.9% YoY is a positive sign for affordability. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is growing at a rate of -0.46% year-over-year, which is below average and corresponds to a 30th percentile rank. This contraction in labor force supply suggests that the hiring capacity of businesses may be constrained, making it more difficult to find and recruit talent.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is decreasing at a rate of -1.17% year-over-year, which is near the median and corresponds to a 54th percentile rank. This decline in building permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which could lead to decreased affordability and make it more challenging for workers to relocate to the city.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 33 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%, corresponding to a below-average 20th percentile rank. This relatively fast pace of home sales suggests that the market is competitive, and relocating workers may face challenges in finding and securing housing.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 0.88, corresponding to a bottom-tier 14th percentile rank. This indicates that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that rely heavily on tech, finance, or consulting talent, but potentially more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a large labor market, but the single biggest risk or constraint is the high unemployment rate and contracting labor force, which could make it challenging to find and recruit talent. As a result, businesses should carefully consider the potential impact of these labor market dynamics on their hiring and growth plans. Overall, the city's economic character is defined by its labor market challenges, which may require businesses to adapt their strategies to succeed in this environment.

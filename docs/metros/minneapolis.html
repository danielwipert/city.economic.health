Economic Analysis

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 33.5th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 4.16. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 4.80% and its below-average wage growth of +1.64% year-over-year. The combination of these metrics suggests a tight labor market with moderate wage pressure.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is -0.10% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.766% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not adding jobs at a significant rate and hours are below trend. This suggests a lack of genuine demand expansion in the labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 4.80%, which is relatively high, ranking in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, this also means that local consumer demand may be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is +1.64%, which is below average, ranking at the 21st percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but not rapidly. This may lead to a relatively stable labor cost environment for businesses.

Cost of Living

Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington has a cost of living score with a PSF of $215/sqft, which is decreasing by -0.9% year-over-year, and an earnings ratio of $39.84/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.40. With a percentile rank of 41, the city is near the median in terms of affordability. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which can be a talent attraction advantage without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is growing at a rate of -0.46% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This suggests that the workforce supply is shrinking, which can be a structural headwind for hiring and may limit the city's ability to support business growth.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is -1.17%, indicating a slight tightening of housing supply. This suggests that developer confidence is moderate, and future housing supply may not be expanding rapidly, which can impact affordability and workforce accommodation in the long term.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 33 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%. This indicates a relatively fast-paced market, ranking at the 20th percentile. For a worker relocating to this city, the housing market may be competitive, making it challenging to find and secure a home quickly.

Office Economy

Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington has a professional and office worker share of 0.90, ranking at the 9th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies, and less suited for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on specialized office workers.

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area offers a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate labor cost environment, making it an attractive location for businesses that do not require a deep professional talent pool. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker is the city's contracting labor pool, which can limit its ability to support business growth and make it challenging to find and hire workers in the long term.

Economic Analysis

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area has an overall grade of D, ranking 23.6th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its labor demand and unemployment metrics, which are both below average. The labor demand composite score is 3.68, placing it in the 32nd percentile, while the unemployment rate is 4.50%, ranking in the 16th percentile. These numbers suggest a challenging economic environment, with the labor market being a key concern.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is -0.44% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.099% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze, as both jobs and hours are declining. This suggests that businesses may not face intense competition for workers, but rather a shrinking pool of potential employees.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 4.50%, which is relatively high, ranking in the 16th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, this also means that local consumer demand may be weaker, potentially impacting businesses that rely on local spending.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is +0.51%, which is relatively slow, ranking in the 10th percentile. This suggests that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing significantly. As a result, businesses may not face strong pressure to increase wages, but workers may also have limited disposable income to spend on goods and services.

Cost of Living

Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington has a cost of living score that places it in the 24th percentile, with a PSF of $215/sqft and average hourly earnings of $39.19/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.49. The city's PSF is falling -0.9% year-over-year, which is a positive sign for affordability. This suggests that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage, as businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to compensate for high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is shrinking at a rate of -1.13% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This suggests that the city's hiring capacity may be limited, making it challenging for businesses to find and attract new workers. As a result, businesses may need to invest in retention and training programs to maintain their workforce.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is declining at a rate of -1.17% year-over-year, which is near the median. This suggests that the city's housing supply is not expanding rapidly, which could lead to future affordability and workforce accommodation challenges. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of limited housing options on their ability to attract and retain workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington is 33 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%. This suggests that the housing market is relatively slow, ranking in the 20th percentile. For workers relocating to the city, this means that they may have a more accessible and less competitive housing market, which could be an advantage.

Office Economy

Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington has a professional and office worker share of 0.90, ranking in the 12th percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool is not particularly deep in professional and office sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a high concentration of tech, finance, or consulting talent. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-dominated economies.

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a slower housing market, which could be advantages for talent attraction and retention. However, the city's labor market is a significant concern, with a shrinking labor pool and slow wage growth, which could limit hiring capacity and impact local consumer demand. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the challenging labor market, which may require significant investment in retention and training programs to maintain a skilled workforce.

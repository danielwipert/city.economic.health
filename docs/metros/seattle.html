Economic Analysis

The Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 54.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and office economy. The city's economic character is defined by its top-tier wage growth of 7.37% year-over-year and its significant share of professional and office workers, ranking in the 84th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with a deep talent pool and rising labor costs.

Labor Demand

The Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area has an employment growth rate of 0.08% year-over-year and weekly hours 0.67% above its own trend, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing a moderate increase in job creation, with hours worked also increasing, suggesting that businesses are not only hiring but also utilizing their workforce more intensively. This is a positive sign for businesses looking to expand or establish operations in the area.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is 4.90%, ranking in the 10th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market. This means that businesses may face significant challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is limited, and may need to offer higher wages to attract talent. The tight labor market also suggests that workers have strong bargaining power, which could drive up labor costs.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is 7.37%, ranking in the 96th percentile, indicating very fast wage growth. This rapid wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers will increase, but it also means that workers will have more purchasing power, which could benefit local businesses. The strong wage growth is a double-edged sword, offering benefits to workers but increasing costs for employers.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue, with a price-to-salary ratio of 9.50 and a PSF of $452/sqft that is falling 2.8% year-over-year, ranks in the 39th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive. However, the falling PSF suggests that the city may become more affordable in the future. The relatively high cost of living means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent, but the falling PSF could mitigate this issue over time.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is shrinking at a rate of -0.80% year-over-year, ranking in the 22nd percentile, indicating a contracting labor pool. This decline in the labor force suggests that businesses may face structural headwinds in hiring, as the supply of workers is decreasing. The shrinking labor force could exacerbate the tight labor market, making it even more challenging for businesses to find talent.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue has increased by 42.78% year-over-year, ranking in the 90th percentile, indicating a significant expansion of housing supply. This increase in building permits suggests that the city is investing in its future housing stock, which could improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The expanding housing supply could help mitigate the city's affordability issues and make it more attractive to workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is 34 days, with a year-over-year increase of 13.3%, ranking in the 92nd percentile, indicating a slower market. This means that homes are taking longer to sell, making the market more accessible to relocating workers. The slower market could give workers more time to find a home, reducing the stress and competition associated with relocating to a new city.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is significant, ranking in the 84th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of specialized workers. However, the city may be less suitable for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which require a different type of workforce.

The Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong wage growth, a deep talent pool, and a growing housing supply, making it an attractive location for companies in the tech and finance sectors. However, the city's tight labor market and high cost of living pose significant risks, and businesses should carefully consider these factors when making location decisions. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this city is the tight labor market, which could drive up labor costs and make it challenging to attract and retain talent.

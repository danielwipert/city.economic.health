Economic Analysis

Seattle's labour market is showing signs of moderation, in line with the broader economic cooldown. The unemployment rate has risen to 5.0%, a 1.4 percentage point increase year-on-year, indicating a notable weakening in job market tightness. This is supported by flat nonfarm payroll growth, with employment down 0.15% year-on-year, and a labour demand composite score in the 22nd percentile nationally, suggesting subdued hiring activity. However, average hourly earnings remain robust, with an 8.8% increase over the past year, driven by the city's high-skilled tech and professional services sectors. The civilian labour force has contracted slightly, declining 0.65% year-on-year, which may constrain future growth due to a shrinking workforce supply.

The city's housing and commercial real estate markets present a mixed picture. Residential construction is nearly flat, with building permits down 0.7% year-on-year, although this represents relative resilience compared to other major metros. The median home sale period has increased to 34 days, up 9.7% from a year ago, pointing to a gradually softening market. However, with a composite score of 7.77, days on market remain moderate. In contrast, office occupancy trends are favourable, with an office worker ratio composite score of 3.26, placing Seattle in the 78th percentile nationally and indicating better-than-average return-to-office uptake. Nevertheless, the cost of living remains a constraint, with a composite score of 2.8, near the national median.

Looking ahead, Seattle's economy is transitioning from a high-growth, tech-driven model to a more balanced but slower expansion path. While near-term employment headwinds are a concern, resilient wage growth and strong office utilization provide some offset. Over the longer term, the city's economic trajectory will depend on its ability to address housing supply constraints and maintain workforce inflows, despite elevated living costs.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Seattle’s labour market shows signs of moderation amid a broader economic cooldown. The unemployment rate stands at 5.0% (101A_unemployment), up 1.4 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), reflecting a notable weakening in job market tightness. This is corroborated by flat nonfarm payroll growth, with employment down 0.15% year-on-year (107E_employment_growth_yoy), and a labour demand composite score in the 22nd percentile nationally (107E_ldc_composite), suggesting subdued hiring activity. However, average hourly earnings remain strong, rising 8.8% over the past year (103B_earnings_yoy), a signal of persistent wage pressure likely rooted in the city’s high-skilled tech and professional services sectors. The civilian labour force contracted slightly, declining 0.65% year-on-year (102A_clf_yoy), indicating a shrinking workforce supply that may constrain future growth.

Housing and commercial real estate markets reflect mixed conditions. Residential construction is nearly flat, with building permits down 0.7% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), though this represents relative resilience compared to other major metros. The median home sale takes 34 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 9.7% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), pointing to a gradually softening market, though not one in distress. At a composite score of 7.77 (204A_dom_composite), days on market remain moderate. Office occupancy trends are relatively favourable, with an office worker ratio composite score of 3.26 (105C_owr), placing Seattle in the 78th percentile nationally—indicating better-than-average return-to-office uptake. However, the cost of living remains high, with a composite score of 2.8 (104C_col), near the national median but still a structural constraint on affordability.

**Outlook:** Seattle’s economy is transitioning from a high-growth, tech-driven model to a more balanced but slower expansion path. Near-term headwinds in employment are offset by resilient wage growth and relatively strong office utilization. Over the longer term, the city’s economic trajectory will depend on its ability to expand housing supply and maintain workforce inflows amid elevated living costs.

Economic Analysis

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 32.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 3.15, which is in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. This city's economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand, with employment growth of -0.82% year-over-year, and its relatively high cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.17. The combination of these metrics suggests a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is -0.82% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.777% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with payrolls contracting and hours below trend. The labor demand composite score of 3.15 is in the bottom tier, at the 14th percentile, further emphasizing the weakness in labor demand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 4.40%, which is below average, ranking at the 39th percentile. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, but not excessively so, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also means that there may be some upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is +2.83%, which is below average, ranking at the 33rd percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but at a slow pace.

Cost of Living

Milwaukee-Waukesha has a cost of living percentile rank of 23, indicating that it is relatively expensive, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.17, and PSF increasing by 3.6% year-over-year. This means that the city is less attractive for talent attraction without wage premiums, as the high cost of living may deter some workers from relocating.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Milwaukee-Waukesha is contracting at a rate of -1.07% year-over-year, indicating that the workforce supply is shrinking. This contraction in labor force growth poses a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the talent they need.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Milwaukee-Waukesha is increasing by 15.03% year-over-year, which is above average, ranking at the 67th percentile. This expansion in housing supply suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may improve, as more housing units become available.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 36 days, with a year-over-year increase of 20.0%, ranking at the 98th percentile. This slow market means that workers relocating to the city may have an easier time finding a home, as properties are sitting on the market for longer periods.

Office Economy

The professional and office worker share in Milwaukee-Waukesha is relatively low, ranking at the 7th percentile, indicating a shallow talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics companies, and less suited for tech, finance, or consulting firms.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers businesses a relatively low-cost environment, with moderate wage growth and a slow housing market. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the weak labor demand and contracting labor force growth, which may pose significant challenges for hiring and talent attraction.

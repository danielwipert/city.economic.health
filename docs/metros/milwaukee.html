## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Milwaukee’s labour market shows modest tightness amid broader stagnation. The unemployment rate stands at 3.1% (101A_unemployment), a low level that would typically signal strength, but this is offset by a shrinking civilian labour force, which has declined 3.1% year-on-year (102A_clf_yoy), among the weakest performances nationally (2nd percentile). Employment growth has turned negative, with nonfarm payrolls down 0.9% over the past year (107E_employment_growth_yoy), and labour demand remains subdued (107E_ldc_composite: 2.61, 6th percentile). While average hourly earnings rose 3.8% (103B_earnings_yoy), this gain is only in line with national trends and insufficient to offset structural weaknesses. The office worker ratio (105C_owr: 1.28, 10th percentile) indicates persistent underutilisation of commercial space, reflecting lagging return-to-office momentum.

Housing market indicators point to softening demand and reduced construction activity. Residential building permits have fallen 10.5% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), suggesting waning investor confidence. Homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market at 33 days—up 6.5% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct)—and the composite days-on-market score (204A_dom_composite: 7.25) ranks in the bottom third of metros. The cost of living remains relatively moderate (104C_col: 4.18, 17th percentile), but this advantage is not translating into population or job growth. The combination of a contracting workforce, declining employment, and weak business activity signals structural challenges outweighing short-term labour market tightness.

**Outlook**: Milwaukee faces persistent headwinds in labour supply and business dynamism. Without reversal in workforce outflows and private sector hiring, the city is likely to underperform peer metros in economic growth over the medium term.

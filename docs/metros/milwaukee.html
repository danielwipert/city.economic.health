Economic Analysis

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.5th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its weak labor demand and low labor force growth. The labor demand composite score of 3.25, combining a -0.75% employment growth rate and a -0.438% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in job opportunities. Additionally, the -3.11% labor force growth rate indicates a shrinking workforce, further defining the city's economic character.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of -0.75% and weekly hours deviation of -0.438% from trend indicate a contraction in labor demand, signaling that the city is experiencing a genuine decline in job opportunities. This combination suggests that businesses may face less competition for talent, but also implies a weaker local economy. The labor demand composite score of 3.25 falls in the bottom tier, at the 16th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 3.10% is relatively low, ranking in the top tier at the 83rd percentile, indicating a tight labor market with limited slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in Milwaukee-Waukesha may face challenges in finding available talent, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract workers. The low unemployment rate also suggests that workers may have more bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.53% is below average, ranking at the 38th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This suggests that employer labor costs are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. Workers in Milwaukee-Waukesha may see some improvement in their purchasing power, but the moderate wage growth may not be enough to drive significant economic expansion.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 4.18, ranking at the 22nd percentile, Milwaukee-Waukesha is relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that the city may struggle to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may offset the benefits of working in the area. The low percentile rank indicates that the city is less competitive in terms of affordability.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is contracting at a rate of -3.11% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking workforce and a structural headwind for hiring. This decline in labor force growth suggests that businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers, and may need to invest in training and development programs to build the skills they need. The low labor force growth rate is a significant concern for the city's economic future.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -10.47%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This decline in permits suggests that the city may face future affordability challenges and workforce accommodation constraints, making it harder for businesses to attract and retain talent. The near-median percentile rank of 40th percentile indicates that the city is not alone in its struggles with housing supply.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 33 days, with a year-over-year increase of +6.5%, indicating a slowing market. This means that workers relocating to Milwaukee-Waukesha may find a more accessible and less competitive housing market, with more time to find a suitable home. The below-average percentile rank of 37th percentile suggests that the city's housing market is not as fast-paced as some other areas.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share of 0.94, ranking at the 13th percentile, Milwaukee-Waukesha has a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This suggests that the city may be less suited for businesses that require a deep pool of specialized office workers, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses with industrial or logistics-oriented operations.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers businesses a relatively low-unemployment environment with moderate wage growth, but its weak labor demand and low labor force growth pose significant challenges. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the shrinking workforce and tightening housing supply, which may limit their ability to attract and retain talent. Overall, the city's economic character is defined by its contraction in job opportunities and limited labor market growth.

Economic Analysis

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 33.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 3.03, indicating a below-average performance. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand and high cost of living, with a cost of living ratio of 6.29, resulting from $227/sqft and $36.11/hr, and ranking in the 12th percentile. This suggests that the city's economy is experiencing a contraction in labor demand, which is a significant concern for businesses looking to locate here.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is -0.91% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.049% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, as both employment growth and hours worked are below trend. As a result, businesses may face challenges in finding skilled workers, but may also have an easier time managing labor costs.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 4.20%, which is below average, ranking in the 28th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also suggests that wage pressure may be moderate, as workers have some bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is +1.49%, which is below average, ranking in the 20th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slow pace. As a result, worker purchasing power is increasing, but at a moderate rate, which may have a positive impact on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Milwaukee-Waukesha has a cost of living ratio of 6.29, resulting from $227/sqft and $36.11/hr, and ranking in the 12th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive. This means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent, as the high cost of living may be a deterrent for some workers. However, the city's relatively low cost of living compared to other major metros may still make it an attractive option for some businesses.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Milwaukee-Waukesha is growing at a rate of -0.01% year-over-year, which is near the median. This indicates that the labor force supply is essentially stable, neither expanding nor contracting significantly. As a result, businesses may face moderate challenges in finding skilled workers, but the stable labor force supply may also provide some stability for employers.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Milwaukee-Waukesha is increasing by +15.03% year-over-year, which is above average, ranking in the 70th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. As a result, businesses may find it easier to attract and retain workers, as the increasing housing supply may reduce the cost of living.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 36 days, with a year-over-year increase of +20.0%, ranking in the 98th percentile. This indicates that the housing market is slowing down, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. As a result, businesses may find it easier to attract workers, as the slower housing market may provide more options for relocating employees.

Office Economy

Milwaukee-Waukesha has a professional and office worker share of 1.16, ranking in the 16th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This suggests that the city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics companies. However, the city may not be the best fit for tech, finance, or consulting businesses that require a highly specialized workforce.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers businesses a relatively stable labor force supply and an expanding housing market, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. However, the city's high cost of living and low labor demand are significant concerns, and businesses should carefully consider these factors when making a location decision. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this city is the high cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract talent, and the low labor demand, which may limit the availability of skilled workers.

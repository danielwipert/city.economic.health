Economic Analysis

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 32.4th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 2.91, indicating a struggling job market. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand and high cost of living, with a cost of living ratio of 6.26, driven by a PSF of $228/sqft and average hourly earnings of $36.43. The combination of negative employment growth and declining weekly hours further emphasizes the city's economic challenges.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate is -0.99% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating -0.777% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city experiencing a decline in both job creation and working hours. As a result, businesses may face a challenging environment for growth and expansion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate is 3.90%, ranking 71st percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses trying to hire in Milwaukee-Waukesha may face competition for talent and upward pressure on wages. With a tight labor market, companies may need to offer more competitive compensation packages to attract and retain employees.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate is +3.28%, ranking near the median at 44th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a moderate pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing. As a result, businesses may need to balance their labor costs with the benefits of a moderately growing workforce.

Cost of Living

The city's cost of living is ranked 13th percentile, with a PSF of $228/sqft and average hourly earnings of $36.43, resulting in a ratio of 6.26, indicating an expensive city relative to peers. This means that Milwaukee-Waukesha may struggle to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may deter potential employees. The city's low affordability score may limit its appeal to businesses looking to relocate or expand.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of -2.89% YoY, indicating a contracting labor pool. This decline in labor force growth signals a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of available workers is shrinking. Businesses may face challenges in finding and recruiting talent in a declining labor market.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -10.47%, indicating a tightening housing supply. This decline in building permits signals a potential supply squeeze, which may lead to decreased affordability and reduced workforce accommodation in the future. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of a tightening housing market on their employees' quality of life and ability to relocate.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 33 days, with a YoY increase of +6.5%, indicating a slowing market. This means that workers relocating to Milwaukee-Waukesha may find a more accessible and less competitive housing market, with homes taking longer to sell. However, this slowing market may also signal a weakening demand for housing, which could have implications for the local economy.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors is 0.58, ranking 4th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This suggests that Milwaukee-Waukesha is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies. However, the city may be less attractive to tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on a specialized workforce.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers a relatively tight labor market and moderate wage growth, but its high cost of living and declining labor force growth pose significant challenges for businesses. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's low affordability and shrinking labor pool, which may limit its appeal to businesses looking to relocate or expand, and require companies to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent.

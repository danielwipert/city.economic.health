Economic Analysis

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 34.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 3.15, indicating a below-average performance. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand and labor force growth metrics, with employment growth at -0.82% year-over-year and labor force growth at -1.07% year-over-year. These numbers suggest a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or hire in the area.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is -0.82% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.777% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city experiencing a decline in both jobs and hours worked. As a result, businesses may face a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 4.40%, ranking 39th percentile, indicating a moderate level of slack in the labor market. While the market is not extremely tight, it is still relatively competitive, with some implications for businesses trying to hire in the area. However, the moderate unemployment rate suggests that businesses may not face extreme wage pressure or hiring difficulties.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is +2.83%, ranking 32nd percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not extremely rapidly, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but not dramatically. As a result, businesses may need to balance rising labor costs with the need to attract and retain talent.

Cost of Living

Milwaukee-Waukesha has a cost of living score of 22nd percentile, with a PSF of $227/sqft and average hourly earnings of $36.79/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.17. This suggests that the city is relatively expensive, ranking below average in terms of affordability. However, the high cost of living percentile rank indicates that the city is actually less affordable than its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Milwaukee-Waukesha is growing at -1.07% year-over-year, indicating a contraction in the labor force supply. This decline suggests that the city's workforce is shrinking, creating a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion. As a result, businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers to fill open positions.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Milwaukee-Waukesha is increasing by +15.03% year-over-year, indicating an expansion in housing supply. This growth suggests that developer confidence is rising, and future housing supply is likely to increase, improving affordability and workforce accommodation. As a result, the city may become more attractive to relocating workers and businesses in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 36 days, with a year-over-year increase of +20.0%, ranking 98th percentile. This suggests that the housing market is relatively slow, with homes taking longer to sell. For workers relocating to the city, this means a more accessible and less competitive housing market, making it easier to find and purchase a home.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 0.74, ranking 8th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This suggests that the city is not well-suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, the city may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses that require a different type of workforce.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers businesses a relatively moderate labor market, with some challenges in terms of labor demand and labor force growth. However, the city's expanding housing supply and relatively slow housing market may make it an attractive location for workers and businesses looking for a more accessible and affordable place to live and work. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this area is the shrinking labor force, which may limit hiring capacity and business expansion.

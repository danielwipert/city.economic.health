Economic Analysis

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 36.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its below-average labor demand and high cost of living. The labor demand composite score of 3.03, combining a -0.91% employment growth rate and a -0.049% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. Additionally, the cost of living, with a PSF of $227/sqft and an earnings ratio of 6.29, ranks in the 18th percentile, indicating a relatively expensive market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is -0.91% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.049% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates that the city is experiencing a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze. The labor demand composite score of 3.03 is below average, ranking in the 22nd percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 3.70%, ranking in the 49th percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also means that there may be some upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is +1.49%, ranking in the 20th percentile, indicating below-average wage growth. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in other cities. Worker purchasing power is also increasing, but at a slower rate than in other metros.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Milwaukee-Waukesha, with a PSF of $227/sqft and an earnings ratio of 6.29, ranks in the 18th percentile, indicating a relatively expensive market. This means that the city is less competitive in terms of talent attraction, as workers may require wage premiums to afford the cost of living. The city's affordability score is low, making it challenging to attract talent without offering higher salaries.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Milwaukee-Waukesha is growing at a rate of -0.58% year-over-year, ranking in the 49th percentile, indicating a near-median level of labor force growth. However, the negative growth rate suggests that the labor force is actually contracting, which may pose a structural headwind for hiring. This contraction may limit the city's ability to support business growth.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Milwaukee-Waukesha is increasing by +13.09% year-over-year, ranking in the 70th percentile, indicating an above-average expansion of housing supply. This suggests that developer confidence is strong, and future housing supply is likely to increase, which may improve affordability and support workforce growth.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 32 days, with a year-over-year increase of +10.3%, ranking in the 66th percentile. This indicates a slower market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. The rising days on market suggests a healthy normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand erosion.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Milwaukee-Waukesha ranks in the 16th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, the city may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers a relatively slow-paced labor market with moderate wage growth, but its high cost of living and contracting labor force pose significant challenges for businesses. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker is the city's limited labor force growth, which may hinder business expansion and require wage premiums to attract talent. Overall, the city's economic character is defined by its below-average labor demand and high cost of living, making it a less competitive location for businesses seeking to attract and retain top talent.

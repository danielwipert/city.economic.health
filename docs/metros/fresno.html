Economic Analysis

Fresno, with an overall grade of B and a composite score ranking it at the 53.4th percentile among 50 US metros, is characterized by its strong labor demand and wage growth, with a labor demand composite score of 5.40, ranking it in the top tier at the 82nd percentile, and a wage growth rate of +8.37% year-over-year, ranking it in the top tier at the 89th percentile. These two metrics define the city's current economic character, signaling a growing economy with increasing labor costs. The city's strong employment growth and labor force expansion also contribute to its economic profile.

Labor Demand

Fresno's employment growth rate of +1.86% year-over-year, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -1.602% from its own trend, signals genuine demand expansion, as the city is adding jobs and hours are running above trend. This combination indicates a strong labor market, with employers increasing their workforce and workers putting in more hours. The labor demand composite score of 5.40, ranking in the top tier at the 82nd percentile, further supports this conclusion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Fresno is 8.20%, ranking it in the bottom tier at the 0th percentile, indicating a loose labor market with significant slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in Fresno may find it easier to staff their operations, but may also face weaker local consumer demand due to the higher unemployment rate. The low unemployment percentile rank suggests that the city has a relatively high number of unemployed workers seeking jobs.

Wage Growth

Fresno's year-over-year wage growth rate of +8.37% is fast, ranking it in the top tier at the 89th percentile, which implies rising labor costs for employers and increasing worker purchasing power. This rapid wage growth may lead to higher costs for businesses operating in the city, but it also means that workers have more disposable income to spend on goods and services. The strong wage growth is a double-edged sword for businesses, offering both benefits and drawbacks.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 2.18, ranking near the median at the 54th percentile, Fresno is neither extremely affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. This means that businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent to the city, but may still face some competition for workers due to the relatively moderate cost of living. The near-median percentile rank suggests that Fresno's cost of living is close to the national average.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Fresno is growing at a rate of +1.87% year-over-year, ranking it in the top tier at the 87th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This growth in the labor force means that businesses in Fresno may have an easier time finding qualified workers to hire, as the pool of potential employees is increasing. The strong labor force growth is a positive sign for the city's economy.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Fresno has decreased by -22.67% year-over-year, ranking it in the bottom tier at the 15th percentile, signaling a tightening of the housing supply. This decline in building permits may lead to future affordability issues and make it more difficult for workers to find housing in the city, potentially affecting workforce accommodation and attraction. The sharp decline in permits is a concern for the city's future growth.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Fresno is 51 days, with a year-over-year increase of +10.9%, ranking it near the median at the 55th percentile. This means that homes in Fresno are taking slightly longer to sell than they were last year, making the market slightly more accessible for relocating workers. The rising days on market may indicate a normalization of the housing market, but it is still relatively competitive.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share of 0.51, ranking in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile, Fresno has a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This means that the city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep pool of specialized office workers, such as industrial or logistics companies, and may not be the best fit for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on a strong knowledge economy. The low percentile rank suggests that Fresno's office economy is not as developed as other cities.

Fresno offers businesses a growing economy with increasing labor costs, a loose labor market, and a relatively moderate cost of living. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering locating in Fresno is the potential for future affordability issues and housing supply constraints, due to the sharp decline in residential building permits. This may affect the city's ability to attract and accommodate workers, potentially limiting its long-term growth prospects.

Economic Analysis

Fresno has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 44.9th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its high labor demand composite score of 5.87, ranking it in the 76th percentile, and its high unemployment rate of 8.90%, which ranks it in the bottom tier. These two metrics suggest a complex labor market with both strong job growth and significant slack.

Labor Demand

Fresno's employment growth rate is 1.67% year-over-year, and its weekly hours deviation is -0.768% from its own 12-month baseline, indicating genuine demand expansion. The combination of these two metrics signals that the city is experiencing job growth with hours worked slightly below trend, suggesting that the labor market is expanding but not yet overheating. This is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire in the area.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Fresno is 8.90%, ranking it in the bottom tier. This indicates that the labor market has significant slack, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the high unemployment rate also suggests weaker local consumer demand, which could impact businesses that rely on local spending.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Fresno is 4.17% year-over-year, ranking it near the median in the 52nd percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so. At the same time, workers are seeing their purchasing power increase, which could have positive effects on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Fresno has a cost of living score ranking it in the 49th percentile, with a PSF of $262/sqft and average hourly earnings of $33.53/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.81. This near-median ranking suggests that the city is neither extremely affordable nor extremely expensive relative to its peers. The stable PSF growth rate of 0.0% year-over-year indicates that housing costs are not increasing significantly, making it easier for workers to afford housing without requiring large wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in Fresno is growing at a rate of -0.17% year-over-year, ranking it near the median in the 46th percentile. This slight contraction in the labor force supply suggests that hiring capacity may be limited in the future, making it more challenging for businesses to find workers.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Fresno is decreasing by 6.89% year-over-year, ranking it near the median in the 42nd percentile. This decline in housing permits suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for housing in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Fresno is 50 days, with a year-over-year increase of 4.2%. This suggests that the housing market is slowing down, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, the relatively fast pace of sales still indicates a competitive market.

Office Economy

Fresno's office economy is ranked in the 26th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers of 1.74. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies.

In conclusion, Fresno offers a complex labor market with both strong job growth and significant slack, making it an attractive location for businesses looking to hire workers. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in Fresno is the potential for decreased affordability and increased competition for housing due to the tightening housing supply, which could impact their ability to attract and retain workers.

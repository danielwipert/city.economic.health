Economic Analysis

The city of Fresno has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 46.1th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its high labor demand composite score, which ranks at the 76th percentile, and its low unemployment rate, which, despite being 8.10%, actually indicates a challenging hiring environment due to its ranking at the 0th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with strong job growth but significant competition for labor.

Labor Demand

Fresno's employment growth rate is 1.67% year-over-year, and its weekly hours deviation from trend is -0.768%, indicating a genuine demand expansion despite the slight decrease in hours worked. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs at a rate above the national average, with a labor demand composite score of 5.87. The negative hours deviation may suggest some efficiency in labor utilization.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Fresno is 8.10%, ranking at the 0th percentile, which indicates a very tight labor market with little slack. This tight market implies that businesses trying to hire in Fresno will face significant competition for workers, potentially driving up wages. The low unemployment rate suggests that workers have more bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Fresno is 4.17%, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are increasing, but not at an alarming rate. Workers in Fresno are seeing their purchasing power increase, which can be beneficial for local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Fresno has a cost of living score ranking at the 47th percentile, with a price per square foot of $261 and average hourly earnings of $33.53, resulting in a ratio of 7.78. The city's PSF is decreasing by 0.8% year-over-year, making it relatively more affordable compared to its peers. This affordability can be a talent attraction advantage, as workers may not require significant wage premiums to maintain their standard of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Fresno is growing at a rate of -0.20% year-over-year, indicating a slight contraction in the labor pool. This negative growth rate suggests that the supply of workers is not expanding, which could pose a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion in the city.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Fresno is -6.89%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This decrease in permits suggests that developer confidence may be waning, and the future housing supply might not keep pace with demand, potentially leading to affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Fresno is 52 days, with a year-over-year increase of 10.6%. This increase indicates a slowing market, which can be beneficial for workers relocating to the city as it becomes more accessible. However, in the context of a strong job market, this slowing could also signal a healthy normalization rather than demand erosion.

Office Economy

Fresno's professional and office worker share ranks at the 26th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. The city is less suited for businesses requiring deep knowledge-economy talent, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but might be more appropriate for industries with different workforce requirements.

The city of Fresno offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand and moderate wage growth, but it also presents significant challenges, including a very tight labor market and potential future affordability issues due to a tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker considering Fresno as a business location is the difficulty in hiring and retaining workers due to the competitive labor market, which could drive up labor costs and impact business operations.

Economic Analysis

Fresno has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 46.2th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its high labor demand, with a composite score in the 76th percentile, and its high unemployment rate, which stands at 8.10%. These two metrics suggest a complex labor market with both opportunities and challenges for businesses.

Labor Demand

Fresno's employment growth rate is 1.67% year-over-year, and its weekly hours are deviating from trend by -0.768%, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 5.87. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as the city is adding jobs and hours are running above trend. However, the negative deviation in weekly hours may indicate some inefficiencies in the labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Fresno is 8.10%, ranking it in the bottom tier with a 0th percentile score. This indicates a slack labor market with significant room for hiring. For a business trying to hire in Fresno, this means that there is a large pool of available workers, which could lead to easier staffing but also weaker local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

Fresno's year-over-year wage growth rate is 4.17%, ranking it near the median with a 52nd percentile score. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so. At the same time, workers in Fresno are experiencing an increase in purchasing power, which could have positive effects on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Fresno has a cost of living score ranking it in the 47th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $261/sqft to $33.53/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.78. The city's PSF is also falling by 0.8% year-over-year. This makes Fresno relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Fresno is growing at a rate of -0.20% year-over-year, ranking it near the median with a 53rd percentile score. This negative growth rate indicates that the labor force supply is contracting, which could lead to structural headwinds for hiring in the future.

Building Permits

Fresno's residential building permits are decreasing by 6.89% year-over-year, ranking it near the median with a 42nd percentile score. This decline in building permits suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Fresno is 52 days, with a year-over-year increase of 10.6%, ranking it in the 70th percentile. This indicates a slower market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold. For a worker relocating to Fresno, this could make the city's housing market more accessible, but it may also signal a weakening demand for housing.

Office Economy

Fresno's office economy is ranked in the 26th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers at 1.74. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy workforce, such as tech, finance, or consulting companies.

In conclusion, Fresno offers a unique combination of a high labor demand and a relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for businesses that can navigate its challenges. However, the city's high unemployment rate and tightening housing supply are significant risks that decision-makers should carefully consider when evaluating Fresno as a potential business location.

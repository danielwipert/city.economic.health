Economic Analysis

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 46.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the 28th percentile, and its tight unemployment market, with a rate of 3.50% ranking in the 80th percentile. The combination of these metrics suggests a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire and expand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is -0.24% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +0.467%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job growth, and the slight increase in hours worked may be due to a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 3.50%, which ranks in the 80th percentile, indicating a tight labor market with little slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent, leading to potential wage pressure and difficulties in finding qualified candidates.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is +0.74%, which is in the bottom tier, ranking 12th percentile. This stagnant wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also implies weak bargaining power for workers, which may impact their purchasing power and overall economic well-being.

Cost of Living

Denver-Aurora-Centennial has a cost of living ratio of $290/sqft to $42.65/hr, with a percentile rank of 53rd, indicating a near-median affordability level. The fact that PSF is falling by -3.3% year-over-year relative to wages is a positive sign, but the city's affordability is still not a major advantage, meaning that businesses may not be able to attract talent without offering wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is shrinking at a rate of -2.22% year-over-year, which is in the bottom tier, ranking 12th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply poses a significant structural headwind for hiring, making it challenging for businesses to find and recruit new talent.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is increasing by +30.79% year-over-year, which ranks in the top tier, 80th percentile. This expansion in housing supply is a positive sign, indicating that the city is likely to experience improving affordability and a more accommodating environment for its workforce in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 43 days, with a year-over-year increase of +13.2%, ranking in the top tier, 96th percentile. This slower market means that workers relocating to this city will have a more accessible and less competitive housing market, which can be an advantage for businesses looking to attract talent.

Office Economy

Denver-Aurora-Centennial has an office economy percentile rank of 56th, indicating a near-median depth of professional talent pool. This city is relatively well-suited for businesses that require a moderate level of specialized office skills, but it may not be the best fit for companies that demand a very deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance hubs.

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a tight labor market and expanding housing supply, which may lead to improving affordability and a more attractive environment for talent. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's low labor demand and shrinking labor force, which poses significant challenges for hiring and expansion, and may require businesses to offer competitive wages to attract and retain talent.

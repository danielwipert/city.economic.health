Economic Analysis

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 46.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the 28th percentile, and its high unemployment rate ranking, which is in the 80th percentile, indicating a tight labor market. Specifically, the employment growth rate of -0.24% and weekly hours deviation of +0.467% suggest a complex labor market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is -0.24%, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +0.467%. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 3.50 is in the 28th percentile, indicating a below-average labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 3.50%, which ranks in the 80th percentile, indicating a tight labor market with low unemployment. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the market has little slack, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight labor market also implies upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is +0.74%, which is in the 12th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also implies weak bargaining power for workers and limited growth in worker purchasing power. The slow wage growth may make it harder for businesses to attract and retain top talent.

Cost of Living

Denver-Aurora-Centennial has a cost of living score in the 53rd percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $290/sqft to $42.65/hr, or 6.80. The fact that the PSF is falling by -3.3% YoY is a key driver of the city's near-median affordability score. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which can be a talent attraction advantage, as businesses may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is shrinking at a rate of -2.22% year-over-year, which is in the 12th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply implies a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of available workers is decreasing. Businesses may need to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent in this shrinking labor market.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is increasing by +30.79% year-over-year, which is in the 80th percentile. This rapid expansion in housing supply signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation are likely to improve, as more housing units become available to meet demand. This is a positive sign for businesses looking to attract and retain talent in the area.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 43 days, with a year-over-year increase of +13.2%, which is in the 96th percentile. This means that the housing market is slowing down, with homes taking longer to sell. For workers relocating to this city, this slower market may make it easier to find a home, as there is less competition and more time to make a decision.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 2.80, which is in the 56th percentile. This indicates a moderately deep talent pool, suited for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors. However, the city may not be as well-suited for businesses that require a large pool of industrial or logistics workers.

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a tight labor market with low unemployment, but the single biggest risk or constraint is the shrinking labor force supply, which may limit hiring capacity and require businesses to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent. Overall, the city's economic character is complex, with both positive and negative factors that businesses should carefully consider when making location decisions.

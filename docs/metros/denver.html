Economic Analysis

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking at the 54.0th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its low unemployment rate of 3.60% and a cost of living ratio of 1.68. The city's economic character is defined by these two metrics, which signal a tight labor market and relatively affordable housing. With a percentile rank of 89th for unemployment and 70th for cost of living, Denver stands out for its unique balance of labor market tightness and housing affordability.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Denver is -0.10% year-over-year, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -0.025% from its own trend, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.16, which ranks near the median at the 43rd percentile. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, as both job growth and hours worked are below trend. This suggests that the city is not experiencing genuine demand expansion, but rather a slight pullback in labor market activity.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Denver is 3.60%, ranking at the 89th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This tight market implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is relatively small, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The low unemployment rate also suggests that workers have significant bargaining power, which could drive up labor costs for employers.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Denver is +4.74% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 57th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and that workers are experiencing an increase in purchasing power. However, the moderate pace of wage growth may not be sufficient to keep up with the cost of living in the city, potentially affecting worker affordability.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 1.68 and a percentile rank of 70th, Denver is relatively affordable compared to its peers. This affordability advantage means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to compensate for high housing costs. The relatively low cost of living also suggests that workers may have more disposable income, which could benefit local businesses and the overall economy.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Denver is shrinking at a rate of -1.50% year-over-year, ranking at the 11th percentile, indicating a significant contraction in labor force supply. This decline in labor force growth implies that businesses may face structural headwinds in hiring, as the pool of available workers is not only tight due to low unemployment but also shrinking in absolute terms. This could exacerbate labor market challenges and increase competition for workers.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Denver has decreased by -15.96% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 23rd percentile, signaling a tightening of housing supply. This decline in building permits suggests that the city's housing market may become less affordable in the future, potentially eroding the current advantage in cost of living and making it more challenging for workers to relocate to the area.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Denver is currently 38 days, with a year-over-year increase of +8.6%, ranking above average at the 67th percentile. This indicates a slower market, which could be beneficial for workers relocating to the city, as they may have more time to find suitable housing. However, the rising days on market could also signal a normalization of the housing market, potentially leading to more stable and predictable conditions for both buyers and sellers.

Office Economy

Denver's professional and office worker share is 2.93, ranking near the median at the 57th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This suggests that the city is suited for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, but may not offer the same level of specialization as cities with a more pronounced office economy. Denver's economy is likely less suited for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses, which may require a different type of workforce.

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a tight labor market, relatively affordable housing, and moderate wage growth, making it an attractive location for certain types of businesses. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force, which could exacerbate labor market challenges and increase competition for workers, potentially offsetting the city's current advantages.

Economic Analysis

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.6th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its low unemployment rate of 4.30% and a strong office economy. The city's economic character is defined by its tight labor market and slow wage growth, with the latter being a notable concern. Specifically, the unemployment rate and wage growth metrics, at 86th percentile and 19th percentile respectively, most define this city's current economic situation, with the unemployment rate indicating a tight market and the wage growth rate signaling stagnant labor costs.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is -0.44% year-over-year, while weekly hours are running 0.319% above trend, indicating a scenario where hours are increasing despite job losses, which suggests a survivor squeeze. This combination signals that the labor market is not expanding, and the increase in hours worked is likely due to remaining workers absorbing the load of eliminated roles. The labor demand composite score of 3.36 is below average, at the 24th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 4.30%, which is at the 86th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market. This means that there is little slack in the market, making it harder for businesses to hire new employees. The practical implication for a business trying to hire in this city is that they may face significant competition for talent and upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is +1.57%, which is at the 19th percentile, indicating slow wage growth. This means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing significantly. The slow wage growth rate may make it harder for workers to afford the cost of living in the city.

Cost of Living

Denver-Aurora-Centennial has a cost of living ratio of $293/sqft to $42.41/hr, with a PSF that is falling -3.3% year-over-year, resulting in a near-median affordability score at the 56th percentile. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage without requiring significant wage premiums. The falling PSF is a key driver of the city's affordability.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force growth rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is not available, but the city's labor force growth is ranked at the 50th percentile, indicating a stable labor pool. This means that the supply of workers is neither expanding nor contracting significantly, which has a neutral implication for hiring capacity.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is +12.87%, which is at the 64th percentile, indicating an expansion of housing supply. This signals that developer confidence is strong, and future housing supply is likely to increase, which could improve affordability and make it easier for workers to relocate to the city.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 39 days, with an 8.3% year-over-year increase, resulting in a top-tier percentile rank of 82nd. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively long time, indicating a slower and more buyer-friendly market. For a worker relocating to this city, this could make it easier to find a home, but it may also signal a softer demand for housing.

Office Economy

The office economy in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is above average, with a percentile rank of 70th, indicating a deep talent pool in professional and office sectors. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less suited for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area offers a business a tight labor market with slow wage growth, a relatively affordable cost of living, and a strong office economy. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker is the tight labor market, which may make it challenging to hire new employees and could drive up labor costs in the long run.

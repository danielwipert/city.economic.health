Economic Analysis

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking at the 47.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the 30th percentile, and its tight unemployment market, with a rate of 4.30% ranking in the 90th percentile. The combination of these metrics suggests a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire and expand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is -0.44% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +0.319%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, as hours are above trend during a period of job losses, indicating a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This metric is a concern for businesses looking to expand their workforce.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 4.30%, which ranks in the 90th percentile, indicating a very tight market. This means that there is little slack in the labor market, making it harder for businesses to hire new employees and potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. Businesses looking to hire in this market may need to offer competitive salaries and benefits to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is +1.57%, which ranks in the 18th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also suggests that workers may have weak bargaining power and limited purchasing power. Businesses may be able to keep labor costs under control, but this could also limit consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Denver-Aurora-Centennial has a cost of living score ranking in the 53rd percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $293/sqft to $42.41/hr, or 6.91. The fact that the PSF is falling by -3.3% year-over-year is a positive sign for affordability. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage for businesses, as they may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is shrinking at a rate of -2.12% year-over-year, which ranks in the 4th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply is a structural headwind for hiring, making it even more challenging for businesses to find the talent they need. This metric suggests that businesses may need to look outside the local market to attract workers.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is increasing by +12.87% year-over-year, which ranks in the 68th percentile. This expansion in housing supply is a positive sign for future affordability and workforce accommodation, as it suggests that the city is investing in its infrastructure to support growing demand. This could help to mitigate some of the labor market challenges.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Denver-Aurora-Centennial is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of +8.3%, which ranks in the 82nd percentile. This means that the market is slowing down, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this could also be a sign of demand erosion if the job market is not strong.

Office Economy

Denver-Aurora-Centennial has an office economy score ranking in the 66th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers indicating a deep talent pool. This city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, but may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. The presence of a strong office economy is a positive sign for businesses looking to tap into a skilled workforce.

The Denver-Aurora-Centennial metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a deep talent pool in the office economy, but the biggest risk or constraint is the tight labor market and shrinking labor force supply. Businesses looking to expand in this market will need to be competitive in their hiring practices and may need to look outside the local market to attract workers, making it essential to factor in these challenges when making a location decision.

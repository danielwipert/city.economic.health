Economic Analysis

Columbus, with an overall grade of B+ and a composite score ranking it at the 56.6th percentile among 50 US metros, is characterized by its low unemployment rate of 4.10% and a cost of living that is more affordable than most peers, with a ratio of $206/sqft to $34.14/hr, resulting in a score in the 77th percentile. The city's economic character is also defined by its labor force growth and building permits trends. Specifically, the labor force is growing at a rate of 1.69% YoY, while building permits have decreased by 41.29% YoY.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Columbus is 0.49% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by -0.934%, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.05, which ranks in the 47th percentile. This combination signals a moderate labor demand expansion, as the city is adding jobs, but the hours worked are slightly below trend. This suggests that the labor market is growing, but not at an accelerated pace.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Columbus is 4.10%, ranking in the 86th percentile, indicating a tight labor market with low unemployment. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The low unemployment rate also suggests that workers have more bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Columbus is 2.79%, which ranks in the 30th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This means that labor costs for employers are rising, but not at an accelerated pace, and workers have moderate purchasing power. The wage growth rate is not high enough to significantly impact the cost of living, but it is still a factor to consider for businesses.

Cost of Living

Columbus has a cost of living score in the 77th percentile, with a PSF of $206/sqft and an average hourly earnings of $34.14/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.03. This means that the city is more affordable than most peers, which can be a talent attraction advantage, as workers may not require wage premiums to maintain their standard of living. The affordability of the city can help businesses attract and retain talent.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force in Columbus is growing at a rate of 1.69% YoY, which ranks in the 77th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This means that businesses have a growing pool of potential workers to hire from, which can facilitate hiring and talent acquisition. The growing labor force can support business expansion and growth.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Columbus has decreased by 41.29% YoY, ranking in the 2nd percentile, indicating a tightening housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of housing is not keeping pace with demand. The decrease in building permits can lead to increased housing costs and reduced affordability.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Columbus is 36 days, with a year-over-year increase of 16.1%, ranking in the 96th percentile. This means that the housing market is slowing down, and homes are taking longer to sell, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. The slower market can give workers more time to find a home and can reduce the pressure on the housing market.

Office Economy

Columbus has an office economy score ranking in the 35th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This means that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. The city's office economy can support businesses that require a more general workforce.

In conclusion, Columbus offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living, a growing labor force, and a tight labor market, which can be both an advantage and a challenge. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses is the tightening housing supply, which can lead to increased housing costs and reduced affordability, making it essential for decision-makers to factor this into their location decisions.

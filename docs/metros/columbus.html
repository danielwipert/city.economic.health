Economic Analysis

Columbus, Ohio, has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 49.4th percentile among 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its below-average labor demand, with a composite score of 3.53, and its above-average unemployment rate of 3.60%, which ranks in the 79th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with a relatively tight labor market but struggling to create new job opportunities, with employment growth at +0.29% year-over-year.

Labor Demand

Columbus has an employment growth rate of +0.29% and weekly hours deviation of -1.175% from its own trend, signaling a labor demand contraction. This combination indicates that the city is not experiencing genuine demand expansion, as hours are below trend despite modest job growth. The labor demand composite score of 3.53, ranking in the 22nd percentile, further reinforces this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Columbus is 3.60%, ranking in the 79th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This suggests that there is less slack in the market, making it harder for businesses to hire new employees. As a result, businesses may face upward pressure on wages to attract and retain talent.

Wage Growth

Columbus has a year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.05%, ranking in the 30th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are experiencing some improvement in their purchasing power. However, the moderate pace of wage growth may not be sufficient to drive significant economic expansion.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 1.84 and a percentile rank of 65th, Columbus is relatively affordable compared to its peers. This means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset high living costs. The affordability of the city can be a talent attraction advantage for businesses locating in Columbus.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Columbus is growing at a rate of +0.35% year-over-year, ranking in the 28th percentile, indicating a slowly expanding workforce supply. While this growth is positive, its slow pace may limit the city's ability to support rapid hiring or expansion by businesses.

Building Permits

Columbus has seen an +8.67% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking in the 64th percentile, signaling an expansion in housing supply. This increase suggests that developer confidence is rising, and future housing affordability may improve, making it easier for workers to relocate to the city.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Columbus is 41 days, with a year-over-year increase of +10.8%, ranking in the 86th percentile. This indicates a slower market, which can be more accessible for workers relocating to the city, as they have more time to find suitable housing.

Office Economy

Columbus has a professional and office worker share of 3.30, ranking in the 77th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool in these sectors. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less suited for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented profiles.

In conclusion, Columbus offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a deep professional talent pool, but its slow labor force growth and below-average labor demand pose significant risks. The biggest constraint for decision-makers is the city's tight labor market, which may drive up wages and make it challenging to hire new employees, potentially limiting the growth and expansion of businesses locating in Columbus.

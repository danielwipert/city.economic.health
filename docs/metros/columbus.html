Economic Analysis

Columbus, with an overall grade of B- and a composite score ranking it at the 49.5th percentile among 50 US metros, is characterized by its low unemployment rate of 2.80% and a labor demand composite score of 4.36, which places it near the median. The city's economic character is also defined by its below-average labor force growth and bottom-tier building permits growth. Specifically, the labor demand and unemployment metrics suggest a complex labor market.

Labor Demand

Columbus has an employment growth rate of +0.22% and weekly hours deviation of -0.246% from its own trend, indicating a near-median labor demand composite score. This combination signals a modest expansion in jobs, but with hours worked below trend, suggesting some level of survivor squeeze where remaining workers may be absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This scenario implies that while there is some job growth, the labor market is not as strong as the employment growth rate alone might suggest.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Columbus is 2.80%, ranking it in the top tier at the 96th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market. This low unemployment rate means that businesses may face significant challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is small, leading to potential wage pressure. For a business trying to hire in Columbus, this tight market implies that attracting and retaining talent will be highly competitive.

Wage Growth

Columbus experiences a year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.26%, placing it near the median at the 42nd percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit not at a rapid pace. The implication is that businesses will need to adjust their compensation packages to keep pace with the market, but the pressure is not extreme.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $207/sqft to $34.44/hr, Columbus ranks at the 63rd percentile in terms of affordability, indicating it is slightly above average in cost. However, the key factor is the year-over-year direction, with PSF decreasing by -0.5%, which slightly improves affordability. This means Columbus has a moderate talent attraction advantage without needing significant wage premiums, as the cost of living, while not the lowest, is manageable relative to earnings.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Columbus is shrinking at a rate of -1.44% year-over-year, placing it below average at the 24th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply implies a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of potential workers is decreasing. Businesses looking to expand or establish operations in Columbus will need to consider strategies to attract workers from other areas or invest in workforce development.

Building Permits

Columbus has seen a significant decline in residential building permits, with a year-over-year change of -20.69%, ranking it in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. This sharp decrease signals a tightening in future housing supply, which could lead to affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. The implication for businesses is that relocating workers may face a competitive and potentially expensive housing market.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Columbus is 33 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -5.7%, placing it in the bottom tier at the 12th percentile. This fast-paced market means that homes are selling quickly, which can be challenging for relocating workers trying to find housing. The competitive housing market suggests that businesses may need to offer relocation assistance or flexible timing for starts to help new hires secure housing.

Office Economy

Columbus has a professional and office worker share that ranks below average at the 32nd percentile. This indicates that while there is some depth to the professional talent pool, it is not as pronounced as in other cities. Columbus is less suited for businesses requiring a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting, but may be more appropriate for industries with different workforce needs.

In conclusion, Columbus offers a business environment with a tight labor market and moderate wage growth, suggesting both opportunities and challenges. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker considering Columbus is the shrinking labor force and the tightening housing supply, which could exacerbate hiring difficulties and talent retention challenges.

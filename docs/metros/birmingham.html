Economic Analysis

Birmingham has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking it at the 58.5th percentile among 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a top-tier composite score of 82nd percentile, and its low unemployment rate of 2.80%, ranking it in the 94th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with a thriving job market and intense competition for labor.

Labor Demand

Birmingham's employment growth rate of +0.23% year-over-year, combined with a +0.705% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend, signals genuine demand expansion. This indicates that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, a sign of a strong and growing economy. The top-tier labor demand composite score of 82nd percentile reinforces this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Birmingham is 2.80%, ranking it in the 94th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as there are fewer available workers, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The low unemployment rate suggests that the city has a strong economy with limited labor slack.

Wage Growth

Birmingham's year-over-year wage growth rate is -1.63%, ranking it in the bottom tier. This stagnant wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also means that workers may have limited bargaining power and purchasing power. The slow wage growth may be a concern for businesses relying on a highly skilled and motivated workforce.

Cost of Living

Birmingham's cost of living, with a PSF of $160/sqft and average hourly earnings of $32.95/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.86, ranks it in the 15th percentile, indicating a relatively expensive city. However, considering the inversion, a high percentile rank would mean more affordability, Birmingham's low rank suggests that it may be challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Birmingham is growing at a rate of +0.62% year-over-year, ranking it in the 67th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire. The growing labor force suggests that the city has a relatively healthy hiring environment.

Building Permits

Birmingham has seen a +55.25% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking it in the 91st percentile. This significant increase suggests that housing supply is expanding, which may lead to improved affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce. The rising permits indicate developer confidence in the city's future growth.

Days on Market

Unfortunately, the current median days on market is not available for Birmingham. However, if it were to follow the national trend, a rising days on market would indicate a slower market, while a declining days on market would suggest a competitive market. This metric is crucial for businesses to understand the relocation experience of their workers.

Office Economy

Birmingham's professional and office worker share ranks it in the 54th percentile, indicating a near-median depth of talent pool. This suggests that the city is moderately suited for businesses requiring a knowledge-economy workforce, such as tech, finance, or consulting, but may not be the best fit for companies requiring a highly specialized or abundant professional workforce.

In conclusion, Birmingham offers a strong labor market with low unemployment and expanding workforce supply, making it an attractive location for businesses. However, the city's relatively expensive cost of living and stagnant wage growth may pose challenges for talent attraction and retention, and businesses should carefully consider these factors when making a location decision.

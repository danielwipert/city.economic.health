## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Birmingham’s labour market shows robust demand, with a 107E_ldc_composite score of 6.65—reflecting solid employment growth and above-trend weekly hours—placing it in the 88th percentile nationally. The unemployment rate stands at 2.2% (101A_unemployment), down 0.8 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), indicating a tightening market. Employment growth remains modest at 0.61% YoY (107E_employment_growth_yoy), while the civilian labour force expanded by 1.06% (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting supply is growing in line with demand. However, wage growth is subdued at 1.16% YoY (103B_earnings_yoy), ranking in the 14th percentile, which may signal limited upward pressure on incomes despite low unemployment.

Housing market dynamics reveal strong construction activity, with residential permits up 55.25% YoY (200B_permits_yoy)—a 92nd percentile performance—pointing to responsive supply expansion. Yet, affordability remains a constraint, with a cost of living composite score of 3.61 (104C_col), placing Birmingham in the 23rd percentile, indicating relatively high housing costs relative to earnings. Homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market at 56 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 3.7% YoY (204A_dom_yoy_pct), though this remains moderate compared to peer markets. Office occupancy remains weak, as reflected in a low office worker ratio score of 1.4 (105C_owr), suggesting limited return-to-office momentum.

**Outlook:** Birmingham’s economy is underpinned by strong labour demand and vigorous housing construction, but constrained by modest wage growth and affordability pressures. Near-term momentum appears stable, though long-term competitiveness will depend on broadening income growth and absorbing new supply without oversaturation.

Economic Analysis

Birmingham's labour market is characterised by robust demand, with a 107E_ldc_composite score of 6.65, indicating solid employment growth and above-trend weekly hours, placing it in the 88th percentile nationally. The unemployment rate has fallen to 2.2% (101A_unemployment), a decrease of 0.8 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), signifying a tightening market. While employment growth is modest at 0.61% YoY (107E_employment_growth_yoy), the civilian labour force has expanded by 1.06% (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting that supply is growing in line with demand. However, wage growth remains subdued at 1.16% YoY (103B_earnings_yoy), ranking in the 14th percentile, which may indicate limited upward pressure on incomes despite low unemployment.

The city's housing market is experiencing strong construction activity, with residential permits increasing by 55.25% YoY (200B_permits_yoy), a performance that ranks in the 92nd percentile. Nevertheless, affordability remains a concern, with a cost of living composite score of 3.61 (104C_col) placing Birmingham in the 23rd percentile, indicating relatively high housing costs relative to earnings. The median days on market has risen to 56 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 3.7% YoY (204A_dom_yoy_pct), although this remains moderate compared to peer markets. Office occupancy, however, remains weak, as reflected in a low office worker ratio score of 1.4 (105C_owr), suggesting limited momentum in the return to office.

**Outlook:** Birmingham's economy is underpinned by strong labour demand and vigorous housing construction. However, modest wage growth and affordability pressures pose challenges. Near-term momentum appears stable, but the city's long-term competitiveness will depend on its ability to broaden income growth and absorb new supply without oversaturation.

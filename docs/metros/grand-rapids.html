Economic Analysis

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking 41.4th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the 26th percentile, and its extremely low labor force growth rate of -4.96% YoY. The labor force growth metric, in particular, stands out as a significant concern, with a 0th percentile rank indicating a rapidly contracting labor pool.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is -0.43% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating -0.944% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job creation and a decrease in working hours. This suggests that businesses may face a relatively easy time hiring, but may also struggle with low consumer demand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 4.00%, which ranks in the 70th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in finding available workers, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. However, the tight labor market also suggests that workers may have more bargaining power, which could drive up labor costs.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is +3.86%, which ranks near the median at the 40th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so. At the same time, workers are seeing some improvement in their purchasing power, which could support local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood has a cost of living ratio of 5.08, which ranks in the bottom tier at the 9th percentile. This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, making it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The low affordability of the city may deter some workers from relocating, which could exacerbate hiring challenges.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is contracting at a rate of -4.96% YoY, which ranks in the bottom tier at the 0th percentile. This indicates that the labor pool is shrinking rapidly, creating a significant structural headwind for hiring. Businesses may struggle to find available workers, and may need to invest in training and development programs to build the skills they need.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is increasing at a rate of +55.17% YoY, which ranks in the top tier at the 89th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which could improve affordability and support workforce accommodation in the future. However, the current cost of living challenges may persist for some time, making it essential for businesses to factor in the potential need for wage premiums.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 44 days, with a year-over-year increase of +15.8%, which ranks in the top tier at the 88th percentile. This indicates a relatively slow market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, the rising days on market may also suggest a normalization of the housing market, which could be a positive sign for the local economy.

Office Economy

Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood has a professional and office worker share of 0.80, which ranks in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy workforce, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in industrial or logistics sectors.

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area offers businesses a relatively tight labor market with moderate wage growth, but also poses significant challenges, including a rapidly contracting labor force and high cost of living.

Economic Analysis

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking at the 51.0th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its low unemployment rate of 4.00% and a labor demand composite score of 4.12. The city's economic character is most defined by its tight labor market and stagnant labor force growth, with a labor force growth rate of -5.05% year-over-year. This combination of low unemployment and contracting labor force signals a challenging hiring environment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is -0.13% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.798% from the city's own 12-month baseline, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.12, which is near the median. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The near-median percentile rank of 52nd indicates a relatively stable labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 4.00%, ranking at the 94th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This tight market implies that businesses may face significant challenges in hiring, with upward pressure on wages. The low unemployment rate suggests that workers have strong bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is +3.79%, which is near the median, ranking at the 52nd percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are experiencing some improvement in purchasing power. The wage growth rate is neither fast nor stagnant, indicating a relatively stable labor cost environment.

Cost of Living

Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood has a cost of living percentile rank of 6th, indicating that it is relatively expensive, with a PSF of $222/sqft and an earnings ratio of $32.39/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.85. The 7.2% year-over-year increase in PSF further exacerbates the affordability issue. This low affordability score means that the city may struggle to attract talent without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is contracting at a rate of -5.05% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This decline in labor force supply implies a structural headwind for hiring, making it challenging for businesses to find and recruit workers. Days on Market

The current median days on market in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 37 days, with a year-over-year increase of 12.1%, ranking at the 94th percentile. This slow market indicates that homes are sitting longer before being sold, making it a more accessible market for relocating workers. The high percentile rank suggests a buyer-friendly market.

Office Economy

Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood has an office economy percentile rank of 12th, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses requiring a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area offers businesses a tight labor market with low unemployment, but also presents significant challenges in hiring and labor force growth. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the contracting labor force, which may limit the city's ability to accommodate growing businesses. Overall, the city's economic character is defined by its tight labor market and stagnant labor force growth, making it essential for businesses to carefully consider these factors when making location decisions.

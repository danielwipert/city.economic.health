Economic Analysis

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 49.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 4.00% and its high cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.85, driven by a $222/sqft price point that is rising 7.2% year-over-year. The combination of these metrics suggests a tight labor market with significant pressure on wages and affordability.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is -0.13% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.798% from the city's own 12-month baseline, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.12, which ranks near the median at the 49th percentile. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The negative employment growth rate indicates that the city is not adding jobs at a significant pace.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 4.00%, ranking at the 91st percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent and may need to offer higher wages to attract workers. The low unemployment rate also suggests that workers have strong bargaining power in this market.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is +3.79%, ranking near the median at the 54th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that employer labor costs are rising, but not at an extremely rapid pace. At the same time, workers in this city are seeing their purchasing power increase, which can have positive effects on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood has a cost of living percentile rank of 5th, indicating that it is one of the more expensive cities in the US, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.85. The city's $222/sqft price point is rising 7.2% year-over-year, relative to hourly earnings of $32.39. This means that the city is not well-suited for talent attraction without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is contracting at a rate of -5.05% year-over-year, ranking at the 0th percentile. This significant decline in labor force supply indicates that the city's hiring capacity is constrained, and businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers. The shrinking labor pool is a structural headwind for hiring in this city.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is -0.59%, ranking near the median at the 55th percentile. This slight decline in permits suggests that housing supply is not expanding rapidly, which may lead to future affordability and workforce accommodation challenges. The slow growth in building permits may exacerbate the city's existing affordability issues.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 37 days, with a year-over-year increase of 12.1%, ranking at the 94th percentile. This indicates a slower market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold. For workers relocating to this city, the slower market may make it easier to find a home, but it also suggests that the city's housing market is not as competitive as others.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is relatively low, ranking at the 12th percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool is not as deep in these areas, making it less suited for businesses that require a high concentration of tech, finance, or consulting talent. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in other sectors, such as manufacturing or logistics.

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area offers businesses a tight labor market with low unemployment, but also significant challenges in terms of affordability and labor force growth. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's high cost of living and shrinking labor pool, which may require businesses to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent.

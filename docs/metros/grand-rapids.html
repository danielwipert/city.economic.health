Economic Analysis

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking at the 42.9th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor force growth rate of -4.53% YoY and its high cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.82, ranking in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. The combination of these metrics suggests significant challenges for businesses looking to expand or relocate in this area.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is -0.10% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating -0.605% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, with hours below trend during a period of job losses, suggesting a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This metric ranks near the median at the 42nd percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 4.00%, ranking above average at the 78th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market with low unemployment. This means that businesses trying to hire in this area may face challenges in finding available workers, leading to potential wage pressure. With a tight market, employers may need to offer competitive salaries to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is +2.97%, ranking below average at the 38th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This implies that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but not as quickly as in other areas. As a result, businesses may face gradually increasing labor costs, but workers may not have as much bargaining power as they would in areas with faster wage growth.

Cost of Living

Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood has a cost of living ranking in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.82, indicating that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. The PSF is $222/sqft, which has increased by 7.2% YoY, making it even more challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums. This high cost of living may deter some businesses from relocating to the area, as they may need to offer higher salaries to compensate for the expensive living conditions.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is contracting at a rate of -4.53% YoY, indicating a shrinking labor pool and a structural headwind for hiring. This implies that businesses may face significant challenges in finding qualified workers, as the supply of labor is decreasing. As a result, companies may need to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent in this area.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is -0.59%, indicating a slight tightening of housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the demand for housing may outstrip the available supply. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of limited housing options on their ability to attract and retain workers.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 37 days, with a year-over-year increase of 12.1%, ranking in the top tier at the 94th percentile. This indicates a slower market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold, making it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, this may also signal a weakening demand for housing, which could have implications for the local economy.

Office Economy

The professional and office worker share in Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood is 0.84, ranking in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies, and worst suited for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on specialized office workers.

The Grand Rapids-Wyoming-Kentwood metro area offers businesses a relatively tight labor market with low unemployment, but also presents significant challenges, including a high cost of living and a shrinking labor pool. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the limited availability of qualified workers, which may require businesses to invest in training and development programs or offer competitive salaries to attract and retain talent.

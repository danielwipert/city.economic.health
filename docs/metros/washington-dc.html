Economic Analysis

The Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it at the 27.7th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand, with a composite score of 1.50, and its cost of living, which has a ratio of $309/sqft to $45.45/hr, resulting in a relatively affordable score of 70th percentile. The labor demand and cost of living metrics are critical in understanding the city's current economic state, with employment growth at -3.52% and weekly hours deviation at -1.463%.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is -3.52%, and the weekly hours deviation is -1.463%, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze, as both employment and hours are declining. This suggests that the city is experiencing a decrease in job opportunities.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is 4.40%, ranking at the 21st percentile, indicating a below-average labor market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it relatively easier to hire workers, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. The unemployment rate suggests that the labor market is not extremely tight, but still has some challenges.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is +2.12%, ranking at the 28th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This implies that employer labor costs are rising, but at a moderate pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing. The wage growth rate suggests that businesses may need to adjust their compensation packages to attract and retain talent.

Cost of Living

Washington-Arlington-Alexandria has a cost of living score ranking at the 70th percentile, with a PSF of $309/sqft and hourly earnings of $45.45, resulting in a ratio of 6.80. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, with a decreasing PSF YoY, making it an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums. The affordable cost of living is a major advantage for businesses looking to attract workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is declining at a rate of -3.05% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This implies that the city's hiring capacity may be limited, and businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers. The declining labor force growth rate is a concern for businesses looking to expand their workforce.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is decreasing at a rate of -3.97% year-over-year, indicating a tightening housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. The decline in building permits is a concern for businesses looking to attract and retain workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is 30 days, with a year-over-year increase of 25.0%, ranking at the 85th percentile. This means that the housing market is relatively slow, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. The increasing days on market suggests that the city's housing market is normalizing, but may still be competitive for relocating workers.

Office Economy

Washington-Arlington-Alexandria has a professional and office worker share ranking at the 30th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This means that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies, but may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. The city's office economy is not as strong as other metros, making it less attractive for certain types of businesses.

The Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate wage growth environment, but also presents challenges such as a contracting labor demand and a declining labor force. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this city is the limited hiring capacity due to the declining labor force growth rate, which may impact their ability to expand and attract talent. Overall, businesses must carefully weigh the advantages and disadvantages of locating in this city.

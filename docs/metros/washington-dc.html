Economic Analysis

The Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking at the 28.7th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score of 3.85 ranking at the 31st percentile, and its stagnant wage growth, with a year-over-year increase of only 1.90%, ranking at the 19th percentile. These metrics suggest a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate is not available, but the weekly hours deviation from the city's own trend is -1.151%, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, which may be a concern for businesses looking to hire and expand. The labor demand composite score of 3.85 ranks at the 31st percentile, further emphasizing the below-average labor market conditions.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is 3.80%, ranking at the 21st percentile, indicating a relatively high level of unemployment. This suggests that the market has some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but also potentially weakening local consumer demand. However, the relatively high unemployment rate may also lead to less wage pressure, as workers may be more willing to accept lower wages due to the availability of labor.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is 1.90%, ranking at the 19th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also implies weak bargaining power for workers and potentially limited purchasing power. As a result, businesses may not face significant pressure to increase wages, but workers may not have the disposable income to drive local economic growth.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 2.12, ranking at the 56th percentile, Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is near the median in terms of affordability. This means that the city is neither particularly expensive nor exceptionally affordable compared to its peers. As a result, businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent, but the city's affordability may not be a major draw for workers either.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Washington-Arlington-Alexandria is contracting at a rate of -1.82% year-over-year, ranking at the 6th percentile. This decline in labor force supply poses a significant challenge for businesses looking to hire, as it may lead to a shortage of skilled workers and increased competition for available talent. The shrinking labor pool may also limit the city's ability to support long-term economic growth.

Days on Market

Office Economy

With an office economy ranking at the 50th percentile, Washington-Arlington-Alexandria has a near-median share of professional and office workers. The Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metro area offers businesses a relatively low-cost environment with stagnant wage growth, but it also poses significant challenges, including a contracting labor force and potentially limited housing supply.

Economic Analysis

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 43.2th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 3.50% and stagnant wage growth of +0.62% YoY, indicating a tight labor market with limited upward pressure on wages. The labor demand composite score of 3.59, ranking in the 31st percentile, also suggests a sluggish job market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate is -0.47% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating +0.050% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand rather than genuine expansion. This combination indicates a lack of job creation, which may lead to a survivor squeeze where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles. The below-average labor demand composite score reinforces this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate is 3.50%, ranking in the 93rd percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with limited slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent, potentially driving up wages and making it harder to staff open positions. The low unemployment rate suggests that workers have strong bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The YoY wage growth rate is +0.62%, ranking in the 12th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that employer labor costs are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing significantly. The implication is a flat cost environment for businesses, but limited upward mobility for workers.

Cost of Living

The city has a cost of living percentile rank of 8th, with a PSF of $168/sqft and an earnings ratio of 5.02, indicating that it is relatively expensive. The PSF is rising +5.7% YoY, further decreasing affordability. This means that the city is at a disadvantage in attracting talent without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The YoY growth rate of the civilian labor force is -0.09%, ranking in the 36th percentile, indicating a contracting labor pool. This means that the supply of workers is shrinking, creating a structural headwind for hiring and potentially limiting business growth. The implication is that businesses may need to invest in training and development to build the workforce they need.

Building Permits

The YoY change in building permits is +32.81%, ranking in the 79th percentile, indicating an expansion in housing supply. This suggests that developer confidence is rising, and future housing supply is increasing, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The increase in building permits is a positive signal for the city's future economic growth.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 41 days, with a YoY increase of +2.5%, ranking in the 44th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced market, neither extremely competitive nor extremely accessible. For workers relocating to this city, the market is moderately competitive, but not overly challenging.

Office Economy

The city has an office economy percentile rank of 19th, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This means that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, it may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses that require a different type of workforce.

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area offers businesses a tight labor market with limited wage pressure, but also a relatively expensive cost of living and a shallow professional talent pool. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's stagnant wage growth and limited labor force expansion, which may limit business growth and require investments in workforce development.

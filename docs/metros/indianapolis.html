Economic Analysis

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 38.7th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier at 18th percentile, and its tight unemployment market, with a rate of 3.50% ranking it in the top tier at 93rd percentile. The combination of these metrics, including a labor demand score of 2.97 and an unemployment rate of 3.50%, suggests a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire and expand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is -0.95% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.372% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job growth and hours worked. This suggests that businesses may face a challenging environment in terms of finding and retaining talent.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is 3.50%, which ranks in the top tier at 93rd percentile. This indicates a tight labor market with little slack, making it challenging for businesses to hire new employees. As a result, businesses may face upward pressure on wages as they compete for a limited pool of qualified workers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is +2.01%, which ranks below average at 24th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. While this may be beneficial for workers' purchasing power, it also means that businesses may need to budget for increasing labor costs.

Cost of Living

Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood has a cost of living score in the bottom tier at 9th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $168/sqft to $33.41/hr, or 5.03. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, which could make it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The fact that PSF is rising 5.7% year-over-year further exacerbates this issue.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is growing at a rate of -0.09% year-over-year, which ranks below average at 39th percentile. This indicates that the labor force supply is contracting, which could create a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion. As a result, businesses may need to consider alternative strategies for finding and retaining talent.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is declining at a rate of -10.26% year-over-year, which ranks near the median at 43rd percentile. This suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of housing costs on their employees' quality of life.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is 41 days, with a year-over-year increase of 2.5%. This suggests that the housing market is slowing down, which could make it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, the relatively fast pace of sales still indicates a competitive market, which could pose challenges for businesses trying to attract talent from other areas.

Office Economy

Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood has a professional and office worker share of 1.42, which ranks in the bottom tier at 20th percentile. This indicates that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting industries. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in industrial or logistics sectors.

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area offers businesses a tight labor market with low unemployment, but also poses challenges in terms of labor demand, cost of living, and labor force growth. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the potential difficulty in attracting and retaining talent due to the city's relatively high cost of living and limited labor force growth.

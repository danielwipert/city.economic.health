Economic Analysis

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking at the 42.0th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier at the 18th percentile, and its tight unemployment market, with a rate of 2.50% ranking in the top tier at the 96th percentile. Specifically, the employment growth rate of -0.66% and weekly hours deviation of -0.174% contribute to this low labor demand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is -0.66%, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by -0.174%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not adding jobs at a significant rate and hours are not increasing, suggesting a lack of genuine demand expansion. This scenario may lead to a surplus of labor, making it easier for businesses to hire but potentially weakening consumer demand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is 2.50%, ranking in the top tier at the 96th percentile. This tight market implies that there is little slack in the labor market, making it harder for businesses to hire and potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. For a business trying to hire in this city, the tight labor market may require offering competitive wages and benefits to attract top talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is +3.99%, ranking near the median at the 45th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively, and worker purchasing power is increasing. However, this growth rate may not be sufficient to offset the high cost of living in the city.

Cost of Living

Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood has a cost of living ratio of 5.25, ranking in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile. This low percentile rank indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The high cost of living may be a significant constraint for businesses looking to relocate or expand in this city.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is growing at a rate of -0.36% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 21st percentile. This contraction in labor force supply implies that the city's workforce is shrinking, potentially creating a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion. Businesses may need to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent in this city.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is -10.26%, ranking near the median at the 43rd percentile. This decline in building permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for housing in the future. This could negatively impact the city's ability to attract and retain workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is 51 days, with a year-over-year increase of +15.9%, ranking above average at the 74th percentile. This slower market means that homes are taking longer to sell, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this trend may also indicate a normalization of the market after a period of rapid growth.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood is 1.48, ranking below average at the 23rd percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool is not as deep in professional and office sectors, making it less suited for tech, finance, or consulting businesses. However, the city may be more attractive to industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area offers a tight labor market with moderate wage growth, but its high cost of living and shrinking labor force supply are significant constraints. The single biggest risk for businesses considering this city is the potential difficulty in attracting and retaining talent due to the high cost of living, which may require offering wage premiums to remain competitive.

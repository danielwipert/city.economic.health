Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Indianapolis economy presents a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals evident in its key indicators. On the labour market front, the city boasts a low unemployment rate of 2.5%, which has decreased by 1.2 percentage points year-over-year. However, the civilian labour force has contracted by 0.36% over the same period, suggesting a potential tightening of the labour market. Average hourly earnings have grown at a respectable 5.64% year-over-year, indicating a degree of wage pressure.

In terms of business activity, the labour demand composite score stands at 4.08, while nonfarm payroll employment has grown at a modest 0.11% year-over-year. The office worker ratio composite score of 3.06 suggests a relatively healthy demand for office space. However, the weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline has fallen by 0.373%, which may indicate some softening in economic activity. On the housing front, residential building permits have declined by 10.26% year-over-year, while the median days on market has increased by 15.91% to 51 days. The cost of living composite score of 3.57 is relatively low, suggesting that Indianapolis remains an attractive location for businesses and individuals alike.

The city's economic strengths, including its low unemployment rate and growing average hourly earnings, are somewhat offset by weaknesses such as the decline in residential building permits and the softening in labour demand. Overall, Indianapolis appears to be experiencing a period of moderate growth, with some signs of slowing momentum. The city's relatively low cost of living and healthy office worker ratio suggest that it remains an attractive location for businesses and individuals, but policymakers will need to monitor the labour market and housing sector closely to ensure that the economy continues to grow and thrive.

Closing outlook: Indianapolis is likely to continue growing at a moderate pace, driven by its strong labour market and attractive cost of living. However, policymakers will need to address potential stress points, such as the decline in residential building permits and the softening in labour demand, to ensure that the city's economy remains competitive and resilient in the face of changing economic conditions.

Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 31.5th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score of 3.00 ranking it in the 20th percentile, and its high unemployment rate of 5.30%, which ranks it in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.93% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.632% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job growth and hours worked. This suggests that businesses may face a relatively easy time hiring, but may also struggle with a lack of skilled workers.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 5.30%, ranking it in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. This indicates a relatively slack labor market, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, this also suggests that local consumer demand may be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +2.32%, ranking it in the 30th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a relatively slow pace. This also means that worker purchasing power is increasing, but not at a rapid rate.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living score ranking it in the 45th percentile, with a PSF of $177/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.15/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.76. This near-median ranking suggests that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. This affordability can be a talent attraction advantage for businesses, as workers may not require significant wage premiums to relocate.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is growing at a rate of +0.92% year-over-year, ranking it in the 76th percentile. This expanding labor force supply suggests that businesses have a growing pool of potential workers to hire from, making it easier to find skilled talent.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has decreased by -12.03% year-over-year, ranking it in the 34th percentile. This decline in building permits suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which may lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for workforce accommodation in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is currently 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of +5.4%. This relatively slow market, ranking in the 68th percentile, suggests that workers relocating to the city may have a more accessible and less competitive housing market.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn ranks in the 48th percentile, indicating a relatively deep talent pool. This suggests that the city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, but may not be as suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and an expanding labor force, but also poses significant challenges due to its low labor demand and high unemployment rate. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for a lack of skilled workers and decreasing affordability in the future, making it essential to carefully consider these factors when evaluating the city as a potential location.

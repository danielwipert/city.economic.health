Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.6th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its below-average labor demand and unemployment rates, specifically -0.93% employment growth YoY and 4.40% unemployment rate. The city's economic character is defined by these metrics, indicating a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate. With a labor demand composite score of 3.00, ranking in the 20th percentile, the city faces significant headwinds in terms of job growth and hours worked.

Labor Demand

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an employment growth rate of -0.93% YoY and weekly hours deviation of -0.632% from its own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with fewer jobs being added and hours worked below trend. As a result, businesses may face a challenging environment in terms of finding skilled workers and managing workload.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 4.40%, ranking in the 22nd percentile, indicating a degree of slack in the labor market. While this may make it easier for businesses to hire, it also suggests weaker local consumer demand. However, the relatively high unemployment rate compared to other metros may provide some relief for businesses looking to staff their operations without excessive wage pressure.

Wage Growth

The YoY wage growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +2.32%, ranking in the 30th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other metros, which may help maintain profitability. Meanwhile, workers in the area will see some improvement in their purchasing power, although it may not be as strong as in other regions.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living score ranking in the 51st percentile, with a PSF of $183/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.15/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.93. This indicates that the city is near the median in terms of affordability, neither particularly expensive nor cheap compared to its peers. As a result, businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent, although the city's affordability advantage is not particularly strong.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is growing at a rate of -0.07% YoY, ranking in the 56th percentile, indicating a nearly stagnant labor force. This suggests that the supply of workers is not expanding, which may pose a structural headwind for hiring and business growth in the area. Businesses looking to expand their operations may need to consider strategies to attract workers from other regions or invest in training and development programs.

Building Permits

The YoY change in building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -7.41%, ranking in the 40th percentile, indicating a tightening of housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing units is not keeping pace with demand. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of rising housing costs on their employees' quality of life and their ability to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 38 days, with a YoY increase of 8.6%, ranking in the 90th percentile. This indicates a slower market, where homes are taking longer to sell, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this may also suggest a degree of softness in the local real estate market, which could have implications for businesses looking to attract talent or invest in the area.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 2.54, ranking in the 48th percentile, indicating a moderate depth of professional talent pool. This suggests that the city is suited for businesses that require a mix of industrial and professional skills, but may not be the best fit for businesses that require a deep pool of specialized office or tech talent. As a result, businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting sectors may need to consider alternative locations with stronger professional talent pools.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate wage growth environment, but its below-average labor demand and stagnant labor force growth pose significant risks. The single biggest constraint for businesses considering relocation or expansion in this area is the challenging labor market, which may require significant investment in talent attraction and retention strategies to overcome.

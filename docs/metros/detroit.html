Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 31.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand composite score of 3.18, ranking it in the 26th percentile, and its wage growth rate of +1.37%, which places it in the bottom tier at the 15th percentile. These metrics suggest a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.80% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -1.311% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job growth and hours worked. This suggests that businesses may face a more competitive environment for talent, but also may have fewer job opportunities to offer.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 5.30%, which ranks it in the 29th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, this also means that local consumer demand may be weaker due to the relatively higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +1.37%, which is relatively slow and places the city in the bottom tier at the 15th percentile. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also indicates that workers may have weaker bargaining power and lower purchasing power.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living score that ranks it in the 46th percentile, with a PSF of $173/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.25/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.64. This suggests that the city is near the median in terms of affordability, neither particularly expensive nor cheap compared to its peers. This means that businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent, but the city's affordability advantage is not particularly strong.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is growing at a rate of -0.19% year-over-year, indicating a slight contraction in the labor pool. This means that the supply of workers is not expanding, which could create a structural headwind for hiring and business expansion.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is declining at a rate of -10.94% year-over-year. This suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability challenges and make it more difficult for workers to relocate to the city.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 45 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.1%. This indicates a slightly slower market, which could make it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, the market is still relatively competitive, and workers may face some challenges in finding suitable housing.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn ranks in the 44th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This suggests that the city is suited for businesses that require a mix of professional and industrial skills, but may not be the best fit for businesses that require a highly specialized or knowledge-intensive workforce.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a labor market with some slack, but it also poses significant challenges due to its slow wage growth, declining labor force, and tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the potential difficulty in attracting and retaining talent due to the city's limited labor pool growth and affordability challenges.

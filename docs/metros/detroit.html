Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 32.8th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score reflecting a challenging economic environment. The city's labor demand and wage growth metrics, at 26th and 15th percentiles respectively, most define its current economic character, with employment growth at -0.80% and wage growth at +1.37% year-over-year. These numbers indicate a struggling job market with limited opportunities for expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.80%, and weekly hours are deviating -1.311% from the city's own 12-month baseline, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the city experiencing a decline in both jobs and working hours. As a result, businesses may face a challenging environment for growth and expansion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 5.30%, ranking at the 33rd percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This rate suggests that while it may not be extremely difficult to hire, the local consumer demand may be weaker than in other cities. For businesses trying to hire, this means they may have a slightly easier time finding candidates, but may also face a less robust local market.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +1.37%, ranking at the 15th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing significantly. As a result, businesses may enjoy a relatively flat cost environment, but workers may have limited bargaining power.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living score at the 50th percentile, with a PSF of $173/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.25, resulting in a ratio of 4.64. This near-median ranking indicates that the city is neither extremely affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. For talent attraction, this means that businesses may not have a significant advantage in terms of cost of living, and may need to consider wage premiums to attract top talent.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is growing at a rate of -0.19% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This decline in labor force supply means that businesses may face structural headwinds when trying to hire, as the available workforce is shrinking. As a result, companies may need to invest more in recruitment and training to attract and retain talent.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is declining at a rate of -10.94% year-over-year, indicating a tightening housing supply. This decline in permits signals that the city's future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the available housing stock is not expanding to meet demand. As a result, businesses may face challenges in attracting and retaining workers due to limited housing options.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is currently 45 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.1%. This rising days on market indicates a slower, more buyer-friendly market, which may make it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, this trend also suggests that the local real estate market may be normalizing, which could impact the city's attractiveness to businesses and workers.

Office Economy

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has an office economy percentile rank of 50th, indicating a moderate depth of professional talent pool. This ranking suggests that the city is suited for a variety of businesses, but may not be the best fit for companies requiring a highly specialized or knowledge-intensive workforce. As a result, businesses in industries such as tech, finance, or consulting may find the city's talent pool to be adequate, but not exceptional.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively flat cost environment and a moderate talent pool, but its struggling job market and limited labor force growth pose significant risks. The single biggest constraint for decision-makers is the city's lack of genuine demand expansion, which may limit opportunities for growth and expansion. As a result, businesses should carefully consider these factors when evaluating Detroit-Warren-Dearborn as a potential location.

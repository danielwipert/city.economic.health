Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 30.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand and unemployment metrics, with a labor demand composite score of 3.70 ranking below average at the 33rd percentile, and an unemployment rate of 5.60% ranking in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. These numbers indicate a challenging labor market with limited job growth and high unemployment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.43% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -1.239% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not experiencing genuine demand expansion. Instead, the labor market is characterized by payrolls contracting and hours below trend.

Unemployment

The actual unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 5.60%, which is relatively high and indicates a labor market with slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city may find it easier to staff, but they will also face weaker local consumer demand. The high unemployment rate suggests that the city has a surplus of workers, making it a buyer's market for employers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +2.04%, which is below average and indicates moderate wage growth. This means that employer labor costs are rising, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but not rapidly. The moderate wage growth suggests that businesses may face some upward pressure on labor costs, but it is not extreme.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living score ranking near the median at the 44th percentile, with a PSF of $177/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.18/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.76. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which is a talent attraction advantage. Businesses can attract workers without having to offer significant wage premiums to offset high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is contracting at a rate of -0.59% year-over-year, indicating that the labor pool is shrinking. This contraction implies a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the workers they need. The declining labor force growth rate suggests that the city's workforce is not expanding, which may limit business growth.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -12.03%, indicating that housing supply is tightening. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the limited supply of new housing may drive up prices and rents. The decline in building permits suggests that the city's housing market may become less accessible to workers and businesses.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of +5.4%. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively long time, indicating a slower and more buyer-friendly market. For workers relocating to this city, the slower market may make it easier to find a home, but it also suggests that the city's housing market is not as competitive as others.

Office Economy

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has an office economy score ranking near the median at the 54th percentile, indicating a moderate depth of professional talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. However, it may not be the best fit for tech, finance, or consulting businesses that require a highly skilled and specialized workforce.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a surplus of workers, but it also poses significant challenges, including a contracting labor market, high unemployment, and limited labor force growth. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the limited availability of skilled workers and the potential for future affordability and workforce accommodation issues due to the tightening housing supply.

Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 27th out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand and unemployment metrics, with a labor demand composite score of 3.18 ranking below average at the 24th percentile, and an unemployment rate of 5.60% ranking in the bottom tier at the 11th percentile. These numbers indicate a challenging labor market with limited job growth and high unemployment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.80% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -1.311% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job growth and hours worked. This suggests that businesses may face a relatively easy time hiring, but may also struggle to find skilled workers.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 5.60%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 11th percentile. This indicates a labor market with significant slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire workers. However, this also implies weaker local consumer demand, which could impact businesses that rely on local spending.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +1.37%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 13th percentile. This indicates stagnant wage growth, suggesting that labor costs for employers are relatively flat, but worker purchasing power is limited. This could make it challenging for businesses to attract and retain top talent.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living ratio of $177/sqft to $37.25/hr, with a percentile rank of 50th, indicating a near-median affordability score. The city's PSF is rising 0.6% year-over-year, which may impact affordability in the future. This means that the city is neither particularly affordable nor expensive relative to peers, and businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is growing at a rate of -0.59% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This suggests that the city's workforce supply is shrinking, which could create structural headwinds for hiring and business growth.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is declining at a rate of -10.94% year-over-year. This indicates a tightening housing supply, which could lead to decreased affordability and make it more challenging for workers to relocate to the city.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.4%. This suggests a slowing market, which could make it more accessible for workers to relocate to the city. However, this also indicates a potential decrease in demand for housing.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 2.46, ranking near the median at the 44th percentile. This indicates a moderate depth of professional talent, making the city suitable for businesses that require a mix of office and industrial skills. However, it may not be the best fit for businesses that require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively low-cost environment with a large pool of potential workers, but its slow labor market growth and high unemployment rate pose significant risks. The single biggest constraint for businesses in this city is the limited labor market growth, which could make it challenging to find skilled workers and achieve long-term success.

Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.6th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score largely defined by its below-average labor demand and moderate cost of living. The labor demand composite score of 3.00, combining a -0.93% employment growth rate and a -0.632% weekly hours deviation from trend, suggests a contraction in job market activity. The cost of living, with a PSF of $183/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.15, results in a ratio of 4.93, placing it near the median in terms of affordability.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of -0.93% and weekly hours deviation of -0.632% from the city's own trend indicate a contraction in labor demand, signaling that the job market is not expanding. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze. The below-average labor demand composite score of 3.00 reinforces this assessment, ranking in the 20th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.40% is slightly below the national average, but still indicates some slack in the labor market, ranking in the 22nd percentile. This suggests that while it may not be extremely difficult to hire, there is still some availability of labor. However, the relatively high unemployment rate compared to other metros may lead to weaker local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +2.32% is moderate, indicating that labor costs are rising, but not extremely rapidly. This rate, ranking in the 30th percentile, suggests that worker purchasing power is increasing, but at a slower pace than in many other metros. Employers can expect to see some upward pressure on wages, but it is not excessively high.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank of 51st, the city is near the median in terms of affordability, making it neither extremely attractive nor deterrent for talent. The PSF of $183/sqft, combined with average hourly earnings of $37.15, results in a ratio of 4.93, indicating that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. This affordability can be a talent attraction advantage without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The year-over-year growth rate of the civilian labor force is -0.07%, indicating a very slight contraction in the labor pool. This near-median growth rate, ranking in the 55th percentile, suggests that the workforce supply is not expanding rapidly, which may pose a structural headwind for hiring in the future.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -7.41%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. Although this change ranks in the 40th percentile, which is near the median, it still signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk due to a potential supply squeeze.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 38 days, with a year-over-year increase of 8.6%, placing it in the top tier with a 90th percentile rank. This suggests a slower market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold, making it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors is 2.54, ranking in the 48th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require an extremely specialized or large professional workforce, such as industrial or logistics companies, but may not be ideal for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely heavily on a deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate wage growth environment, which can be attractive for certain types of businesses. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker is the below-average labor demand and slight contraction in the labor force, which may pose challenges for hiring and workforce expansion in the future.

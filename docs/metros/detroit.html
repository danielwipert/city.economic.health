Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic indicators for Detroit present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the unemployment rate stands at 4.7%, which is relatively stable with no year-over-year change. However, the civilian labour force growth is modest at 0.59% year-over-year, suggesting a slow expansion of the workforce supply. Average hourly earnings have increased by 1.03% year-over-year, indicating some upward pressure on wages. The labour demand composite score is 3.97, which is a neutral signal, while the nonfarm payroll employment growth is virtually flat at 0.01% year-over-year.

In terms of housing and business activity, the residential building permits have declined by 10.94% year-over-year, which may indicate a slowdown in construction activity. The median days on market for homes has increased by 7.14% year-over-year to 45 days, suggesting a slightly softer housing market. The office worker ratio composite score is 2.28, which is a relatively low reading, indicating a potential mismatch between office space supply and demand. The cost of living composite score is not available, but the overall economic grade for Detroit is a C-, indicating some stress points in the economy.

A closer look at the data reveals some stress points, including the decline in residential building permits and the slow growth in labour force and nonfarm payroll employment. However, the stable unemployment rate and modest wage growth are positive signals. The weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is -0.34%, which may indicate some underlying weakness in labour demand. Overall, Detroit's economy appears to be experiencing a period of slow growth, with some challenges in the labour market and housing sector.

Closing outlook: Detroit's economic outlook is cautiously neutral, with some signs of stress in the labour market and housing sector. While the stable unemployment rate and modest wage growth are positive signals, the slow growth in labour force and nonfarm payroll employment, combined with the decline in residential building permits, suggest that the city's economy may face some challenges in the near term.

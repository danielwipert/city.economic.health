Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 32.1th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score reflecting a mix of challenges and opportunities. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand composite score of 3.70, which is near the median, and its unemployment rate of 5.60%, indicating a bottom-tier labor market. These metrics suggest a city with sluggish job growth and a relatively high level of unemployment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.43% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -1.239% from the city's own trend, signaling a contraction in labor demand. This combination indicates a lack of genuine demand expansion, with payrolls contracting and hours below trend. The labor demand composite score of 3.70, near the median, further reinforces this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 5.60%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 12th percentile. This suggests a labor market with significant slack, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the practical implication is that local consumer demand may be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +2.04%, which is below average, ranking at the 24th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate implies rising labor costs for employers, but at a slower pace, and relatively weak bargaining power for workers. As a result, worker purchasing power is not increasing rapidly.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living score near the median, with a PSF of $177/sqft and an earnings ratio of 4.76, based on $37.18/hr average hourly earnings. The city's cost of living percentile rank is 43, indicating a relatively affordable market. This means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is shrinking at a rate of -0.59% year-over-year, indicating a contracting labor pool. This implies a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of workers is not expanding. Businesses may face challenges in finding qualified candidates to fill open positions.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -12.03%, indicating a tightening housing supply. This signals a potential future affordability crisis and workforce accommodation challenges, as the supply of housing is not expanding to meet demand.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is currently 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of +5.4%. This suggests a slower market, ranking above average at the 68th percentile. For workers relocating to this city, the housing market is relatively accessible, with homes taking longer to sell.

Office Economy

The professional talent pool in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is near the median, ranking at the 50th percentile. This indicates a moderate depth of knowledge-economy talent, making the city suited for a range of businesses, but not exceptionally strong in tech, finance, or consulting. The city's office economy is not particularly specialized, with a mix of industrial and logistics-dominant sectors.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a slower housing market, making it accessible for relocating workers. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's sluggish labor demand and shrinking labor force, which may limit hiring capacity and create challenges for businesses looking to expand or establish operations in the area.

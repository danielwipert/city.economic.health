Economic Analysis

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its labor demand and unemployment metrics, which are both below average at 39th percentile and 23rd percentile, respectively. The labor demand composite score of 4.08, combining a -0.16% employment growth rate and a -0.947% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. The unemployment rate of 4.70% also suggests a degree of slack in the labor market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is -0.16% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.947% from the city's own 12-month trend, indicating a contraction in labor demand rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. This combination suggests that the city is not adding jobs at a pace that would indicate strong labor demand. The labor demand composite score of 4.08 is below average, ranking 39th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 4.70%, which is below average, ranking 23rd percentile, indicating some slack in the labor market. This suggests that businesses may find it easier to hire in this city compared to others with tighter labor markets. However, the practical implication is that local consumer demand may be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is +1.58%, which is in the bottom tier, ranking 17th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth implies a flat cost environment for employers but weak bargaining power for workers. As a result, businesses may not face significant labor cost pressures, but workers may not have strong purchasing power.

Cost of Living

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has a cost of living ratio of 2.78, ranking near the median at 44th percentile, indicating that the city is moderately affordable relative to its peers. This means that businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent to the area, as the cost of living is not excessively high. The city's affordability could be a talent attraction advantage.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is growing at a rate of +0.59% year-over-year, which is below average, ranking 38th percentile, indicating a slow expansion of the workforce supply. This slow growth implies that hiring capacity may be limited in the future, as the labor pool is not expanding rapidly. Businesses may face challenges in finding skilled workers as the labor force grows slowly.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is not available for Detroit-Warren-Dearborn, but the city ranks near the median at 50th percentile, suggesting a stable housing market. This stability implies that housing supply is not significantly expanding or contracting, which may have neutral implications for future affordability and workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is 45 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.1%, ranking near the median at 57th percentile, indicating a moderately slow market. This suggests that workers relocating to the city may find a relatively accessible housing market, as homes are not selling extremely quickly. The rising days on market may signal a healthy normalization of the housing market.

Office Economy

Detroit-Warren-Dearborn has an office economy percentile rank of 45th, indicating a moderately deep professional talent pool. The city is best suited for businesses that do not require a highly specialized or knowledge-economy dominant workforce, such as industrial or logistics companies. However, it may not be the ideal location for tech, finance, or consulting businesses that rely heavily on a deep talent pool of professional workers.

The Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a slow-growing labor force, which may limit hiring capacity in the future. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's below-average labor demand and stagnant wage growth, which may indicate a lack of economic momentum and limited opportunities for business expansion.

Economic Analysis

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 41.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 4.90% and its negative labor force growth rate of -2.60% YoY, indicating a tight labor market with a shrinking workforce. The combination of these metrics suggests that while there is demand for labor, the supply of workers is constrained.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is +0.08% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating +0.475% from the city's own 12-month baseline, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.89, which ranks near the median at 58th percentile. This combination signals a genuine demand expansion, albeit a modest one. The positive hours deviation indicates that existing workers are taking on more hours, which is a sign of a growing economy.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is 4.90%, ranking in the bottom tier at 10th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for workers, leading to upward pressure on wages. As a result, companies may need to offer higher salaries to attract and retain talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is +4.27%, ranking near the median at 56th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while worker purchasing power is increasing. As a result, businesses can expect to pay more for labor over time, but workers will have more disposable income to spend.

Cost of Living

Chicago-Naperville-Elgin has a cost of living ratio of $220/sqft to $37.11/hr, with a PSF increase of +1.4% YoY, ranking near the median at 43rd percentile in terms of affordability. This means that the city is neither particularly affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. However, the rising PSF suggests that affordability may be decreasing over time, which could make it harder to attract talent without offering wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is shrinking at a rate of -2.60% YoY, ranking in the bottom tier at 4th percentile, indicating a significant contraction in the workforce. This decline in labor force supply will make it even harder for businesses to find workers, exacerbating the existing tight labor market conditions. As a result, companies may need to consider alternative locations or strategies to access the talent they need.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is increasing by +30.83% YoY, ranking in the top tier at 82nd percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This growth in permits suggests that housing affordability may improve in the future, making it easier for workers to relocate to the city. As a result, businesses can expect a more accessible and affordable housing market for their employees.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is currently 33 days, with a +3.1% YoY increase, ranking below average at 32nd percentile. This means that the housing market is relatively competitive, but not extremely so, making it accessible for relocating workers to find homes. However, the rising days on market suggests that the market may be normalizing, giving buyers a bit more time to make decisions.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is 1.98, ranking below average at 34th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool for tech, finance, and consulting businesses. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics companies. However, businesses that rely on specialized office workers may find it challenging to attract and retain talent in this city.

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area offers businesses a tight labor market with moderate wage growth, but also a shrinking workforce and a relatively competitive housing market. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the limited labor force supply, which may require companies to offer higher wages or consider alternative locations to access the talent they need. Overall, businesses must carefully weigh the pros and cons of locating in this city, considering both the benefits of a growing economy and the challenges of a constrained labor market.

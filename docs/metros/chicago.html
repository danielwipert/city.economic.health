Economic Analysis

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 48.9th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its near-median labor demand and below-average unemployment rate, with the latter sitting at 5.40%. The combination of a -0.13% employment growth rate and -0.150% weekly hours deviation signals a labor market that is not expanding, with potential survivor squeeze effects.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is -0.13% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating -0.150% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a labor market that is not experiencing genuine demand expansion, but rather may be undergoing a contraction or survivor squeeze. The labor demand composite score of 4.12 ranks near the median at the 52nd percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is 5.40%, ranking below average at the 22nd percentile. This indicates a labor market with some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, the practical implication for a business trying to hire here is that it may not face extreme wage pressure, but still needs to compete for talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is +4.16%, ranking near the median at the 56th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that employer labor costs are rising, but not excessively so, while worker purchasing power is also increasing. This balance may be beneficial for businesses looking to attract and retain talent without breaking the bank.

Cost of Living

Chicago-Naperville-Elgin has a cost of living score ranking it at the 63rd percentile, with a PSF of $217/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.50/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.79. This indicates that the city is relatively more affordable compared to its peers, which can be a talent attraction advantage without requiring significant wage premiums. The fact that PSF is rising +0.9% year-over-year, however, may erode some of this affordability in the future.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is growing at a rate of +0.16% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 48th percentile. This slow expansion of the labor force supply implies that hiring capacity may be limited, and businesses may face challenges in finding the talent they need. However, the growth is still positive, indicating some potential for workforce expansion.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is increasing by +29.19% year-over-year, ranking above average at the 78th percentile. This significant expansion of housing supply signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may improve, making the city more attractive to relocating workers and businesses alike.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is 34 days, with a year-over-year increase of +3.0%. This indicates a relatively slow market, ranking below average at the 34th percentile. For a worker relocating to this city, the housing market may be more accessible, with less competition and pressure to act quickly.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin ranks below average at the 36th percentile, with a composite score of 2.14. This suggests that the city's professional talent pool is not as deep as in other metros, making it less suited for businesses relying heavily on tech, finance, or consulting talent. However, it may still be attractive for businesses in other sectors, such as logistics or manufacturing.

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate wage growth environment, but its below-average labor demand and unemployment rate may pose challenges for hiring and talent attraction. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential survivor squeeze effect in the labor market, which could lead to increased competition for talent and rising labor costs if not managed carefully.

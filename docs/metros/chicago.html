Economic Analysis

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 34.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its near-median labor demand and wage growth, but significantly dragged down by its bottom-tier labor force growth and building permits. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.22, combining a +0.28% employment growth rate and a -0.475% weekly hours deviation, suggests a mixed signal. The unemployment rate of 4.50% and labor force growth rate of -1.72% YoY are the two metrics that most define this city's current economic character.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.28% and weekly hours deviation of -0.475% indicate a modest expansion in jobs, but with hours worked below trend, suggesting some degree of survivor squeeze where remaining workers may be absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This combination signals that while there is some job growth, it is not accompanied by a significant increase in hours worked, which could indicate a less robust labor market. The labor demand composite score of 4.22 is near the median, reflecting this mixed picture.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.50% is below average, ranking at the 30th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market with some slack. For a business trying to hire in this market, the practical implication is that it may face moderate competition for talent, potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. However, the market is not extremely tight, suggesting that hiring is still feasible without excessive wage premiums.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +4.02% is near the median, indicating a moderate pace of wage increases. This rate suggests that employer labor costs are rising, but not extremely rapidly, while worker purchasing power is also increasing, albeit at a moderate pace. The implication for businesses is a need to balance wage growth with productivity and pricing strategies to maintain profitability.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 2.74 and a percentile rank of 46th, Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is near the median in terms of affordability relative to local wages. This means the city is neither particularly affordable nor expensive compared to its peers, suggesting that talent attraction may not be significantly hindered or helped by cost of living factors alone. Businesses may need to consider other factors, such as career opportunities and quality of life, to attract talent without relying on wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is contracting at a rate of -1.72% YoY, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This contraction implies a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of potential workers is decreasing, which could exacerbate competition for talent and drive up labor costs over time.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -27.41%, signaling a sharp decline in housing supply expansion. This decline suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as a tightening housing supply could lead to higher housing costs and reduced attractiveness for relocating workers.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 35 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -2.8%, indicating a relatively fast-moving market. For a worker relocating to this city, the fast pace of home sales could make it challenging to find and secure housing, potentially affecting the decision to relocate.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share composite score of 2.29, ranking at the 36th percentile, Chicago-Naperville-Elgin has a below-average depth of professional talent pool. This city is less suited for businesses requiring a deep pool of tech, finance, consulting, or HQ talent, but may be more appropriate for industries with different skill requirements.

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area offers a business a location with moderate labor demand and wage growth, but significant challenges in labor force growth and housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker is the shrinking labor pool and tightening housing market, which could lead to increased competition for talent and higher labor costs, making it essential to carefully consider these factors in any location decision.

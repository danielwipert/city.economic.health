Economic Analysis

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 49.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its near-median labor demand and below-average unemployment rate, with the latter sitting at 5.40%. The combination of a -0.13% employment growth rate and a -0.150% weekly hours deviation signals a labor market that is not experiencing genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is -0.13% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating -0.150% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a labor market that is contracting, with hours below trend, indicating a lack of genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 4.12 ranks near the median at the 49th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is 5.40%, ranking below average at the 25th percentile. This indicates a labor market with some slack, making it easier for businesses to hire, but also suggesting weaker local consumer demand. The relatively high unemployment rate may put downward pressure on wages, but it also means that businesses may have a larger pool of potential employees to draw from.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is +4.16%, ranking near the median at the 58th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a manageable pace. Workers in the area are seeing their purchasing power increase, which could have a positive impact on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Chicago-Naperville-Elgin has a cost of living score that ranks above average at the 69th percentile, with a PSF of $217/sqft and average hourly earnings of $37.50/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.79. However, the PSF is increasing by +0.9% year-over-year, which may erode affordability over time. Despite this, the city's relatively high cost of living percentile score means that it is more affordable than many of its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage without requiring wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is growing at a rate of +0.16% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 46th percentile. This slow growth rate indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, but at a modest pace. While this growth is positive, it may not be enough to keep up with demand, potentially leading to hiring challenges in the future.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is increasing by +29.19% year-over-year, ranking above average at the 76th percentile. This rapid growth in permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which could improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. However, it may take time for these new units to come online and have a significant impact on the market.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is currently 34 days, with a year-over-year increase of +3.0%. This relatively fast pace of sales suggests that the market is competitive, making it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. The below-average percentile rank of 34th percentile indicates that homes are selling quickly, which may put upward pressure on prices.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Chicago-Naperville-Elgin is below average, ranking at the 37th percentile. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these areas, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy workforce, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in industries that are less dependent on professional and office workers.

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metro area offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate pace of wage growth, but its below-average unemployment rate and slow labor force growth may pose challenges for hiring and talent attraction. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this area is the potential for a tightening labor market, which could drive up wages and make it harder to find qualified workers.

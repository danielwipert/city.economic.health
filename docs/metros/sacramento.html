Economic Analysis

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 43.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its above-average labor demand, with an 80th percentile ranking, and its below-average cost of living, ranking 20th percentile. The labor demand composite score of 5.13, driven by 0.94% employment growth and -0.482% weekly hours deviation, signals genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 0.94% and weekly hours deviation of -0.482% indicate that the city is experiencing genuine demand expansion, with more jobs being added and hours running slightly below trend. This combination suggests that the labor market is growing, but not at an unsustainable pace. The labor demand composite score of 5.13 further reinforces this notion, ranking the city in the 80th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 5.00% ranks the city in the 32nd percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it relatively easier to hire workers, but may also face less wage pressure. However, the unemployment rate is not extremely low, suggesting that there is still some competition for talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of 1.83% is below average, ranking the city in the 22nd percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not at an alarming rate. Workers in the city may see some increase in purchasing power, but it is not exceptionally strong.

Cost of Living

The city's cost of living, with a PSF of $344/sqft and average hourly earnings of $38.17/hr, resulting in a ratio of 9.01, ranks it in the 20th percentile. This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The -0.3% year-over-year change in PSF is a slight positive, but not enough to significantly impact affordability.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of 1.05% year-over-year, ranking the city in the 66th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire. The growing labor force suggests that the city has a relatively healthy hiring environment.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -15.51%, ranking the city in the 26th percentile. This decline suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. The decrease in building permits is a concern for businesses that rely on a steady supply of housing for their employees.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 38 days, with a 0.0% year-over-year change. This relatively fast market, ranking in the 24th percentile, means that homes are selling quickly, making it competitive for workers relocating to the city. However, the stable days on market suggests that the market is not becoming significantly more or less competitive over time.

Office Economy

The city's office economy, with a professional worker share ranking in the 33rd percentile, indicates a relatively shallow talent pool. This makes the city less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area offers businesses a growing labor force and relatively low unemployment, but also presents challenges such as a tightening housing supply and relatively expensive cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for future affordability issues and workforce accommodation challenges, which could impact the city's attractiveness to businesses and talent.

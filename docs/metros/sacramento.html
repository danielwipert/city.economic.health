Economic Analysis

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 40.4th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand, with a composite score ranking it in the 71st percentile, and its cost of living, which ranks in the 13th percentile, indicating a relatively expensive market. The labor demand and cost of living metrics are crucial in understanding the city's economic landscape, with employment growth at 0.69% and weekly hours deviation at 0.025%.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 0.69%, and the weekly hours deviation is 0.025%, signaling genuine demand expansion as hours are running above trend during job growth. This combination indicates that the city is experiencing a moderate increase in jobs, with workers putting in slightly more hours than usual. The labor demand composite score of 5.29 further supports this, ranking the city in the 71st percentile.

Unemployment

Although the unemployment rate is not available, the city's ranking in the 39th percentile suggests that the market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire. However, this also implies weaker local consumer demand. With an unemployment rate that is presumably higher than the national average, businesses may find it easier to staff, but may also face challenges in terms of local demand.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 1.22%, which is relatively stagnant, ranking the city in the 16th percentile. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also implies weak bargaining power for workers and limited growth in worker purchasing power. As a result, businesses may not face significant pressure to increase wages, but workers may not have the same level of disposable income.

Cost of Living

Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom has a cost of living ratio of $344/sqft to $38.23/hr, with a PSF that is falling by 0.3% year-over-year, resulting in a relatively expensive market, ranking in the 13th percentile. This means that the city is less competitive in terms of talent attraction, as the high cost of living may require wage premiums to attract and retain workers. The falling PSF is a positive sign, but the overall cost of living remains a challenge.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is growing at a rate of 1.05% year-over-year, indicating that the workforce supply is expanding. This growth rate, ranking the city in the 66th percentile, suggests that businesses may have a relatively easier time finding workers, as the labor pool is increasing. However, the growth rate is moderate, and businesses should still be mindful of the overall labor market conditions.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom has decreased by 21.43% year-over-year, indicating that housing supply is tightening. This decline, ranking the city in the 21st percentile, signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. This could lead to increased competition for housing and higher costs for workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 38 days, with no year-over-year change. This relatively fast market, ranking the city in the 26th percentile, means that workers relocating to the city may face a competitive housing market, making it challenging to find and secure housing. The lack of change in days on market suggests that the market is stable, but still relatively hot.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 1.89, ranking the city in the 28th percentile. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in terms of professional and office workers, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area offers businesses a moderate labor demand and expanding workforce supply, but also presents challenges in terms of cost of living and housing affordability. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses in this city is the relatively expensive cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain workers, and the tightening housing supply, which could lead to increased competition for housing and higher costs for workers.

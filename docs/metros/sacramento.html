Economic Analysis

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it at the 41.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand, with a top-tier percentile rank of 80th, and its cost of living, which ranks in the bottom tier at the 20th percentile with a PSF to wages ratio of 9.01. Specifically, the labor demand composite score of 5.13 and the cost of living ratio of $344/sqft to $38.17/hr drive the city's economic profile.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is +0.94% year-over-year, while weekly hours are -0.482% below the city's own trend. This combination signals a genuine demand expansion, as jobs are being added, but hours are slightly below trend, indicating a moderate pace of growth. The labor demand composite score of 5.13 reinforces this signal, suggesting a strong but not overheated labor market.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 5.20%, ranking in the 23rd percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city may find it relatively easier to staff, but may also face weaker local consumer demand due to the higher unemployment rate. The implication is that wage pressure may be moderate, as workers have some, but not excessive, bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is +1.83%, which is below average, ranking in the 23rd percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not rapidly, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but at a slow pace. The implication is that businesses may face some upward pressure on wages, but it is not extreme.

Cost of Living

Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom has a cost of living ratio of $344/sqft to $38.17/hr, with a year-over-year increase of +0.3% in PSF, ranking it in the bottom tier at the 20th percentile. This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The high cost of living may offset some of the benefits of the city's labor market, as businesses may need to compensate workers for the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is not available, but the city ranks near the median at the 50th percentile. This suggests that the workforce supply is neither expanding nor contracting rapidly, implying a stable hiring environment. However, the lack of growth may limit the city's ability to accommodate rapid business expansion.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is -15.51%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. The implication is that businesses may face challenges in attracting and retaining workers due to the limited housing options.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of +8.3%. This suggests a relatively slow market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. The rising days on market may indicate a healthy normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand erosion.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 2.06, ranking in the 31st percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies. However, it may not be the best fit for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on a specialized workforce.

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area offers businesses a labor market with genuine demand expansion and moderate wage growth, but also a relatively expensive cost of living and a tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the high cost of living, which may require businesses to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially offsetting some of the benefits of the city's labor market.

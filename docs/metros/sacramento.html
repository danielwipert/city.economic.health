Economic Analysis

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 44.2th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with an 80th percentile ranking, and its below-average unemployment rate of 5.20%, which ranks at the 21st percentile. The labor demand and unemployment metrics suggest a city with genuine job growth but also some challenges in finding the right talent.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is +0.94% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.482% from the city's own 12-month baseline, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 5.13, which ranks at the 80th percentile. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as jobs are being added and hours are running above trend. The strong labor demand indicates a healthy job market with opportunities for businesses to grow.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is 5.20%, ranking at the 21st percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it relatively easier to hire workers, but the local consumer demand may be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate. The practical implication for a business trying to hire here is that they may have a larger pool of potential candidates to choose from.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is +1.83%, ranking at the 23rd percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This means that employer labor costs are rising, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but not rapidly. The implication for businesses is that they should expect some upward pressure on labor costs, but it may not be as intense as in other cities.

Cost of Living

Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom has a cost of living score ranking at the 21st percentile, with a PSF of $344/sqft and an earnings ratio of $38.17/hr, resulting in a ratio of 9.01. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. The high cost of living means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent, as the city's affordability is a disadvantage in attracting workers without additional compensation.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is growing at a rate of +0.84% year-over-year, ranking at the 70th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire. The growing labor force means that businesses have a increasing pool of potential workers to choose from, making it easier to find the right talent.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom has decreased by -15.51% year-over-year, ranking at the 27th percentile. This indicates that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. The decline in building permits signals that the city's housing market may become less accessible to relocating workers, potentially affecting businesses' ability to attract talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom is currently 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of +8.3%, ranking at the 65th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively longer period, indicating a slower market. For workers relocating to this city, the slower market may make it easier to find a home, but it also suggests that the city's housing market is not as competitive as others.

Office Economy

Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom has an office economy score ranking at the 35th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers at 2.22 out of 5. This indicates that the city has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Sacramento-Roseville-Folsom metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand and a growing labor force, but it also presents challenges such as a high cost of living and a tightening housing market. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the potential for future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce, which may affect their ability to attract and retain talent.

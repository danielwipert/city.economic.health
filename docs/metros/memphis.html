## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Memphis’s labour market shows modest improvement amid mixed signals. The unemployment rate stands at 4.0% (101A_unemployment), down 0.2 percentage points year-on-year, reflecting a tightening market. Labour force growth remains positive at 1.36% YoY (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting sustained workforce expansion. Employment growth is minimal, with nonfarm payrolls rising just 0.06% YoY (107E_employment_growth_yoy), but broader labour demand—captured by the composite index incorporating employment and weekly hours—is healthier at 5.44 (107E_ldc_composite). Weekly hours are 1.058% above their 12-month baseline, indicating underlying strength in current workloads. Wage growth is robust, with average hourly earnings up 7.45% YoY (103B_earnings_yoy), outpacing inflation and supporting household purchasing power.

Housing market dynamics reveal increasing pressure. Residential building permits have surged 19.69% YoY (200B_permits_yoy), signaling strong builder confidence and supply-side response. However, inventory remains tight: homes are on the market for a median of 62 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 6.9% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), a rare increase in turnover time that may reflect waning buyer momentum. The cost of living is notably low, with a composite score of 0.36 (104C_col), placing Memphis among the most affordable metros. In contrast, the office worker ratio score of 0.48 (105C_owr) ranks in the bottom percentile, underscoring limited white-collar density and a structural reliance on logistics and industrial employment.

**Outlook:** Memphis maintains solid economic fundamentals driven by wage growth, affordability, and construction activity. Near-term risks include sluggish employment growth and a potential mismatch between housing demand and evolving market conditions. Over the longer term, diversification beyond its logistics-centric base will be critical to sustaining inclusive growth.

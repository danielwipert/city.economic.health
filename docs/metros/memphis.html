Economic Analysis

Memphis has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 66.5th percentile among 50 US metros, with its labor demand and cost of living being the two metrics that most define its current economic character, scoring 86th and 97th percentiles, respectively. The city's strong labor demand is driven by a combination of top-tier employment growth and hours worked above trend, while its cost of living is highly affordable, with a PSF to earnings ratio of $155/sqft to $32.13/hr, which is falling by 6.1% YoY. This unique combination makes Memphis an attractive location for businesses looking to expand.

Labor Demand

Memphis has a labor demand composite score of 6.32, driven by top-tier employment growth and a +1.318% deviation in weekly hours worked above its own 12-month trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth, indicating a strong and growing labor market. The city's labor market is characterized by a high level of job creation and increasing work hours, making it an ideal location for businesses looking to hire and expand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Memphis is 4.70%, which is below average, ranking in the 32nd percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire workers compared to other cities with tighter labor markets. However, the relatively low unemployment rate still suggests that businesses may face some competition for top talent, and may need to offer competitive wages and benefits to attract and retain workers.

Wage Growth

Wages in Memphis are growing at a rate of +4.99% YoY, which is above average, ranking in the 67th percentile. This moderate to fast wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing, which can have a positive impact on local consumer demand. As a result, businesses may need to factor in rising labor costs when making location decisions, but can also benefit from a growing and affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

Memphis has a highly affordable cost of living, with a PSF to earnings ratio of $155/sqft to $32.13/hr, which is falling by 6.1% YoY, ranking in the 97th percentile. This makes the city an attractive location for talent, as workers can enjoy a high standard of living without requiring significant wage premiums. The falling PSF to earnings ratio is a key driver of Memphis' high affordability score, making it an ideal location for businesses looking to attract and retain top talent without breaking the bank.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Memphis is growing at a rate of +0.98% YoY, which is above average, ranking in the 64th percentile. This indicates that the labor force is expanding, providing businesses with a growing pool of potential workers to hire from. As a result, businesses can benefit from a increasing supply of labor, making it easier to find and hire the talent they need to grow and expand.

Building Permits

The number of building permits issued in Memphis is growing at a rate of +39.08% YoY, which is top-tier, ranking in the 88th percentile. This suggests that housing supply is expanding, which can help to improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. As a result, businesses can benefit from a growing and affordable housing stock, making it easier for workers to relocate to the city and find affordable housing.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Memphis is 54 days, which is decreasing by -3.6% YoY, ranking in the 24th percentile. This indicates that the housing market is relatively fast-paced, making it competitive for workers relocating to the city. However, the decreasing days on market suggests that the market is still accessible, and workers can find homes relatively quickly, making it easier for businesses to attract and retain talent.

Office Economy

Memphis has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, ranking in the 16th percentile. This makes the city less suited for businesses that require a deep pool of specialized office workers, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses that require a more general workforce, such as industrial or logistics companies.

In conclusion, Memphis offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand, highly affordable cost of living, and growing labor force, making it an attractive location for expansion. However, the city's relatively shallow professional talent pool may be a constraint for businesses that require specialized office workers, and decision-makers should factor this into their location decisions. Overall, Memphis is a great option for businesses looking to tap into a growing and affordable labor market, but may require additional investment in talent development and attraction.

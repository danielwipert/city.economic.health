Economic Analysis

Memphis's labour market is showing signs of modest improvement, despite mixed signals. The unemployment rate has fallen to 4.0%, a 0.2 percentage point decrease year-on-year, indicating a tightening market. Labour force growth remains positive, with a 1.36% year-on-year increase, suggesting sustained workforce expansion. Although employment growth is minimal, with nonfarm payrolls rising just 0.06% year-on-year, broader labour demand is healthier, as reflected in a composite index score of 5.44. This is supported by a 1.058% increase in weekly hours over the past 12 months, indicating underlying strength in current workloads. Furthermore, wage growth is robust, with average hourly earnings up 7.45% year-on-year, outpacing inflation and supporting household purchasing power.

In contrast, the housing market is experiencing increasing pressure. Residential building permits have surged 19.69% year-on-year, signaling strong builder confidence and a supply-side response. However, inventory remains tight, with homes taking a median of 62 days to sell, a 6.9% increase from last year. This rare rise in turnover time may indicate waning buyer momentum. On the other hand, Memphis boasts a low cost of living, with a composite score of 0.36, making it one of the most affordable metros. In contrast, the city's office worker ratio score of 0.48 ranks in the bottom percentile, highlighting limited white-collar density and a structural reliance on logistics and industrial employment.

Looking ahead, Memphis's solid economic fundamentals, driven by wage growth, affordability, and construction activity, are expected to support the city's economy. However, near-term risks include sluggish employment growth and a potential mismatch between housing demand and evolving market conditions. In the longer term, diversifying beyond its logistics-centric base will be crucial to sustaining inclusive growth.

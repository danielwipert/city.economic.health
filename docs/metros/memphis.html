Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Memphis's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 4.0%, with a modest year-over-year decline of 0.2 percentage points. This suggests a relatively stable labour market, although the growth in the civilian labour force, at 1.36% year-over-year, is somewhat subdued. However, average hourly earnings have seen a robust increase of 7.45% year-over-year, indicating strong wage growth.

In terms of business activity, Memphis's labour demand composite score is 5.44, with nonfarm payroll employment growth of 0.06% year-over-year. While this growth rate is relatively slow, the city's weekly hours deviation from its 12-month baseline is a positive 1.058%, suggesting some underlying momentum. The office worker ratio composite score, at 0.48, is a relative weakness, indicating a lower proportion of office workers compared to other cities. On the housing front, residential building permits have seen a significant increase of 19.69% year-over-year, which could help address any potential supply shortages. The median days on market has increased by 6.9% year-over-year, standing at 62 days, which may indicate a slight slowdown in the housing market.

The cost of living in Memphis is a notable strength, with a composite score of 0.36, indicating a relatively low cost of living compared to other cities. This, combined with strong earnings growth, suggests that Memphis may be an attractive location for businesses and individuals looking for a low-cost, high-wage environment. However, the city's office worker ratio and slow employment growth rate are areas that warrant closer attention. Overall, Memphis's economic outlook is cautiously positive, with a mix of short-term challenges and longer-term strengths that could support sustained growth.

Closing outlook: Memphis's economy is expected to continue growing, driven by its low cost of living and strong wage growth. However, the city will need to address its slow employment growth rate and relatively low office worker ratio to achieve its full potential. With its robust earnings growth and attractive cost of living, Memphis is well-positioned to attract new businesses and talent, which could help drive future economic expansion.

Economic Analysis

Memphis has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 48.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier, and its high wage growth, with a year-over-year change of +6.18% ranking it in the 76th percentile. These metrics suggest a complex economic environment with both challenges and opportunities.

Labor Demand

Memphis has an employment growth rate of -0.11% and a weekly hours deviation of +1.898% from its own trend, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 2.65, which ranks in the bottom tier. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, with hours above trend during job losses indicating a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This scenario suggests that businesses may face challenges in finding skilled workers to fill new positions.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Memphis is 4.10%, ranking it in the 35th percentile, indicating a moderate level of slack in the labor market. This means that while it may not be extremely difficult to hire workers, there is still some competition for talent, and businesses may need to offer competitive wages to attract the best candidates. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also suggests that local consumer demand may be weaker than in other cities.

Wage Growth

Memphis has a year-over-year wage growth rate of +6.18%, ranking it in the 76th percentile, indicating fast wage growth. This rapid increase in wages suggests that employer labor costs are rising, but it also means that workers have stronger purchasing power, which can be beneficial for businesses that rely on local consumer spending. However, this fast wage growth may also lead to increased labor costs for businesses.

Cost of Living

Memphis has a cost of living score ranking it in the 98th percentile, with a PSF of $155/sqft and average hourly earnings of $32.45/hr, resulting in a ratio of 4.78. This high percentile rank indicates that Memphis is highly affordable relative to its peers, with a PSF that is falling by -6.1% year-over-year. This affordability advantage can be a significant talent attraction factor for businesses, as workers may not require wage premiums to maintain their standard of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Memphis is growing at a rate of -0.89% year-over-year, ranking it near the median. This contraction in labor force supply suggests that the hiring capacity of businesses may be limited, and they may face challenges in finding skilled workers to fill new positions. However, this trend may also lead to increased competition for workers among existing businesses.

Building Permits

Memphis has a year-over-year change in building permits of +39.70%, ranking it in the 84th percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This increase in permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which can lead to improved affordability and a more attractive environment for workers to relocate to the city. This trend is positive for businesses that rely on a growing and diverse workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Memphis is 58 days, with a year-over-year increase of +1.8%. This moderate days on market suggests that the housing market is relatively balanced, neither extremely competitive nor extremely accessible. However, the rising trend in days on market may indicate a healthy normalization of the market, making it easier for relocating workers to find housing.

Office Economy

Memphis has an office economy score ranking it in the 6th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers of 0.58. This low percentile rank indicates that the city has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in industries that require a stronger logistics or industrial presence.

In conclusion, Memphis offers businesses a unique combination of high affordability and fast wage growth, making it an attractive location for companies that can navigate its complex economic environment. However, the city's low labor demand and limited labor force growth pose significant risks and constraints for businesses, particularly those that require a deep talent pool or rapid hiring capacity. Decision-makers should carefully weigh these factors when considering Memphis as a potential location for their business.

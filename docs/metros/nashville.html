Economic Analysis

Nashville-Davidson--Murfreesboro--Franklin has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 67.3th percentile among 50 US metros, with its labor demand and unemployment rate being the two metrics that most define its current economic character, at 82nd and 88th percentiles, respectively. The city's strong labor demand, combined with low unemployment, signals a competitive job market with genuine demand expansion. With an employment growth rate of 0.28% and weekly hours 1.321% above trend, Nashville's economy is experiencing a period of sustained growth.

Labor Demand

Nashville's labor demand composite score is 6.01, indicating a top-tier performance, with employment growth of 0.28% and weekly hours 1.321% above trend, signaling genuine demand expansion. This combination suggests that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, indicating a strong and growing economy. The labor market is characterized by increasing demand, with no signs of contraction or survivor squeeze.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Nashville is 2.90%, ranking in the top tier at the 88th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This low unemployment rate means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is limited, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight labor market also puts upward pressure on wages, as workers have more bargaining power.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Nashville is 5.81% year-over-year, ranking above average at the 74th percentile, indicating moderate to fast wage growth. This rate of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing, which can have positive effects on local consumer demand. As wages continue to grow, businesses may need to adjust their pricing strategies to maintain profitability.

Cost of Living

Nashville's cost of living is relatively affordable, with a percentile rank of 65th, and a price-to-salary ratio of $262/sqft to $35.91/hr, or 7.30, which is above average. The fact that the price per square foot is falling by 1.1% year-over-year relative to wages makes the city more attractive for talent, as workers can afford a higher quality of life without requiring significant wage premiums. This affordability advantage can be a major draw for businesses looking to attract and retain top talent.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Nashville is shrinking, with a year-over-year growth rate of -0.40%, ranking below average at the 38th percentile. This contraction in the labor force supply means that businesses may face structural headwinds in hiring, as the pool of available workers is decreasing. As the labor force continues to shrink, businesses may need to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Nashville is falling sharply, with a year-over-year change of -31.30%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile. This decline in building permits suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which can lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for housing, making it more challenging for workers to relocate to the city. The lack of new housing supply can also put upward pressure on rents and home prices.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Nashville is 50 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.4%, ranking above average at the 60th percentile. This indicates a slowing market, where homes are taking longer to sell, making it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, the relatively fast pace of sales still suggests a competitive market, and workers may need to act quickly to secure housing.

Office Economy

Nashville's professional and office worker share is 3.74, ranking in the top tier at the 88th percentile, indicating a deep and talented pool of knowledge-economy workers. This makes the city an attractive location for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require specialized talent. However, the city may be less suited for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which require a different type of workforce.

In conclusion, Nashville offers businesses a strong and growing economy, with a talented and deep pool of knowledge-economy workers, making it an attractive location for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors. However, the city's tight labor market and shrinking labor force supply pose significant risks and constraints, and businesses should factor in the potential challenges of hiring and retaining talent in this competitive market.

Economic Analysis

The Nashville-Davidson--Murfreesboro--Franklin metro area has an overall grade of A- with a composite score ranking at the 61.1th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 3.10% and its strong labor force growth of +1.87% YoY. The combination of these metrics signals a tight labor market with expanding workforce supply.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate is +0.05% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating +0.024% from the city's own 12-month baseline, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.39, which ranks at the 66th percentile. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This indicates a strong labor market with increasing demand for workers.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate is 3.10%, ranking at the 92nd percentile, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent, leading to potential wage pressure. With such a low unemployment rate, companies may need to offer competitive salaries to attract and retain workers.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate is +1.57%, which ranks at the 19th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that employer labor costs are not rising rapidly, but it also implies weak bargaining power for workers and potentially limited purchasing power. As a result, businesses may not face significant labor cost increases, but workers may not have strong disposable income.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Nashville is below average, with a percentile rank of 35, and a PSF to earnings ratio of $260/sqft to $35.63/hr, resulting in a ratio of 7.30. The PSF is also falling by -1.1% YoY, making the city more affordable relative to peers. This affordability advantage can be a significant talent attraction factor, as workers may not require wage premiums to maintain their standard of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +1.87% YoY, ranking at the 89th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This growth in labor force supply is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire, as it suggests a increasing pool of potential workers. With a growing labor force, companies may find it easier to staff their operations.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -8.94%, ranking near the median at the 50th percentile. This decline in building permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability concerns and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. As a result, businesses should be aware of potential future housing supply constraints.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 53 days, with a year-over-year increase of +12.8%. This indicates a relatively slow market, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile. For workers relocating to this city, the slower market may make it easier to find a home, but it also suggests a less competitive housing market.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors is 3.57, ranking at the 89th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This city is well-suited for businesses that require specialized knowledge-economy workers, but may be less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-dominant workforces.

The Nashville-Davidson--Murfreesboro--Franklin metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a tight labor market, expanding workforce supply, and a relatively affordable cost of living. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the potential for future housing supply constraints due to declining building permits, which may impact workforce accommodation and affordability.

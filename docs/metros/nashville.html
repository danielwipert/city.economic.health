Economic Analysis

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Nashville’s labour market remains robust, underpinned by a 3.0% unemployment rate—unchanged year-on-year—and a civilian labour force expanding at 3.4% annually, a rate in the 98th percentile nationally, signalling strong workforce supply growth. Labour demand is solid, with a nonfarm employment growth of 1.0% year-on-year and a composite labour demand score in the 80th percentile, reflecting sustained hiring activity. Average hourly earnings rose 3.6% over the past year, though this growth lags behind top-tier markets, placing earnings momentum in the 40th percentile. Weekly hours worked are slightly above the city’s 12-month trend (+0.6%), suggesting stable near-term demand for labour.

Housing market dynamics point to moderating conditions. Residential building permits declined 8.9% year-on-year, near the national median, while homes are taking longer to sell—53 days on average, up 12.8% from a year ago—indicating softening demand or rising inventory. The cost of living relative to earnings, at a composite score of 3.07, is below average affordability (38th percentile), reflecting persistent price pressures. Office market signals are relatively strong (80th percentile), suggesting continued demand for commercial space, likely supported by professional and business services expansion.

**Outlook:** Nashville’s economic fundamentals remain resilient, driven by labour force expansion and steady employment growth. However, housing construction headwinds and elevated living costs pose risks to long-term inclusivity and workforce attraction. Absent a rebound in housing supply, affordability constraints could dampen the city’s competitive edge.

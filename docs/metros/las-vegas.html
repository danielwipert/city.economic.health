Economic Analysis

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area has earned an overall grade of A+ with a composite score of 68.2th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its exceptional labor demand, with a composite score in the 94th percentile, and its strong labor force growth, at 3.24% year-over-year. These metrics signal a city with a thriving job market and an expanding workforce.

Labor Demand

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area has seen employment growth of 1.70% year-over-year, combined with a 0.049% deviation in weekly hours above its own trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as both jobs and hours worked are increasing, indicating a strong and growing labor market. This suggests that businesses can expect to find a talented and available workforce.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas stands at 5.20%, which corresponds to a below-average percentile rank of 30th. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, this also means that local consumer demand may be somewhat weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The city has experienced year-over-year wage growth of 6.67%, placing it in the top tier with an 81st percentile rank. This fast wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers but also means that workers have increasing purchasing power, which can benefit local businesses through increased consumer spending. This is a significant factor for businesses to consider when evaluating labor costs and potential returns.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 2.31 and a percentile rank of 52nd, Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is near the median in terms of affordability relative to its peers. This means that the city is neither exceptionally affordable nor overly expensive, suggesting that businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent solely based on cost of living considerations. However, this also means that the city does not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to low costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas has grown by 3.24% year-over-year, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This positive growth rate suggests that businesses have a growing pool of potential employees to draw from, making it easier to find and hire new talent. This is a significant advantage for companies looking to expand or establish operations in the area.

Building Permits

The city has seen a year-over-year decrease in building permits of 9.83%, which corresponds to a near-median percentile rank of 45th. This decline in permits suggests that housing supply may be tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. This is a critical factor for businesses to consider, especially if they anticipate significant hiring or relocation needs.

Days on Market

Homes in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas currently sit on the market for a median of 52 days, with a year-over-year increase of 18.2%. This increase in days on market, combined with the absolute level, indicates a slower market that is more accessible to relocating workers. However, the relatively moderate days on market suggest that the housing market is not overly competitive, providing a balanced environment for both buyers and sellers.

Office Economy

The city's professional and office worker share is ranked in the top tier with an 85th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and headquarters operations. This makes Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas an attractive location for knowledge-economy businesses but less ideal for those in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors.

In conclusion, Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas offers businesses a thriving job market, an expanding workforce, and a relatively balanced cost of living. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers to factor in is the tightening housing supply, signaled by the decline in building permits, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

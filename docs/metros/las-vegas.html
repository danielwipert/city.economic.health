Economic Analysis

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking it at the 58.9th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a top-tier composite score of 94th percentile, and its low unemployment rate, which stands at 5.40% and ranks at the 10th percentile. The combination of these metrics suggests a highly competitive job market with genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is +2.04% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +0.024%, indicating a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing a period of job growth, with hours worked above the trend, suggesting that businesses are hiring and workers are in high demand. The labor demand composite score of 7.18 further reinforces this assessment, ranking the city at the 94th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is 5.40%, which is relatively high and ranks at the 10th percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it easier to hire workers, but the local consumer demand may be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate. However, the strong labor demand suggests that the city is still experiencing job growth, which could lead to a tightening of the labor market in the future.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is +5.63%, which is above average and ranks at the 70th percentile. This suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but worker purchasing power is also increasing. The moderate wage growth rate indicates a relatively healthy labor market, where workers have some bargaining power, but employers are not facing extreme cost pressures.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is near the median, with a percentile rank of 47th, and a price-to-salary ratio of $265/sqft to $33.16/hr, or 7.99. The fact that the PSF is falling by -2.2% year-over-year is a key driver of the city's affordability. This means that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage, as businesses may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for a high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is growing at a rate of +2.43% year-over-year, which is a top-tier growth rate ranking at the 94th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire workers. The growing labor force suggests that the city has a strong pipeline of potential employees, which could support future business growth.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas has decreased by -25.89% year-over-year, which is a significant decline and ranks at the 10th percentile. This suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and make it more difficult for workers to relocate to the city. The sharp decline in building permits is a concern for businesses that rely on a steady supply of housing for their employees.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is 51 days, which is increasing by +15.9% year-over-year, ranking at the 86th percentile. This suggests that the housing market is slowing down, making it more accessible for workers to relocate to the city. However, the relatively fast days on market indicates that the city is still experiencing a competitive housing market, which could be a challenge for relocating workers.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas is 3.66, which ranks at the 86th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool. This suggests that the city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of professional workers. However, the city may be less suited for businesses that require a large industrial or logistics workforce.

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, a relatively affordable cost of living, and a deep talent pool of professional workers. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses is the tightening housing supply, which could lead to future affordability issues and make it more difficult for workers to relocate to the city.

Economic Analysis

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 66.4th percentile among 50 US metros, with a strong labor demand and labor force growth being the two metrics that most define its current economic character, at 96th and 98th percentiles, respectively. The labor demand composite score of 6.65, driven by a 1.96% year-over-year employment growth rate, signals a genuine demand expansion. This growth, combined with a top-tier labor force growth rate, positions the city for continued economic expansion.

Labor Demand

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area has seen a 1.96% year-over-year employment growth rate, coupled with a -0.392% deviation in weekly hours from its own 12-month baseline, indicating a genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs and that the existing workforce is not being overburdened, suggesting a healthy labor market. The labor demand composite score of 6.65 further reinforces this assessment, placing the city in the top tier.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in the Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area stands at 5.80%, which is in the bottom tier, ranking at the 15th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, this also suggests that local consumer demand might be weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in the Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area is +6.16%, placing it in the top tier at the 81st percentile. This rate of wage growth is relatively fast, implying rising labor costs for employers but also increased purchasing power for workers. Businesses should factor in these rising labor costs when considering relocation or expansion in this market.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $265/sqft to $33.08/hr, resulting in a ratio of 8.01, and a percentile rank of 59th, the Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area is near the median in terms of affordability. The fact that PSF is decreasing by -1.9% year-over-year is a positive indicator for affordability. This level of affordability suggests that the city can attract talent without necessarily requiring significant wage premiums, although it does not stand out as particularly advantageous or disadvantageous in this regard.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in the Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area has grown by 2.95% year-over-year, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This positive growth rate suggests that the city has a structural advantage in terms of hiring capacity, as the pool of potential workers is increasing.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -9.83%, indicating a tightening in housing supply. This decrease in building permits suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation might be at risk, as the supply of new housing units is not keeping pace with demand.

Days on Market

The current median days on market stands at 52 days, with an 18.2% year-over-year increase, placing it in the 72nd percentile. This increase in days on market, combined with the absolute level, indicates a slower market that is more accessible for relocating workers, as homes are not selling as quickly as they might in hotter markets.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors, combined with its growth trend, places the Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area in the top tier at the 85th percentile. This deep talent pool makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less ideal for those in industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Las Vegas-Henderson-North Las Vegas metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion and a growing labor force, positioning it well for economic growth. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers to factor in is the tightening housing supply, signaled by the decrease in building permits, which could impact future affordability and workforce accommodation.

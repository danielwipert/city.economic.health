Economic Analysis

Oklahoma City has an overall grade of C, ranking 37th out of 50 US metros, with a composite score defined largely by its low labor demand and high wage growth. The city's labor demand composite score of 2.28, combining a -0.96% employment growth rate and a +0.677% weekly hours deviation, signals a contraction in labor demand. Additionally, the 6.23% year-over-year wage growth rate is a notable aspect of the city's economic character.

Labor Demand

Oklahoma City's employment growth rate is -0.96%, and weekly hours are deviating +0.677% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand rather than genuine demand expansion. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing a decline in job creation, which may lead to a surplus of labor. The labor demand composite score of 2.28 falls in the bottom tier, at the 10th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Oklahoma City is 3.60%, which is below average, ranking at the 29th percentile. This indicates a relatively tight labor market, making it harder for businesses to hire new employees. As a result, businesses may face upward pressure on wages to attract and retain talent.

Wage Growth

Oklahoma City is experiencing a year-over-year wage growth rate of 6.23%, which is relatively fast, ranking at the 80th percentile. This rapid wage growth may lead to increased labor costs for employers, but it also contributes to stronger worker purchasing power. Businesses should factor in the potential for rising labor costs when considering relocation to this city.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $173/sqft to $33.90/hr, Oklahoma City is relatively affordable, ranking at the 90th percentile. The fact that PSF is falling by -0.6% year-over-year relative to wages further supports this affordability. This makes the city an attractive location for talent, as businesses may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for a high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Oklahoma City is shrinking at a rate of -3.01% year-over-year, indicating a contraction in labor supply. This decline in the labor force may create a structural headwind for hiring, making it challenging for businesses to find the talent they need.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Oklahoma City has decreased by -24.28% year-over-year, signaling a tightening of housing supply. This decline in building permits may lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce.

Days on Market

Homes in Oklahoma City are currently sitting on the market for 49 days, with a year-over-year increase of 13.9%. This slower pace of home sales, ranking at the 94th percentile, may indicate a more accessible market for relocating workers, giving them more time to find a suitable home.

Office Economy

Oklahoma City has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, ranking at the 2nd percentile. This makes the city less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, it may be more suitable for industries with fewer specialized roles, such as logistics or manufacturing.

In conclusion, Oklahoma City offers a unique combination of relatively low labor demand and high wage growth, making it an attractive location for businesses that can adapt to these conditions. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's shrinking labor force, which may create challenges in finding and retaining talent.

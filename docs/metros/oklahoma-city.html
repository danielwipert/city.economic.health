Economic Analysis

Oklahoma City has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking at the 47.4th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier, and its high wage growth, at 6.23% year-over-year. These metrics suggest a complex environment where businesses may face challenges in finding and retaining talent.

Labor Demand

Oklahoma City's employment growth rate is -0.96% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from trend by +0.677%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, with hours above trend potentially indicating a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. The labor demand composite score of 2.28 is in the bottom tier, confirming this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Oklahoma City is 3.40%, ranking in the 64th percentile, indicating a relatively tight market. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city may face challenges in finding available talent, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract workers. The tight labor market implies upward pressure on wages.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Oklahoma City is 6.23% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier. This fast wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing purchasing power for workers. Businesses operating in this city should be prepared for higher labor costs, but may also benefit from a more affluent local consumer base.

Cost of Living

Oklahoma City has a cost of living score in the top tier, with a PSF of $173/sqft and average hourly earnings of $33.90/hr, resulting in a ratio of 5.10. The city's PSF is also falling by -0.6% year-over-year, making it more affordable relative to wages. This means that Oklahoma City has a talent attraction advantage, as workers can afford a higher standard of living without requiring wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Oklahoma City is shrinking at a rate of -1.21% year-over-year. This contraction in labor force supply implies a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find and recruit talent. The shrinking labor pool may limit the city's ability to support rapid business growth.

Building Permits

Residential building permits in Oklahoma City are falling by -9.74% year-over-year. This decline in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation. The decrease in building permits is a concern for businesses that rely on a growing and affordable housing stock to attract workers.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Oklahoma City is 51 days, with a year-over-year increase of 18.6%. This slower market, ranking in the top tier, means that workers relocating to this city may have a more accessible and less competitive housing market. The rising days on market may indicate a healthy normalization of the housing market, rather than a demand erosion.

Office Economy

Oklahoma City's professional and office worker share is relatively low, ranking in the 2nd percentile. This suggests that the city's talent pool is not particularly deep in specialized office roles, making it less suited for businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting sectors. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in industrial or logistics sectors.

Oklahoma City offers businesses a unique combination of high wage growth and a relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for talent. However, the city's low labor demand and shrinking labor force supply are significant concerns that may limit business growth and hiring capacity. Decision-makers should carefully weigh these factors when considering Oklahoma City as a business location.

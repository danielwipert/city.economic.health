Economic Analysis

Oklahoma City has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.7th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and affordable cost of living, scoring 86th and 94th percentiles respectively. The city's economic character is defined by these two metrics, with wage growth at 6.84% year-over-year and a cost of living ratio of $173/sqft to $34.04/hr, indicating a relatively affordable market. This combination suggests that Oklahoma City may offer a unique blend of labor cost management and worker purchasing power.

Labor Demand

Oklahoma City's employment growth rate is -0.87% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating 0.444% above trend, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 2.64, ranking in the bottom tier at 14th percentile. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The negative employment growth rate indicates that the city is experiencing a decline in job creation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Oklahoma City is 4.00%, ranking near the median at 51st percentile, indicating a relatively balanced labor market with neither significant tightness nor slack. For a business trying to hire in this city, the moderate unemployment rate suggests that finding talent may not be overly challenging, but may not be extremely easy either. This rate implies a stable labor market with manageable hiring conditions.

Wage Growth

Wage growth in Oklahoma City is 6.84% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier at 86th percentile, indicating fast wage growth. This rapid wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing worker purchasing power, which can be beneficial for local consumer demand. As a result, businesses may need to factor in higher labor costs when operating in this city.

Cost of Living

Oklahoma City has a cost of living ratio of $173/sqft to $34.04/hr, with the price per square foot falling 0.6% year-over-year, resulting in a top-tier percentile rank of 94th. This high percentile rank indicates that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers. The affordable cost of living in Oklahoma City can be a significant talent attraction advantage, as businesses may not need to offer wage premiums to compensate for high living costs.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Oklahoma City is growing at a rate of -0.08% year-over-year, ranking near the median at 42nd percentile, indicating a slightly contracting labor force supply. This slow growth rate implies that the labor pool is not expanding rapidly, which may pose a structural headwind for hiring in the long term. Businesses may need to consider strategies to attract and retain talent in a relatively stagnant labor market.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Oklahoma City is decreasing by 9.74% year-over-year, ranking near the median at 40th percentile, indicating a tightening housing supply. This decline in building permits signals that the city's housing supply may not be expanding to meet future demand, potentially leading to affordability issues and constraints on workforce accommodation. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of limited housing supply on their ability to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Oklahoma City is 51 days, with a year-over-year increase of 18.6%, ranking in the top tier at 90th percentile. This high percentile rank indicates a slower market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold. For a worker relocating to this city, the slower market may make it easier to find a home, as there is less competition and pressure to act quickly.

Office Economy

Oklahoma City's professional and office worker share is 0.70, ranking in the bottom tier at 6th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies, and less suited for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on specialized office workers. As a result, businesses in these sectors may need to consider alternative locations with more robust talent pools.

In conclusion, Oklahoma City offers a unique combination of strong wage growth and affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for businesses seeking to manage labor costs while maintaining worker purchasing power. However, the city's slow labor force growth and tightening housing supply pose significant risks and constraints that decision-makers should carefully consider when evaluating this location for their business operations.

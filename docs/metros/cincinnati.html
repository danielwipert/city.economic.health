Economic Analysis

The Cincinnati metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 54.9th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and low unemployment rate, at 3.60%. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.77, combining a +0.30% employment growth rate and a +0.050% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals genuine demand expansion. This, coupled with its top-tier unemployment rate, defines the city's current economic character.

Labor Demand

Cincinnati's employment growth rate of +0.30% and weekly hours deviation of +0.050% from its own trend indicate a genuine demand expansion, as both jobs and hours worked are increasing. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing a period of economic growth, with employers adding jobs and workers putting in more hours. The labor demand composite score of 4.77, ranking in the 61st percentile, further supports this interpretation.

Unemployment

The city's unemployment rate of 3.60% ranks in the top tier, at the 85th percentile, indicating a tight labor market with limited slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in Cincinnati may face challenges in finding available workers, potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. The low unemployment rate suggests that workers have more bargaining power, which could impact hiring strategies.

Wage Growth

Cincinnati's year-over-year wage growth rate of +0.55% is relatively stagnant, ranking in the bottom tier at the 8th percentile. This slow wage growth implies that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also means that workers may not have strong bargaining power or increasing purchasing power. The slow wage growth could be a concern for businesses relying on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 3.61, ranking in the 28th percentile, Cincinnati is relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that the city may not have a significant talent attraction advantage without offering wage premiums to compensate for the higher cost of living. Businesses may need to consider this when determining compensation packages to attract and retain talent.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Cincinnati is contracting at a rate of -0.79% year-over-year, ranking in the bottom tier at the 15th percentile. This decline in labor force supply could pose a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the workers they need. The shrinking labor pool may require businesses to adapt their hiring strategies or consider alternative locations.

Building Permits

The city has seen a significant increase in residential building permits, with a year-over-year change of +57.47%, ranking in the top tier at the 94th percentile. This surge in building permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which could improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The increase in permits is a positive signal for future workforce attraction and retention.

Days on Market

Homes in Cincinnati are currently sitting on the market for a median of 45 days, with a year-over-year increase of +21.6%, ranking in the top tier at the 92nd percentile. This slower market means that relocating workers may have a more accessible and less competitive housing market, which could be an advantage for businesses trying to attract talent. The rising days on market indicates a healthy normalization of the housing market.

Office Economy

Cincinnati has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy composite score ranking in the top tier at the 81st percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, or HQ sectors, which rely on specialized knowledge-economy workers. However, the city may be less suited for industries with more industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Cincinnati metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand and low unemployment, making it an attractive location for companies looking to expand or relocate. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's relatively expensive cost of living and stagnant wage growth, which may require businesses to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent.

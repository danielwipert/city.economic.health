## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Cincinnati’s labor market shows mixed signals, with a low unemployment rate of 3.6%—down 0.5 percentage points year-over-year (101A_unemployment, 101A_unemp_yoy_pp)—indicating a tight labor market in the short term. However, this strength is offset by a shrinking civilian labor force, which has declined 0.79% annually (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting waning workforce participation or outward migration. Employment growth has turned negative, with nonfarm payrolls down 0.52% year-over-year (107E_employment_growth_yoy), and labor demand remains weak at a composite score of 2.85 (107E_ldc_composite, 8th percentile). Wage growth is subdued at just 1.04% (103B_earnings_yoy), below inflation trends in recent years, while weekly hours worked are slightly above the 12-month baseline (0.72% deviation). Office occupancy remains low, reflected in a composite office worker ratio of 1.82 (105C_owr, 24th percentile), consistent with hybrid work persistence.

Housing market indicators point to cooling demand. Homes are on the market for a median of 45 days, up 21.6% year-over-year (204A_dom_level_days, 204A_dom_yoy_pct), a trend reflected in a high days-on-market composite score (9.16, 92nd percentile). Despite this softening demand, residential construction has surged, with building permits up 57.5% (200B_permits_yoy, 94th percentile), signaling developer confidence or pipeline momentum. The cost of living remains relatively moderate, though still elevated (104C_col: 3.62, 21st percentile), meaning housing costs are not fully outpacing incomes. The divergence between rising supply and slowing demand warrants monitoring for potential oversupply risks.

**Outlook:** Cincinnati faces structural headwinds in labor demand and workforce growth, despite a nominally strong unemployment rate. Near-term housing construction momentum contrasts with longer-term softening in sales velocity. Without a rebound in employment growth or wage gains, economic expansion will likely remain constrained.

Economic Analysis

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area has an overall grade of A- with a composite score ranking at the 59.6th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score in the top tier at the 84th percentile, and its high wage growth rate of 5.68% year-over-year. The combination of these metrics signals a city with genuine demand expansion and rising labor costs.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 1.83% year-over-year, while weekly hours are deviating -2.224% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as the city is adding jobs and hours are running above trend, indicating a strong labor market. The labor demand composite score of 5.36 further supports this, ranking in the top tier at the 84th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 5.10%, ranking near the median at the 44th percentile. This indicates a labor market with some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to cities with tighter labor markets. However, the unemployment rate is not low enough to indicate a significant surplus of workers, and businesses may still face some competition for talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 5.68%, ranking above average at the 77th percentile. This signals fast wage growth, which implies rising labor costs for employers but also increasing worker purchasing power. Businesses should factor in the potential for higher labor costs when considering this location.

Cost of Living

Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim has a cost of living percentile rank of 5, indicating that it is an expensive city relative to its peers. The PSF to earnings ratio is $666/sqft vs $41.96/hr, resulting in a ratio of 15.87. This means that the city is less competitive for talent attraction without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may deter some workers from relocating.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is growing at a rate of 0.08% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 43rd percentile. This indicates a slowly expanding labor force supply, which may provide some relief for businesses looking to hire. However, the slow growth rate also suggests that the labor market may not be able to accommodate rapid expansion.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is increasing by 20.24% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier at the 83rd percentile. This signals an expansion of housing supply, which may improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. However, the current cost of living remains a concern, and businesses should monitor the impact of new housing supply on affordability.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 45 days, with a year-over-year increase of 7.1%. This indicates a relatively competitive market for relocating workers, as homes are selling at a moderate pace. However, the rising days on market may signal a normalization of the market, which could make it easier for workers to find housing.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 3.46, ranking in the top tier at the 80th percentile. This indicates a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. The city is well-suited for businesses that require specialized knowledge-economy talent, but may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion and a deep talent pool of professional workers. However, the city's high cost of living and rising labor costs are significant risks that decision-makers should factor in, as they may impact a business's ability to attract and retain talent without offering wage premiums.

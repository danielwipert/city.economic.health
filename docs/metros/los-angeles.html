Economic Analysis

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area has an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 55.2th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score at the 74th percentile, and its high cost of living, ranking at the 4th percentile. The labor demand is driven by employment growth of 1.83% year-over-year, while the cost of living is characterized by a PSF of $667/sqft, which is decreasing by 3.3% year-over-year, compared to hourly earnings of $41.74.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 1.83% year-over-year, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -1.401% from its own trend. This signals a genuine demand expansion, as the city is adding jobs while hours worked are slightly below trend, indicating a healthy labor market. The labor demand composite score of 5.46 further supports this, ranking at the 74th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 5.20%, ranking at the 39th percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city may have an easier time finding candidates, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. The relatively high unemployment rate compared to other metros suggests that the labor market is not overly tight.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 5.19%, ranking at the 68th percentile, indicating fast wage growth. This implies that employer labor costs are rising, while worker purchasing power is increasing. Businesses should be prepared for a relatively high-cost labor environment, but one that also supports a strong local economy.

Cost of Living

Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim has a cost of living ranking at the 4th percentile, making it one of the more expensive cities relative to peers. The PSF of $667/sqft, decreasing by 3.3% year-over-year, compared to hourly earnings of $41.74, results in a ratio of 15.98. This means that the city may struggle to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the high cost of living may be a deterrent for some workers.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is growing at a rate of -0.12% year-over-year, ranking at the 36th percentile, indicating a contracting labor supply. This implies that businesses may face structural headwinds when trying to hire, as the labor pool is shrinking. The city's ability to attract and retain workers may be compromised by this trend.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is increasing by 112.64% year-over-year, ranking at the 96th percentile, indicating a rapid expansion of housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may improve, as the increased supply of housing may help to mitigate the high cost of living.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of 4.5%, ranking at the 58th percentile. This means that the market is relatively balanced, neither extremely competitive nor extremely accessible, for workers relocating to this city. The rising days on market may indicate a healthy normalization of the housing market.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers in Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim is 3.26, ranking at the 72nd percentile, indicating a deep talent pool. This city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but may be less suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses. The strong office economy suggests that businesses requiring specialized knowledge workers may find this city an attractive location.

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand and a deep professional talent pool, but also presents challenges such as a high cost of living and a contracting labor supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the high cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, and the potential for labor shortages due to the shrinking labor pool.

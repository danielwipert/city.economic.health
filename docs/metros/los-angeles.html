Economic Analysis

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 55.9th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with an employment growth rate of 1.83% and a labor demand composite score in the top tier at the 86th percentile, as well as its rapidly expanding housing supply, signaled by a 112.64% year-over-year increase in building permits. These metrics suggest a city with genuine job growth and a proactive approach to accommodating its workforce.

Labor Demand

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area is experiencing employment growth at a rate of 1.83% year-over-year, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -0.727% from its own 12-month baseline, indicating a genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs and, despite a slight decrease in hours worked, is still experiencing a strong labor market. The labor demand composite score of 6.14 further supports this, ranking in the top tier at the 86th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area stands at 4.80%, ranking below average at the 36th percentile. This indicates that the job market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, the relatively moderate unemployment rate also suggests that local consumer demand may not be as strong as in areas with lower unemployment rates.

Wage Growth

Year-over-year wage growth in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area is 4.84%, placing it above average at the 60th percentile. This rate of wage growth is relatively fast, implying rising labor costs for employers but also increased purchasing power for workers. Businesses should anticipate higher wage bills but also a workforce with enhanced consumer spending potential.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of $667/sqft to $41.74/hr, resulting in a ratio of 15.98, and a year-over-year decrease of 3.3% in PSF, the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area ranks in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile for affordability. This means the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, which could hinder talent attraction without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area is shrinking at a rate of -0.54% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 32nd percentile. This contraction in labor force supply poses a structural headwind for hiring, as businesses may face challenges in finding sufficient skilled workers to fill open positions.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is a significant 112.64% increase, ranking in the top tier at the 96th percentile. This substantial expansion in housing supply signals improving affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce in the future. It suggests that the city is proactively addressing potential housing shortages and supporting its growing population.

Days on Market

Homes in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area currently sit on the market for a median of 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of 4.5%. This places the city near the median at the 58th percentile for days on market. For workers relocating to this city, the market is relatively competitive but not overly challenging, indicating a balanced environment for buyers and sellers.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area is above average, ranking at the 60th percentile. This indicates a deep talent pool suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions, making it an attractive location for knowledge-economy businesses but less ideal for those requiring large industrial or logistics workforces.

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion and a rapidly expanding housing supply, which are significant advantages for attracting and accommodating a workforce. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the relatively high cost of living, which could necessitate wage premiums to attract top talent, potentially increasing labor costs for employers.

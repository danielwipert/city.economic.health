## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Los Angeles’s economy shows a mixed but broadly stable picture, with solid labor demand offset by persistent structural constraints. The metropolitan area’s labor market remains resilient, as evidenced by a 1.83% year-on-year increase in nonfarm employment (107E_employment_growth_yoy) and a strong labor demand composite score (107E_ldc_composite at 6.59, 86th percentile), indicating sustained hiring activity. Unemployment stands at 4.8%, down 0.4 percentage points from a year earlier (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), while average hourly earnings rose 4.84% (103B_earnings_yoy), outpacing inflation and supporting household purchasing power. The civilian labor force grew modestly at 0.69% (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting steady labor supply expansion without signs of strain.

However, structural challenges weigh on long-term growth potential. The cost of living remains exceptionally high, with a composite score of 5.59 (104C_col, 6th percentile), reflecting severe housing affordability pressures that constrain resident welfare and workforce accessibility. Residential construction has responded, with building permits up 20.24% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy, 82nd percentile), signaling developer efforts to alleviate supply shortages. Nevertheless, commercial real estate faces headwinds: the office worker ratio composite score is weak at 1.48 (105C_owr, 20th percentile), consistent with prolonged hybrid work adoption. Meanwhile, housing market liquidity has softened, with median days on market at 45 days, up 7.14% from last year (204A_dom_yoy_pct), though not yet indicative of a broad slowdown.

**Outlook:** Los Angeles is navigating a period of adjustment, where robust labor demand and rising construction activity are counterbalanced by high living costs and structural shifts in work patterns. Near-term stability appears intact, but long-term competitiveness will depend on sustained housing supply growth and adaptation in the commercial real estate sector.

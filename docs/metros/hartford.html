## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Hartford’s labour market shows strong momentum, underpinned by a low unemployment rate of 2.7%—down 1.3 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp)—and solid employment growth of 1.88% in nonfarm payrolls (107E_employment_growth_yoy). The civilian labour force expanded by 2.77% over the past year (102A_clf_yoy), indicating rising workforce supply, while weekly hours worked remain slightly above the city’s 12-month baseline (107E_wh_trend_deviation_pct: +0.445%). These signals suggest sustained labour demand, corroborated by a high labour demand composite score (107E_ldc_composite: 7.38, 94th percentile). However, wage growth has turned negative, with average hourly earnings declining 0.45% year-on-year (103B_earnings_yoy), a notable weakness amid broader labour market strength.

Housing market dynamics point to softening activity. Residential building permits fell 12.7% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), reflecting restrained construction. Existing home sales face headwinds, with median days on market rising to 39 days (204A_dom_level_days), a 30% increase from the prior year (204A_dom_yoy_pct), resulting in a high days-on-market composite score (204A_dom_composite: 10.0, 98th percentile). This prolonged market time suggests weakening buyer demand, possibly due to affordability pressures, as the cost of living composite score (104C_col: 3.28) sits above median levels. Office market conditions remain subdued, with a low office worker ratio composite score (105C_owr: 2.08, 28th percentile), indicating limited return-to-office momentum.

**Outlook:** Hartford’s economy is fundamentally resilient, supported by a tight labour market and steady job growth. However, stagnant wages and cooling housing activity pose near-term risks. Over the longer term, the city’s economic trajectory will depend on its ability to revitalise commercial real estate demand and align wage growth with living costs.

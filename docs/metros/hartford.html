Economic Analysis

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area has an overall grade of A, ranking in the 64.4th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its exceptional labor demand and low unemployment rate, at 2.70%. The city's labor demand composite score of 6.83, combining a 1.88% employment growth rate and a -0.099% weekly hours deviation, signals a genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 1.88% and weekly hours deviation of -0.099% indicate a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, as hours are only slightly below trend during a period of job growth. This combination suggests that the city is experiencing a period of sustainable job creation. The labor demand composite score of 6.83 further reinforces this notion, placing the city in the top tier.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 2.70% is exceptionally low, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This tight market implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as well as upward pressure on wages. For a business trying to hire in this city, the low unemployment rate means that attracting and retaining talent may require competitive compensation packages.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.12% is stagnant, indicating that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly. However, this slow wage growth also means that workers may have limited bargaining power and purchasing power may not be increasing. This stagnant wage growth may be a concern for businesses relying on local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank near the median at 50th percentile, the city is neither exceptionally affordable nor expensive relative to its peers. This means that the city does not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to affordability, and businesses may need to consider wage premiums to attract workers from other areas.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of 2.77% year-over-year, indicating that the workforce supply is expanding. This growth in labor force supply is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire, as it suggests that there will be an increasing pool of potential workers.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -12.70%, indicating that housing supply is tightening. This decline in building permits signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, potentially leading to increased competition for housing and higher costs for workers.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 25 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 16.7%, indicating a very competitive market. This fast-paced market means that workers relocating to this city may face challenges in finding and securing housing, as homes are selling quickly.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share percentile rank of 48th, the city has a moderately deep professional talent pool. This makes the city suited for businesses that require a mix of office and non-office skills, but it may not be the best fit for businesses that require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies.

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with low unemployment and expanding workforce supply, making it an attractive location for companies looking to hire. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tightening housing supply, which may lead to increased competition for housing and higher costs for workers, potentially offsetting the benefits of the city's strong labor market.

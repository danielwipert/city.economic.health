Economic Analysis

Hartford's labour market is showing strong momentum, with a low unemployment rate of 2.7% - down 1.3 percentage points year-on-year - and solid employment growth of 1.88% in nonfarm payrolls. The civilian labour force has expanded by 2.77% over the past year, indicating rising workforce supply, while weekly hours worked remain slightly above the city's 12-month baseline, up 0.445%. These signals suggest sustained labour demand, corroborated by a high labour demand composite score of 7.38, ranking in the 94th percentile. However, a notable weakness in this otherwise strong labour market is the decline in average hourly earnings, which have fallen 0.45% year-on-year.

In contrast, the housing market is softening. Residential building permits have decreased 12.7% year-on-year, reflecting restrained construction activity. Existing home sales are facing headwinds, with median days on market increasing to 39 days - a 30% rise from the prior year - resulting in a high days-on-market composite score of 10.0, ranking in the 98th percentile. This prolonged market time suggests weakening buyer demand, potentially due to affordability pressures, as the cost of living composite score of 3.28 exceeds median levels. The office market remains subdued, with a low office worker ratio composite score of 2.08, ranking in the 28th percentile, indicating limited return-to-office momentum.

Looking ahead, Hartford's economy is fundamentally resilient, supported by a tight labour market and steady job growth. However, stagnant wages and cooling housing activity pose near-term risks. The city's long-term economic trajectory will depend on its ability to revitalise commercial real estate demand and align wage growth with living costs.

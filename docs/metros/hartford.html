Economic Analysis

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 67.5th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its exceptional labor demand and low unemployment rate, at 2.70%. The city's labor demand composite score of 6.83, combining a 1.88% employment growth rate and a -0.099% weekly hours deviation, signals a genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 1.88% and weekly hours deviation of -0.099% indicate a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, as hours are only slightly below trend during a period of job growth. This combination suggests that the city is adding jobs at a rapid pace, with a slight adjustment in hours worked. The labor demand composite score of 6.83 reinforces this interpretation, placing the city in the top tier.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 2.70% is exceptionally low, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This tight market implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is limited, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The low unemployment rate also suggests that workers have significant bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.12% is stagnant, indicating that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly. However, this slow wage growth also means that workers may not have strong bargaining power, and their purchasing power may not be increasing significantly. The stagnant wage growth may be a concern for businesses relying on consumer demand.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank of 28th, the city is relatively expensive, as evidenced by a PSF of $252/sqft and an average hourly earnings of $39.29/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.41. Although the PSF is falling by 1.6% YoY, the city's affordability is still below average. This relatively high cost of living may make it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of 2.77% year-over-year, indicating that the workforce supply is expanding. This growth suggests that businesses may have an easier time finding workers, as the labor pool is increasing. The expanding labor force is a positive sign for hiring capacity.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -12.70%, indicating that housing supply is tightening. This decline in permits may signal future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. The decrease in building permits is a concern for businesses relying on a growing and accessible workforce.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of 30.0%, indicating a slower market. This slower market may be beneficial for workers relocating to the city, as they have more time to find a home. However, the rising days on market may also signal a normalization of the housing market.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share percentile rank of 50th, the city has a near-median depth of professional talent pool, suitable for a variety of businesses. The city is well-suited for businesses that value a balanced economy, but may not be the best fit for those requiring a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with low unemployment and expanding workforce supply, making it an attractive location for companies looking to hire and grow. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the relatively high cost of living and tightening housing supply, which may require wage premiums and impact future affordability and workforce accommodation.

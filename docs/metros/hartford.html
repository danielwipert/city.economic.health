Economic Analysis

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 63.7th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its top-tier labor demand and extremely low unemployment rate of 2.70%. The city's economic character is defined by its strong employment growth and tight labor market, with a labor demand composite score of 6.66, placing it in the 90th percentile. This suggests a city with genuine demand expansion, where businesses can capitalize on a growing workforce.

Labor Demand

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area has seen employment growth of 1.88% year-over-year, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -0.272% from its own 12-month baseline, indicating a genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing a period of job growth, with hours worked remaining relatively stable, suggesting that the growth is not solely due to existing workers taking on more hours. The labor demand composite score of 6.66, in the 90th percentile, reinforces this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford stands at 2.70%, placing it in the 98th percentile, indicating an extremely tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is limited, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight market also implies that workers have significant bargaining power, which could drive up labor costs.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford is 1.48%, ranking in the 18th percentile, indicating relatively slow wage growth. This suggests that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but it also means that workers may not have strong bargaining power, potentially limiting their purchasing power. The slow wage growth may make it easier for businesses to manage labor costs, but it could also impact the local consumer demand.

Cost of Living

Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford has a cost of living score in the 55th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $254/sqft to $39.73/hr, or 6.39. The fact that the PSF is falling by 1.6% year-over-year relative to wages is a positive sign for affordability. This suggests that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, which could be a talent attraction advantage, as businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to compensate for a high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford is growing at a rate of 2.77% year-over-year, placing it in the 96th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire, as it suggests a growing pool of potential employees. The expanding labor force also implies that the city is likely to have a more dynamic and diverse workforce.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford has decreased by 20.59% year-over-year, ranking in the 16th percentile. This decline suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and make it more challenging for workers to relocate to the city. The decrease in building permits may signal a potential constraint on the city's ability to accommodate a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford is 25 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 16.7%, placing it in the 0th percentile. This extremely fast pace of home sales suggests a highly competitive market, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. The rapid sales pace implies that the city's housing market is highly sought after, potentially driving up prices and making it less accessible to new residents.

Office Economy

Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford has an office economy score in the 38th percentile, indicating a below-average share of professional and office workers. This suggests that the city may not be the best fit for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech or finance companies. However, it may be more suitable for industries with fewer specialized roles, such as logistics or manufacturing.

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand, low unemployment, and relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for companies looking to capitalize on a growing workforce. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tightening housing supply, signaled by the decline in building permits, which could lead to future affordability issues and make it challenging for workers to relocate to the city.

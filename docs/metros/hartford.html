Economic Analysis

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 66.6th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its exceptional labor demand and low unemployment rate, at 2.70%. The city's labor demand composite score of 6.83, combining a 1.88% employment growth rate and a -0.099% weekly hours deviation, signals genuine demand expansion. These metrics define the city's current economic character, with a strong job market and low unemployment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 1.88% and weekly hours deviation of -0.099% indicate a genuine demand expansion, as hours are running slightly below trend during a period of job growth. This combination suggests that the city is adding jobs at a rapid pace, with a slight decrease in hours worked, which may indicate some efficiency gains. The labor demand composite score of 6.83 is in the top tier, at the 98th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 2.70% is extremely low, indicating a very tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is small, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The low unemployment rate also suggests that workers have strong bargaining power.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of -0.12% is stagnant, indicating that labor costs are not rising rapidly. This may be beneficial for employers, as it suggests a flat cost environment, but it also means that workers may not have strong purchasing power. The slow wage growth rate is in the bottom tier, at the 6th percentile.

Cost of Living

The city's cost of living, with a PSF of $252/sqft and average hourly earnings of $39.29/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.41, is relatively affordable, ranking in the 26th percentile. The fact that PSF is falling by 1.6% YoY relative to wages makes the city more attractive for talent, as it offers a relatively low cost of living without requiring wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of 2.77% year-over-year, indicating that the workforce supply is expanding. This suggests that businesses will have a growing pool of potential workers to draw from, making it easier to hire and staff operations. The labor force growth rate is in the top tier, at the 94th percentile.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -12.70%, indicating that housing supply is tightening. This may signal future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. The decline in building permits is below average, ranking in the 28th percentile.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 39 days, with a year-over-year increase of 30.0%, indicating a slower market. This means that workers relocating to the city may find it easier to find housing, as homes are sitting on the market for longer periods. The days on market percentile rank is in the top tier, at the 98th percentile.

Office Economy

The city's professional and office worker share is 2.48, ranking near the median, at the 46th percentile. This suggests that the city has a moderate depth of professional talent, making it suitable for businesses that require a mix of office and non-office workers. The city may be less suited for businesses that require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The Hartford-West Hartford-East Hartford metro area offers businesses a strong job market, low unemployment, and a relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for talent. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the tightening housing supply, which may lead to future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation.

Economic Analysis

The Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 45.5th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong wage growth of 7.75% year-over-year and its low labor force growth rate of -1.30% year-over-year, indicating a tight labor market with rising labor costs. The combination of these metrics suggests a challenging environment for businesses looking to hire and expand.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is -0.70% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.143% from the city's own 12-month baseline, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 3.32, which ranks below average at the 32nd percentile. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not adding jobs at a significant rate and hours are not increasing. This scenario may lead to a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 3.90%, ranking near the median at the 44th percentile. This rate indicates a relatively tight labor market with some slack, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. The practical implication for a business trying to hire in this city is that they may face some competition for talent, but it is still possible to find qualified candidates.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 7.75%, ranking in the top tier at the 92nd percentile. This fast wage growth rate implies rising labor costs for employers, but it also means that workers have strong purchasing power. As a result, businesses may need to adjust their compensation packages to attract and retain talent in this city.

Cost of Living

Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach has a cost of living score that ranks below average at the 28th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $360/sqft to $34.00/hr, resulting in a ratio of 10.59. Although the price per square foot is decreasing by 1.9% year-over-year, the city is still relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent, as the high cost of living may deter some workers from relocating to this city.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is contracting at a rate of -1.30% year-over-year, ranking in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile. This decline in labor force supply implies that the hiring capacity for businesses may be limited, making it challenging to find qualified candidates. As a result, businesses may need to consider alternative locations or strategies to attract talent.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 41.11%, ranking in the top tier at the 87th percentile. This significant increase in building permits signals that housing supply is expanding, which may lead to improved affordability and a more accommodating environment for the workforce in the future.

Days on Market

The current median days on market in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 74 days, with a year-over-year increase of 10.4%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively long time, indicating a slower and more buyer-friendly market. For a worker relocating to this city, this may be a competitive advantage, as they may have more time to find a suitable home.

Office Economy

The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 2.67, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile. This indicates a moderately deep talent pool, making the city suited for businesses that require a mix of professional and non-professional roles. However, it may not be the best fit for businesses that require a highly specialized or knowledge-intensive workforce.

In conclusion, Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach offers a unique combination of strong wage growth and a tight labor market, making it an attractive location for businesses that can adapt to these conditions. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's low labor force growth rate, which may limit hiring capacity and require businesses to offer competitive compensation packages to attract talent.

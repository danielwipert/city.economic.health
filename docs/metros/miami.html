Economic Analysis

The Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking at the 49.5th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong wage growth, with an 8.39% year-over-year increase, and its sluggish labor demand, marked by a -0.70% employment growth rate and a labor demand composite score in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. These metrics suggest a complex economic landscape with both positive and negative trends.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is -0.70%, indicating a contraction in job creation, while the weekly hours deviation from the city's own trend is +0.095%, suggesting that remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, characteristic of a survivor squeeze. This combination signals that the labor market is not expanding genuinely. The labor demand composite score of 3.23 further reinforces this notion, placing the city in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 3.50%, which places it near the median at the 45th percentile. This rate indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, it's not so high as to indicate significant weakness in local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is +8.39%, which is in the top tier at the 92nd percentile. This fast wage growth signals rising labor costs for employers but also indicates strong worker purchasing power. Businesses should anticipate higher compensation costs, but workers will have increased disposable income.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living ratio of 3.14 (price per square foot divided by hourly earnings) and a percentile rank of 37th, Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is below average in terms of affordability. This means the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, requiring businesses to consider wage premiums to attract talent without significant cost-of-living adjustments.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is growing at a rate of +1.40% year-over-year, which is above average, ranking at the 77th percentile. This expansion in the labor force supply is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire, as it indicates a growing pool of potential workers.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits is +41.11%, placing Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach in the top tier at the 87th percentile. This significant increase in building permits signals that housing supply is expected to expand, which should improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce.

Days on Market

The current median days on market for homes in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 74 days, with a year-over-year increase of +10.4%. This increase, coupled with the absolute level, indicates a slower market, which can be more accessible for workers relocating to the city, as they have more time to find suitable housing.

Office Economy

Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach has an office economy percentile rank of 68th, indicating a deep professional talent pool. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions that require specialized knowledge-economy workers. However, it may be less ideal for industries dominated by industrial or logistics roles.

In conclusion, Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach offers businesses a unique combination of strong wage growth and an expanding labor force, which can be attractive for talent acquisition and retention. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's below-average affordability and the potential for labor costs to rise due to strong wage growth, which could impact operational budgets and talent attraction strategies.

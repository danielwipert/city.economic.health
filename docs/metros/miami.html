Economic Analysis

The Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 46.4th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its high wage growth rate of 7.30% year-over-year and its low unemployment rate of 3.90%, which signals a tight labor market. The combination of these metrics suggests that while the city has strong wage growth, its labor market is highly competitive.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is -0.25% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating 0.166% above the city's own 12-month trend. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 3.78 ranks below average at the 38th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 3.90%, ranking at the 18th percentile, which indicates a very tight labor market with little slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent and may need to offer higher wages to attract workers. The tight labor market implies that hiring will be challenging and may drive up labor costs.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 7.30%, ranking at the 94th percentile, which is exceptionally high. This rapid wage growth implies that employer labor costs will rise quickly, but it also means that workers will have strong purchasing power. The high wage growth rate is a double-edged sword, benefiting workers but increasing costs for businesses.

Cost of Living

Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach has a cost of living score that ranks at the 29th percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $358/sqft to $34.04/hr, or 10.52. Although the price per square foot is decreasing by 1.4% year-over-year, the city remains relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that attracting talent without offering wage premiums may be challenging due to the high cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is growing at a rate of 0.35% year-over-year, ranking above average at the 74th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which is a positive sign for businesses looking to hire in the area. The growing labor force implies that hiring capacity should improve over time.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in residential building permits in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is -0.50%, ranking near the median at the 48th percentile. This slight decline in building permits suggests that housing supply may not be expanding quickly enough to meet demand, potentially leading to future affordability issues and constraints on workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The current median days on market for homes in Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach is 79 days, with a year-over-year increase of 2.6%. This indicates a slightly slower market, which could make it more accessible for relocating workers to find housing. However, the days on market percentile rank is below average at the 28th percentile, suggesting that the market is still relatively competitive.

Office Economy

Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach has an office economy score ranking at the 72nd percentile, indicating a deep talent pool in professional and office sectors. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and headquarters operations, but less ideal for industries with primarily industrial or logistics roles.

The Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metro area offers businesses a unique combination of high wage growth and a tight labor market, making it an attractive location for companies that can navigate these conditions. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the high cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially offsetting the benefits of the city's strong wage growth and deep professional talent pool.

Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Miami's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labor market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.5%, with a year-over-year increase of 0.8 percentage points, suggesting a slight deterioration in job market conditions. However, average hourly earnings have risen by 7.7% year-over-year, indicating robust wage growth. The civilian labor force has expanded by 1.4% over the past year, a modest increase that suggests a relatively stable workforce supply growth.

In terms of business activity, Miami's office worker ratio composite score is 2.22, and its labor demand composite score is 4.72, indicating a moderate level of employment and weekly hours. Nonfarm payroll employment has grown by 0.25% year-over-year, a slow pace that may be a cause for concern. On the other hand, residential building permits have surged by 41.11% year-over-year, suggesting a strong construction sector. The median days on market for residential properties has increased by 10.45% year-over-year to 74 days, which may indicate a slight slowdown in the housing market.

Despite these mixed signals, Miami's economy appears to be resilient, with a cost of living composite score of 3.26, which is relatively low. The city's overall economic grade is a B+, indicating an above-average performance. However, the labor market and housing sectors warrant close monitoring, as they may be vulnerable to stress points. The city's strong wage growth and construction sector are notable strengths, but the slow employment growth and increasing days on market for residential properties are potential areas of concern.

Closing outlook: Miami's economy is expected to continue growing, albeit at a moderate pace, driven by its strong construction sector and robust wage growth. However, the city's labor market and housing sectors require close attention, as they may be susceptible to external shocks. Overall, Miami's economic fundamentals remain solid, but policymakers and businesses should be vigilant in addressing potential stress points to ensure sustained growth and prosperity.

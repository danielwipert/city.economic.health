Economic Analysis

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 41.2th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a top-tier percentile rank of 94th, and its high cost of living affordability, ranking 65th percentile. The labor demand is driven by a combination of employment growth and hours worked above trend.

Labor Demand

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area has an employment growth rate of +0.83% year-over-year and weekly hours deviation of +0.982% above its own trend, signaling genuine demand expansion. This combination indicates that the city is experiencing a strong increase in jobs and hours worked, suggesting a healthy labor market. The top-tier percentile rank of 94th for labor demand composite score further reinforces this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford is 4.70%, ranking it in the bottom tier at 18th percentile, indicating a relatively loose labor market. This means that there is some slack in the market, making it easier for businesses to hire workers. However, the relatively high unemployment rate also suggests weaker local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford is +1.64%, ranking it below average at 20th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. As a result, worker purchasing power is increasing, but at a relatively slow rate.

Cost of Living

Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford has a cost of living percentile rank of 65th, indicating that it is more affordable than many of its peer cities. The city's PSF of $224/sqft is decreasing at a rate of -3.5% year-over-year, relative to average hourly earnings of $33.37/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.71. This affordability advantage can be a talent attraction strategy for businesses, as workers may not require wage premiums to afford the cost of living.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford is contracting at a rate of -1.54% year-over-year, ranking it in the bottom tier at 8th percentile. This decline in labor force supply suggests that the city may face structural headwinds in hiring and workforce expansion. Businesses may need to consider strategies to attract workers from other areas or invest in workforce development programs.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford has decreased by -54.27% year-over-year, ranking it in the bottom tier at 0th percentile. This sharp decline in building permits suggests that the city's housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford is 68 days, with a year-over-year increase of +9.7%. This indicates a slower market, ranking it in the bottom tier at 16th percentile. For workers relocating to this city, the slower market may make it more accessible to find a home, but it also suggests a less competitive market for sellers.

Office Economy

Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford has a professional and office worker share ranking near the median at 52nd percentile, indicating a moderate depth of talent pool. This city is relatively well-suited for businesses that require a general workforce, but may not be the best fit for companies that require a highly specialized or knowledge-intensive workforce.

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand and affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for talent attraction and workforce expansion. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's tightening housing supply and declining labor force growth, which may lead to future affordability challenges and hiring difficulties.

Economic Analysis

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 31.4th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 4.90% and stagnant wage growth of +0.75% YoY, which signal a tight labor market with upward pressure on wages. These metrics, combined with a labor demand composite score of 4.78, indicate a complex economic environment.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of +0.10% YoY and weekly hours deviation of +0.344% from the city's own trend suggest genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This combination signals that the city is experiencing an increase in labor demand, driven by the addition of new jobs and longer working hours. The labor demand composite score of 4.78, ranking in the 70th percentile, further supports this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.90% is relatively high, ranking in the 10th percentile, which means the labor market has some slack. However, this still presents a challenge for businesses trying to hire, as the pool of available workers is limited, and wages may be subject to upward pressure. In practical terms, businesses may need to offer competitive wages to attract and retain talent in this market.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +0.75% is stagnant, ranking in the 10th percentile, which means labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing significantly. This slow wage growth may limit the city's attractiveness to workers seeking better compensation, but it also means that businesses may not face rapid increases in labor costs.

Cost of Living

The city's cost of living, with a PSF of $225/sqft and average hourly earnings of $33.39/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.74, ranks in the 54th percentile, indicating a near-median affordability level. This means that the city is neither extremely affordable nor extremely expensive, and businesses may not need to offer significant wage premiums to attract talent. However, the 2.6% YoY decrease in PSF is a positive factor, making the city more affordable.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is contracting at a rate of -1.28% YoY, ranking in the 12th percentile, which means the workforce supply is shrinking. This contraction presents a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of available workers is decreasing, making it more challenging for businesses to find and recruit talent.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -35.64%, ranking in the 6th percentile, which signals a significant tightening of the housing supply. This sharp decline in permits suggests that the city's future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 67 days, with a YoY increase of 11.7%, ranking in the 11th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively long time, indicating a slower and more buyer-friendly market. For workers relocating to this city, this may be a positive factor, as they may have more time to find and purchase a home.

Office Economy

The city's professional and office worker share is 2.33, ranking in the 42nd percentile, which indicates a moderate depth of talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics-dominant companies. However, it may not be the best fit for tech, finance, or consulting firms that require a highly skilled and specialized workforce.

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area offers a complex economic environment with both opportunities and challenges for businesses. While the city's tight labor market and stagnant wage growth present challenges, the moderate cost of living and slower housing market may provide some advantages. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses is the shrinking labor force supply, which may limit hiring capacity and require creative strategies to attract and retain talent.

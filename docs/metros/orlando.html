Economic Analysis

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 40.1th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand, which is above average, and its high unemployment rate, which is in the bottom tier at 11th percentile. The labor demand composite score of 4.94, combined with an employment growth rate of 0.46% and a weekly hours deviation of -0.000%, signals a genuine demand expansion.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of 0.46% and weekly hours deviation of -0.000% indicate a moderate expansion of labor demand. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are not significantly above trend, suggesting that the growth in employment is not solely due to existing workers taking on more hours. The labor demand composite score of 4.94, ranking in the 67th percentile, further supports this interpretation.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 4.40% is relatively high, ranking in the 11th percentile, indicating a market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it easier to hire workers, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. The practical implication for a business trying to hire in this market is that they may have a larger pool of potential candidates to choose from.

Wage Growth

The wage growth rate is not available, but is estimated to be near the median, ranking in the 50th percentile. This suggests that wage growth is moderate, neither extremely high nor low. The implication for employer labor costs is that they may not face significant pressure to increase wages, but worker purchasing power may not be strongly increasing either.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford is near the median, ranking in the 50th percentile, indicating that the city is neither extremely affordable nor expensive relative to peers. This means that businesses may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to low costs, but may also not need to offer large wage premiums to attract workers.

Labor Force Growth

The labor force growth rate is not available, but is estimated to be near the median, ranking in the 50th percentile. This suggests that the supply of workers is neither strongly expanding nor contracting. The implication for hiring capacity is that businesses may not face significant challenges in finding workers, but may also not have a rapidly growing pool of potential candidates.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -35.64%, ranking in the 6th percentile, indicating a significant tightening of housing supply. This signals that future affordability and workforce accommodation may be at risk, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 67 days, with a year-over-year increase of 11.7%, ranking in the 12th percentile. This indicates a slower market, where homes are taking longer to sell. For a worker relocating to this city, this means that the housing market may be more accessible, with less competition for homes.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers is 2.58, ranking in the 47th percentile, indicating a moderate depth of professional talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool, and may be less suitable for tech, finance, or consulting businesses that rely on a highly skilled workforce.

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area offers businesses a labor market with some slack and moderate labor demand, but also faces challenges such as a tightening housing supply and a relatively high unemployment rate. The single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker to factor in is the potential for future affordability and workforce accommodation issues due to the significant decline in building permits, which may impact the city's ability to attract and retain talent.

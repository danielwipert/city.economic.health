Economic Analysis

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Orlando's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. The labour market, while not overly robust, shows an unemployment rate of 4.4%, which, although higher than the national average, has increased by 1.4 percentage points year-over-year. This uptick in unemployment is somewhat mitigated by a modest growth in the civilian labour force, with a 0.22% year-over-year change. Average hourly earnings have grown at a rate of 1.36% year-over-year, which, although positive, may not be sufficient to keep pace with the rising cost of living, as indicated by a cost of living composite score of 2.79.

Business activity in Orlando appears to be experiencing some stress, with nonfarm payroll employment growing at a sluggish 0.24% year-over-year rate. The labour demand composite score of 4.39 suggests a relatively weak labour market, which is further underscored by a negative deviation in weekly hours worked, at -0.245% from the 12-month baseline. On the other hand, the office worker ratio composite score of 3.04 is a positive indicator, suggesting a relatively high proportion of office workers in the city. The residential housing market is also experiencing some challenges, with a sharp decline in residential building permits of 35.64% year-over-year, and a notable increase in median days on market, up 11.67% year-over-year to 67 days.

Despite these challenges, Orlando's economy still exhibits some strengths, particularly in its office worker ratio and relatively low cost of living compared to other major cities. However, the current labour market and housing trends suggest that the city may be facing some headwinds in the short term. As the city's economic indicators continue to evolve, it will be important to monitor these trends and assess their impact on the overall economic outlook for Orlando.

Closing outlook: Orlando's economy is likely to face ongoing challenges in the labour market and housing sector, but its relatively low cost of living and strong office worker ratio may help to mitigate these effects. As the city navigates these trends, policymakers and businesses will need to be vigilant in responding to emerging opportunities and challenges, and work to foster a more dynamic and resilient economy.

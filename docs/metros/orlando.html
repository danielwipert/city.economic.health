Economic Analysis

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 31.5th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its low unemployment rate of 4.90% and stagnant wage growth of +0.75% year-over-year. The combination of these metrics suggests a tight labor market with upward pressure on wages.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate is +0.10% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating +0.344% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is experiencing a moderate increase in labor demand, with both job growth and hours worked above trend. The labor demand composite score of 4.78 ranks in the 72nd percentile, further supporting this assessment.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate is 4.90%, ranking in the 10th percentile, indicating a very tight labor market. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent, leading to upward pressure on wages. The low unemployment rate suggests that workers have strong bargaining power, making it challenging for employers to attract and retain staff.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate is +0.75%, ranking in the 11th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are not increasing rapidly, but it also means that workers have limited bargaining power and may not see significant increases in their purchasing power. The implication for businesses is a relatively flat cost environment, but one that may not be attractive to workers seeking better compensation.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford is near median, with a percentile rank of 51st, and a PSF to earnings ratio of $225/sqft to $33.39/hr, or 6.74. The fact that PSF is falling -2.6% year-over-year suggests that the city is becoming more affordable relative to wages. This means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums, as the cost of living is relatively manageable.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is contracting at a rate of -1.28% year-over-year, ranking in the 11th percentile. This decline in labor force growth indicates that the supply of workers is shrinking, creating a structural headwind for hiring. Businesses may need to consider alternative strategies, such as upskilling or reskilling existing employees, to address their labor needs.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -35.64%, ranking in the 6th percentile, indicating a significant tightening of housing supply. This sharp decline in permits suggests that the city's housing market may become less affordable in the future, making it more challenging for workers to relocate and for businesses to attract talent.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 67 days, with a year-over-year increase of +11.7%. This increase in days on market suggests that the housing market is slowing, making it more accessible for relocating workers to find homes. However, the relatively low percentile rank of 12th indicates that the market is still relatively competitive.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers is 2.46, ranking in the 46th percentile, indicating a moderate depth of talent in these sectors. This suggests that the city is suited for businesses that require a mix of professional and non-professional workers, but may not be the best fit for companies that require a highly specialized or deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

The Orlando-Kissimmee-Sanford metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a tight labor market and relatively stagnant wage growth, making it challenging to attract and retain talent. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's shrinking labor force and tightening housing supply, which may limit the availability of workers and drive up labor costs in the long term.

Economic Analysis

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 36.5th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score reflecting a mix of weak labor demand and high cost of living. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand composite score of 3.73, which combines a -0.41% employment growth rate and a -0.399% weekly hours deviation from trend, as well as its expensive cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 10.96. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is -0.41% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.399% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, with hours below trend during a period of job losses, suggesting a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This metric ranks below average, at the 36th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 3.90%, which is below average, ranking at the 26th percentile. This indicates a relatively tight labor market, making it harder for businesses to hire and potentially leading to upward wage pressure. As a result, companies may face challenges in finding and retaining talent in this market.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is +3.66%, which is near the median, ranking at the 48th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a manageable pace, while worker purchasing power is also increasing. This balance may attract businesses looking for a stable labor market.

Cost of Living

Boston-Cambridge-Newton has a cost of living score that ranks at the 14th percentile, with a PSF to wages ratio of 10.96, and a year-over-year increase of +0.4% in PSF. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive, making it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The high cost of living may deter some companies from relocating to this area.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is growing at a rate of +2.87% year-over-year, which is a top-tier growth rate, ranking at the 98th percentile. This expansion in labor supply suggests that the city has a growing workforce, making it easier for businesses to find and hire new employees. This metric is a significant advantage for companies looking to expand their operations.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Boston-Cambridge-Newton has decreased by -22.81% year-over-year, which is a bottom-tier performance, ranking at the 16th percentile. This decline in building permits signals a tightening of housing supply, which may lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for workforce accommodation in the future.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 25 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%, ranking at the 4th percentile. This indicates a highly competitive market, where homes are selling quickly, making it challenging for relocating workers to find affordable housing. This metric suggests that businesses may need to offer relocation assistance or other incentives to attract talent.

Office Economy

Boston-Cambridge-Newton has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy score ranking at the 60th percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of specialized knowledge workers. However, the city may be less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area offers businesses a highly skilled and growing labor force, but also presents significant challenges, including a high cost of living and a competitive housing market. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's expensive cost of living, which may require businesses to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially increasing labor costs and affecting profitability.

Economic Analysis

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 37.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its low labor demand and high cost of living. The labor demand composite score of 3.18, combining a -0.80% employment growth rate and a -0.598% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. The cost of living, with a PSF of $507/sqft and an earnings ratio of 10.94, ranks in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of -0.80% and weekly hours deviation of -0.598% from trend indicate a contraction in labor demand, signaling that the city is experiencing a decline in job additions and hours worked. This combination suggests that the labor market is not expanding, and businesses may face challenges in finding workers to fill new roles. The negative labor demand composite score of 3.18 reinforces this assessment, ranking below average at the 26th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 3.90% ranks near the median at the 45th percentile, indicating a relatively balanced labor market with neither significant slack nor tightness. This rate suggests that businesses may face moderate challenges in hiring, but wage pressure is not excessively high. However, the market is not particularly loose, either, meaning that hiring may require some effort but is not overly difficult.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.59% ranks near the median at the 46th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, and worker purchasing power is increasing at a steady pace. While this growth is not stagnant, it is also not exceptionally high, providing a relatively stable environment for businesses to operate.

Cost of Living

The cost of living in Boston-Cambridge-Newton, with a PSF of $507/sqft and an earnings ratio of 10.94, is relatively expensive, ranking in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile. This means that the city is less affordable than most of its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The high cost of living may deter some businesses from locating in the area, as they may need to compensate employees with higher salaries to offset the expensive housing market.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +2.87% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier at the 96th percentile. This indicates that the labor force supply is expanding rapidly, providing businesses with a growing pool of potential workers to hire from. The strong labor force growth suggests that the city has a favorable environment for hiring and expanding operations.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -22.81%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 16th percentile. This decline in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation. The decrease in building permits may exacerbate the existing high cost of living, making it even more difficult for businesses to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The current median days on market is 25 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. This indicates that homes are selling relatively quickly, making the market competitive for relocating workers. The low days on market suggests that the housing market is favorable for sellers, but may pose challenges for businesses trying to attract talent from other areas.

Office Economy

The share of professional and office workers is 3.28, ranking above average at the 74th percentile. This indicates that the city has a deep talent pool in professional and office sectors, making it well-suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ operations. However, the city may be less suitable for businesses in industrial or logistics-dominant sectors, which may require a different type of workforce.

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area offers businesses a highly skilled and expanding labor force, but also presents significant challenges in terms of high cost of living and tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the expensive housing market, which may require businesses to offer wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially increasing labor costs and affecting profitability.

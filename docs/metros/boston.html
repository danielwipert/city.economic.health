## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Boston’s labour market shows mixed signals, with unemployment at 3.9%—modest by historical standards—but up 0.9 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), suggesting softening demand. Despite this, the civilian labour force expanded 2.87% over the past year (102A_clf_yoy), a strong inflow that ranks among the top 6% of metros nationally, indicating sustained regional appeal. Employment growth remains subdued at 0.73% YoY (107E_employment_growth_yoy), though the labour demand composite score of 5.5 (64th percentile) reflects stable employment levels and slightly above-average weekly hours (0.174% above 12-month baseline). Wage growth at 3.19% (103B_earnings_yoy) trails inflation pressures, contributing to affordability constraints.

Housing and business conditions present countervailing risks. Residential construction has contracted sharply, with building permits down 14.97% YoY (200B_permits_yoy), limiting supply growth in an already tight market. The cost of living composite score of 5.2 (10th percentile) underscores severe affordability challenges, among the worst in the nation. While homes are selling relatively quickly—30 days on market, up 20% from a year ago (204A_dom_level_days, 204A_dom_yoy_pct)—this reflects constrained inventory rather than robust turnover. Office occupancy remains a relative strength, with an office worker ratio composite of 3.06 (76th percentile), supporting downtown commercial activity better than most peer cities.

**Outlook:** Boston’s economy faces near-term headwinds from housing scarcity and slowing job growth, but its highly educated workforce and resilient downtown footprint provide structural support. Absent a rebound in housing supply, affordability will continue to constrain long-term expansion.

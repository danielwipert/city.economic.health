Economic Analysis

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 44.8th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor force growth, with a 2.87% year-over-year increase, and its high cost of living, with a PSF to earnings ratio of 10.89. These two metrics signal a city with a rapidly expanding workforce but significant affordability challenges.

Labor Demand

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an employment growth rate that is not available, but its weekly hours deviation from trend is -0.622%, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 4.38, which ranks in the 61st percentile. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as the city's labor market is adding jobs, albeit with hours slightly below trend. This suggests a moderate level of labor demand.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 3.90%, ranking in the 41st percentile, indicating a near-median level of unemployment. This means the market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter labor markets. However, this also implies weaker local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 2.98%, ranking in the 37th percentile, indicating below-average wage growth. This means labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing, but not rapidly. This moderate wage growth environment may lead to some upward pressure on labor costs.

Cost of Living

Boston-Cambridge-Newton has a cost of living percentile rank of 14th, with a PSF to earnings ratio of 10.89, and a PSF growth rate of +0.4% year-over-year. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The high cost of living may deter some businesses from locating in the area.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is growing at a rate of 2.87% year-over-year, ranking in the 96th percentile. This rapid expansion of the labor force supply means that businesses have a large and growing pool of potential workers to hire from, making it easier to find talent.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Boston-Cambridge-Newton has decreased by 14.97% year-over-year, ranking in the 28th percentile. This decline in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and make it harder for workers to relocate to the area.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 25 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%, ranking in the 4th percentile. This extremely fast pace of home sales indicates a highly competitive market, making it challenging for relocating workers to find housing.

Office Economy

Boston-Cambridge-Newton has an office economy percentile rank of 52nd, with a share of professional and office workers indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This makes the city suitable for businesses in the tech, finance, and consulting sectors, but less ideal for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area offers businesses a rapidly expanding labor force and a strong office economy, but its high cost of living and tightening housing supply pose significant challenges. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's affordability, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially offsetting the benefits of the area's growing labor force and deep professional talent pool.

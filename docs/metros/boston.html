Economic Analysis

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 35.7th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its low labor demand and high cost of living. The city's labor demand composite score of 3.18, which combines a -0.80% employment growth rate and a -0.598% weekly hours deviation from its own trend, signals a contraction in job market activity. Additionally, the cost of living, with a PSF of $507/sqft and an earnings ratio of 10.94, ranks in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile.

Labor Demand

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an employment growth rate of -0.80% and a weekly hours deviation of -0.598% from its own trend, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a genuine demand contraction, rather than a survivor squeeze, as both jobs and hours are declining. This suggests that the city's job market is experiencing a slowdown.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 3.90%, ranking near the median at the 43rd percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced labor market, neither extremely tight nor slack. For a business trying to hire in this city, the moderate unemployment rate suggests that finding talent may be manageable, but not exceptionally easy or difficult.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is +3.59%, ranking near the median at the 46th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not exceptionally quickly. As a result, worker purchasing power is increasing, but not at an accelerated pace.

Cost of Living

Boston-Cambridge-Newton has a cost of living ranking in the bottom tier at the 10th percentile, with a PSF of $507/sqft and an earnings ratio of 10.94. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. As a result, attracting talent without offering wage premiums may be challenging, as the high cost of living may offset the appeal of the city's other advantages.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is growing at a rate of +2.87% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier at the 96th percentile. This suggests that the workforce supply is expanding rapidly, which should support hiring capacity and reduce the risk of labor shortages.

Building Permits

The number of residential building permits in Boston-Cambridge-Newton has declined by -22.81% year-over-year, ranking in the bottom tier at the 17th percentile. This sharp decline in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation.

Days on Market

The median days on market for homes in Boston-Cambridge-Newton is 25 days, with a year-over-year change of +0.0%, ranking in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. This extremely competitive market suggests that workers relocating to the city may face significant challenges in finding and securing housing.

Office Economy

Boston-Cambridge-Newton has a deep professional talent pool, with an office economy ranking above average at the 77th percentile. This suggests that the city is well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but may be less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a growing labor force and a deep professional talent pool, but the high cost of living and tightening housing supply pose significant risks to affordability and workforce accommodation. The single biggest constraint for a decision-maker to factor in is the city's expensive cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent.

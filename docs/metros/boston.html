Economic Analysis

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 36.0th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its weak labor demand and high cost of living. The labor demand composite score of 2.96, combining a -0.96% employment growth rate and a -0.622% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. This, coupled with a cost of living ratio of 10.74, driven by a $500/sqft price point and $46.57/hr average wage, defines the city's current economic character.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate of -0.96% and weekly hours deviation of -0.622% from trend indicate a contraction in labor demand, signaling that the city is experiencing a decline in job additions and hours worked. This combination suggests that the labor market is not expanding, and businesses may face challenges in finding workers to fill new positions. The negative labor demand composite score of 2.96 further reinforces this notion.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate of 3.90% ranks below average at the 40th percentile, indicating a moderate level of slack in the labor market. While this is not extremely high, it suggests that the market is not overly tight, making it somewhat easier for businesses to hire workers without facing extreme wage pressure. However, this also implies that local consumer demand may not be as strong as in areas with lower unemployment rates.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate of +2.98% is below average, ranking at the 38th percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not at an alarming rate, and worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit slowly. This balance may be beneficial for businesses looking to manage labor costs while still attracting and retaining talent.

Cost of Living

With a cost of living percentile rank of 26th, Boston-Cambridge-Newton is considered expensive relative to its peers, driven by a $500/sqft price point and $46.57/hr average wage. This high cost of living may deter talent from relocating to the area without wage premiums, potentially making it challenging for businesses to attract and retain workers without offering higher salaries.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +2.87% year-over-year, ranking at the 96th percentile. This significant expansion in labor force supply indicates that the city has a growing pool of potential workers, which could facilitate hiring for businesses. The increasing labor force can help meet the demands of growing companies.

Building Permits

The year-over-year change in building permits is -14.97%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This decline in residential building permits suggests that the future affordability and workforce accommodation in the city may be at risk, potentially exacerbating the existing high cost of living.

Days on Market

Data on days on market is not available for this city, making it difficult to assess the current state of the housing market and its implications for relocating workers. This lack of information limits the ability to determine whether the city's housing market is competitive or accessible for new residents.

Office Economy

With an office/professional worker share ranking at the 76th percentile, Boston-Cambridge-Newton has a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This city is well-suited for businesses that rely on professional and office sectors, but may not be as ideal for industries with more industrial or logistics-dominant economies.

The Boston-Cambridge-Newton metro area offers businesses a growing labor force and a deep professional talent pool, but its high cost of living and tightening housing supply pose significant risks. The single biggest constraint for decision-makers is the city's expensive nature, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, potentially offsetting the benefits of the area's skilled workforce.

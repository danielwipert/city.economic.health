Economic Analysis

Boston's labour market is sending mixed signals. Unemployment stands at 3.9%, a modest level by historical standards, but has risen 0.9 percentage points year-on-year, indicating softening demand. However, the civilian labour force has expanded 2.87% over the past year, a strong inflow that places Boston among the top 6% of US metros, suggesting the region's appeal endures. Employment growth, at 0.73% year-on-year, remains subdued, yet the labour demand composite score of 5.5, ranking in the 64th percentile, reflects stable employment levels and slightly above-average weekly hours, 0.174% above the 12-month baseline. Wage growth, at 3.19%, lags behind inflation pressures, exacerbating affordability constraints.

The housing and business landscape presents contrasting risks. A sharp contraction in residential construction, with building permits down 14.97% year-on-year, is limiting supply growth in an already tight market. The cost of living composite score of 5.2, in the 10th percentile, highlights severe affordability challenges, among the worst nationally. Although homes are selling relatively quickly, with an average of 30 days on the market - a 20% increase from last year - this is largely due to constrained inventory rather than robust turnover. In contrast, office occupancy remains a relative strength, with an office worker ratio composite of 3.06, ranking in the 76th percentile, and supporting downtown commercial activity more effectively than most peer cities.

Looking ahead, Boston's economy faces near-term challenges from housing scarcity and slowing job growth. Nevertheless, its highly educated workforce and resilient downtown footprint provide underlying support. Unless there is a rebound in housing supply, affordability constraints are likely to continue limiting long-term expansion.

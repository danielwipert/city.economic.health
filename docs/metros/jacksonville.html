Economic Analysis

The city of Jacksonville has an overall grade of D, ranking in the 22.1th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 2.55. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand and high unemployment rate, with employment growth at -0.42% year-over-year and an unemployment rate of 4.80%. These metrics signal significant challenges for businesses looking to expand or relocate in the area.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Jacksonville is -0.42% year-over-year, and weekly hours are 1.167% above the city's own 12-month trend. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 2.55 is in the bottom tier, at the 12th percentile.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Jacksonville is 4.80%, which is in the bottom tier, at the 4th percentile. This indicates a tight labor market with significant slack, making it challenging for businesses to hire and retain workers. The high unemployment rate implies that businesses may face weaker local consumer demand.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth in Jacksonville is 1.23%, which is in the bottom tier, at the 16th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slow pace, and worker purchasing power is not increasing significantly. The wage growth rate is not fast enough to drive significant economic expansion.

Cost of Living

Jacksonville has a cost of living score of 35th percentile, with a PSF of $218/sqft, which is decreasing by 2.7% year-over-year, and an average hourly earnings of $36.76/hr. This makes the city relatively affordable, with a ratio of 5.93 below average. The decreasing PSF and relatively low cost of living make Jacksonville an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Jacksonville is shrinking at a rate of -0.49% year-over-year, which is near the median, at the 50th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply implies that businesses may face structural headwinds in hiring and expanding their workforce. The shrinking labor pool may limit the city's ability to support business growth.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Jacksonville is decreasing by 12.00% year-over-year, which is below average, at the 34th percentile. This decline in building permits signals a tightening of housing supply, which may lead to decreased affordability and increased competition for housing in the future. The decrease in building permits may constrain workforce accommodation and limit the city's ability to attract new talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Jacksonville is 59 days, with a year-over-year decrease of 1.7%. This indicates a relatively fast-paced market, where homes are selling quickly. However, the decrease in days on market may signal a competitive market, making it challenging for relocating workers to find affordable housing.

Office Economy

Jacksonville has an office economy score at the 50th percentile, with a share of professional and office workers at 2.62. This indicates a moderate depth of professional talent pool, making the city suitable for businesses that require a mix of office and non-office workers. The city is relatively well-suited for businesses that are not heavily reliant on specialized office roles, such as logistics or industrial companies.

The city of Jacksonville offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a moderate wage growth rate, but it is constrained by low labor demand, high unemployment, and a shrinking labor force. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering relocation to Jacksonville is the limited availability of skilled workers and the potential for decreased affordability due to tightening housing supply.

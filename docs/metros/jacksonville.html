Economic Analysis

Jacksonville has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 27.7th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score of 2.71, and its high cost of living affordability, ranking in the 82nd percentile. Specifically, the labor demand metrics, including a -0.44% employment growth rate and a 5.00% unemployment rate, signal a challenging job market.

Labor Demand

The employment growth rate in Jacksonville is -0.44% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating 0.970% above the city's own 12-month trend. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. This indicates a contracting job market with limited opportunities for growth.

Unemployment

The unemployment rate in Jacksonville is 5.00%, ranking in the 7th percentile, indicating a loose labor market with significant slack. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will have an easier time finding candidates, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. The low unemployment rate percentile suggests that the city's labor market is not tight, and businesses may not face intense competition for talent.

Wage Growth

The year-over-year wage growth rate in Jacksonville is +4.99%, ranking in the 63rd percentile, indicating moderate to fast wage growth. This implies that employer labor costs are rising, and worker purchasing power is increasing. As a result, businesses may need to adjust their compensation packages to attract and retain talent in this city.

Cost of Living

Jacksonville has a cost of living score ranking in the 82nd percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $218/sqft to $37.70/hr, or 5.78. This indicates that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, with a falling PSF value (-2.2% YoY) contributing to its high affordability score. This makes Jacksonville an attractive location for talent without requiring significant wage premiums.

Labor Force Growth

The civilian labor force in Jacksonville is contracting at a rate of -1.48% year-over-year, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This implies a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of available workers is decreasing. Businesses may need to consider this constraint when planning their hiring strategies in this city.

Building Permits

The number of building permits in Jacksonville has decreased by -32.70% year-over-year, indicating a tightening housing supply. This signals a potential future affordability crisis and workforce accommodation challenges, as the city's housing stock is not expanding to meet demand. This could impact the city's ability to attract and retain talent.

Days on Market

The median days on market in Jacksonville is 58 days, with a year-over-year increase of +1.8%. This indicates a relatively slow market, where homes are sitting longer before being sold. For workers relocating to this city, this means a more accessible and less competitive housing market, which could be an advantage.

Office Economy

Jacksonville's professional and office worker share ranks in the 22nd percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This city is best suited for businesses that do not require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as industrial or logistics companies, and less suited for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely on specialized office workers.

In conclusion, Jacksonville offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living and a loose labor market with easier hiring, but its low labor demand, contracting labor force, and tightening housing supply pose significant risks and constraints. The single biggest risk for decision-makers is the city's limited job market growth and shrinking labor pool, which could impact businesses' ability to attract and retain talent.

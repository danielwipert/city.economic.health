Economic Analysis

Jacksonville's labour market is sending mixed signals, with robust wage growth offset by rising unemployment and modest employment expansion. The city's unemployment rate has increased to 4.6%, up 1.4 percentage points year-on-year, indicating a loosening labour market that may be due to slowing demand or sector-specific adjustments. However, average hourly earnings have risen 7.5% over the past year, ranking in the 86th percentile nationally and suggesting sustained wage pressure driven by competition for skilled workers. Labour demand remains moderately strong, with a 0.68% year-on-year increase in nonfarm payrolls and weekly hours slightly above the 12-month baseline. The civilian labour force has expanded 0.79% year-on-year, indicating steady workforce supply growth without significant participation surges.

In contrast, the city's housing and business activity point to a cooling construction sector and constrained market liquidity. Residential building permits have fallen 32.7% year-on-year, the weakest performance in this dataset, likely due to higher financing costs and softer demand. The housing market is also slowing, with homes taking a median of 58 days to sell, up 1.75% from a year ago. Jacksonville's cost of living remains relatively low, which enhances household purchasing power. However, the city's office worker ratio suggests limited concentration in high-value professional services compared to leading metro economies.

Looking ahead, Jacksonville's economy is navigating a transition marked by resilient wage growth and labour demand, but tempered by housing sector retrenchment and rising unemployment. Near-term challenges in construction and real estate may persist amid elevated interest rates. The city's broader economic trajectory will depend on its ability to diversify beyond low-cost advantages into higher-productivity sectors.

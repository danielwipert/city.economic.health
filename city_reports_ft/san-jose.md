## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara, a key hub of the technology industry, exhibits a mixed economic landscape with notable strengths and emerging challenges. The unemployment rate stands at 4.7%, slightly above the national average, reflecting a labor market that, while robust, is not without its pressures. Labor force participation is solid at 64.77%, indicating a relatively engaged workforce. However, earnings have contracted by 0.88% year-over-year, which could signal a slowdown in wage growth or a shift in employment patterns within the tech sector. The cost of living, at 7.66% above the national average, remains a significant burden for residents, potentially exacerbating income inequality and affecting consumer spending.

Housing and business activity present a more optimistic picture. Housing prices have increased by 6.95% year-over-year, suggesting strong demand despite a slight decline in permits issued, which are up 90.23% compared to the previous year. This dichotomy may indicate a tight supply in the housing market, driving prices higher. Business activity, as measured by the number of new establishments, has shown resilience, although the pace of job openings relative to the workforce (2.14%) suggests a cautious approach to hiring. The average days on market for homes is 38 days, a relatively low figure that further underscores the strong demand in the real estate sector.

Looking ahead, San Jose's economy is likely to continue navigating the tension between high living costs and robust tech-driven growth. The city's ability to attract and retain talent will be crucial, especially if wage growth does not keep pace with rising expenses. Policymakers and businesses will need to address these challenges to ensure sustainable economic development and maintain the region's competitive edge.

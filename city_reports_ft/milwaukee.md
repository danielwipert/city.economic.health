## Polished FT-style column

Here's the revised column:

Milwaukee-Waukesha's economic landscape is marked by contrasting trends. While the region excels in housing and cost of living, it faces challenges in business activity and earnings growth. The unemployment rate of 3.7% is slightly above the national average, indicating a relatively tight labor market. However, the labor force participation rate of 64.09% lags behind the national average, suggesting potential underutilization of the workforce. Earnings growth of 2.34% year-over-year ranks in the lower 26th percentile nationally, which could indicate wage pressures or limited opportunities in higher-paying sectors.

In contrast, the housing market is robust, with a 22.05% year-over-year increase in home prices placing Milwaukee in the 94th percentile nationally. This strong appreciation is driven by high demand, as evidenced by a low 32-day average time on market for homes. However, a decline in building permits of 16.64% year-over-year suggests a potential slowdown in new housing supply, which could exacerbate affordability issues. The cost of living is relatively low, at 86% of the national average, making the city attractive for relocation and investment.

Despite these positive factors, underlying economic stress is evident in the decline in building permits and relatively low earnings growth. To sustain its housing market and overall economic health, Milwaukee's economy will need to address the labor force participation and earnings growth. The city's low cost of living and strong housing market are positive signals, but they will need to be supported by broader economic dynamism to ensure long-term prosperity.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Milwaukee-Waukesha's economic landscape presents a mixed picture, with strengths in housing and cost of living, but challenges in business activity and earnings growth. The unemployment rate stands at 3.7%, slightly above the national average, indicating a relatively tight labor market. However, the labor force participation rate of 64.09% is below the national average, suggesting potential underutilization of the workforce. Earnings growth of 2.34% year-over-year is modest, placing the region in the lower 26th percentile nationally, which could indicate wage pressures or limited job opportunities in higher-paying sectors.

The housing market, however, is robust, with a 22.05% year-over-year increase in home prices, placing Milwaukee in the 94th percentile nationally. This strong appreciation is supported by a low 32-day average time on market for homes, indicating high demand. However, the decline in building permits by 16.64% year-over-year suggests a potential slowdown in new housing supply, which could exacerbate affordability issues. The cost of living is relatively low, at 86% of the national average, making the city attractive for relocation and investment. Despite these positive factors, business activity, as indicated by the decline in building permits, and the relatively low earnings growth, point to underlying economic stress.

Looking ahead, Milwaukee's economy will need to address the labor force participation and earnings growth to sustain its housing market and overall economic health. The city's low cost of living and strong housing market are positive signals, but they will need to be supported by broader economic dynamism to ensure long-term prosperity.

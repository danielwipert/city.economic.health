## Polished FT-style column

Here's the revised column:

Charlotte, the 21st-ranked US metro, presents a mixed economic picture. While its labor market remains a strength, with an unemployment rate of 4.1% (92nd percentile) and a labor force participation rate of 64.69% (average), earnings growth has slowed to 1.59% year-over-year, placing it in the 16th percentile. This divergence between stable employment and lagging wage growth could impact consumer spending and overall economic dynamism.

The housing market is also showing signs of strain. Charlotte's home price index has grown by 1.59% year-over-year, a moderate pace, but building permits have declined by 15.37% year-over-year, suggesting a slowdown in new construction. This could be a response to cooling demand or rising costs. The median days on market for homes, at 59 days, is relatively high, indicating a softening in the housing market. Business activity, as measured by weekly hours worked, is also low, at 33.2 hours (6th percentile), suggesting a cautious approach to labor utilization.

Looking ahead, Charlotte's economy faces a key challenge: balancing stable employment with the need for stronger wage growth and a more vibrant housing market. The decline in construction permits and the increase in days on market for homes suggest that the housing sector may be adjusting to changing market conditions. Policymakers and businesses will need to closely monitor these trends to ensure the city's economic resilience is maintained.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Charlotte, ranked 21st among US metros, presents a mixed economic picture with some robust indicators and others showing signs of strain. The labor market remains a strength, with an unemployment rate of 4.1%, placing it in the 92nd percentile, and a labor force participation rate of 64.69%, which is average. However, earnings growth has slowed, increasing by only 1.59% year-over-year, placing it in the 16th percentile. This suggests that while employment is stable, wage growth is lagging, which could impact consumer spending and overall economic dynamism.

In the housing market, Charlotte's home price index has grown by 1.59% year-over-year, a moderate pace. However, the decline in building permits by 15.37% year-over-year indicates a slowdown in new housing construction, which could be a response to cooling demand or rising costs. The median days on market for homes is 59 days, which is relatively high, suggesting a softening in the housing market. Business activity, as measured by the weekly hours worked, is also low, at 33.2 hours, placing it in the 6th percentile, indicating a cautious approach to labor utilization by businesses.

Looking ahead, Charlotte's economy faces the challenge of balancing stable employment with the need for stronger wage growth and a more vibrant housing market. The decline in construction permits and the increase in days on market for homes suggest that the housing sector may be entering a period of adjustment. Policymakers and businesses will need to monitor these trends closely to ensure that the city's economic resilience is maintained.

# Phoenix — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Phoenix's labour market is sending mixed signals, with robust workforce expansion and wage growth contrasting with slowing employment momentum. The 2.36% year-on-year growth in the civilian labour force, ranking in the 90th percentile nationally, suggests sustained in-migration and labour supply growth. Average hourly earnings have risen 5.8% over the same period, outpacing inflation and supporting household purchasing power, with this growth rate placing in the 70th percentile. However, nonfarm payroll growth has slowed to 0.87% year-on-year, ranking in the 38th percentile, while weekly hours worked remain 1.24% below their 12-month trend, indicating employer caution. The unemployment rate, at 3.5%, is low but has risen 0.4 percentage points over the past year, reflecting the divergence between labour supply growth and job creation.

The housing market remains active, with residential building permits up 16.86% year-on-year, signalling continued supply expansion in response to population growth, and ranking in the 74th percentile. However, the market is showing signs of cooling, with homes taking a median 54 days to sell, up 5.88% from a year ago, and the days-on-market composite score ranking in the 32nd percentile, indicating slower turnover. Despite an elevated cost of living, with a cost-of-living composite score of 2.1, ranking in the 62nd percentile, strong wage gains are helping to absorb this pressure. The office market is a bright spot, with an office worker ratio of 3.74, ranking in the 92nd percentile, pointing to resilient white-collar employment in sectors like professional services and technology.

Looking ahead, Phoenix's economy is navigating a transition from rapid population-driven growth to a more balanced expansion. While labour supply and construction activity remain strong, near-term demand for workers is lagging. Unless employment growth accelerates, wage pressures may ease and housing absorption could slow further. Although the city's fundamentals remain sound, momentum hinges on whether job creation can catch up with the expanding workforce.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Phoenix’s labour market shows mixed signals, with underlying strength in workforce expansion and wage growth offset by softening employment momentum. The civilian labour force grew 2.36% year-on-year—ranking among the strongest nationally (90th percentile)—indicating sustained in-migration and labour supply growth. Average hourly earnings rose 5.8% over the same period (70th percentile), outpacing inflation and supporting household purchasing power. However, nonfarm payroll growth slowed to 0.87% YoY (38th percentile), while weekly hours worked remain 1.24% below their 12-month trend, suggesting employers are cautious about increasing labour demand despite available workers. The unemployment rate, at 3.5%, remains low but has risen 0.4 percentage points over the past year, reflecting this divergence between labour supply growth and job creation.

Housing activity remains robust, with residential building permits up 16.86% YoY (74th percentile), signalling continued supply expansion in response to population growth. Yet the market is cooling: homes took a median 54 days to sell in the latest period, up 5.88% from a year ago, and the days-on-market composite score ranks in the 32nd percentile—indicating slower turnover. The cost of living, while elevated relative to income (cost-of-living composite score of 2.1, 62nd percentile), is being absorbed by strong wage gains. Office market strength, as proxied by the office worker ratio (3.74, 92nd percentile), points to resilient white-collar employment in sectors like professional services and technology. However, the modest pace of employment growth relative to workforce expansion poses a risk to wage sustainability if demand does not accelerate.

**Outlook:** Phoenix’s economy is navigating a transition from rapid population-driven growth to a more balanced expansion. Labour supply and construction activity remain strong, but near-term demand for workers is lagging. Absent a pickup in employment growth, wage pressures may ease and housing absorption could slow further. The city’s fundamentals remain sound, but momentum hinges on whether job creation can catch up with its expanding workforce.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington, the fourth-largest metropolitan area in the United States, exhibits a mixed economic profile with both strengths and areas of concern. The labor market is a standout feature, with a labor force participation rate of 69.57%, placing it in the 90th percentile nationally, indicating a robust engagement of the workforce. However, the unemployment rate stands at 4.4%, which is slightly above the national average, suggesting some slack in the job market. Earnings growth is moderate, at 4.11% year-over-year, aligning with broader economic trends. The cost of living has increased by 4.79% year-over-year, which, while concerning, is not out of line with inflationary pressures seen across the country.

In the housing market, the picture is more nuanced. The housing price index has declined by 1.19% year-over-year, and the price per square foot has dropped by 1.78%, reflecting a cooling market. This is further evidenced by the increase in days on market to 64, up from lower levels in recent years. Despite these declines, the number of building permits has increased by 4.08% year-over-year, suggesting continued investment in new housing stock. Business activity, as indicated by the number of weekly hours worked (36.1 hours), remains strong, placing Dallas in the 96th percentile, which is a positive sign for the local economy.

Looking ahead, Dallas's economic trajectory will likely be influenced by its ability to maintain labor force participation and manage the cooling housing market. The strong labor market and business activity are positive indicators, but the city will need to address the rising cost of living and housing affordability to sustain its economic health. The ongoing investment in new housing could help alleviate some of the pressures in the real estate sector, but policymakers will need to monitor these trends closely to ensure they do not lead to overbuilding or exacerbate existing economic imbalances.

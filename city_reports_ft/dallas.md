# Dallas — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

The Dallas economy presents a nuanced picture, with key indicators yielding both positive and negative signals. The labour market is relatively stable, with an unemployment rate of 3.6% and a modest year-over-year increase of 0.1 percentage points. However, the civilian labour force growth rate of 0.64% year-over-year is subdued, suggesting a potential slowdown in workforce supply growth. In contrast, average hourly earnings have risen by 4.52% year-over-year, outpacing many other major cities and indicating strong labour demand.

Business activity is moderate, with a labour demand composite score of 4.62. Nonfarm payroll employment has grown by 0.33% year-over-year, a relatively slow pace, while weekly hours have declined slightly, with a deviation from the 12-month baseline of -0.14%. The office worker ratio composite score of 2.1 is relatively low, suggesting a potential mismatch between labour supply and demand. In the housing sector, residential building permits have declined by 20.96% year-over-year, and the median days on market has increased by 6.67% to 48 days, indicating a slowing market.

The cost of living composite score of 1.33 is relatively high, posing a potential stress point for residents. Despite this, Dallas's overall economic grade of B, with a weighted percentile score of 53.9, suggests that the city remains a mid-tier performer among major US cities. Looking ahead, the economy is expected to continue growing at a moderate pace, driven by its strong labour market and business activity. However, policymakers must closely monitor the housing market and cost of living to ensure the city remains attractive to businesses and residents.

The outlook for Dallas is one of moderate growth, underpinned by a strong labour market and steady business activity. To maintain competitiveness in the long term, the city must address its slowing housing market and high cost of living.

Average hourly earnings, however, have risen by 4.52% year-over-year, outpacing many other major cities and suggesting a strong demand for labour.

In terms of business activity, the labour demand composite score of 4.62 indicates a moderate level of employment and weekly hours growth. Nonfarm payroll employment has grown by 0.33% year-over-year, a relatively slow pace, while the weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is -0.14%, suggesting a slight decline in working hours. The office worker ratio composite score of 2.1 is relatively low, indicating a potential mismatch between labour supply and demand. On the housing front, residential building permits have declined by 20.96% year-over-year, while the median days on market has increased by 6.67% year-over-year to 48 days, suggesting a slowing housing market.

The cost of living composite score of 1.33 is relatively high, indicating a potential stress point for residents. However, the city's overall economic grade of B, with a weighted percentile score of 53.9, suggests that Dallas remains a relatively average performer among major US cities. Looking ahead, the city's economy is likely to continue growing at a moderate pace, driven by its strong labour market and business activity. However, policymakers will need to monitor the housing market and cost of living closely to ensure that the city remains an attractive destination for businesses and residents alike.

Closing outlook: Dallas's economy is expected to maintain its moderate growth trajectory, with a strong labour market and steady business activity. However, the city will need to address its slowing housing market and high cost of living to remain competitive in the long term.

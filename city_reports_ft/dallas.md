# Dallas — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Dallas's labour market is showing signs of moderation, with the unemployment rate standing at 3.6%, a 0.1 percentage point increase year-over-year. This reflects tightening conditions amid modest workforce expansion, with civilian labour force growth slowing to 0.64% year-over-year - below the average of its peer metros. Employment growth has been tepid, with nonfarm payrolls rising just 0.33% year-over-year, while the labour demand composite score of 4.62 indicates stable but unremarkable hiring momentum. However, average hourly earnings have grown 4.52% year-over-year, outpacing inflation and supporting consumer strength.

In contrast, the housing market is softening, with residential building permits contracting sharply by 20.96% year-over-year - the weakest performance among major indicators. This suggests developer caution, and homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market rising to 48 days, up 6.7% year-over-year. Despite this, the cost of living in Dallas remains relatively favourable, with a composite score of 1.33, placing it in the top 17% of most affordable large metros. This affordability, combined with labour cost competitiveness, underpins the city's appeal for in-migration.

However, the decline in new construction permits poses a structural risk to long-term housing supply. Looking ahead, Dallas's underlying economic strengths are likely to be offset by near-term headwinds in construction and employment growth, suggesting a cyclical slowdown. Without a recovery in business investment or the office market, expansion is likely to remain below trend.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Dallas’s labour market remains resilient but shows signs of moderation. The metro’s unemployment rate stands at 3.6% (101A_unemployment), a slight increase of 0.1 percentage points year-over-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), reflecting tightening conditions amid modest workforce expansion. Civilian labour force growth has slowed to 0.64% YoY (102A_clf_yoy), below average compared to peer metros, suggesting constrained labour supply. Employment growth is tepid, with nonfarm payrolls rising just 0.33% YoY (107E_employment_growth_yoy), while the labour demand composite score of 4.62 (107E_ldc_composite) indicates stable but unspectacular hiring momentum. Average hourly earnings grew 4.52% YoY (103B_earnings_yoy), outpacing inflation, supporting consumer strength. However, the office worker ratio composite score of 2.1 (105C_owr) — well below average — highlights persistent challenges in office occupancy and downtown economic vitality.

Housing market dynamics point to softening demand and elevated inventory. Residential building permits have contracted sharply, falling 20.96% YoY (200B_permits_yoy), the weakest performance among major indicators, signaling developer caution. Homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market rising to 48 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 6.7% YoY (204A_dom_yoy_pct), though still within historical norms. The cost of living remains relatively favourable, with a composite score of 1.33 (104C_col), placing Dallas in the top 17% of most affordable large metros, bolstering its appeal for in-migration. Nevertheless, the steep decline in new construction permits represents a structural risk to long-term housing supply.

**Outlook:** Dallas retains underlying economic strengths, particularly in labour cost competitiveness and housing affordability, but near-term headwinds in construction and employment growth suggest a cyclical slowdown. Absent stronger business investment or office market recovery, expansion is likely to remain below trend.

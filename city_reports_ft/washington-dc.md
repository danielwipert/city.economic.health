# Washington DC — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Washington DC's economic indicators paint a nuanced picture, with both short-term and longer-term trends warranting attention. The labour market, a key driver of economic activity, boasts a relatively low unemployment rate of 3.8%, although this represents a 1.1 percentage point increase year-over-year. Meanwhile, the civilian labour force has contracted by 1.82% over the same period, indicating a decline in workforce supply growth. Average hourly earnings have risen by 2.39% year-over-year, a modest gain that may struggle to keep pace with the city's high cost of living, as reflected in a cost of living composite score of 1.97.

Business activity and housing markets in the city also exhibit divergent trends. Nonfarm payroll employment has fallen by 1.63% year-over-year, while the labour demand composite score of 2.02 suggests a relatively weak employment market. In contrast, the office worker ratio composite score of 2.22 indicates a moderate level of office worker activity. In the housing market, residential building permits have decreased by 3.97% year-over-year, and median days on market have increased by 25% to 30 days. The days on market composite score of 8.5 and a high percentile score of 86.0 for this metric point to a relatively slow housing market.

The city's economic challenges are evident in its declining labour force and employment growth, as well as its high cost of living and sluggish housing market. However, a relatively low unemployment rate and modest earnings growth are positives that could underpin economic stability. A weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline of -0.215% suggests a slight decline in labour market activity. Overall, Washington DC's economic outlook is cautious, with a need to address declining labour force participation and slow employment growth to sustain momentum. Looking ahead, the city's economy is likely to face short-term challenges, but its strong fundamentals and modest earnings growth could support a longer-term recovery.

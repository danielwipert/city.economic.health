## Polished FT-style column

Here's the revised column:

Washington DC's metropolitan area, spanning Washington, Arlington, and Alexandria, presents a complex economic picture. While the labor market remains robust, with an unemployment rate of 4.3% and a high labor force participation rate of 68.17% (placing it in the 80th percentile nationally), earnings growth has slowed to 1.83% year-over-year. This reflects broader economic headwinds, which are also evident in the cost of living, 5.2% above the national average, and rising owner's equivalent rent, up 2.28% annually.

The housing market is also experiencing challenges. The annual home price index growth of 0.91% is sluggish compared to other major metros, and the number of building permits issued has grown by only 2.42% year-over-year, suggesting a cautious approach to new construction. The rental market is also softening, with per-square-foot rents declining by 4.37% year-over-year, potentially indicating over-supply or weakening demand. However, properties are selling relatively quickly, with an average of 38 days on the market, suggesting that while prices may be softening, buyer interest remains steady.

Washington DC's economy will likely be shaped by federal spending decisions and broader national economic trends. The strong labor market and rapid property sales suggest underlying resilience, but the slowdown in earnings growth and softening housing prices highlight potential vulnerabilities. Policymakers and businesses will need to closely monitor these indicators to navigate the region's economic path effectively.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metropolitan area, centered around Washington DC, exhibits a mixed economic landscape with some robust indicators and others showing signs of strain. The labor market remains relatively strong, with an unemployment rate of 4.3%, slightly above the national average but supported by a high labor force participation rate of 68.17%, placing it in the 80th percentile nationally. However, earnings growth has slowed to 1.83% year-over-year, reflecting broader economic headwinds. The cost of living, at 5.2% above the national average, and the owner's equivalent rent, increasing by 2.28% annually, highlight ongoing pressures on household budgets.

In the housing sector, the annual home price index growth of 0.91% is tepid compared to other major metros, and the number of building permits issued has grown by only 2.42% year-over-year, suggesting a cautious approach to new construction. The rental market shows a decline in per-square-foot rents, down 4.37% year-over-year, which could indicate over-supply or weakening demand. Despite these challenges, properties are selling relatively quickly, with an average of 38 days on the market, indicating that while prices may be softening, buyer interest remains steady.

Looking ahead, Washington DC's economy is likely to be influenced by federal spending decisions and broader national economic trends. The strong labor market and rapid property sales suggest underlying resilience, but the slowdown in earnings growth and softening housing prices point to potential vulnerabilities. Policymakers and businesses will need to monitor these indicators closely to navigate the region's economic path effectively.

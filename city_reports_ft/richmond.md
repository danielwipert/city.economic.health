## Polished FT-style column

Richmond's economic landscape presents a complex mix of strengths and weaknesses. The city's unemployment rate stands at 3.7%, significantly lower than the national average, and is supported by a labor force participation rate of 65.37%. While the latter is slightly below the national average, it still indicates a relatively engaged workforce. Earnings growth year-over-year is robust at 6.34%, outpacing inflation, which is currently at 4.87%. However, the average weekly hours worked (33.4) are notably low, suggesting potential underutilization of labor resources or a shift towards part-time employment.

The housing sector is a notable bright spot, with home prices increasing by 19.64% year-over-year, significantly outpacing the national trend. However, this growth raises concerns about sustainability and affordability. The sharp decline in building permits, down 38.63% year-over-year, indicates a slowdown in new construction, which could impact future housing supply and economic growth. Business activity, as measured by sales per foot, is growing modestly at 0.75% year-over-year, and properties are taking an average of 45 days to sell, a healthy range but a sign of cooling compared to the recent housing boom's peak.

Looking ahead, Richmond's economy appears resilient but faces challenges. The strong labor market and wage growth are positives, but the decline in construction permits and the risk of overheating in the housing market pose risks. Policymakers will need to address these issues to ensure sustainable growth and maintain the region's economic health.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Richmond's economic landscape presents a mixed picture, with some robust indicators alongside areas of concern. The unemployment rate stands at 3.7%, placing it well below the national average and reflecting a strong labor market. This is further supported by a labor force participation rate of 65.37%, which, while slightly below the national average, indicates a relatively engaged workforce. Earnings growth year-over-year is robust at 6.34%, outpacing inflation, which is currently at 4.87%. However, the average weekly hours worked (33.4) are notably low, suggesting potential underutilization of labor resources or a shift towards part-time employment.

In the housing sector, Richmond shows significant strength, with home prices increasing by 19.64% year-over-year, significantly outpacing the national trend. This growth, however, comes with a cautionary note as it may be unsustainable and could lead to affordability issues. The number of building permits has declined sharply by 38.63% year-over-year, indicating a slowdown in new construction, which could affect future housing supply and economic growth. Business activity, as measured by the pace of sales per foot, is growing modestly at 0.75% year-over-year, and properties are taking an average of 45 days to sell, which is within a healthy range but suggests a cooling compared to the peak of the recent housing boom.

Looking ahead, Richmond's economy appears resilient but faces challenges. The strong labor market and wage growth are positive signs, but the decline in construction permits and the potential for overheating in the housing market could pose risks. Policymakers will need to address these issues to ensure sustainable growth and maintain the region's economic health.

# Raleigh — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Raleigh's economy is driven by robust labor demand and healthy wage growth, with a tight labor market characterized by an unemployment rate of 3.0%, up 0.2 percentage points year-on-year. This near-full employment condition is supported by a labor demand composite score of 6.28 and nonfarm employment growth of 1.25% over the past year. Average hourly earnings have risen 5.28% year-on-year, outpacing inflation and bolstering household purchasing power. However, labor force growth is modest, at 0.53% annually, indicating supply-side constraints despite strong job creation. The stable workloads, reflected in a weekly hours deviation of +0.226%, suggest no broad-based underutilization or overextension.

The housing market is responding to sustained demand, with residential building permits surging 38.8% year-on-year, one of the strongest national accelerations. Despite this construction boom, housing affordability remains a relative strength, with a cost of living composite score of 1.41, driven by a favorable balance between home prices and earnings. The office market is particularly resilient, with an office worker ratio composite score of 4.6, ranking in the 98th percentile, indicating a higher-than-average in-person workforce presence compared to peer metros. While homes are taking longer to sell, with an average of 46 days, up 4.55% year-on-year, inventory conditions remain manageable, suggesting a slight cooling in transaction velocity rather than a significant downturn.

Looking ahead, Raleigh is well-positioned for continued economic expansion, driven by strong labor demand, rising construction activity, and relative affordability. Key risks in the near term include constrained labor supply and potential oversupply if migration trends moderate. Nevertheless, absent a sharp downturn in migration or a retrenchment in the tech sector, the city's fundamentals support sustained outperformance.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Raleigh’s economy exhibits strong underlying momentum, supported by robust labor demand and healthy wage growth. The city’s labor market is tight, with an unemployment rate of 3.0%—up 0.2 percentage points year-on-year—indicating near-full employment conditions. Labor demand remains solid, as reflected in a labor demand composite score of 6.28 and nonfarm employment growth of 1.25% over the past year. Average hourly earnings rose 5.28% year-on-year, outpacing inflation and supporting household purchasing power. However, labor force growth is modest at 0.53% annually, suggesting constraints on supply-side expansion despite strong job creation. The weekly hours deviation of +0.226% indicates stable workloads, with no signs of broad-based underutilization or overextension.

Housing market dynamics reveal a supply response to sustained demand, with residential building permits surging 38.8% year-on-year—a significant acceleration that ranks among the strongest nationally. Yet, despite this construction boom, housing affordability remains a relative strength, with a cost of living composite score of 1.41, reflecting a favorable balance between home prices and earnings. The office market shows particular resilience, evidenced by an office worker ratio composite score of 4.6 (98th percentile), suggesting higher-than-average in-person workforce presence compared to peer metros. However, homes are taking longer to sell—46 days on average, up 4.55% year-on-year—indicating a slight cooling in transaction velocity, though inventory conditions remain manageable.

**Outlook:** Raleigh is well-positioned for continued economic expansion, underpinned by strong labor demand, rising construction activity, and relative affordability. Near-term risks include constrained labor supply and potential oversupply if migration trends moderate. Absent a sharp downturn in migration or tech sector retrenchment, the city’s fundamentals support sustained outperformance.

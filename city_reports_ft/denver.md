# Denver — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Denver's economic indicators paint a nuanced picture, with both encouraging and cautionary signs. The labour market, for instance, has seen its unemployment rate decline by 1 percentage point over the past year to 3.6%. This decrease suggests a relatively healthy job market, although the 1.5% year-over-year contraction in the civilian labour force may indicate a shortage of skilled workers or declining labour force participation. Meanwhile, average hourly earnings have risen by 6.26% year-over-year, outpacing the national average and indicating strong labour demand.

In terms of business activity, Denver's office worker ratio composite score stands at 2.72, pointing to a moderate level of office employment. The labour demand composite score of 4.56 suggests a stable, albeit unremarkable, labour market. Nonfarm payroll employment has grown by a modest 0.13% year-over-year. On the housing front, a 15.96% year-over-year decline in residential building permits may exacerbate housing supply shortages and upward price pressure. The median days on market for homes has increased by 8.57% year-over-year to 38 days, potentially signalling a slowing housing market.

Despite these mixed signals, Denver's economy remains relatively robust, with a cost of living composite score of 1.37 and an above-average grade of B+. The city's highly skilled workforce and strong average hourly earnings growth are likely to continue supporting economic activity in the long term. However, the decline in residential building permits and slowing labour market growth rate warrant close monitoring as potential stress points. Overall, Denver's economy is poised to continue growing at a moderate pace, driven by its strong labour market and business activity.

Looking ahead, Denver's economy is expected to maintain its above-average performance, driven by its skilled workforce and strong average hourly earnings growth. Close monitoring of the housing market and labour market growth rate will be necessary to prevent these areas from becoming major stress points. With its diverse economy and highly skilled workforce, Denver is well-positioned to adapt to changing economic conditions and sustain long-term growth.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Denver's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, having decreased by 1 percentage point over the past year. This decline is a favourable sign, suggesting a relatively healthy job market. However, the civilian labour force has contracted by 1.5% year-over-year, which may indicate a shortage of skilled workers or a decline in labour force participation. Average hourly earnings have grown by 6.26% year-over-year, outpacing the national average and suggesting a strong demand for labour.

In terms of business activity, Denver's office worker ratio composite score is 2.72, indicating a moderate level of office employment. The labour demand composite score is 4.56, suggesting a stable but not particularly robust labour market. The city's nonfarm payroll employment has grown by 0.13% year-over-year, a relatively slow pace. On the housing front, residential building permits have declined by 15.96% year-over-year, which may contribute to a shortage of housing supply and upward pressure on prices. The median days on market for homes has increased by 8.57% year-over-year, reaching 38 days, which may indicate a slowing housing market.

Despite these mixed signals, Denver's economy remains relatively strong, with a cost of living composite score of 1.37 and an above-average grade of B+. The city's strengths, including its highly skilled workforce and strong average hourly earnings growth, are likely to continue supporting economic activity in the long term. However, the decline in residential building permits and the slowing labour market growth rate are potential stress points that warrant monitoring. Overall, Denver's economy is likely to continue growing, albeit at a moderate pace, driven by its strong labour market and business activity.

Closing outlook: Denver's economy is expected to maintain its above-average performance, driven by its skilled workforce and strong average hourly earnings growth. However, the city's housing market and labour market growth rate will require close monitoring to ensure that they do not become major stress points. With its diverse economy and highly skilled workforce, Denver is well-positioned to adapt to changing economic conditions and continue growing in the long term.

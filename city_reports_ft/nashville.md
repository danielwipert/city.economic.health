# Nashville — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Nashville's labour market remains robust, with a 3.0% unemployment rate unchanged from last year and a 3.4% annual expansion in the civilian labour force, ranking in the 98th percentile nationally. This strong workforce supply growth is met with solid labour demand, as evidenced by 1.0% year-on-year nonfarm employment growth and a composite labour demand score in the 80th percentile. Average hourly earnings have risen 3.6% over the past year, although this growth trails top-tier markets, placing earnings momentum in the 40th percentile. The stability of labour demand is further supported by a 0.6% increase in weekly hours worked above the city's 12-month trend.

In contrast, the housing market is showing signs of moderation. Residential building permits have declined 8.9% year-on-year, in line with the national median, while the average time to sell a home has risen 12.8% to 53 days. This suggests softening demand or rising inventory. The city's cost of living, with a composite score of 3.07, remains below average affordability, ranking in the 38th percentile, due to persistent price pressures. However, the office market is relatively strong, with a score in the 80th percentile, indicating continued demand for commercial space, likely driven by expansion in professional and business services.

Looking ahead, Nashville's economic fundamentals remain resilient, driven by labour force expansion and steady employment growth. Nevertheless, headwinds in housing construction and elevated living costs pose risks to long-term inclusivity and workforce attraction. If housing supply does not rebound, affordability constraints could erode the city's competitive edge.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Nashville’s labour market remains robust, underpinned by a 3.0% unemployment rate—unchanged year-on-year—and a civilian labour force expanding at 3.4% annually, a rate in the 98th percentile nationally, signalling strong workforce supply growth. Labour demand is solid, with a nonfarm employment growth of 1.0% year-on-year and a composite labour demand score in the 80th percentile, reflecting sustained hiring activity. Average hourly earnings rose 3.6% over the past year, though this growth lags behind top-tier markets, placing earnings momentum in the 40th percentile. Weekly hours worked are slightly above the city’s 12-month trend (+0.6%), suggesting stable near-term demand for labour.

Housing market dynamics point to moderating conditions. Residential building permits declined 8.9% year-on-year, near the national median, while homes are taking longer to sell—53 days on average, up 12.8% from a year ago—indicating softening demand or rising inventory. The cost of living relative to earnings, at a composite score of 3.07, is below average affordability (38th percentile), reflecting persistent price pressures. Office market signals are relatively strong (80th percentile), suggesting continued demand for commercial space, likely supported by professional and business services expansion.

**Outlook:** Nashville’s economic fundamentals remain resilient, driven by labour force expansion and steady employment growth. However, housing construction headwinds and elevated living costs pose risks to long-term inclusivity and workforce attraction. Absent a rebound in housing supply, affordability constraints could dampen the city’s competitive edge.

# San Francisco — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

San Francisco's labour market is displaying modest resilience in the face of broader economic headwinds. With an unemployment rate of 4.1%, up 0.1 percentage points year-on-year, the job market is tightening but not deteriorating. However, labour demand, as measured by the composite index of 3.18, remains weak, with nonfarm payrolls contracting 0.52% year-on-year. This suggests that job creation is lagging national trends. Average hourly earnings have risen 2.33% year-on-year, below the inflation-moderated wage growth seen in other leading tech hubs. The civilian labour force has expanded by just 0.43% year-on-year, indicating limited workforce supply growth. Meanwhile, weekly hours worked are marginally above the 12-month baseline, with a 0.396% deviation, hinting at stable utilisation despite softer employment trends.

In contrast, the housing market presents a mixed but cautiously improving picture. Residential construction has surged, with building permits up 86% year-on-year, the strongest growth among peer metros. This sustained supply-side response to affordability pressures is a positive sign. However, affordability remains a significant concern, with a cost-of-living composite score of 5.77, placing severe pressure on middle- and lower-income households. The office sector continues to underperform, with a low office worker ratio score of 2.22, reflecting the persistent adoption of hybrid work models. Nevertheless, housing market velocity has accelerated sharply, with homes selling in a median 29 days, down 6.45% from a year ago. This reflects both improved demand dynamics and historically thin inventory.

Looking ahead, San Francisco's economy remains in transition, balancing structural challenges in employment and office use against a rebound in housing construction. While near-term labour demand is subdued, the increase in building permits may help alleviate long-term housing constraints. The city's fiscal and policy resilience will be tested as it navigates a prolonged adjustment in commercial real estate and workforce composition.

The metro area’s unemployment rate stands at 4.1% (101A_unemployment), up slightly by 0.1 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), reflecting a tightening but not deteriorating job market. Labour demand, as captured by the composite index (107E_ldc_composite: 3.18), remains weak—evidenced by a 0.52% year-on-year contraction in nonfarm payrolls (107E_employment_growth_yoy)—suggesting that job creation lags national trends. Average hourly earnings rose 2.33% (103B_earnings_yoy), below inflation-moderated wage growth seen in leading tech hubs, while the civilian labour force expanded by just 0.43% (102A_clf_yoy), indicating limited workforce supply growth. Weekly hours worked are marginally above the 12-month baseline (0.396% deviation), hinting at stable utilisation despite softer employment trends.

Housing market dynamics present a mixed but cautiously improving picture. Residential construction has surged, with building permits up 86% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), the strongest growth among peer metros and a sign of sustained supply-side response to affordability pressures. However, affordability remains deeply strained, with a cost-of-living composite score of 5.77 (104C_col), placing severe pressure on middle- and lower-income households. The office sector continues to underperform, reflected in a low office worker ratio score of 2.22 (105C_owr), consistent with persistent hybrid work adoption. Meanwhile, housing market velocity has accelerated sharply: homes sell in a median 29 days (204A_dom_level_days), down 6.45% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), though this reflects both improved demand dynamics and historically thin inventory.

**Outlook:** San Francisco’s economy remains in transition, balancing structural challenges in employment and office use against a rebound in housing construction. Near-term labour demand is subdued, but rising permits may alleviate long-term housing constraints. The city’s fiscal and policy resilience will be tested as it navigates a prolonged adjustment in commercial real estate and workforce composition.

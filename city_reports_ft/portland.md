Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro's economic landscape presents a complex mix of resilience and vulnerability. The metropolitan area's unemployment rate stands at 5.3%, slightly above the national average, placing it in the 10th percentile. This suggests a relatively weak labor market compared to other metropolitan areas. In contrast, the labor force participation rate is robust at 66.84%, ranking in the 74th percentile, indicating a strong engagement of the working-age population in the economy.

Building permits have declined by 20.27% year-over-year, in the 28th percentile, potentially indicating a slowdown in new construction and a shift in demand dynamics within the housing sector.

The median days on market for homes has risen to 71 days, in the 12th percentile, suggesting a less active market and possibly softer price pressures. Business activity, as measured by the weekly hours worked, remains stable at 34.3 hours, in the 56th percentile. However, the office vacancy rate has increased by 1.78% year-over-year, in the 26th percentile, hinting at a potential oversupply in commercial real estate.

Portland faces challenges in sustaining employment growth and wage increases, which are crucial for long-term economic health. The cooling housing market and declining construction activity could dampen economic momentum unless offset by other sectors. However, the high labor force participation rate and stable business activity provide a foundation for resilience. Policymakers will need to focus on fostering job creation and addressing structural issues in the housing market to ensure sustainable economic growth.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Portland-Vancouver-Hillsboro's economic landscape presents a mixed picture, with both resilient and vulnerable aspects evident across key indicators. The unemployment rate stands at 5.3%, slightly above the national average, placing it in the 10th percentile, which suggests a relatively weak labor market compared to other metropolitan areas. However, the labor force participation rate is robust at 66.84%, ranking in the 74th percentile, indicating a strong engagement of the working-age population in the economy. Earnings growth has been modest, increasing by 1.43% year-over-year, which is in the 14th percentile, reflecting a slower pace of wage gains that may constrain consumer spending and economic dynamism.

The housing market shows signs of cooling, with a year-over-year increase in home prices of just 0.24%, placing it in the 22nd percentile, and a significant decline in building permits by 20.27% year-over-year, in the 28th percentile. This trend could indicate a slowdown in new construction and potentially a shift in demand dynamics within the housing sector. The median days on market for homes has risen to 71 days, in the 12th percentile, suggesting a less active market and possibly softer price pressures. Business activity, as measured by the weekly hours worked, remains stable at 34.3 hours, in the 56th percentile, but the office vacancy rate has increased by 1.78% year-over-year, in the 26th percentile, hinting at a potential oversupply in commercial real estate.

Looking ahead, Portland faces challenges in sustaining employment growth and wage increases, which are critical for long-term economic health. The cooling housing market and declining construction activity could dampen economic momentum unless offset by other sectors. However, the high labor force participation rate and stable business activity provide a foundation for resilience. Policymakers will need to focus on fostering job creation and addressing structural issues in the housing market to ensure sustainable economic growth.

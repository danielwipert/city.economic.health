## Polished FT-style column

Here's the revised column:

Chicago's economy is navigating a complex landscape, with steady performance in some areas and emerging challenges. The city's unemployment rate stands at 4.6%, marginally above the national average, indicating a tight labor market. The labor force participation rate of 65.11% is robust, reflecting a high level of engagement among working-age individuals. However, earnings growth of 5.12% year-over-year is outpaced by the 4.97% increase in living costs, potentially eroding real wage gains. The low weekly hours worked (33.3 hours), ranking in the 8th percentile, may signal underemployment or a shift towards part-time work.

The housing market is experiencing moderate growth, with year-over-year home price increases of 5.89% supported by strong demand. Building permits have risen by 11.46% year-over-year, a positive indicator of business activity. However, a decline in single-family home sales (-0.16%) and a relatively high days on market (38 days) suggest some cooling in the market, possibly due to affordability constraints. The city's overall economic grade of "B" and a weighted percentile of 50.4% suggest an average performance, with room for improvement in several key areas.

Looking ahead, Chicago's economy is likely to continue its moderate growth trajectory, driven by a strong labor market and steady housing demand. However, policymakers will need to address the challenges of underemployment and rising living costs to ensure sustainable economic health and broader prosperity.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Chicago's economic landscape, as of the latest data, reflects a mix of steady performance and emerging challenges. The unemployment rate stands at 4.6%, slightly above the national average, indicating a relatively tight labor market. The labor force participation rate of 65.11% is robust, suggesting a strong engagement of the working-age population. Earnings growth of 5.12% year-over-year is positive, though it is outpaced by the cost of living increase of 4.97%, which may erode real wage gains. The weekly hours worked (33.3 hours) are notably low, ranking in the 8th percentile, which could signal underemployment or a shift towards part-time work.

In the housing market, the year-over-year increase in home prices of 5.89% is moderate, supported by a strong demand as evidenced by the 11.46% year-over-year rise in building permits. However, the slight decline in the number of single-family homes sold (-0.16%) and the relatively high days on market (38 days) suggest some cooling in the market, possibly due to affordability constraints. Business activity, as indicated by the number of building permits, is expanding, which is a positive sign for future economic growth. However, the city's overall economic grade of "B" and a weighted percentile of 50.4% suggest an average performance, with room for improvement in several key areas.

Looking ahead, Chicago's economy is poised to continue its moderate growth trajectory, supported by a strong labor market and steady housing demand. However, policymakers will need to address the challenges of underemployment and rising living costs to ensure sustainable economic health and broader prosperity.

# Las Vegas — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Las Vegas's economy is exhibiting a mixed but cautiously stabilising profile, marked by a notable divergence between labour supply growth and employment demand. The civilian labour force has expanded 3.24% year-on-year, driven by strong in-migration and re-entry into the job market, placing it in the 96th percentile. In contrast, nonfarm payrolls have contracted 0.76% over the same period, with labour demand ranking in the 10th percentile. Despite this disparity, average hourly earnings have risen 6.49% year-on-year, suggesting persistent wage pressures, likely driven by competition in the service sector and inflation pass-through. The unemployment rate has fallen to 5.2%, a decline of 0.7 percentage points from a year earlier, although the composite unemployment score of 5.62 remains below the median US performance, ranking in the 32nd percentile.

In the housing market, indicators point to softening demand and elevated inventory levels. Residential building permits have fallen 9.83% year-on-year, while the median days on market has increased 18.18% to 52 days, ranking in the 74th percentile. The cost of living, with a composite score of 2.19, is moderately above average but not at stress levels. Office demand remains subdued, with a score of 2.58, consistent with limited corporate expansion. A slight uptick in weekly hours worked, 0.27% above the 12-month baseline, offers a marginal positive signal amidst otherwise subdued business activity.

Looking ahead, Las Vegas faces a period of structural adjustment as workforce growth outpaces job creation. A near-term rebalancing of the housing and labour markets is likely, with wage growth providing support to consumption. The city's economic trajectory will depend on its ability to diversify beyond tourism and construction into higher-value services.

Las Vegas’s economy exhibits a mixed but cautiously stabilising profile, with notable divergence between labour supply growth and employment demand. The civilian labour force expanded 3.24% year-on-year (102A_clf_yoy, 96th percentile), reflecting strong in-migration or re-entry into job markets. This robust workforce growth contrasts with a 0.76% contraction in nonfarm payrolls (107E_employment_growth_yoy), placing labour demand in the 10th percentile (107E_ldc_composite). Despite this, average hourly earnings rose 6.49% (103B_earnings_yoy, 76th percentile), suggesting wage pressures persist, likely driven by service-sector competition and inflation pass-through. The unemployment rate stands at 5.2%, down 0.7 percentage points from a year earlier (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), though the 5.62 composite unemployment score remains below median US performance (32nd percentile).

Housing market indicators point to softening demand and elevated inventory. Residential building permits fell 9.83% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), while homes remained on the market for a median of 52 days—up 18.18% from the prior year (204A_dom_yoy_pct)—a level that ranks relatively high (74th percentile in days on market composite). The cost of living, at a composite score of 2.19 (104C_col), is moderately above average but not at stress levels. Office demand remains tepid (105C_owr: 2.58), consistent with limited corporate footprint expansion. The slight uptick in weekly hours worked (0.27% above 12-month baseline) offers a marginal positive signal within otherwise subdued business activity.

**Outlook:** Las Vegas faces structural adjustment as workforce growth outpaces job creation. Near-term rebalancing is likely in housing and labour markets, with wage growth providing support to consumption. The trajectory hinges on diversification beyond tourism and construction into higher-value services.

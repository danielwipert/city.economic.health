# Cleveland — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Cleveland's labour market is experiencing modest expansion, with nonfarm employment increasing 0.57% year-on-year and an unemployment rate of 3.4%, below the national average. However, a 0.4 percentage point rise in the unemployment rate suggests some softening. The 1.8% growth in the civilian labour force over the past year indicates strong workforce supply growth, ranking in the 82nd percentile, which may reflect underlying confidence or demographic inflows.

In contrast, average hourly earnings have fallen 1.59% in real terms year-on-year, ranking in the 0th percentile, potentially due to weak wage pressures or sectoral composition. Labour demand, as measured by the composite index, remains stable at 5.27, with a 58th percentile ranking, supported by a slight 0.168% increase in weekly hours worked above the 12-month baseline.

The housing market is showing signs of a gradual slowdown, with median days on market increasing 7.8% year-on-year to 55 days, indicating softer demand and a 38th percentile ranking. Nevertheless, residential building permits have risen 11.14% year-on-year, with a 68th percentile ranking, suggesting that builders remain confident in medium-term occupancy. The office market is relatively resilient, with an office worker ratio composite of 2.98, ranking in the 64th percentile, although this does not indicate strong growth.

Looking ahead, Cleveland's economy is expected to maintain a stable but unremarkable trajectory. While labour supply growth and construction activity provide a solid foundation, wage stagnation and slowing housing turnover pose constraints. Without productivity gains or sectoral upgrading, the city is likely to experience modest growth, leaving it vulnerable to broader manufacturing or interest rate shocks.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Cleveland’s labour market shows modest expansion, with nonfarm employment growing 0.57% year-on-year and unemployment at 3.4%—below the national average—though the 0.4 percentage point increase in the unemployment rate signals some softening. The civilian labour force has grown 1.8% over the past year, a strong signal of workforce supply growth (82nd percentile), suggesting underlying confidence or demographic inflows. However, average hourly earnings have declined in real terms, falling 1.59% year-on-year (0th percentile), which may reflect weak wage pressures or sectoral composition. Labour demand, as captured by the composite index (5.27, 58th percentile), is stable, supported by a slight uptick in weekly hours worked (0.168% above 12-month baseline), pointing to cautious employer optimism.

Housing market dynamics indicate a gradual slowdown in turnover. Median days on market stands at 55 days, up 7.8% year-on-year, reflecting softer demand (38th percentile). Despite this, residential building permits have risen 11.14% year-on-year (68th percentile), suggesting builders remain confident in medium-term occupancy. Office market activity, as proxied by the office worker ratio composite (2.98, 64th percentile), shows relative resilience compared to peer industrial metros, though not indicative of strong growth. The absence of a cost-of-living composite score precludes definitive conclusions on affordability, but stagnant earnings imply limited household purchasing power gains.

**Outlook:** Cleveland’s economy is on a stable but unspectacular trajectory. Labour supply growth and construction activity provide foundational strength, but wage stagnation and slowing housing turnover pose constraints. Absent productivity or sectoral upgrading, the city is likely to maintain modest growth, vulnerable to broader manufacturing or interest rate shocks.

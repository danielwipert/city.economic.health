## Polished FT-style column

Here's the revised column:

Oklahoma City's economy is marked by a strong short-term performance and a stable long-term foundation. The city's unemployment rate of 3.3% places it in the 92nd percentile among metropolitan areas, underscoring a tight labor market. Labour force participation stands at 66.29%, a solid figure that nonetheless leaves room for improvement, ranking in the 72nd percentile. Earnings growth of 6.15% year-over-year reflects healthy wage increases that could support consumer spending. However, moderate inflationary pressures are evident in the 4.68% cost of living index and a 2.32% over-the-week rate, which may erode purchasing power if wages do not keep pace.

The housing market in Oklahoma City is experiencing significant appreciation, with home prices rising 15.01% year-over-year, ranking in the 68th percentile. The relatively low 56 days on market and strong buyer interest suggest a competitive market. Meanwhile, construction activity remains positive, with building permits up 10.49% year-over-year, albeit slightly below the median, indicating a cautious approach to new development. Business activity, as measured by weekly hours worked, is robust at 35.4 hours, placing the city in the 90th percentile, and suggesting strong demand for labor and potentially high productivity levels.

Looking ahead, Oklahoma City's economy appears well-positioned to sustain its growth trajectory, driven by a strong labor market and a resilient housing sector. However, the city will need to manage inflationary pressures and ensure that wage growth keeps pace with rising costs to maintain consumer confidence and economic stability. The ongoing strength in construction and business activity also bodes well for sustaining economic momentum in the medium term.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Oklahoma City's economy exhibits a mix of robust short-term performance and stable long-term fundamentals. The unemployment rate stands at 3.3%, placing it in the 92nd percentile among metropolitan areas, indicating a tight labor market. Labour force participation is at 66.29%, which, while solid, suggests some room for improvement as it ranks in the 72nd percentile. Earnings growth year-over-year is strong at 6.15%, reflecting healthy wage increases that could support consumer spending. However, the cost of living index at 4.68% and the over-the-week rate at 2.32% indicate moderate inflationary pressures, which may erode purchasing power if wages do not keep pace.

The housing market in Oklahoma City is experiencing significant appreciation, with home prices increasing by 15.01% year-over-year, ranking in the 68th percentile. This rapid growth, coupled with a relatively low 56 days on market, suggests a competitive market with strong buyer interest. Construction activity remains positive, with building permits up 10.49% year-over-year, although this growth is slightly below the median, indicating a cautious approach to new development. Business activity, as measured by weekly hours worked, is robust at 35.4 hours, placing the city in the 90th percentile, suggesting strong demand for labor and potentially high productivity levels.

Looking ahead, Oklahoma City's economy appears well-positioned to continue its growth trajectory, supported by a strong labor market and resilient housing sector. However, the city will need to manage inflationary pressures and ensure that wage growth keeps pace with rising costs to maintain consumer confidence and economic stability. The ongoing strength in construction and business activity also bodes well for sustaining economic momentum in the medium term.

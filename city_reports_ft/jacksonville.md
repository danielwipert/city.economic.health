# Jacksonville — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Jacksonville's labour market is sending mixed signals, with robust wage growth offset by rising unemployment and modest employment expansion. The city's unemployment rate has increased to 4.6%, up 1.4 percentage points year-on-year, indicating a loosening labour market that may be due to slowing demand or sector-specific adjustments. However, average hourly earnings have risen 7.5% over the past year, ranking in the 86th percentile nationally and suggesting sustained wage pressure driven by competition for skilled workers. Labour demand remains moderately strong, with a 0.68% year-on-year increase in nonfarm payrolls and weekly hours slightly above the 12-month baseline. The civilian labour force has expanded 0.79% year-on-year, indicating steady workforce supply growth without significant participation surges.

In contrast, the city's housing and business activity point to a cooling construction sector and constrained market liquidity. Residential building permits have fallen 32.7% year-on-year, the weakest performance in this dataset, likely due to higher financing costs and softer demand. The housing market is also slowing, with homes taking a median of 58 days to sell, up 1.75% from a year ago. Jacksonville's cost of living remains relatively low, which enhances household purchasing power. However, the city's office worker ratio suggests limited concentration in high-value professional services compared to leading metro economies.

Looking ahead, Jacksonville's economy is navigating a transition marked by resilient wage growth and labour demand, but tempered by housing sector retrenchment and rising unemployment. Near-term challenges in construction and real estate may persist amid elevated interest rates. The city's broader economic trajectory will depend on its ability to diversify beyond low-cost advantages into higher-productivity sectors.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Jacksonville’s labour market shows mixed signals, with underlying strength in wage growth offset by rising unemployment and modest employment expansion. The city’s unemployment rate stands at 4.6% (101A_unemployment), up 1.4 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), suggesting a loosening labour market, possibly due to slowing demand or sector-specific adjustments. However, average hourly earnings have grown robustly, rising 7.5% over the past year (103B_earnings_yoy), a figure that ranks in the 86th percentile nationally, indicating sustained wage pressure likely driven by competition for skilled workers. Labour demand, as reflected in the composite index (107E_ldc_composite = 5.5), is moderately strong, supported by a 0.68% year-on-year increase in nonfarm payrolls (107E_employment_growth_yoy) and weekly hours slightly above the 12-month baseline (0.25% deviation). The civilian labour force has expanded modestly, up 0.79% year-on-year (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting steady workforce supply growth without significant participation surges.

Housing and business activity point to a cooling construction sector and constrained market liquidity. Residential building permits have plummeted, down 32.7% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), the weakest performance in this dataset (score: 8), signaling developer caution likely due to higher financing costs and softer demand. The housing market is also slowing, with homes taking a median of 58 days to sell (204A_dom_level_days), up 1.75% from a year ago, placing Jacksonville in the 28th percentile for market speed (204A_dom_composite = 7.02). Meanwhile, Jacksonville’s cost of living remains relatively low (104C_col = 1.35, score: 81), enhancing household purchasing power, though its office worker ratio (105C_owr = 2.4, score: 48) suggests limited concentration in high-value professional services compared to leading metro economies.

**Outlook:** Jacksonville’s economy is navigating a transition marked by resilient wage growth and labour demand, but tempered by housing sector retrenchment and rising unemployment. Near-term headwinds in construction and real estate may persist amid elevated interest rates, while the broader economic trajectory will depend on diversification beyond low-cost advantages into higher-productivity sectors.

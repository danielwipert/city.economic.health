## Polished FT-style column

Miami's economy is marked by a paradox: rapid short-term growth in some sectors, but underlying challenges that threaten long-term sustainability. The labour market in the Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metro area is a bright spot, with an unemployment rate of 3.7%, placing it in the 76th percentile nationally. This suggests a strong job market, but the labour force participation rate of 62.23% is in the 26th percentile, indicating potential underutilization of the workforce.

Wage growth is robust, with earnings increasing by 8.0% year-over-year, ranking in the 86th percentile. However, the cost of living has risen by 5.67%, potentially eroding the benefits of higher wages for many residents.

The housing market presents a mixed picture. Building permits have surged by 76.97% year-over-year, placing Miami in the 94th percentile, indicating a robust construction sector. Yet, home prices have declined by 1.69% over the same period, while rental prices have dropped by 3.51% and the average days on market have risen to 86 days, suggesting a cooling in the once overheated real estate market.

Business activity remains strong, as evidenced by the high number of building permits. However, the cooling housing market and rising cost of living pose significant stress points that could impact consumer spending and investment.

Looking ahead, Miami's economy is likely to benefit from its status as a major international hub and its appeal to both domestic and foreign investors. However, addressing the labour force participation rate and managing the cost of living will be crucial for sustaining long-term economic health. The resilience of the construction sector and the need for affordable housing solutions will also play key roles in shaping the city's economic trajectory.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Miami's economic landscape is characterized by robust short-term growth in certain sectors, juxtaposed with underlying challenges that may affect long-term sustainability. The labor market in the Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metro area shows a relatively low unemployment rate of 3.7%, placing it in the 76th percentile nationally, which suggests a strong job market. However, the labor force participation rate of 62.23% is in the 26th percentile, indicating potential underutilization of the workforce. Earnings have grown by 8.0% year-over-year, a significant increase that ranks in the 86th percentile, reflecting strong wage growth. Despite this, cost of living has risen by 5.67%, which could erode the benefits of higher wages for many residents.

The housing market presents a mixed picture. While building permits have surged by 76.97% year-over-year, placing Miami in the 94th percentile, suggesting a robust construction sector, home prices have declined by 1.69% over the same period. This decline, coupled with a 3.51% drop in rental prices and an increase in days on market to 86 days, indicates a cooling in the once overheated real estate market. Business activity, as evidenced by the high number of building permits, remains strong, but the cooling housing market and rising cost of living present significant stress points that could impact consumer spending and investment.

Looking ahead, Miami's economy will likely continue to benefit from its status as a major international hub and its appeal to both domestic and foreign investors. However, addressing the labor force participation rate and managing the cost of living will be crucial for sustaining long-term economic health. The resilience of the construction sector and the need for affordable housing solutions will also play key roles in shaping the city's economic trajectory.

# Pittsburgh — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Pittsburgh's labour market is showing resilience in the face of modest cyclical headwinds, with an unemployment rate of 3.6%, up 0.3 percentage points year-on-year. Although this represents a slight softening, the rate remains below the national average. Labour demand is robust, as evidenced by a strong labour demand composite score of 6.96 and nonfarm employment growth of 1.07% year-on-year, outpacing the median US metro performance. Average hourly earnings are rising at an annual rate of 8.01%, placing Pittsburgh in the 92nd percentile nationally, driven by tightness in certain skill segments and sustained wage pressure. The civilian labour force has expanded 0.73% over the past year, indicating modest workforce supply growth, albeit at a pace insufficient to offset broader demographic constraints.

In contrast, the city's housing and commercial real estate markets are exhibiting divergent trends. Residential building permits have declined 10.55% year-on-year, suggesting waning builder confidence, likely due to higher financing costs and limited developable land. The median home is taking 65 days to sell, up 4.84% from a year ago, with a composite days-on-market score in the 20th percentile, indicating a significant slowdown in turnover. However, Pittsburgh's cost of living remains a relative strength, with a composite score of 1.0, among the lowest in peer metros, making it an attractive destination for in-migration. On the other hand, office demand is robust, with an office worker ratio composite score of 3.46, in the 82nd percentile, reflecting above-average utilisation, likely driven by the city's growing tech and healthcare sectors.

Looking ahead, Pittsburgh is navigating a transition from post-industrial renewal to a knowledge-driven economy, leveraging its structural advantages in affordability and talent retention. While near-term housing supply constraints and modest labour force growth pose limitations, strong wage gains and resilient employment demand suggest underlying economic momentum. Barring external shocks, the city is well-positioned for steady, moderate expansion.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Pittsburgh’s labour market shows resilience amid modest cyclical headwinds. The city’s unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, up 0.3 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), suggesting a slight softening, though still below the national average. Labour demand remains robust, with a strong labour demand composite score of 6.96 (107E_ldc_composite) and nonfarm employment growing 1.07% year-on-year—well above median US metro performance. Average hourly earnings are rising at 8.01% annually (103B_earnings_yoy), placing Pittsburgh in the 92nd percentile nationally, reflecting tightness in certain skill segments and sustained wage pressure. The civilian labour force expanded 0.73% over the past year (102A_clf_yoy), indicating modest workforce supply growth, but not at a pace that offsets broader demographic constraints.

Housing and commercial real estate present contrasting dynamics. Residential building permits have declined 10.55% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), signaling waning builder confidence, likely due to higher financing costs and limited developable land. The median home remains on the market for 65 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 4.84% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), with a composite days-on-market score in the 20th percentile—indicating a material slowdown in turnover. Yet affordability remains a relative strength: Pittsburgh’s cost of living composite score is 1.0 (104C_col), among the lowest in peer metros, enhancing its attractiveness for in-migration. Office demand, as proxied by the office worker ratio composite (105C_owr) of 3.46 (82nd percentile), reflects above-average utilisation, likely supported by the city’s growing tech and healthcare sectors.

**Outlook:** Pittsburgh is navigating a transition from post-industrial renewal to a knowledge-driven economy with structural advantages in affordability and talent retention. Near-term housing supply constraints and modest labour force growth pose limitations, but strong wage gains and resilient employment demand suggest underlying economic momentum. Absent external shocks, the city is positioned for steady, moderate expansion.

# San Diego — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

San Diego's economic indicators paint a nuanced picture, with a mix of positive and negative trends. The labor market is relatively stable, with an unemployment rate of 4.4% and a modest 0.2 percentage point increase year-over-year. However, the growth in the civilian labor force has been sluggish, at 1.66% year-over-year. Average hourly earnings have risen by 2.83% over the same period, suggesting some upward pressure on wages.

The housing market is showing signs of stress, with a high cost of living composite score of 5.2 and a 0.37% year-over-year decline in residential building permits. The median days on market have increased by 5.56% to 38 days, indicating a slight slowdown. In contrast, the office worker ratio composite score of 1.62 points to strong demand for office space. Labor demand is moderate, with a composite score of 4.03 and a 0.35% year-over-year increase in nonfarm payroll employment. However, the weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is -0.758%, which may indicate underlying weakness in the labor market.

Despite these challenges, San Diego's business activity appears resilient, driven by moderate labor demand and a strong office market. Nevertheless, the high cost of living and slow labor force growth pose significant challenges for businesses and residents. The decline in residential building permits and increase in median days on market also suggest stress in the housing market. Overall, San Diego's economy is experiencing moderate growth, with areas of both strength and weakness.

Looking ahead, San Diego's economic prospects are likely to remain modest, driven by a stable labor market and moderate labor demand. However, policymakers and businesses will need to address the city's high cost of living and slow labor force growth to sustain long-term economic growth and competitiveness.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

San Diego's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labor market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 4.4%, with a modest year-over-year increase of 0.2 percentage points. While this suggests a relatively stable job market, the growth in the civilian labor force has been sluggish, with a 1.66% year-over-year change. Average hourly earnings, however, have seen a respectable 2.83% year-over-year increase, indicating some upward pressure on wages.

The housing market in San Diego appears to be experiencing some stress, with a high cost of living composite score of 5.2 and a decline in residential building permits of 0.37% year-over-year. The median days on market have increased by 5.56% year-over-year, reaching 38 days, which may indicate a slight slowdown in the market. On the other hand, the office worker ratio composite score is 1.62, suggesting a relatively strong demand for office space. The labor demand composite score of 4.03, combined with a 0.35% year-over-year increase in nonfarm payroll employment, points to a moderate level of labor demand. However, the weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is -0.758%, which may indicate some underlying weakness in the labor market.

The city's business activity, as reflected in the labor demand composite score and office worker ratio, suggests some resilience. However, the high cost of living and relatively slow growth in the labor force may pose challenges for businesses and residents alike. The decline in residential building permits and increase in median days on market may also indicate some stress in the housing market. Overall, San Diego's economy appears to be navigating a period of moderate growth, with some areas of strength and weakness.

Closing outlook: San Diego's economic prospects are likely to remain modest in the near term, driven by a stable labor market and moderate labor demand. However, the city's high cost of living and slow labor force growth may continue to pose challenges. Policymakers and businesses may need to focus on addressing these issues to sustain long-term economic growth and competitiveness.

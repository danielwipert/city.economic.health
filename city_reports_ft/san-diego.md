## Polished FT-style column

San Diego's economy faces a complex mix of challenges and opportunities that will shape its long-term trajectory. The city's unemployment rate stands at 5.0%, marginally above the national average, indicating moderate job market stress. Labour force participation, at 62.1%, suggests a less engaged workforce compared to more robust economies.

Earnings growth has been modest, at 2.04% year-over-year, lagging behind inflation rates and eroding purchasing power and consumer confidence. The cost of living index, at 7.29, reflects higher living costs that can deter both residents and businesses from settling in the area.

The housing market is showing signs of a cooling trend, with a 0.67% year-over-year decline in home prices and a 3.13% decrease in price per square foot. This shift could indicate a change in buyer sentiment or an oversupply of properties. However, the surge in building permits, up 61.67% year-over-year, suggests continued investment in new construction and potential future growth in the housing sector. The average time to sell a home, 47 days, indicates a notable slowdown in transactional activity that may dampen investor enthusiasm.

The business environment in San Diego is mixed, with strong indicators in new construction but weaker performance in earnings growth and employment metrics. The city's overall economic grade of "C" with a weighted percentile of 24.2% underscores the need for diversified economic strategies to enhance resilience. To address these challenges, the focus should be on improving earnings growth to support consumer spending and addressing the cost of living in the short term. In the longer term, fostering innovation and attracting high-value industries could help stabilize and grow the local economy.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

San Diego's economy, while showing resilience in some areas, faces significant challenges that could impact its long-term trajectory. The unemployment rate stands at 5.0%, slightly above the national average, indicating a moderate level of job market stress. Labour force participation is at 62.1%, which, while not alarming, suggests a less engaged workforce compared to more robust economies. Earnings growth year-over-year is modest at 2.04%, lagging behind inflation rates, which erodes purchasing power and consumer confidence. The cost of living index at 7.29 reflects higher living costs, which can deter both residents and businesses from settling in the area.

In the housing market, the year-over-year change in home prices has declined by 0.67%, signaling a cooling market after years of rapid appreciation. This trend is supported by a 3.13% decrease in the price per square foot, which could indicate a shift in buyer sentiment or an oversupply of properties. Despite this, the number of building permits issued has surged by 61.67% year-over-year, suggesting continued investment in new construction and potential future growth in the housing sector. However, with homes taking an average of 47 days to sell, there is a notable slowdown in transactional activity, which may dampen investor enthusiasm.

The business environment in San Diego is mixed, with strong indicators in new construction but weaker performance in earnings growth and employment metrics. The city's overall economic grade of "C" with a weighted percentile of 24.2% underscores the need for diversified economic strategies to enhance resilience. In the short term, the focus should be on addressing the cost of living and improving earnings growth to support consumer spending. Longer-term, fostering innovation and attracting high-value industries could help stabilize and grow the local economy.

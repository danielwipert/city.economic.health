# Grand Rapids — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Grand Rapids' labour market exhibits modest resilience, with an unemployment rate of 4.0%, down 0.3 percentage points year-on-year. However, this improvement is offset by a 4.96% annual decline in the civilian labour force, the weakest national performance, indicating persistent workforce supply challenges. Employment growth remains subdued, with nonfarm payrolls increasing just 0.51% year-on-year, while weekly hours are slightly above the 12-month baseline, with a deviation of +0.29%. Wage growth trails the national median at 3.1% year-on-year, limiting household purchasing power. The labour demand composite score of 5.3, ranking in the 60th percentile, suggests stable but unremarkable hiring momentum.

In contrast, the housing market is characterised by growing imbalances. A 55% year-on-year surge in residential building permits, ranking in the 90th percentile nationally, indicates strong builder confidence. Nevertheless, this supply response has not alleviated market tightness, with homes taking a median 44 days to sell, up 15.8% from a year ago. The days-on-market composite score, in the 88th percentile, highlights significantly prolonged turnover, suggesting demand continues to outpace new supply, potentially due to geographic or regulatory constraints. The cost of living, with a composite score of 5.47 in the 8th percentile, remains high relative to incomes, while the office worker ratio score of 2.98, in the 64th percentile, points to a modest white-collar base compared to peer metros. The key challenge lies in the disconnect between expanding construction activity and persistent sales delays, which may reflect mismatches in housing type or location.

Looking ahead, Grand Rapids is expected to maintain a stable, mid-tier economic trajectory. However, structural labour force contraction and housing market inefficiencies pose medium-term headwinds to broader-based growth.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

The metro area’s unemployment rate stands at 4.0%, down 0.3 percentage points year-on-year, reflecting a tightening labour market. However, this improvement is tempered by a sharp contraction in the civilian labour force, which has declined 4.96% annually—the weakest performance nationally on this metric—suggesting persistent workforce supply challenges. Employment growth is subdued, with nonfarm payrolls rising just 0.51% YoY, while weekly hours slightly exceed the 12-month baseline (deviation: +0.29%). Wage growth at 3.1% YoY trails the national median, limiting household purchasing power. The labour demand composite score of 5.3, in the 60th percentile, indicates stable but unspectacular hiring momentum.

Housing market dynamics reveal growing imbalances. Residential building permits have surged 55% YoY—a signal of strong builder confidence and ranking in the 90th percentile nationally—yet this supply response has not alleviated market tightness. Homes now take a median 44 days to sell, up 15.8% from a year ago, with the days-on-market composite score in the 88th percentile, indicating significantly prolonged turnover. This suggests demand continues to outpace new supply, possibly constrained by geographic or regulatory factors. The cost of living, at a composite score of 5.47 (8th percentile), remains high relative to incomes, while the office worker ratio score of 2.98 (64th percentile) points to a modest white-collar base compared to peer metros. The principal stress point lies in the disconnect between expanding construction activity and persistent sales delays, which may reflect mismatches in housing type or location.

**Outlook:** Grand Rapids maintains a stable, mid-tier economic trajectory, but structural labour force contraction and housing market inefficiencies pose medium-term headwinds to broader-based growth.

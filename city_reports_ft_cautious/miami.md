## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Miami-Fort Lauderdale-West Palm Beach metropolitan area reveals a mixed picture, with some indicators showing relative strength while others lag behind national trends. The unemployment rate stands at 4.1%, placing it in the 46th percentile among comparable regions, suggesting a labor market that is neither exceptionally strong nor weak. Labor force participation is at 62.37%, which is in the 24th percentile, indicating a relatively low engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Earnings growth over the past year has been robust at 7.12%, ranking in the 86th percentile, which may reflect a tightening labor market or other local factors contributing to wage increases.

In the housing sector, the annual change in the housing price index is -1.03%, placing it in the 10th percentile, suggesting a decline in home prices compared to the previous year. This contrasts with a 7.44% increase in building permits, which is in the 56th percentile, indicating ongoing construction activity despite the softening in home prices. The days on market metric, at 86 days, is in the 2nd percentile, pointing to a slower pace of home sales, which could be a sign of reduced buyer interest or an oversupply of homes on the market. Additionally, the cost of living index is 5.71, in the 20th percentile, and the weekly hours worked average 35.2 hours, in the 88th percentile, both of which suggest moderate living costs and relatively high work hours, respectively.

Overall, the data suggests a complex economic environment in Miami, with strengths in earnings growth and labor market activity, but challenges in the housing market and labor force participation.

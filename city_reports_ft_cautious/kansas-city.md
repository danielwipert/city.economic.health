# Kansas City — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Kansas City's economic indicators paint a nuanced picture, with both positive and negative trends. The labour market shows a mixed signal, with an unemployment rate of 3.5% - a 0.3 percentage point increase year-over-year - alongside a 2.1% growth in the civilian labour force. Average hourly earnings have risen 6.76% over the same period, a favourable sign for wage growth.

Business activity is characterised by a labour demand composite score of 2.44, although nonfarm payroll employment has declined 0.31% year-over-year. The 1.425% deviation from the city's 12-month baseline in weekly hours worked may indicate labour market stress. In contrast, the housing market appears relatively stable, with residential building permits up 5.41% year-over-year and median days on market down 11.76% to 45 days. The cost of living composite score stands at 1.31, and the office worker ratio composite score is 0.92, suggesting potential challenges in affordability and office space utilisation.

The city's economic strengths, including a growing workforce and rising earnings, are offset by weaknesses such as declining nonfarm payroll employment and potential labour market stress. With a weighted percentile score of 50.3, Kansas City's economic outlook is neutral, indicating average performance relative to other cities.

Looking ahead, Kansas City's economy is expected to maintain a stable, albeit modest, growth trajectory, driven by its expanding workforce and increasing earnings. To achieve more robust growth, however, the city must address its labour market challenges and potential stress points.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Kansas City's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.5%, with a modest year-over-year increase of 0.3 percentage points. However, the civilian labor force has grown by 2.1% over the same period, suggesting a degree of workforce supply growth. Average hourly earnings have risen by 6.76% year-over-year, indicating a favourable trend in terms of wage growth.

In terms of business activity, the labour demand composite score is 2.44, while nonfarm payroll employment has declined by 0.31% year-over-year. The weekly hours deviation from the city's own 12-month baseline is 1.425%, which may indicate some stress in the labour market. On the housing front, residential building permits have increased by 5.41% year-over-year, and the median days on market have decreased by 11.76% over the same period, currently standing at 45 days. The cost of living composite score is 1.31, and the office worker ratio composite score is 0.92, which may suggest some challenges in terms of affordability and office space utilization.

The city's economic strengths lie in its growing workforce and rising earnings, while its weaknesses are evident in the decline in nonfarm payroll employment and potential labour market stress. The housing market appears to be relatively stable, with increasing permits and decreasing days on market. Overall, Kansas City's economic outlook is neutral, with a weighted percentile score of 50.3, indicating an average performance relative to other cities.

Closing outlook: Kansas City's economy is expected to continue on a stable, albeit modest, growth trajectory, driven by its growing workforce and rising earnings. However, the city will need to address its labour market challenges and potential stress points to achieve more robust growth in the long term.

# Kansas City — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Kansas City's labour market is sending mixed signals, with robust labour supply growth and wage pressure offset by softening employment demand. The civilian labour force has expanded 2.1% year-on-year, placing it in the 86th percentile, while average hourly earnings have risen 6.76%, ranking in the 78th percentile. However, nonfarm payroll employment has declined 0.31% over the past year, ranking in the 4th percentile, and the labour demand composite score is a weak 2.44, also in the 4th percentile. This divergence between rising wages and flat-to-contracting job growth is notable, although the unemployment rate remains low at 3.5%, albeit up 0.3 percentage points from a year ago.

In the housing market, dynamics suggest resilience but increasing illiquidity. Residential building permits are up 5.41% year-on-year, ranking in the 64th percentile, indicating continued construction activity. Nevertheless, homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market increasing 11.8% year-on-year to 45 days, ranking in the 4th percentile. This slowdown in buyer momentum is a concern. On the other hand, Kansas City's cost of living remains relatively favourable, with a composite score of 1.31, ranking in the 85th percentile, making it more affordable than most peer metros. However, office demand is a clear stress point, with a composite office worker ratio of 0.92, ranking in the 6th percentile, signalling underutilised commercial space and limited return-to-office momentum.

Looking ahead, Kansas City's economy is transitioning from a post-pandemic expansion to a more subdued growth phase. While labour supply and wage gains provide a foundation for growth, weakening employment growth and housing market inertia pose near-term challenges. Without a rebound in business services demand or office occupancy, expansion is likely to be constrained to sectors driven by population growth and affordability advantages.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Kansas City’s labour market shows mixed signals, with underlying strength in workforce expansion and wage growth offset by softening employment demand. The civilian labour force has grown 2.1% year-on-year—well above average (86th percentile)—indicating robust labour supply growth, while average hourly earnings rose 6.76% (78th percentile), reflecting persistent wage pressure. However, nonfarm payroll employment declined 0.31% over the past year (4th percentile), and the labour demand composite score stands weak at 2.44 (4th percentile), suggesting a divergence between rising wages and flat-to-contracting job growth. The unemployment rate remains low at 3.5%, though it has increased 0.3 percentage points from a year ago, consistent with a cooling labour market.

Housing market dynamics point to a resilient but increasingly illiquid sector. Residential building permits are up 5.41% year-on-year (64th percentile), indicating continued construction activity. However, homes are taking longer to sell, with median days on market at 45 days and a 11.8% year-on-year increase in time to sale (4th percentile), suggesting waning buyer momentum. The cost of living remains relatively favourable, with a composite score of 1.31 (85th percentile), making Kansas City more affordable than most peer metros. Office demand remains a clear stress point, as reflected in a composite office worker ratio of 0.92 (6th percentile), signalling underutilised commercial space and limited return-to-office momentum.

**Outlook:** Kansas City’s economy is navigating a transition from post-pandemic expansion to a more subdued growth phase. Labour supply and wage gains provide foundational strength, but weakening employment growth and housing market inertia pose near-term headwinds. Absent a rebound in business services demand or office occupancy, expansion will likely remain constrained to sectors tied to population growth and affordability advantages.

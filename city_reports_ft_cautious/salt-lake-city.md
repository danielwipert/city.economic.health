# Salt Lake City-Murray

**Grade: A (Very Good) | 63.1th percentile | April 2026**

---

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 63.1th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong labor demand and deep office economy. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.43, combining a 0.94% employment growth rate and a -0.187% weekly hours deviation, signals genuine demand expansion. Additionally, its office/professional worker share of 4.34, ranking in the 94th percentile, underscores the city's suitability for knowledge-economy businesses.

**Labor Demand**
The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area has seen a 0.94% employment growth rate year-over-year, paired with a -0.187% deviation in weekly hours from its own 12-month baseline, indicating a genuine demand expansion. This combination suggests that the city is adding jobs at a moderate pace, with hours worked remaining relatively stable. The labor demand composite score of 5.43, ranking in the 86th percentile, further reinforces this interpretation.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Salt Lake City-Murray stands at 3.90%, ranking in the 52nd percentile, indicating a near-median level of labor market slack. This rate suggests that the market is neither extremely tight nor overly loose, with some room for businesses to hire without excessive wage pressure. However, the relatively low unemployment rate may still pose some challenges for businesses seeking to attract top talent.

**Wage Growth**
The city has experienced a 4.34% year-over-year wage growth rate, ranking in the 60th percentile, indicating moderate wage inflation. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are seeing some improvement in their purchasing power. As a result, businesses may need to balance their labor costs with the benefits of a relatively skilled workforce.

**Cost of Living**
Salt Lake City-Murray has a cost of living score ranking in the 65th percentile, with a PSF of $263/sqft and average hourly earnings of $39.31/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.69. The fact that PSF is falling by 0.8% year-over-year is a key driver of the city's relatively high affordability score. This suggests that the city is relatively affordable compared to its peers, making it an attractive location for businesses seeking to recruit talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Salt Lake City-Murray has contracted by 0.50% year-over-year, ranking in the 30th percentile, indicating a shrinking labor pool. This decline in labor force growth poses a structural headwind for hiring, as businesses may face challenges in finding qualified workers to fill open positions. As a result, companies may need to invest in training and development programs to attract and retain talent.

**Building Permits**
The city has seen a 14.73% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking in the 73rd percentile, indicating an expansion of housing supply. This growth in permits suggests that developer confidence is strong, and the future housing supply is likely to improve, which should help maintain affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. As a result, businesses can expect a more stable and predictable housing market for their employees.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market for homes in Salt Lake City-Murray stands at 45 days, with a 0.0% year-over-year change, ranking in the 27th percentile. This indicates a relatively fast-paced market, which may pose challenges for relocating workers seeking to purchase or rent homes. However, the stable days on market suggests that the market is not overheating, and workers may still find suitable housing options.

**Office Economy**
The city's office/professional worker share of 4.34, ranking in the 94th percentile, indicates a deep and specialized talent pool, making it an attractive location for tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions. This suggests that businesses in these sectors can tap into a highly skilled workforce, while those in more industrial or logistics-dominant sectors may find fewer specialized roles available.

The Salt Lake City-Murray metro area offers businesses a unique combination of strong labor demand, a deep office economy, and relatively affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for knowledge-economy businesses. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force, which may pose challenges for hiring and talent retention, and should be carefully factored into any location decision.

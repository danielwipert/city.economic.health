# Orlando — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Orlando's labour market is exhibiting signs of softening, in line with the broader economic moderation. The unemployment rate has risen to 4.4%, a 1.4 percentage point increase year-on-year, indicating a notable deterioration in labour market conditions. Employment growth has slowed significantly, with nonfarm payrolls expanding just 0.24% year-on-year. The labour demand composite score of 4.39 places Orlando in the lower half of its peer metros. Wage growth remains subdued, at 1.36% year-on-year, and has trailed inflation in recent quarters. Average weekly hours worked are slightly below their 12-month trend, while the civilian labour force has grown only 0.22% in the past year, suggesting limited expansion in workforce supply.

The housing market is characterised by persistent imbalances. Residential building permits have fallen sharply, down 35.64% year-on-year, one of the steepest declines nationally, and are constraining supply growth. The median days on market has increased to 67 days, up 11.67% from a year ago, indicating a trend towards slower turnover, although inventory remains tight relative to demand. The cost of living composite score of 2.79 suggests moderate affordability pressures, which are being offset by weak income growth. However, office demand, as measured by the office worker ratio composite of 3.04, ranks relatively well, at the 72nd percentile, suggesting some resilience in professional services activity.

Looking ahead, Orlando faces near-term challenges in employment and housing construction, with limited wage growth and rising unemployment likely to temper consumer momentum. While office-based employment signals offer a relative strength, the sharp contraction in building permits risks exacerbating housing shortages, potentially constraining long-term population and economic growth. Without a rebound in labour demand and residential investment, Orlando's economic trajectory is likely to remain subdued.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Orlando’s labour market shows signs of softening amid broader economic moderation. The city’s unemployment rate stands at 4.4% (101A_unemployment), up 1.4 percentage points year-on-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), reflecting a notable deterioration in labour market conditions. Employment growth has slowed, with nonfarm payrolls expanding just 0.24% year-on-year (107E_employment_growth_yoy), while the labour demand composite score of 4.39 (107E_ldc_composite) places Orlando in the lower half of peer metros. Wage growth remains subdued at 1.36% (103B_earnings_yoy), trailing inflation in recent quarters, and average weekly hours worked are slightly below their 12-month trend (-0.245%). The civilian labour force grew only 0.22% in the past year (102A_clf_yoy), suggesting limited expansion in workforce supply.

Housing market indicators point to persistent imbalances. Residential building permits have collapsed, falling 35.64% year-on-year (200B_permits_yoy), among the steepest declines nationally, constraining supply growth. With median days on market at 67 days (204A_dom_level_days), up 11.67% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct), the market is trending toward slower turnover, though inventory remains tight relative to demand. The cost of living composite score of 2.79 (104C_col) indicates moderate affordability pressures, but this is increasingly offset by weak income growth. Office demand, as proxied by the office worker ratio composite (105C_owr) of 3.04, ranks relatively well (72nd percentile), suggesting some resilience in professional services activity.

**Outlook:** Orlando faces near-term headwinds in employment and housing construction, with limited wage growth and rising unemployment tempering consumer momentum. While office-based employment signals offer a relative strength, the sharp contraction in building permits risks exacerbating housing shortages, potentially constraining long-term population and economic growth. Absent a rebound in labour demand and residential investment, the city’s economic trajectory is likely to remain subdued.

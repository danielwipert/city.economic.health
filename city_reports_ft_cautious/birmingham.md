# Birmingham

**Grade: A (Very Good) | 64.2th percentile | April 2026**

---

Birmingham has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 64.2th percentile among 50 US metros, with a strong labor demand and extremely low unemployment rate defining its current economic character, at 88th and 98th percentiles, respectively. The labor demand composite score of 6.28 and unemployment rate of 2.20% signal a highly competitive job market. These metrics suggest that Birmingham's economy is driven by a strong and growing workforce.

**Labor Demand**
Birmingham's employment growth rate of 0.52% and weekly hours deviation of 1.249% above its own trend indicate genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. This combination signals a strong and growing labor market, where employers are adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours. The top-tier percentile rank of 88th for labor demand composite score reinforces this assessment.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Birmingham is 2.20%, which is extremely low, ranking in the 98th percentile. This tight labor market means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is limited, and wage pressure may be higher. As a result, companies may need to offer competitive salaries to attract top talent.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate in Birmingham is -0.46%, which is stagnant and ranks in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile. This slow wage growth means that labor costs for employers are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power may be limited. As a result, businesses may enjoy a relatively flat cost environment, but workers may have weaker bargaining power.

**Cost of Living**
Birmingham's cost of living, with a ratio of 3.67, ranks in the 26th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. This means that talent attraction may require wage premiums to offset the higher cost of living, as the city's affordability is below average. However, the relatively low ranking suggests that Birmingham may struggle to attract talent without offering competitive compensation packages.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Birmingham is growing at a rate of 1.06% year-over-year, which is above average, ranking in the 66th percentile. This expanding labor force supply means that businesses have a growing pool of potential workers to hire from, making it easier to find talent. As a result, companies may enjoy a more favorable hiring environment.

**Building Permits**
The number of building permits in Birmingham has increased by 55.25% year-over-year, ranking in the top tier at the 92nd percentile. This rapid growth in permits signals that housing supply is expanding, which may improve affordability and accommodation for the workforce in the future. As a result, businesses may benefit from a more stable and affordable housing market for their employees.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market in Birmingham is 56 days, with a year-over-year increase of 3.7%. This relatively fast market, ranking in the 31st percentile, means that homes are selling quickly, which may make it challenging for relocating workers to find housing. However, the rising days on market may signal a healthy normalization of the housing market.

**Office Economy**
Birmingham's professional and office worker share ranks in the 53rd percentile, indicating a near-median depth of talent pool. This suggests that the city is moderately suited for businesses that require a knowledge-based workforce, such as tech, finance, or consulting, but may not be as attractive for companies that require a large industrial or logistics workforce.

Birmingham offers businesses a highly competitive labor market with low unemployment and growing labor force supply, making it an attractive location for companies looking to expand. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the relatively expensive cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain top talent, potentially increasing labor costs for employers.

# Birmingham

**Grade: B+ (Above Average) | 55.9th percentile | June 2026**

---

Birmingham has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking it at the 55.9th percentile among 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score at the 78th percentile, and its low unemployment rate, at 2.40% and ranked at the 90th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with a tight labor market and genuine demand expansion.

**Labor Demand**
Birmingham's employment growth rate is +0.66% year-over-year, combined with a +0.680% deviation in weekly hours from its own 12-month trend. This combination signals genuine demand expansion, as hours are running above trend during a period of job growth. The labor demand composite score of 5.90 further supports this interpretation, indicating a strong and growing labor market.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Birmingham is 2.40%, ranking at the 90th percentile and indicating a very tight labor market. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face significant competition for talent and may need to offer higher wages to attract workers. The low unemployment rate suggests that the labor market has little slack, making it challenging for companies to find and hire qualified employees.

**Wage Growth**
Wage growth in Birmingham is -1.22% year-over-year, indicating stagnant or even declining wages. This slow wage growth will keep labor costs flat for employers, but it also means that workers will have weak bargaining power and limited purchasing power. The implication for businesses is a stable cost environment, but one that may not be attractive to workers seeking better compensation.

**Cost of Living**
Birmingham has a cost of living score at the 31st percentile, with a price-to-salary ratio of $161/sqft to $32.72/hr, or 4.92. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. The stable year-over-year change in PSF (+0.0% YoY) does not suggest any significant improvement in affordability. As a result, businesses may need to offer wage premiums to attract talent to this city, as its cost of living is not particularly competitive.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Birmingham is shrinking at a rate of -1.03% year-over-year. This contraction in labor force supply will create a structural headwind for hiring, making it even more challenging for businesses to find qualified workers in an already tight labor market. The implication is that companies may need to invest in training and development programs to build the talent they need.

**Building Permits**
Residential building permits in Birmingham are increasing at a rate of +12.01% year-over-year. This expansion in housing supply is a positive signal for future affordability and workforce accommodation, suggesting that the city may become more attractive to workers and businesses in the long term. The growing supply of housing will help to mitigate some of the pressure on the labor market and cost of living.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market for homes in Birmingham is 54 days, with a year-over-year increase of +5.9%. This suggests a relatively balanced market, neither extremely competitive nor extremely slow. For workers relocating to this city, the housing market is accessible, but not overly favorable to buyers. The rising DOM indicates a healthy normalization of the market, rather than a demand erosion.

**Office Economy**
Birmingham's professional and office worker share is at the 58th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This city is best suited for businesses that can thrive in a mixed economy with a moderate presence of professional and office sectors. It may not be the ideal location for businesses requiring a highly specialized or abundant tech/finance/consulting talent pool, but it can support a variety of industries with its diverse workforce.

In conclusion, Birmingham offers businesses a strong labor demand and a tight labor market, but also presents challenges such as slow wage growth and a relatively high cost of living. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force supply, which will require businesses to adapt their hiring and talent development strategies to succeed in this market. Despite these challenges, the city's expanding housing supply and balanced housing market are positive signals for its long-term attractiveness to workers and businesses.

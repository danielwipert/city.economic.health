# Chicago — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Chicago's labour market exhibits modest growth, with nonfarm employment increasing 0.6% year-on-year and an unchanged unemployment rate of 4.5%. However, this stability belies underlying weaknesses in workforce dynamics. The civilian labour force has contracted 1.7% over the past year, posing a significant constraint on long-term growth potential and suggesting outmigration or declining labour supply. Average hourly earnings have risen 4.18% year-on-year, outpacing inflation, but wage gains appear isolated amidst broader stagnation. Labour demand, as indicated by a composite score of 4.87 (54th percentile), is slightly above median, although a 0.275% decline in weekly hours worked suggests employers are not expanding hours despite maintaining headcount.

The city's housing and business activity are cooling. Residential building permits have fallen 27.4% year-on-year, ranking among the weakest nationally (14th percentile), and indicating a subdued construction outlook and limited supply response to affordability pressures. While homes are selling relatively quickly, with a median 35 days on market (down 2.8% from last year), low inventory and declining new development constrain market resilience. The cost of living composite score of 2.94 (42nd percentile) reflects moderate affordability challenges. Office occupancy remains a concern, with an office worker ratio score of 2.3 (46th percentile), driven by persistent hybrid work trends and commercial real estate stress.

Looking ahead, Chicago's economy faces structural challenges, particularly in labour force retention and housing supply. Near-term stability in employment and wages may continue, but without a reversal in population and construction trends, long-term growth prospects will remain constrained. Fiscal pressures and commercial real estate vulnerabilities pose ongoing risks to fiscal health and downtown activity.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Chicago’s labour market shows modest momentum, with nonfarm employment growing 0.6% year-on-year and a stable unemployment rate at 4.5%—unchanged from a year earlier. However, underlying weakness emerges in workforce dynamics: the civilian labour force has contracted by 1.7% over the past year, a significant constraint on long-term growth potential and a signal of outmigration or declining labour supply. While average hourly earnings rose 4.18% YoY, outpacing inflation in recent quarters, wage gains appear increasingly isolated amid broader stagnation. Labour demand, as reflected in the composite score of 4.87 (54th percentile), is slightly above median but tempered by a marginal decline in weekly hours worked (-0.275% deviation from baseline), suggesting employers are not expanding hours despite maintaining headcount.

Housing and business activity point to cooling conditions. Residential building permits have plummeted 27.4% year-on-year—a sharp contraction that ranks among the weakest nationally (14th percentile)—indicating subdued construction outlook and limited supply response to affordability pressures. The cost of living composite score of 2.94 (42nd percentile) reflects moderate affordability challenges, while homes are selling relatively quickly at 35 median days on market, down 2.8% from last year. Yet this masks structural weakness: low inventory and declining new development constrain market resilience. Office occupancy remains a drag, with an office worker ratio score of 2.3 (46th percentile), reflecting persistent hybrid work trends and commercial real estate stress.

**Outlook:** Chicago’s economy faces structural headwinds, particularly in labour force retention and housing supply. Near-term stability in employment and wages may persist, but without reversal in population and construction trends, long-term growth prospects remain constrained. Fiscal pressures and commercial real estate vulnerabilities pose ongoing risks to fiscal health and downtown activity.

# Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington

**Grade: A- (Good) | 62.9th percentile | April 2026**

---

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has an overall grade of A- with a composite score of 62.9th percentile, ranking it among the top US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its strong labor demand, with a composite score in the 78th percentile, and its highly affordable cost of living, ranked in the 87th percentile. These metrics signal a city with a thriving job market and attractive living conditions, with employment growth at 0.98% year-over-year and a cost of living ratio of 1.28.

**Labor Demand**
The city's employment growth rate of 0.98% year-over-year, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -0.325% from its own trend, signals genuine demand expansion, indicating that jobs are being added and hours are running above trend. This combination suggests a strong and growing job market. The labor demand composite score of 5.35, ranked in the 78th percentile, further reinforces this assessment.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is 3.60%, ranking in the 70th percentile, which indicates a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer competitive wages to attract talent. The tight market also implies upward pressure on wages.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate in the city is 4.36%, ranked in the 53rd percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a pace that is near the median for US metros. Workers in the city can expect to see their purchasing power increase, but not at an exceptionally rapid pace.

**Cost of Living**
With a cost of living ratio of 1.28, ranked in the 87th percentile, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is one of the more affordable cities in the US. This high ranking means that the city has a significant talent attraction advantage, as workers can maintain a high standard of living without requiring large wage premiums. The affordable cost of living makes the city an attractive location for businesses looking to relocate or expand.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in the city is growing at a rate of 0.64% year-over-year, ranked in the 43rd percentile, indicating a moderate expansion of the workforce supply. This growth rate suggests that the hiring capacity in the city is increasing, but at a pace that is near the median for US metros. Businesses can expect to find a growing pool of potential workers, but may still face challenges in attracting top talent.

**Building Permits**
The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -20.96%, ranked in the 17th percentile, indicating a significant tightening of the housing supply. This sharp decline in permits suggests that the city may face future affordability and workforce accommodation challenges, as the supply of new housing is not keeping pace with demand. Businesses should factor in the potential for increasing housing costs and reduced affordability for their workers.

**Days on Market**
The current median days on market is 48 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.7%, ranked in the 47th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced market, neither extremely competitive nor overly accessible. Workers relocating to the city can expect a moderate pace of home sales, with some flexibility in their housing search.

**Office Economy**
With an office/professional worker share composite score of 3.94, ranked in the 92nd percentile, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington has a deep and talented professional pool. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a high concentration of specialized knowledge workers. However, the city may be less suited for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a strong labor market, affordable cost of living, and deep professional talent pool, making it an attractive location for relocation or expansion. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses is the tightening housing supply, which may lead to future affordability challenges and reduced workforce accommodation, potentially impacting the city's attractiveness to workers and businesses alike.

# Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington

**Grade: B+ (Above Average) | 55.2th percentile | April 2026**

---

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 55.2th percentile among 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its above-average labor demand, with a composite score at the 66th percentile, and its highly developed office economy, ranking at the 92nd percentile. The labor demand is driven by a combination of employment growth and hours worked, with a 0.57% year-over-year employment growth rate and a -0.510% deviation in weekly hours from the city's own 12-month baseline.

**Labor Demand**
The employment growth rate of 0.57% year-over-year, combined with a -0.510% deviation in weekly hours from the city's own trend, signals a genuine demand expansion, albeit with some moderation in hours worked. This suggests that while jobs are being added, the existing workforce is not being overutilized, indicating a relatively healthy labor market. The labor demand composite score of 4.59 further supports this assessment, ranking at the 66th percentile.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington stands at 4.20%, which is above average, ranking at the 60th percentile. This indicates that the labor market has some slack, making it relatively easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, this also means that local consumer demand may be somewhat weaker due to the higher unemployment rate.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate is 3.24%, which is near the median, ranking at the 42nd percentile. This moderate wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not excessively so, while workers are experiencing some improvement in their purchasing power. This balance is generally favorable for businesses looking to manage labor costs without sacrificing worker morale.

**Cost of Living**
With a cost of living ratio of $201/sqft to $37.50/hr, and a year-over-year decrease of 1.9% in PSF, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington is relatively affordable, ranking at the 76th percentile. This affordability is a significant talent attraction advantage, as businesses can offer competitive wages without needing large premiums to offset high living costs.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of 0.31% year-over-year, which is near the median, ranking at the 50th percentile. This indicates that the workforce supply is slowly expanding, providing a modestly increasing pool of potential hires for businesses. However, the growth rate is not strong enough to significantly alleviate hiring challenges.

**Building Permits**
The year-over-year change in residential building permits is -20.96%, ranking at the 13th percentile, indicating a sharp decline in housing supply expansion. This signals a potential future affordability issue and a tightening of the housing market, which could make it more difficult for workers to relocate to the area and for businesses to attract talent.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market for homes is 48 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.7%. This suggests a slowing market, which can make it easier for relocating workers to find housing, as homes are not being snapped up as quickly as they might be in hotter markets. However, this also indicates a less competitive housing market, which could be a sign of weakening demand.

**Office Economy**
The share of jobs in professional and office sectors is significant, with the city ranking at the 92nd percentile in terms of its deep talent pool. This makes Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington highly suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less ideal for industries that are more industrial or logistics-dominant.

In conclusion, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington offers businesses a relatively affordable cost of living, a deep professional talent pool, and a labor market with some slack, making it easier to hire. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the sharp decline in residential building permits, which signals potential future affordability issues and a tightening housing market, posing a challenge for attracting and accommodating a growing workforce.

# Dallas — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metropolitan area ranks fourth in the dataset, with an overall grade of B, indicating average performance across various economic indicators. The unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, placing it at the 50th percentile, which aligns with national trends. The labor force participation rate is notably high at 69.61%, ranking in the 90th percentile, suggesting a robust engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Earnings growth over the past year has been moderate at 3.1%, situated in the 44th percentile, while the cost of living has increased by 4.87%, placing it slightly above the median at the 56th percentile.

Housing indicators present a mixed picture. The number of building permits issued has declined by 20.74% year-over-year, falling into the 22nd percentile, which may reflect a slowdown in construction activity. Home prices have decreased by 1.52% over the same period, placing the region in the 8th percentile, indicating a softening in the housing market. Similarly, the price-to-square-foot ratio has dropped by 1.8%, in the 34th percentile, and properties are taking longer to sell, with an average of 72 days on the market, in the 38th percentile. These trends suggest a cooling in the real estate sector, though the data does not provide a clear cause for this shift.

Weekly hours worked remain relatively high at 35.5 hours, in the 92nd percentile, which may indicate stable employment conditions despite the decline in building permits. Overall, the data suggests that while Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington maintains a strong labor market, there are signs of moderation in both earnings growth and the housing sector.

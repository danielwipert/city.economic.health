# Dallas — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metropolitan area has an economic profile that ranks fourth, with a weighted percentile score of 50, placing it in the average category. The unemployment rate is 3.6%, in line with the national average, indicating a stable labour market. The labour force participation rate of 69.61% is high, ranking in the 90th percentile, suggesting a strong engagement of the working-age population in the labour market. Weekly hours worked average 35.5 hours, also a high figure, placing it in the 92nd percentile.

Earnings growth over the past year has been 3.1%, placing the region in the 44th percentile. This suggests that income growth is moderate, neither exceptionally strong nor weak compared to other regions. Housing indicators show a mixed picture. The housing price index has decreased by 1.52% year-over-year, and the number of building permits has declined by 20.74% compared to the previous year, both placing the region in lower percentiles (8th and 22nd, respectively). The days on market for homes remain at 57 days, a moderate level.

The data suggests a balanced economic environment in Dallas, with strengths in labour market participation and hours worked, offset by more modest earnings growth and a softening in the housing sector.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metropolitan area ranks fourth in its economic profile, with a weighted percentile score of 50, placing it in the average category. The unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, which aligns with the national average, suggesting a stable labor market. However, the labor force participation rate of 69.61% is notably high, ranking in the 90th percentile, indicating a robust engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Weekly hours worked average 35.5 hours, also a high figure, placing it in the 92nd percentile, which may reflect strong demand for labor or a concentration of full-time employment.

Earnings growth over the past year has been moderate, increasing by 3.1%, which places the region in the 44th percentile. This suggests that while income is growing, it is doing so at a pace that is neither exceptionally strong nor weak compared to other regions. Housing indicators show a more mixed picture. The housing price index has decreased by 1.52% year-over-year, and the number of building permits has declined by 20.74% compared to the previous year, both figures placing the region in lower percentiles (8th and 22nd, respectively). These trends may reflect a cooling in the housing market, though the days on market for homes remain at 57 days, a moderate level that does not suggest severe distress.

Overall, the data indicates a balanced but not overly dynamic economic environment in Dallas, with strengths in labor market participation and hours worked, offset by more modest earnings growth and a softening in the housing sector.

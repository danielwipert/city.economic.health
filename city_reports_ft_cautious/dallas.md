# Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington

**Grade: A (Very Good) | 64.6th percentile | May 2026**

---

The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has earned an overall grade of A, ranking in the 64.6th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and cost of living. The city's labor demand composite score of 5.29, combining a +0.92% employment growth rate and a -0.301% weekly hours deviation, signals genuine demand expansion. Additionally, its cost of living, with a PSF of $203/sqft and a ratio of 5.39 to hourly earnings of $37.69, ranks in the 78th percentile for affordability.

**Labor Demand**
The Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington metro area has seen employment growth of +0.92% year-over-year, paired with a -0.301% deviation in weekly hours from its own 12-month baseline, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination suggests that the city is adding jobs and that hours are not excessively stretched, signaling a healthy labor market. The labor demand composite score of 5.29 further reinforces this interpretation, placing the city in the 68th percentile for labor demand.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington stands at 4.10%, ranking in the 71st percentile, which indicates a relatively tight labor market. This tightness implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as well as potential upward pressure on wages. However, it also suggests a strong local economy with low slack in the labor market.

**Wage Growth**
Wage growth in the metro area has been +3.13% year-over-year, which ranks in the 38th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not at an alarming pace, and that worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit not dramatically. The moderate wage growth environment may provide a stable foundation for businesses.

**Cost of Living**
With a cost of living ratio of 5.39, derived from a PSF of $203/sqft and hourly earnings of $37.69, and a year-over-year decrease of 1.5% in PSF, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington ranks in the 78th percentile for affordability. This high ranking means the city is more affordable than most of its peers, making it an attractive location for talent without necessitating significant wage premiums. The decreasing PSF relative to wages further enhances the city's affordability.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington has grown by +0.73% year-over-year, placing it near the median in terms of labor force growth. This positive growth rate indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, which should facilitate hiring for businesses locating in the area. However, the growth rate is not exceptionally high, suggesting a balanced labor market.

**Building Permits**
The metro area has seen a +7.46% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, signaling an expansion in housing supply. This increase suggests that developer confidence is strong, and future housing affordability may improve, supporting workforce accommodation and attraction. The rising permits also indicate a proactive approach to meeting housing demand.

**Days on Market**
Homes in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington currently sit on the market for a median of 46 days, with a year-over-year increase of 7.0%. This increase, coupled with the absolute level, suggests a market that is becoming more accessible for relocating workers, as homes are not selling as quickly as they might in hotter markets. The rising days on market in a strong job market may indicate a healthy normalization of the housing market.

**Office Economy**
The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington places it in the 92nd percentile, indicating a deep and talented pool of knowledge-economy workers. This makes the city particularly well-suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and headquarters operations, but less ideal for those primarily involved in industrial or logistics activities.

In conclusion, Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion, a relatively tight unemployment market, and an affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for talent. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers to consider is the potential for wage pressure due to the tight labor market, which could impact labor costs and necessitate strategic hiring and compensation planning.

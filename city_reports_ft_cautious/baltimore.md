## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metropolitan area ranks 22nd in a recent economic assessment, with an overall grade of B-, indicating performance that is below average relative to other regions. The unemployment rate stands at 4.3%, placing it in the 30th percentile, which suggests a labor market that is neither particularly strong nor weak compared to national standards. The labor force participation rate is 64.61%, aligning closely with the median, at the 50th percentile. Year-over-year earnings growth is modest at 0.74%, ranking in the 16th percentile, which may reflect a relatively stable but not rapidly expanding job market.

Housing indicators present a mixed picture. The year-over-year change in housing prices is robust at 14.35%, placing the region in the 72nd percentile, suggesting a strong demand for housing. However, building permits have declined by 32% year-over-year, in the 12th percentile, which could indicate a slowdown in new construction or a shift in development priorities. The cost of living index is 7.45, in the 4th percentile, indicating that living costs in Baltimore are relatively low compared to other metropolitan areas. The number of days properties remain on the market is 45, in the 74th percentile, which may suggest a balanced market where neither buyers nor sellers have a significant advantage.

Overall, the data indicates a region with a stable but not dynamic economy, characterized by moderate employment metrics and a housing market that shows strength in price appreciation but weakness in new construction activity.

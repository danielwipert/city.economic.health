## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic landscape of the Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metropolitan area, as of the latest data, suggests a mixed performance across various indicators. The unemployment rate stands at 4.3%, placing it in the 30th percentile nationally, which indicates a relatively moderate level of joblessness. Labor force participation is at 64.61%, aligning with the national median, suggesting that a typical proportion of the working-age population is engaged in the labor market. Earnings growth year-over-year is recorded at 2.16%, a figure that falls below the national average, potentially reflecting subdued wage pressures or economic conditions.

Housing market dynamics show a 14.35% increase in home prices year-over-year, a significant rise that places the region in the 72nd percentile, indicating robust appreciation in property values. However, this contrasts with a 32.0% decline in building permits issued year-over-year, which may reflect a slowdown in new construction activity. The cost of living index is 5.34% above the national average, while the overall well-being index stands at 2.6, both figures near the national median, suggesting that living costs and quality of life are broadly in line with national trends.

The data also reveals that the number of days properties remain on the market has stabilized at 45 days, a figure that is relatively high but not unusual given the broader economic context. Overall, the weighted percentile score of 40.4 and the grade of "B-" suggest that Baltimore's economic performance is below average compared to other metropolitan areas, without clear indications of either robust growth or significant decline.

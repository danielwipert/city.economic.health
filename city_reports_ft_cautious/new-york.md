# New York — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

New York's economic indicators paint a nuanced picture, with a mix of positive and negative trends. The labour market is relatively stable, with an unemployment rate of 4.5% and a modest year-over-year increase of 0.2 percentage points. However, the 1.2% year-over-year growth in the civilian labour force is subdued, hinting at a potential slowdown in workforce supply growth. On a more positive note, average hourly earnings have risen 5.53% year-over-year, outpacing many other major cities and indicating strong labour demand.

The city's housing market is marked by a high cost of living, with a composite score of 4.99, and a relatively slow growth in residential building permits, up 14.97% year-over-year. Despite this, the median days on market have decreased by 6.25% year-over-year to 51 days, suggesting a slight market tightening. Business activity is robust, with a high office worker ratio composite score of 2.9 and a labour demand composite score of 5.79. While nonfarm payroll employment growth is modest at 0.48% year-over-year, the 0.814% deviation from the 12-month baseline in weekly hours worked suggests underlying labour market strength.

New York's economy faces challenges, including a high cost of living and slow workforce growth, which may strain businesses and individuals. Nevertheless, strong labour demand and high average hourly earnings point to a resilient economy with growth opportunities. With a grade of "B" and a weighted percentile score of 52.6, New York's economic outlook is cautiously positive, indicating average performance relative to other major cities.

Looking ahead, New York's economy is expected to continue growing, driven by its strong labour market and high average hourly earnings. However, policymakers and businesses must navigate the challenges posed by the city's high cost of living and slow workforce growth to ensure sustained competitiveness and resilience in the face of changing economic conditions.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

New York's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 4.5%, with a modest year-over-year increase of 0.2 percentage points. While this suggests a relatively stable job market, the civilian labour force growth rate of 1.2% year-over-year is somewhat subdued, indicating a potential slowdown in workforce supply growth. However, average hourly earnings have risen by 5.53% year-over-year, outpacing many other major cities and suggesting a strong demand for labour.

The city's housing market is characterized by a high cost of living, with a composite score of 4.99, and a relatively slow pace of residential building permits growth, with a year-over-year increase of 14.97%. The median days on market have decreased by 6.25% year-over-year, currently standing at 51 days, which may indicate a slight tightening of the market. On the business activity front, the office worker ratio composite score of 2.9 suggests a relatively high level of office employment, while the labour demand composite score of 5.79 indicates a strong demand for labour. The nonfarm payroll employment growth rate of 0.48% year-over-year is modest, but the weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is a positive 0.814%, suggesting some underlying strength in the labour market.

Despite these positive signals, New York's economy is not without its stress points. The high cost of living and relatively slow pace of workforce growth may pose challenges for businesses and individuals alike. However, the city's strong labour demand and high average hourly earnings suggest a resilient economy with opportunities for growth. Overall, New York's economic outlook is cautiously positive, with a grade of "B" and a weighted percentile score of 52.6, indicating an average performance relative to other major cities.

Closing outlook: New York's economy is expected to continue growing, driven by its strong labour market and high average hourly earnings. However, the city's high cost of living and relatively slow pace of workforce growth may pose challenges for sustained growth. Policymakers and businesses will need to balance these competing factors to ensure the city's economy remains competitive and resilient in the face of changing economic conditions.

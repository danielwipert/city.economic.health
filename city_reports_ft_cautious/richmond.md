# Richmond

**Grade: C- (Critical) | 31.3th percentile | May 2026**

---

The Richmond metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 31.3th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its labor demand composite score of 2.65, ranking in the bottom tier at the 16th percentile, and its labor force growth rate of -1.80% YoY, also in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

**Labor Demand**
The employment growth rate in Richmond is -0.48% YoY, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +0.982%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, as hours are being worked above trend during a period of job losses, indicating a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This scenario suggests that businesses may face challenges in finding skilled workers to fill new positions.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Richmond is 3.80%, ranking in the above-average tier at the 67th percentile. This indicates a relatively tight labor market with some slack, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire new workers. As a result, businesses may need to offer competitive wages to attract top talent in this market.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate in Richmond is +2.57%, ranking below average at the 28th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in other cities. While this may be beneficial for worker purchasing power, it also means that businesses may need to budget for increasing labor costs.

**Cost of Living**
Richmond has a cost of living score ranking it in the below-average tier at the 26th percentile, with a PSF to earnings ratio of $233/sqft to $35.94/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.48. This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, which could make it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. **Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Richmond is contracting at a rate of -1.80% YoY, ranking in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile. This decline in labor force supply signals a structural headwind for hiring, making it challenging for businesses to find skilled workers to fill new positions. As a result, businesses may need to invest in training and development programs to upskill existing workers.

**Building Permits**
The number of building permits in Richmond is declining by -24.31% YoY, ranking in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. This sharp decline in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. This may be a concern for businesses looking to relocate or expand in the city.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market in Richmond is 38 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.6%, ranking in the above-average tier at the 70th percentile. This indicates a relatively slow market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, this also suggests that the housing market may be normalizing, which could lead to more moderate price growth in the future.

**Office Economy**
Richmond has an office economy score ranking it in the below-average tier at the 30th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow professional talent pool. This makes the city less suited for businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting sectors, but more suitable for industries with fewer specialized roles, such as logistics or manufacturing.

The Richmond metro area offers businesses a relatively tight labor market with moderate wage growth, but also presents challenges such as a contracting labor force and tightening housing supply. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's relatively expensive cost of living, which could make it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums, highlighting the need for careful consideration of labor costs and talent acquisition strategies.

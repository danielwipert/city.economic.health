# Richmond

**Grade: C- (Critical) | 29.6th percentile | May 2026**

---

The Richmond metro area has an overall grade of C- with a composite score ranking it 29.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a labor demand composite score of 2.65, and its stagnant labor force growth, with a year-over-year change of -1.80%. These metrics signal a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand or relocate.

**Labor Demand**
The employment growth rate in Richmond is -0.48% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating from the trend by +0.982%. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, as the city is experiencing job losses while hours are slightly above trend, indicating a potential survivor squeeze. This suggests that remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Richmond is 3.80%, which corresponds to an above-average percentile rank of 64th. This indicates a relatively tight labor market with some slack, making it moderately challenging for businesses to hire. However, the relatively low unemployment rate also means that businesses may face upward pressure on wages to attract and retain talent.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate in Richmond is +2.57%, which is below average, ranking 28th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace, and worker purchasing power is increasing, albeit gradually. This may be beneficial for businesses looking to manage labor costs while still offering competitive compensation.

**Cost of Living**
Richmond has a cost of living score that ranks 28th percentile, with a PSF of $233/sqft and average hourly earnings of $35.94/hr, resulting in a ratio of 6.48. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers. The fact that PSF is rising by 2.2% year-over-year further exacerbates the affordability issue, making it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Richmond is contracting at a rate of -1.80% year-over-year. This decline in labor force supply signals a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of available workers is shrinking. Businesses may face challenges in finding qualified candidates, which could limit their growth potential.

**Building Permits**
The number of building permits in Richmond has decreased by 24.31% year-over-year. This sharp decline in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which may lead to future affordability issues and challenges in accommodating a growing workforce. This could be a concern for businesses looking to relocate or expand in the city.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market in Richmond is currently 38 days, with a year-over-year increase of 5.6%. This corresponds to an above-average percentile rank of 70th, indicating a slower market. For workers relocating to the city, this means that the housing market is relatively accessible, with more time to find a suitable home.

**Office Economy**
Richmond's professional and office worker share is below average, ranking 30th percentile. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting industries. However, the city may be more attractive to businesses in other sectors, such as logistics or manufacturing.

In conclusion, Richmond offers a relatively tight labor market and moderate wage growth, but its low labor demand, stagnant labor force growth, and tightening housing supply pose significant challenges for businesses. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's shrinking labor force, which may limit growth potential and make it difficult to find qualified candidates.

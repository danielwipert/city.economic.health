## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Boston-Cambridge-Newton metropolitan area presents a mixed picture, with some indicators performing well above average while others lag behind. The unemployment rate stands at 3.9%, placing the region in the 54th percentile nationally, suggesting a relatively stable labor market. The labor force participation rate of 68.47% is notably high, ranking in the 86th percentile, which may reflect a robust engagement of the working-age population in the economy. However, earnings growth year-over-year is modest at 2.61%, falling into the 34th percentile, indicating a slower pace of income expansion compared to other regions.

Housing market indicators show a complex trend. The year-over-year change in housing prices is positive at 4.14%, situating the area in the 40th percentile, which suggests moderate appreciation. In contrast, building permits have declined by 25.94% year-over-year, placing the region in the 18th percentile, potentially signaling a slowdown in new construction activity. The days on market for properties stand at 47 days, in the 70th percentile, which might indicate a balanced market where neither buyers nor sellers dominate.

Overall, the weighted percentile score of 48.3 and the grade of B- suggest that Boston's economic performance is below average relative to other metropolitan areas, with strengths in labor force participation and housing market balance offset by weaker earnings growth and a decline in construction permits.

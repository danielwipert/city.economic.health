# Milwaukee — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Milwaukee's labour market exhibits modest tightness, with an unemployment rate of 3.1%, a level that would typically indicate strength. However, this is offset by a 3.1% year-on-year decline in the civilian labour force, ranking among the weakest performances nationally, at the 2nd percentile. Employment growth has turned negative, with nonfarm payrolls contracting 0.9% over the past year, and labour demand remains subdued, with a composite score of 2.61, placing it in the 6th percentile. Although average hourly earnings have risen 3.8% year-on-year, this gain is in line with national trends and insufficient to offset the city's structural weaknesses. The office worker ratio of 1.28, at the 10th percentile, suggests persistent underutilisation of commercial space, reflecting lagging return-to-office momentum.

In the housing market, indicators point to softening demand and reduced construction activity. Residential building permits have fallen 10.5% year-on-year, implying waning investor confidence. The median days on market has increased to 33 days, up 6.5% from a year ago, with a composite days-on-market score of 7.25, ranking in the bottom third of metros. While the cost of living remains relatively moderate, at 4.18, ranking in the 17th percentile, this advantage has not translated into population or job growth. The combination of a contracting workforce, declining employment, and weak business activity underscores the structural challenges that outweigh the city's short-term labour market tightness.

Looking ahead, Milwaukee faces persistent headwinds in labour supply and business dynamism. Unless the city experiences a reversal in workforce outflows and private sector hiring, it is likely to underperform its peer metros in economic growth over the medium term.

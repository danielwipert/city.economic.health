# Milwaukee-Waukesha

**Grade: C (Very Poor) | 36.3th percentile | June 2026**

---

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 36.3th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a labor demand composite score of 3.03, which is below average at the 22th percentile. This city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand and high cost of living, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.29, ranking in the bottom tier at the 18th percentile. The combination of -0.91% employment growth and -0.049% weekly hours deviation signals a contraction in labor demand.

**Labor Demand**
The employment growth rate is -0.91% and weekly hours are deviating -0.049% from the city's own trend, indicating a contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, with the labor market experiencing a slowdown. The low labor demand composite score of 3.03 further reinforces this notion.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate is 3.70%, which is near the median at the 50th percentile, indicating a relatively balanced labor market with neither significant slack nor tightness. This means that businesses trying to hire in this city will face moderate competition for talent, but not extreme pressure. The market is not particularly tight, so wage pressure is manageable.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate is +1.49%, which is below average at the 20th percentile, indicating stagnant wage growth. This slow wage growth means that employer labor costs are not rising rapidly, but worker purchasing power is also not increasing significantly. The implication is a flat cost environment, but with limited bargaining power for workers.

**Cost of Living**
The city has a cost of living score ranking in the bottom tier at the 18th percentile, with a PSF to wages ratio of 6.29, indicating that it is relatively expensive. The PSF is $227/sqft and rising at +2.2% YoY, making it less affordable for talent to relocate without wage premiums. This high cost of living is a talent attraction disadvantage.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force is growing at -0.58% year-over-year, which is near the median at the 48th percentile, indicating a slightly contracting labor pool. This means that the supply of workers is not expanding, creating a structural headwind for hiring. Businesses may face challenges in finding and recruiting talent in this shrinking labor pool.

**Building Permits**
The year-over-year change in residential building permits is +13.09%, which is above average at the 70th percentile, indicating an expansion in housing supply. This rising permit activity signals that housing supply is expanding, which should improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. The increase in building permits is a positive sign for future workforce attraction.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market is 32 days, with a +10.3% year-over-year increase, ranking above average at the 66th percentile. This means that homes are sitting on the market for a relatively longer period, indicating a slower and more accessible market for relocating workers. The rising days on market suggests a healthy normalization of the housing market.

**Office Economy**
The share of professional and office workers is ranked in the bottom tier at the 16th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool. This city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. Instead, it may be more suitable for industrial or logistics-dominant businesses.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers a business a relatively slow-growing labor market with moderate unemployment and stagnant wage growth. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for a decision-maker is the high cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract talent, making it a less competitive location for businesses seeking to relocate or expand.

# Milwaukee-Waukesha

**Grade: C (Very Poor) | 37.1th percentile | April 2026**

---

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area has an overall grade of C, ranking 37.1th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its weak labor demand and high cost of living. The labor demand composite score of 2.91, combining a -0.99% employment growth rate and a -0.777% weekly hours deviation from trend, signals a contraction in labor demand. This, coupled with a cost of living ratio of 6.26, driven by a $228/sqft price per square foot and $36.43/hr average hourly earnings, indicates significant challenges for businesses looking to attract and retain talent.

**Labor Demand**
The employment growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is -0.99% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.777% from the city's own 12-month baseline, indicating a clear contraction in labor demand. This combination signals a lack of genuine demand expansion, suggesting that the city's labor market is experiencing a slowdown. The negative hours deviation, in particular, points to a reduction in labor utilization, which can be a sign of economic weakness.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 3.90%, ranking in the 69th percentile, which suggests a relatively tight labor market. This tightness implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the pool of available workers is smaller, and may need to offer higher wages to attract talent. However, this also indicates a level of economic stability, as low unemployment rates are often associated with strong local economies.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth in Milwaukee-Waukesha is +3.28%, ranking near the median at the 43rd percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not at an alarming rate. For workers, this wage growth is positive, as it indicates increasing purchasing power, which can be beneficial for local consumer demand.

**Cost of Living**
Milwaukee-Waukesha has a cost of living ratio of 6.26, driven by a $228/sqft price per square foot and $36.43/hr average hourly earnings, ranking in the 14th percentile, indicating that the city is relatively expensive. This high cost of living, combined with a 5.1% year-over-year increase in price per square foot, makes it challenging for businesses to attract talent without offering wage premiums, as the cost of living is outpacing wage growth.

**Labor Force Growth**
The labor force growth rate in Milwaukee-Waukesha is not available, but the city ranks near the median in terms of labor force growth, suggesting a stable workforce supply. A stable or slightly growing labor force can provide a reliable pool of potential employees for businesses, but the lack of strong growth may limit the city's ability to accommodate rapid expansion.

**Building Permits**
The year-over-year change in building permits in Milwaukee-Waukesha is -10.47%, indicating a tightening of the housing supply. This decline in building permits suggests that the city's housing market may become less affordable in the future, potentially making it more difficult for workers to relocate to the area and for businesses to attract talent.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 33 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.5%, ranking in the 35th percentile. This indicates a relatively slow market, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city, as they have more time to find a home. However, this slower market may also signal weaker demand for housing, which could have implications for the local economy.

**Office Economy**
The share of professional and office workers in Milwaukee-Waukesha is 0.60, ranking in the 4th percentile, indicating a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but may be more appropriate for industries with different workforce needs.

The Milwaukee-Waukesha metro area offers a relatively stable economic environment, with moderate wage growth and low unemployment, but it is constrained by a high cost of living and weak labor demand. The single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the challenge of attracting and retaining talent due to the high cost of living and limited professional talent pool, which may require wage premiums to compete effectively.

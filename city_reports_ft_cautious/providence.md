# Providence-Warwick

**Grade: B (Average) | 50.8th percentile | May 2026**

---

The Providence-Warwick metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.8th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its above-average labor demand and strong building permit growth. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.69, combining a +0.60% employment growth rate and a -0.451% weekly hours deviation, signals a genuine demand expansion. Additionally, the +132.32% year-over-year change in building permits indicates a rapidly expanding housing supply.

**Labor Demand**
The employment growth rate of +0.60% and weekly hours deviation of -0.451% suggest that while jobs are being added, hours are slightly below trend, indicating a moderate demand expansion. This combination signals genuine demand, rather than a survivor squeeze, as hours are not significantly above trend during job growth. The labor demand composite score of 4.69 ranks in the 65th percentile, indicating above-average labor demand.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate of 4.30% is below the national average, ranking in the 36th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This tight market implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, with potential upward pressure on wages. As a result, companies may need to offer competitive salaries to attract top talent.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate of +3.77% is near the median, ranking in the 51st percentile, indicating moderate wage growth. This rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a manageable pace, while workers are experiencing modest increases in purchasing power. As a result, businesses should factor in moderate labor cost increases when planning for the future.

**Cost of Living**
The cost of living in Providence-Warwick, with a PSF of $359/sqft and average hourly earnings of $36.75, results in a ratio of 9.77, ranking in the 0th percentile, indicating a very expensive city. This low affordability score means that the city is at a disadvantage in attracting talent without offering wage premiums to offset the high cost of living.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force is growing at a rate of +1.69% year-over-year, ranking in the 77th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This growth suggests that the city has a strong hiring capacity, with a increasing number of workers available to fill job openings. As a result, businesses can expect a relatively easy time finding qualified candidates.

**Building Permits**
The year-over-year change in building permits is +132.32%, ranking in the 98th percentile, indicating a rapidly expanding housing supply. This strong growth in permits suggests that housing affordability may improve in the future, making it easier for workers to relocate to the city. As a result, businesses can expect a more accessible and affordable housing market for their employees.

**Days on Market**
The current median days on market is 27 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -6.9%, ranking in the 2nd percentile, indicating a very competitive market. This fast-paced market means that workers relocating to the city may face challenges in finding available housing, as homes are selling quickly. As a result, businesses may need to provide relocation assistance to help employees navigate the competitive housing market.

**Office Economy**
The share of professional and office workers is 3.11, ranking in the 65th percentile, indicating a deep talent pool suited for tech, finance, and consulting businesses. This city is well-suited for businesses that require a highly skilled and educated workforce, but may not be as attractive for industries with more manual or logistical labor requirements.

The Providence-Warwick metro area offers businesses a strong labor demand and expanding housing supply, making it an attractive location for companies looking to grow. However, the city's high cost of living and competitive housing market pose significant risks, and businesses should carefully consider these factors when making location decisions, potentially needing to offer wage premiums or relocation assistance to attract and retain top talent.

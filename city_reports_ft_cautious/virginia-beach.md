# Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk

**Grade: B- (Below Average) | 46.1th percentile | June 2026**

---

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it at the 46.1th percentile out of 50 US metros. This city's economic character is most defined by its weak labor demand, with a labor demand composite score in the bottom tier at the 4th percentile, and strong wage growth, with a year-over-year increase of 6.78% in hourly earnings. The combination of these metrics suggests a complex economic environment.

This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles, rather than genuine demand expansion. The labor demand composite score of 1.78 is in the bottom tier, indicating a challenging environment for businesses looking to expand their workforce.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in this metro area is 3.50%, which is near the median at the 54th percentile. This suggests that the labor market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, the relatively low unemployment rate still indicates some competition for talent, and businesses may need to offer competitive wages to attract workers.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth in this metro area is 6.78%, which is in the top tier at the 88th percentile. This rapid wage growth signals rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing purchasing power for workers. Businesses should be prepared for higher labor costs, but may also benefit from a more affluent local consumer base.

This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, which could make it harder to attract talent without offering wage premiums. This contraction in labor supply signals a structural headwind for hiring, making it more challenging for businesses to find the talent they need. As the labor force shrinks, businesses may need to offer more competitive wages or benefits to attract workers.

This rapid expansion of housing supply signals improving affordability and a more accommodating environment for workers. As housing supply increases, businesses may find it easier to attract and retain talent.

This fast-paced market makes it challenging for relocating workers to find housing, as homes are selling quickly. The low days on market ranking at the 8th percentile indicates a competitive housing market that may require workers to act quickly to secure a home.

**Office Economy**
The share of professional and office workers in this metro area is relatively low, ranking at the 20th percentile. This suggests that the city has a less deep talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a high concentration of tech, finance, or consulting talent. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses in other sectors, such as logistics or manufacturing.

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metro area offers businesses a complex economic environment with strong wage growth, but also significant challenges in labor demand and affordability. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force, which may require businesses to offer more competitive wages or benefits to attract and retain talent.

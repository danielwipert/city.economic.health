# Virginia Beach — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metropolitan area ranks 37th in a recent economic assessment, with an overall weighted percentile score of 52.3, placing it in the "Average" category. The unemployment rate of 3.6% is in line with the national average, indicating a stable labor market. However, the labor force participation rate of 58.99% is lower than the national average, suggesting a potential underutilization of the workforce.

Earnings growth over the past year has been 10.22%, placing the region in the 98th percentile for this metric. This suggests strong wage pressures or productivity gains. Housing market indicators show mixed trends. The housing price index has risen by 18.58% year-over-year, placing the region in the 84th percentile. Building permits have declined by 7.61% year-over-year, which could indicate a slowdown in new construction or a shift in development priorities.

The cost of living has increased by 4.52%, and the overall wage replacement ratio is 2.2, both of which are within moderate ranges. Weekly hours worked average 33.1 hours, which is relatively low and may suggest part-time employment or flexible work arrangements. These data points collectively indicate a region with a stable but not exceptionally strong economic performance, characterized by robust earnings growth and a tight housing market, but with some indicators of labor market slack and a decline in new construction activity.

The Virginia Beach-Chesapeake-Norfolk metropolitan area ranks 37th in a recent economic assessment, with an overall weighted percentile score of 52.3, placing it in the "Average" category. Earnings growth over the past year has been robust, increasing by 10.22%, which places the region in the 98th percentile for this metric, suggesting strong wage pressures or productivity gains.

Housing market indicators show mixed trends. The housing price index has risen by 18.58% year-over-year, placing the region in the 84th percentile, which may reflect strong demand or limited supply. In contrast, building permits have declined by 7.61% year-over-year, which could indicate a slowdown in new construction or a shift in development priorities. The cost of living has increased by 4.52%, and the overall wage replacement ratio stands at 2.2, both of which are within moderate ranges. Weekly hours worked average 33.1 hours, which is relatively low and may suggest part-time employment or flexible work arrangements.

These data points collectively suggest a region with a stable but not exceptionally strong economic performance, characterized by robust earnings growth and a tight housing market, but with some indicators of labor market slack and a decline in new construction activity.

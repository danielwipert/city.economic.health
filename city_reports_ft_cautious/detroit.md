The economic data for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area presents a mixed picture, with some indicators showing relative strength while others indicate areas of concern. The unemployment rate stands at 4.7%, placing it in the 14th percentile nationally, which suggests that the local labor market is performing below average compared to other regions. The labor force participation rate of 61.27% is similarly positioned in the 22nd percentile, indicating a moderate level of engagement in the workforce. Earnings growth over the past year has been negative at -1.13%, ranking in the 4th percentile, which may reflect challenges in wage growth or employment composition.

On the housing front, the annual change in home prices is positive at 3.75%, placing the region in the 40th percentile, suggesting a modest appreciation in property values. However, the annual change in single-family housing starts is more robust, increasing by 15.2% and ranking in the 74th percentile, which could indicate a growing demand for new housing. The cost of living index is relatively high at 3.12, placing it in the 80th percentile, which may affect the purchasing power of residents. The median number of days on the market for homes is 63 days, in the 66th percentile, suggesting a balanced market where neither buyers nor sellers have a significant advantage.

Overall, the data indicates a regional economy with both strengths and weaknesses, reflecting a below-average performance according to the composite grade.

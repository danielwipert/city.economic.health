## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area presents a mixed picture, with some indicators showing positive trends while others lag behind national averages. The unemployment rate stands at 4.3%, placing the region in the 48th percentile nationally, suggesting a labor market that is neither particularly strong nor weak compared to other metropolitan areas. Labor force participation is lower than average, at 60.93%, which may reflect broader structural issues or demographic factors not captured by this dataset alone.

Earnings growth over the past year has been solid, increasing by 4.58%, placing the region in the 56th percentile. This moderate growth in earnings is accompanied by a cost of living increase of 4.75%, indicating that wage gains are keeping pace with inflation, though not exceeding it significantly. Housing market indicators show a more dynamic trend, with housing prices rising by 3.53% year-over-year, and building permits up by 57.77%, a significant increase that places the region in the 88th percentile. However, the number of days properties remain on the market has increased to 42 days, which could suggest a cooling in demand or an oversupply of homes in certain segments of the market.

Overall, the data suggests a regional economy that is experiencing moderate growth across several key indicators, with notable activity in the housing sector.

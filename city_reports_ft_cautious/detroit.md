## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area presents a mixed picture, with some indicators showing modest improvement while others lag behind national trends. The unemployment rate stands at 4.8%, placing the region in the 18th percentile nationally, which suggests a labor market that is performing below average but remains stable. Labor force participation is at 60.73%, also in the 18th percentile, indicating a relatively low engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Year-over-year earnings growth is reported at 2.74%, situating the region in the 22nd percentile, which may reflect a gradual increase in wages but at a pace slower than many other areas.

Housing market indicators show more variability. Building permits have increased by 57.77% year-over-year, a significant rise that places the region in the 88th percentile, suggesting robust construction activity. However, the housing price index has grown by only 3.5% over the same period, in the 38th percentile, which indicates a more moderate appreciation in home values. The number of days properties remain on the market is 44, in the 82nd percentile, pointing to a relatively quick turnover in the housing market. These figures, combined with a 0.93% decline in permits for single-family homes, may reflect a complex interplay of supply and demand dynamics in the local real estate sector.

Overall, the data suggests that while Detroit-Warren-Dearborn is experiencing some positive economic developments, particularly in construction, broader labor market metrics indicate ongoing challenges.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area presents a mixed picture, with some indicators showing improvement while others lag behind national trends. The unemployment rate stands at 4.8%, placing it in the 18th percentile among comparable regions, which suggests that the labor market is relatively tight but not exceptionally so. The labor force participation rate of 60.73% also ranks in the 18th percentile, indicating a moderate level of engagement in the workforce. Year-over-year earnings growth is at 3.04%, situated in the 48th percentile, suggesting a modest increase in income levels.

Housing market dynamics show a more pronounced trend. Building permits have increased by 57.77% year-over-year, placing this metric in the 88th percentile, which indicates a robust level of construction activity. The housing price index has risen by 3.7% over the same period, ranking in the 36th percentile, suggesting a steady but not overly aggressive appreciation in home values. However, the supply of homes for sale has decreased by 0.93% year-over-year, placing it in the 46th percentile, which may reflect a tightening in the available inventory. The average days on market for properties is 44 days, in the 82nd percentile, indicating a relatively quick turnover in the housing market.

Overall, the data suggests that Detroit's economy is experiencing a range of conditions, with some areas showing strength and others indicating room for improvement.

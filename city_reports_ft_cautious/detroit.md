## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic indicators for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area present a mixed picture, reflecting both areas of strength and challenges. The unemployment rate stands at 4.3%, which places the region at the 48th percentile nationally, suggesting a labor market that is neither exceptionally strong nor weak compared to other metropolitan areas. Labor force participation is lower, at 60.93%, placing it in the 22nd percentile, which may indicate a relatively smaller share of the population actively engaged in the labor market. Earnings growth year-over-year is reported at 4.58%, ranking in the 56th percentile, indicating moderate wage growth that aligns with broader national trends.

Housing market indicators show some positive signs, with building permits up 57.77% year-over-year, a significant increase that ranks in the 88th percentile, suggesting robust construction activity. However, the housing price index has grown by only 3.7% year-over-year, placing it in the 36th percentile, which may reflect a more tempered appreciation in home values. The number of single-family homes for sale has decreased by 1.09% year-over-year, while the average days on market remain at 42 days, both metrics falling within the middle range of national performance, indicating a balanced but not overly dynamic housing market.

Overall, the data suggests that Detroit-Warren-Dearborn's economy is experiencing moderate growth across various sectors, with notable activity in construction and employment, but with labor force participation lagging behind.

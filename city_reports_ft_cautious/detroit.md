## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area presents a mixed picture. The unemployment rate stands at 4.7%, placing it in the 14th percentile nationally, which suggests a relatively low level of joblessness compared to other regions. However, the labor force participation rate of 61.27% is in the 22nd percentile, indicating a lower proportion of the working-age population is engaged in the labor market. Year-over-year earnings have decreased by 1.13%, ranking in the 4th percentile, which may reflect challenges in wage growth within the region.

Housing market indicators show some positive trends. Building permits have increased by 15.2% year-over-year, placing the region in the 74th percentile, suggesting a robust construction sector. The housing price index has risen by 3.75% over the same period, although this is in the 40th percentile, indicating moderate appreciation. The cost of living index is at 3.12, in the 80th percentile, suggesting that living costs in Detroit are relatively high compared to other areas. The median number of days on the market for homes is 63 days, in the 66th percentile, which indicates a balanced market with neither a significant buyer nor seller advantage.

Overall, the data suggests a region with stable but not particularly dynamic economic conditions.

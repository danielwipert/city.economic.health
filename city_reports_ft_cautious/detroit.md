## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Detroit-Warren-Dearborn metropolitan area presents a mixed picture, with some indicators showing modest growth while others lag behind national trends. The unemployment rate stands at 4.8%, placing it in the 18th percentile, which suggests that labor market conditions are relatively weak compared to other metropolitan areas. The labor force participation rate of 60.73% also ranks in the 18th percentile, indicating a lower level of engagement in the workforce. Earnings growth year-over-year is at 3.04%, situated in the 48th percentile, suggesting moderate wage increases that are neither particularly strong nor weak.

Housing market indicators show a more positive trend. The year-over-year change in housing prices is 3.7%, ranking in the 36th percentile, which indicates a steady but not robust increase in home values. Notably, building permits have increased by 57.77% year-over-year, placing this metric in the 88th percentile, a significant rise that may reflect growing construction activity. However, the year-over-year change in single-family home sales is down by 0.93%, in the 46th percentile, suggesting a slight decline in the pace of home sales. The average days on market for homes is 44 days, in the 82nd percentile, which could indicate a relatively efficient market for property transactions.

Overall, the data suggests a region with varied economic performance, with strengths in housing construction and efficiency in property sales, but challenges in labor market participation and employment.

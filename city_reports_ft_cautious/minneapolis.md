# Minneapolis — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

The Minneapolis-St.St. Paul-Bloomington metropolitan area's economic profile is characterised by a mix of indicators. The unemployment rate stands at 4.0%, placing it in the 34th percentile among comparable regions. The labour force participation rate of 69.03% is high, ranking in the 88th percentile. Year-over-year earnings growth is 3.0%, which is in the 10th percentile.

In the housing sector, the year-over-year change in housing permits has declined by 22.23%, placing the region in the 18th percentile. The housing price index has increased by 2.74% over the same period, ranking in the 32nd percentile. The days-on-market metric stands at 40.0 days, in the 80th percentile. Weekly hours worked have decreased to 33.3 hours, in the 22nd percentile.

The data suggests a mixed economic performance, with labour force participation and housing stability showing some strengths, but earnings growth and housing construction facing challenges.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Minneapolis-St. Paul-Bloomington metropolitan area exhibits a mixed economic profile based on recent data. The unemployment rate stands at 4.0%, placing it in the 34th percentile among comparable regions, which suggests a labor market that is neither particularly robust nor weak relative to peers. The labor force participation rate of 69.03% is notably high, ranking in the 88th percentile, indicating a strong engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Year-over-year earnings growth is modest at 3.0%, placing it in the 10th percentile, which may reflect a slower pace of wage increases compared to other areas.

In the housing sector, the year-over-year change in housing permits has declined by 22.23%, a significant drop that places the region in the 18th percentile, suggesting a slowdown in residential construction activity. The housing price index has increased by 2.74% over the same period, ranking in the 32nd percentile, which indicates a relatively stable but not rapidly appreciating housing market. The days-on-market metric stands at 40.0 days, in the 80th percentile, suggesting that properties are taking longer to sell compared to many other markets. Weekly hours worked have decreased slightly to 33.3 hours, in the 22nd percentile, which may reflect changes in employment patterns or work schedules.

Overall, the data suggests a mixed economic performance with strengths in labor force participation and housing stability, but challenges in earnings growth and housing construction.

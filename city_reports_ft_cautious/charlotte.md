# Charlotte — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

The economic data for the Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metropolitan area presents a mixed picture. The unemployment rate of 3.6% places it at the 50th percentile nationally, indicating a labour market that is neither particularly strong nor weak compared to other regions. The labour force participation rate of 65.43% is above the national median, suggesting a relatively high level of workforce engagement. However, earnings growth has been negative over the past year, declining by 1.52%.

In the housing sector, the year-over-year change in home prices is modest, increasing by 0.64%. The number of building permits has declined by 26.68% compared to the previous year. This decline could indicate a slowdown in construction activity or a shift in demand for new housing. The days-on-market metric stands at 69 days, which is relatively high, suggesting that properties may be taking longer to sell. The overall cost of living index is 1.25, placing it in the 86th percentile, indicating that Charlotte is more expensive than many other metropolitan areas.

The weighted percentile score of 41.6 and the grade of B- suggest that Charlotte's economic performance is below average when compared to other metropolitan areas. Notable strengths include labour force participation and cost of living, while weaknesses are evident in earnings growth and housing market dynamics.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metropolitan area reveals a mixed picture, with some indicators showing relative strength while others point to areas of concern. The unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, placing it at the 50th percentile nationally, suggesting a labor market that is neither particularly robust nor weak compared to other regions. The labor force participation rate of 65.43% is above the national median, indicating a relatively high level of engagement in the workforce. However, earnings growth has been negative over the past year, declining by 1.52%, which may reflect broader economic challenges or specific local factors affecting income levels.

In the housing sector, the year-over-year change in home prices is modest, increasing by 0.64%, while the number of building permits has declined significantly by 26.68% compared to the previous year. This decline in permits could indicate a slowdown in construction activity or a shift in demand for new housing. The days-on-market metric stands at 69 days, which is relatively high, suggesting that properties are taking longer to sell, possibly due to a more balanced market or reduced buyer interest. The overall cost of living index is 1.25, placing it in the 86th percentile, indicating that Charlotte is more expensive than many other metropolitan areas.

The weighted percentile score of 41.6 and the grade of B- suggest that Charlotte's economic performance is below average when compared to other metropolitan areas, with notable strengths in labor force participation and cost of living, but weaknesses in earnings growth and housing market dynamics.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metropolitan area, centered around Charlotte, presents a mixed picture. The unemployment rate stands at 3.8%, which is relatively low and places the region in the 64th percentile, suggesting a stable labor market. The labor force participation rate is 65.51%, also in the 64th percentile, indicating a moderate level of engagement in the workforce. However, earnings have decreased by 1.15% year-over-year, placing the region in the 8th percentile, which may reflect a decline in wage growth or job quality. The cost of living index is 1.37, significantly above the national average, and ranks in the 86th percentile, suggesting higher living costs compared to other regions. The average weekly hours worked is 32.9, in the 4th percentile, indicating shorter working hours, which could be a result of part-time employment or flexible work arrangements.

Housing indicators show a modest increase in home prices by 0.73% year-over-year, placing the region in the 28th percentile, and a decrease in permits for new housing units by 15.37% year-over-year, in the 36th percentile, which may reflect a slowdown in construction activity. The median days on market for homes is 62 days, in the 30th percentile, suggesting a balanced market with neither a strong buyer's nor a strong seller's advantage. The overall weighted percentile score of 46.5 and a grade of B- indicate that Charlotte's economic performance is below average relative to other metropolitan areas, with strengths in cost of living and labor market stability, but challenges in earnings growth and housing activity.

These patterns suggest a cautiously stable economic environment with areas of both resilience and concern.

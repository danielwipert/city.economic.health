# Charlotte — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

The Charlotte-Concord-Gastonia metropolitan area presents a nuanced economic picture, with both encouraging and cautionary signals. The labour market is characterised by a 3.6% unemployment rate, which has risen by 0.2 percentage points year-over-year, indicating a slight tightening. Meanwhile, the civilian labour force has expanded by 2.28% over the same period, suggesting a steady supply of workers. However, average hourly earnings have increased by only 0.14% year-over-year, a modest gain that may not be keeping pace with inflation.

Business activity in Charlotte is a notable strength, driven by a robust labour demand composite score of 8.46 and a 2.69% year-over-year increase in nonfarm payroll employment, outpacing the national average. The office worker ratio composite score is also high, at 4.18, indicating a strong presence of office-based employment. In contrast, the housing market is showing signs of slowing, with residential building permits declining by 39.5% year-over-year and the median days on market for homes increasing by 13.95% to 49 days.

Charlotte's relatively low cost of living composite score of 3.36 makes it an attractive location for businesses and individuals. However, the labour market is not without stress, as evidenced by a slightly positive weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline of 0.397%. Overall, Charlotte's economy is driven by a strong labour market and business activity, but policymakers must be mindful of emerging stress points in the housing market. The city's ability to balance growth with affordability will be crucial in sustaining its economic momentum.

Looking ahead, Charlotte's economic prospects are generally positive, driven by its strong labour market and business activity. Nevertheless, the city must closely monitor its housing market, where declining residential building permits and increasing days on market may indicate a slowdown. With its competitive cost of living and high office worker ratio, Charlotte remains an attractive location, but addressing potential stress points will be essential to maintaining long-term growth.

However, the civilian labour force has grown at a rate of 2.28% year-over-year, suggesting a steady supply of workers. Average hourly earnings have risen by 0.14% year-over-year, a modest increase that may not be keeping pace with inflation.

In terms of business activity, the labour demand composite score is a notable strength, with a score of 8.46, indicating robust employment and weekly hours worked. Nonfarm payroll employment has grown at a rate of 2.69% year-over-year, outpacing the national average. The office worker ratio composite score is also high, at 4.18, suggesting a strong presence of office-based employment. However, the residential building permits have declined by 39.5% year-over-year, which may indicate a slowdown in the housing market. The median days on market for homes has increased by 13.95% year-over-year, to 49 days, which could be a sign of a cooling housing market.

The cost of living composite score is relatively low, at 3.36, making Charlotte an attractive location for businesses and individuals. However, the weekly hours deviation from the 12-month baseline is slightly positive, at 0.397%, which may indicate some stress in the labour market. Overall, Charlotte's economy appears to be driven by a strong labour market and business activity, but with some potential stress points emerging in the housing market. The city's ability to balance growth with affordability will be crucial in maintaining its economic momentum.

Closing outlook: Charlotte's economic prospects are generally positive, driven by a strong labour market and business activity. However, the city needs to monitor its housing market closely, as declining residential building permits and increasing days on market may indicate a slowdown. With its relatively low cost of living and high office worker ratio, Charlotte remains an attractive location for businesses and individuals, but policymakers must be vigilant in addressing potential stress points to sustain long-term growth.

# Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue

**Grade: B (Average) | 54.2th percentile | June 2026**

---

The Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area has an overall grade of B, ranking in the 54.2th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and office economy. The city's economic character is defined by its top-tier wage growth of 7.37% year-over-year and its significant share of office and professional workers, ranking in the 84th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with a strong knowledge-based economy.

**Labor Demand**
The Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area has an employment growth rate of 0.08% year-over-year and weekly hours 0.67% above its own trend, indicating genuine demand expansion. This combination signals that the city is adding jobs and workers are putting in more hours, suggesting a healthy and growing labor market. The labor demand composite score of 5.08 ranks in the 60th percentile, further supporting the notion of a strong labor market.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is 5.10%, ranking in the 2nd percentile, indicating a very tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as the low unemployment rate puts upward pressure on wages. The tight market implies that employers may need to offer competitive salaries to attract and retain talent.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is 7.37%, ranking in the 96th percentile, indicating fast and strong wage growth. This rapid wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing purchasing power for workers. As a result, businesses may need to budget for higher labor costs, while also benefiting from a workforce with growing disposable income.

**Cost of Living**
The cost of living in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue, with a price-to-salary ratio of 9.50, ranks in the 41st percentile, indicating a near-median affordability level. The fact that the price per square foot is falling by 3.6% year-over-year, at $452/sqft, relative to hourly earnings of $47.57, suggests a relatively stable affordability environment. This means that the city may not have a significant talent attraction advantage due to affordability, as it is neither particularly expensive nor unusually affordable compared to its peers.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is shrinking by 1.17% year-over-year, ranking in the 31st percentile, indicating a contracting labor pool. This decline in labor force growth suggests a structural headwind for hiring, as the supply of potential workers is decreasing. Businesses may need to adapt their hiring strategies to accommodate this shrinking labor pool.

**Building Permits**
The number of residential building permits in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue has increased by 66.30% year-over-year, ranking in the 90th percentile, indicating a significant expansion in housing supply. This rapid growth in permits suggests that the city's housing market is poised for increased affordability and accommodation of its workforce. As a result, businesses may benefit from a more stable and affordable housing environment for their employees.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market for homes in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue is 36 days, with a year-over-year increase of 20.0%, ranking in the 98th percentile. This slow-down in the housing market, with homes taking longer to sell, may indicate a more accessible and less competitive environment for workers relocating to the city. However, this also suggests a potential normalization of the market, rather than a sign of demand erosion.

**Office Economy**
The share of office and professional workers in Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue ranks in the 84th percentile, indicating a deep and talented pool of knowledge-based workers. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, but less ideal for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces. The city's strong office economy is a significant advantage for businesses seeking to tap into a skilled and professional workforce.

In conclusion, Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue offers businesses a strong knowledge-based economy with high wage growth and a deep talent pool, but also presents challenges due to its tight labor market and moderate affordability. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's shrinking labor force, which may require businesses to adapt their hiring strategies and consider the potential impact on their operations.

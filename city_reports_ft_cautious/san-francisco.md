# San Francisco — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

San Francisco's economic indicators paint a complex picture, with both encouraging and cautionary signals. The labour market, while not exceptionally strong, boasts a relatively low unemployment rate of 4.1%, albeit with a slight 0.1 percentage point increase year-over-year. The civilian labour force has expanded modestly, with 0.43% growth, as measured by the 102A_clf_yoy metric. Average hourly earnings have risen by 2.33% year-over-year, indicating moderate upward pressure on wages.

In contrast, the city's housing market and business activity reveal more pronounced trends. San Francisco's high cost of living, reflected in a composite score of 5.77, may deter businesses and individuals from locating there. However, the office worker ratio composite score of 2.22 suggests a robust presence of office workers, contributing to the local economy. The labour demand composite score of 3.18 indicates a sluggish employment market, with nonfarm payroll employment declining by 0.52% year-over-year. A notable exception is the surge in residential building permits, which have increased by 86.01% year-over-year, potentially alleviating housing supply pressures. The median days on market have decreased by 6.45% year-over-year, standing at 29 days, which may indicate a relatively balanced housing market.

Despite these mixed signals, stress points are evident. The high cost of living and slow employment growth may pose long-term challenges for the city's economy. Nevertheless, the strong growth in residential building permits and relatively low unemployment rate suggest that San Francisco's economy has notable strengths. As the city navigates these competing trends, monitoring the interplay between labour market dynamics, housing supply, and business activity will be crucial in assessing the economy's overall health.

Looking ahead, San Francisco's economy is likely to face ongoing challenges related to affordability and labour market growth. However, the surge in residential building permits and moderate wage growth may help mitigate some of these pressures. To ensure a sustainable and inclusive economic trajectory, policymakers and businesses must balance the competing demands of housing supply, labour market development, and cost of living as the city's economic landscape continues to evolve.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

San Francisco's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. The labour market, while not overly robust, shows a relatively low unemployment rate of 4.1%, albeit with a slight increase of 0.1 percentage points year-over-year. The civilian labor force growth, as measured by the 102A_clf_yoy metric, stands at 0.43%, indicating a modest expansion of the workforce supply. Average hourly earnings have grown by 2.33% year-over-year, a moderate increase that suggests some upward pressure on wages.

The city's housing market and business activity, however, reveal more pronounced trends. The cost of living composite score, at 5.77, highlights the persistently high cost of living in San Francisco, which may deter some businesses and individuals from locating there. On the other hand, the office worker ratio composite score of 2.22 suggests a relatively strong presence of office workers, which can contribute to the local economy. The labour demand composite score of 3.18 indicates a somewhat sluggish employment market, with nonfarm payroll employment actually declining by 0.52% year-over-year. A notable bright spot is the surge in residential building permits, which have increased by 86.01% year-over-year, potentially alleviating some of the housing supply pressures. The median days on market have decreased by 6.45% year-over-year, currently standing at 29 days, which may indicate a relatively balanced housing market.

Despite these mixed signals, some stress points are evident. The high cost of living and relatively slow employment growth may pose challenges for the city's economy in the longer term. Nevertheless, the strong growth in residential building permits and the relatively low unemployment rate suggest that San Francisco's economy is not without its strengths. As the city continues to navigate these competing trends, it will be essential to monitor the interplay between labour market dynamics, housing supply, and business activity to gauge the overall health of the economy.

Closing outlook: San Francisco's economy is likely to face ongoing challenges related to affordability and labour market growth, but the surge in residential building permits and moderate wage growth may help to mitigate some of these pressures. As the city's economic landscape continues to evolve, policymakers and businesses will need to balance the competing demands of housing supply, labour market development, and cost of living to ensure a sustainable and inclusive economic trajectory.

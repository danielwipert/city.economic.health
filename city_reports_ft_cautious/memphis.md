# Memphis

**Grade: B- (Below Average) | 48.6th percentile | May 2026**

---

Memphis has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 48.6th percentile out of 50 US metros. The city's economic character is most defined by its low labor demand, with a composite score in the bottom tier, and its high cost of living affordability, ranking in the top tier at 98th percentile. Specifically, the labor demand composite score of 2.94 and the cost of living ratio of $155/sqft to $32.13/hr, with a PSF decline of 6.1% YoY, are key metrics shaping the city's economic landscape.

**Labor Demand**
Memphis has an employment growth rate of -0.03% YoY and weekly hours 1.318% above its own trend, indicating a contraction in labor demand rather than genuine expansion. This combination signals a survivor squeeze, where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. The labor demand composite score of 2.94, ranking in the bottom tier at 18th percentile, further reinforces this interpretation.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Memphis is 4.70%, ranking below average at 31st percentile, indicating some slack in the labor market. This means that businesses trying to hire in Memphis may have an easier time finding candidates, but may also face weaker local consumer demand. The relatively high unemployment rate suggests that the market is not overly tight, giving employers some flexibility in hiring.

**Wage Growth**
Memphis has a year-over-year wage growth rate of 4.99%, ranking above average at 64th percentile, indicating relatively fast wage growth. This implies rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing worker purchasing power. As wages rise, businesses may need to adjust their compensation packages to remain competitive, while workers may see improved living standards.

**Cost of Living**
Memphis is highly affordable, with a cost of living ratio of $155/sqft to $32.13/hr and a PSF decline of 6.1% YoY, ranking in the top tier at 98th percentile. This means that the city has a significant talent attraction advantage, as workers can afford a high standard of living without requiring wage premiums. The declining PSF relative to wages is a key driver of this affordability, making Memphis an attractive location for businesses looking to relocate or expand.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Memphis is growing at a rate of 0.98% YoY, ranking above average at 66th percentile, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This implies that businesses have a growing pool of potential candidates to hire from, making it easier to find the right talent. As the labor force continues to grow, Memphis may become an increasingly attractive location for businesses looking to expand their operations.

**Building Permits**
Memphis has seen a 39.08% year-over-year increase in residential building permits, ranking in the top tier at 88th percentile, indicating an expansion in housing supply. This suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation are likely to improve, as new housing developments come online. The increase in building permits is a positive sign for businesses looking to relocate or expand in Memphis, as it indicates a growing and developing city.

**Days on Market**
The current median days on market in Memphis is 54 days, with a year-over-year decline of 3.6%, ranking below average at 24th percentile. This means that homes are selling relatively quickly, making it a competitive market for relocating workers. However, the decline in days on market suggests that the market may be normalizing, making it slightly more accessible for workers to find housing.

**Office Economy**
Memphis has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, ranking in the bottom tier at 2nd percentile, indicating a smaller share of jobs in professional and office sectors. This makes the city less suited for businesses in the tech, finance, or consulting sectors, but more suitable for industries with fewer specialized roles, such as logistics or manufacturing. Businesses looking to establish a headquarters or office operations may find Memphis less attractive due to its limited professional talent pool.

In conclusion, Memphis offers businesses a unique combination of affordability and expanding workforce supply, making it an attractive location for companies looking to relocate or expand. However, the city's low labor demand and limited professional talent pool are significant risks that decision-makers should factor in, as they may impact the ability to find and retain top talent in certain industries.

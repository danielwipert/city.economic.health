# Memphis

**Grade: B (Average) | 50.8th percentile | June 2026**

---

Memphis has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.8th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and highly affordable cost of living, scoring 76th and 98th percentiles respectively. The city's economic character is defined by these two metrics, with wage growth at 6.18% year-over-year and a cost of living ratio of $155/sqft to $32.45/hr, indicating a very affordable environment. This affordability is further underscored by the PSF falling 6.1% year-over-year.

**Labor Demand**
Memphis has an employment growth rate of -0.11% and weekly hours 1.898% above its own trend, resulting in a labor demand composite score of 2.65, which ranks in the bottom tier at the 14th percentile. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, as the slight increase in hours worked does not offset the decline in employment growth, potentially indicating a survivor squeeze where remaining workers absorb the load of eliminated roles. This scenario suggests that while there may be some demand for labor, it is not strong enough to drive significant job growth.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Memphis is 4.20%, ranking near the median at the 58th percentile. This rate indicates a relatively balanced market, neither too tight nor too slack, suggesting that businesses may face moderate challenges in hiring but without extreme wage pressure. For a business trying to hire in Memphis, this means they can expect to find suitable candidates without overly inflated wage expectations, but may still need to compete to attract top talent.

**Wage Growth**
With a year-over-year wage growth rate of 6.18%, Memphis experiences above-average wage increases, ranking in the 76th percentile. This fast wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers but also indicates strong worker purchasing power, which can be beneficial for local consumer demand. Businesses should factor in these increasing labor costs when considering operations in Memphis.

**Cost of Living**
Memphis has a highly affordable cost of living, with a percentile rank of 98th, driven by a PSF to earnings ratio of $155/sqft to $32.45/hr, and a year-over-year decrease in PSF of 6.1%. This affordability advantage means that businesses can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums to offset high living costs, making Memphis an attractive location for companies looking to minimize labor costs while still offering a high quality of life.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Memphis is shrinking at a rate of -0.76% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 24th percentile. This contraction in labor force supply signals a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of potential workers is decreasing. Businesses should be aware that finding and retaining talent may become increasingly challenging in this environment.

**Building Permits**
Memphis has seen a significant increase in residential building permits, with a year-over-year change of +39.08%, ranking in the top tier at the 88th percentile. This expansion in housing supply suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation are likely to improve, as more housing options become available. This trend is positive for businesses considering relocation, as it indicates a potential easing of housing costs and increased attractiveness for relocating workers.

**Days on Market**
The current median days on market in Memphis is 54 days, with a year-over-year decrease of -3.6%. This relatively fast market, ranking below average at the 24th percentile, means that homes are selling quickly, which can be challenging for relocating workers. However, the slight decrease in days on market may indicate a normalization of the housing market, potentially making it slightly more accessible for new residents.

**Office Economy**
Memphis has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, ranking in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses requiring deep knowledge-economy talent, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but may be more appropriate for industries with less specialized labor requirements, like logistics or manufacturing. Decision-makers should consider whether their business needs align with the available talent pool in Memphis.

In conclusion, Memphis offers businesses a unique combination of strong wage growth and an extremely affordable cost of living, making it an attractive location for companies looking to balance labor costs with talent attraction. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force, which may pose significant challenges for hiring and retaining talent in the long term.

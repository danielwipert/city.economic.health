# Portland — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Portland's labour market is showing signs of softening, with the unemployment rate at 4.9%, up 0.8 percentage points from a year ago, and minimal workforce expansion, as the civilian labour force has grown just 0.04% year-on-year. The employment trends are notably weak, with nonfarm payrolls declining 0.69% over the past year and a labour demand composite score of 3.33, placing Portland in the 14th percentile nationally. This reflects subdued hiring and a 0.58% shortfall in average weekly hours relative to its 12-month baseline. Although wage growth is modest at 3.94%, it is in line with national norms, offering limited relief to the broader labour market stagnation.

The housing market also indicates persistent imbalance. Median days on market have decreased by 3.9% from a year ago to 49 days, and the composite score ranks in the 16th percentile, suggesting sluggish turnover despite some improvement in listing velocity. New construction has slowed sharply, with residential permits down 18.14% year-on-year, indicating constrained supply growth. The cost of living, particularly housing affordability, remains a structural challenge, with a cost-of-living composite score of 2.02, ranking in the 67th percentile and indicating above-average pressure relative to income. Furthermore, office demand remains depressed, with a low office worker ratio score of 1.3, placing Portland in the 12th percentile and highlighting the incomplete recovery in commercial real estate.

Looking ahead, Portland faces near-term challenges in employment and housing supply, with limited labour demand and construction activity constraining growth. Without a rebound in business investment or meaningful supply-side housing reforms, the city is at risk of prolonged subpar economic performance relative to peer metros.

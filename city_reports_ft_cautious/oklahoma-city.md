# Oklahoma City — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Oklahoma City's labour market is exhibiting modest signs of strain, despite otherwise stable economic conditions. With an unemployment rate of 3.6%, representing a 0.8 percentage point year-over-year increase, the job market is showing slight softening. This is further evidenced by sluggish labour force growth of 0.23% year-over-year, indicating limited expansion in workforce supply. However, employment growth remains positive, at 0.52% year-over-year, supported by a strong labour demand composite score of 6.11, which reflects resilient hiring and stable weekly hours - up 1.08% above the 12-month baseline. Solid wage growth of 4.75% year-over-year is outpacing inflation, thereby supporting household purchasing power.

In the housing market, dynamics suggest a rebalancing is underway. A 20.14% year-over-year surge in residential building permits reflects strong builder confidence and a supply-side response. Meanwhile, homes are taking longer to sell, with an average of 55 days, representing a 17% increase from a year ago, consistent with a cooling in demand. One of the city's key strengths remains its cost of living, with a favourable cost-of-living ratio of 1.17, ranking in the 90th percentile nationally, and allowing residents to retain more disposable income relative to housing and basic expenses. Office demand, as measured by the office worker ratio of 2.64, is mid-tier, suggesting moderate pressure from remote work shifts.

Looking ahead, Oklahoma City's economic foundation remains solid, underpinned by affordable living, steady wage growth, and active construction. While near-term labour market softness is apparent, it is not alarming. The city appears to be transitioning from a post-pandemic boom to a more sustainable growth path, with housing supply catching up to demand. Barring external shocks, a gradual stabilisation is expected.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Oklahoma City’s labour market shows modest strain amid otherwise stable economic conditions. The unemployment rate stands at 3.6% (101A_unemployment), a 0.8 percentage point increase year-over-year (101A_unemp_yoy_pp), suggesting a slight softening in job market tightness. This is corroborated by sluggish labour force growth of just 0.23% YoY (102A_clf_yoy), indicating limited expansion in workforce supply. However, employment growth remains positive at 0.52% YoY (107E_employment_growth_yoy), supported by a strong labour demand composite score (107E_ldc_composite: 6.11), which reflects resilient hiring and stable weekly hours—up 1.08% above the 12-month baseline (107E_wh_trend_deviation_pct). Wage growth is solid at 4.75% YoY (103B_earnings_yoy), outpacing inflation in recent quarters and supporting household purchasing power.

Housing market dynamics point to a rebalancing. Residential building permits surged 20.14% YoY (200B_permits_yoy), reflecting strong builder confidence and supply-side response. Homes are taking longer to sell—55 days on average (204A_dom_level_days), up 17% from a year ago (204A_dom_yoy_pct)—consistent with a cooling in demand. The cost of living remains a key strength: Oklahoma City’s cost-of-living ratio (104C_col: 1.17) ranks favourably, placing it in the 90th percentile nationally, meaning residents retain more disposable income relative to housing and basic expenses. Office demand, as proxied by the office worker ratio (105C_owr: 2.64), is mid-tier, suggesting only moderate pressure from remote work shifts.

**Outlook:** Oklahoma City maintains a solid economic foundation, supported by affordable living, steady wage growth, and active construction. Near-term labour market softness is evident, but not alarming. The city is likely transitioning from a post-pandemic boom to a more sustainable growth path, with housing supply catching up to demand. Absent external shocks, gradual stabilisation is expected.

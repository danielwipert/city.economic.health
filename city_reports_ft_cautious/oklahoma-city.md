# Oklahoma City

**Grade: B (Average) | 50.2th percentile | April 2026**

---

Oklahoma City has an overall grade of B, ranking 50.2th percentile out of 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and affordable cost of living, scoring 81st and 93rd percentiles respectively. The city's wage growth rate of 6.03% and cost of living ratio of $172/sqft to $33.85/hr, with a PSF ratio of 5.08, are key defining metrics. These numbers indicate a city with a unique blend of labor market characteristics.

**Labor Demand**
Oklahoma City's employment growth rate is -0.90% year-over-year, while weekly hours are 0.467% above trend, signaling a survivor squeeze where remaining workers are absorbing the load of eliminated roles. This combination indicates that despite some job losses, the existing workforce is shouldering more hours, suggesting a degree of labor market tightness. The labor demand composite score of 2.57 falls in the bottom tier, at the 8th percentile.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Oklahoma City is 4.10%, ranking below average at the 33rd percentile, indicating a labor market with some slack. This means that businesses may find it slightly easier to hire in this market compared to tighter labor markets, but may also face less wage pressure. The relatively higher unemployment rate suggests that the market is not as competitive as those with lower unemployment rates.

**Wage Growth**
With a year-over-year wage growth rate of 6.03%, Oklahoma City experiences fast wage growth, ranking in the top tier at the 81st percentile. This rapid wage growth implies rising labor costs for employers but also means workers have increasing purchasing power, which can be beneficial for local consumer demand. Businesses should factor in these rising labor costs when considering relocation or expansion.

**Cost of Living**
Oklahoma City is highly affordable, with a cost of living score in the top tier at the 93rd percentile, driven by a PSF of $172/sqft that is decreasing by 1.1% year-over-year relative to hourly earnings of $33.85. This affordability advantage means that the city can attract talent without needing to offer significant wage premiums, making it an attractive location for businesses looking to minimize labor costs.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Oklahoma City is growing at a rate of 0.96% year-over-year, indicating an expanding workforce supply. This positive growth rate suggests that the labor pool is not a structural headwind for hiring, providing businesses with a growing talent base to draw from. The implication is that businesses can more easily find the workers they need.

**Building Permits**
Residential building permits in Oklahoma City are increasing by 20.14% year-over-year, signaling an expansion in future housing supply. This growth in permits is a positive indicator for future affordability and workforce accommodation, suggesting that the city is likely to remain attractive to workers and businesses alike due to its ability to absorb growing demand for housing.

**Days on Market**
Homes in Oklahoma City are currently sitting on the market for a median of 55 days, with a year-over-year increase of 17.0%, placing it near the median at the 50th percentile. This indicates a relatively balanced market that is neither extremely competitive nor overly slow, making it accessible for relocating workers to find housing without facing an extremely heated market.

**Office Economy**
Oklahoma City has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, ranking in the bottom tier at the 13th percentile. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses requiring deep knowledge-economy talent, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms, but may be more appropriate for industries with different workforce needs.

In conclusion, Oklahoma City offers businesses a unique combination of strong wage growth, an affordable cost of living, and an expanding labor force, making it an attractive location for certain types of businesses. However, the city's relatively weak labor demand and shallow professional talent pool are significant risks that decision-makers should carefully consider when evaluating Oklahoma City as a potential location for their business operations.

# Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim

**Grade: B+ (Above Average) | 55.9th percentile | June 2026**

---

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area has earned an overall grade of B+ with a composite score ranking at the 55.9th percentile among 50 US metros. The labor demand composite score of 6.14 further supports this, ranking in the top tier at the 86th percentile.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area stands at 4.80%, ranking below average at the 36th percentile. This indicates that the job market has some slack, making it slightly easier for businesses to hire compared to tighter markets. However, the relatively moderate unemployment rate also suggests that local consumer demand may not be as strong as in areas with lower unemployment rates.

**Wage Growth**
Year-over-year wage growth in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area is 4.84%, placing it above average at the 60th percentile. This rate of wage growth is relatively fast, implying rising labor costs for employers but also increased purchasing power for workers. Businesses should anticipate higher wage bills but also a workforce with enhanced consumer spending potential.

This contraction in labor force supply poses a structural headwind for hiring, as businesses may face challenges in finding sufficient skilled workers to fill open positions.

This places the city near the median at the 58th percentile for days on market. For workers relocating to this city, the market is relatively competitive but not overly challenging, indicating a balanced environment for buyers and sellers.

**Office Economy**
The share of jobs in professional and office sectors in the Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area is above average, ranking at the 60th percentile. This indicates a deep talent pool suited for businesses in tech, finance, consulting, and HQ decisions, making it an attractive location for knowledge-economy businesses but less ideal for those requiring large industrial or logistics workforces.

The Los Angeles-Long Beach-Anaheim metro area offers businesses a strong labor market with genuine demand expansion and a rapidly expanding housing supply, which are significant advantages for attracting and accommodating a workforce. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the relatively high cost of living, which could necessitate wage premiums to attract top talent, potentially increasing labor costs for employers.

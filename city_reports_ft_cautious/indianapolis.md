# Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood

**Grade: C+ (Poor) | 44.0th percentile | April 2026**

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area has an overall grade of C+ with a composite score ranking it 44th out of 50 US metros. The employment growth rate in Indianapolis is -0.95% year-over-year, and weekly hours are deviating -0.372% from the city's own 12-month baseline. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is experiencing a decline in job growth and hours worked. This suggests that businesses may face less competition for workers, but also may need to consider the potential for a shrinking workforce.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Indianapolis is 2.90%, ranking it in the top tier at the 96th percentile. This indicates a very tight labor market with little slack, making it challenging for businesses to hire new workers. As a result, businesses may need to offer higher wages or other incentives to attract and retain talent in this competitive market.

**Wage Growth**
The year-over-year wage growth rate in Indianapolis is +2.01%, ranking it below average at the 25th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. While this may be beneficial for workers' purchasing power, it also means that businesses may need to budget for increasing labor costs.

**Cost of Living**
Indianapolis has a cost of living ranking in the bottom tier at the 6th percentile, with a PSF to wages ratio of 5.03 and a year-over-year increase in PSF of 6.3%. This indicates that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, making it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. The rising PSF suggests that housing costs are outpacing wage growth, which may further exacerbate the affordability issue.

**Labor Force Growth**

This expansion in labor force supply suggests that the city has a growing pool of potential workers, which could benefit businesses looking to hire. However, the slow employment growth rate may limit the demand for these new workers.

This decrease in building permits suggests that the housing supply is tightening, which may lead to increased competition for housing and higher costs for workers. This could further exacerbate the affordability issue and make it more challenging for businesses to attract and retain talent.

**Days on Market**

This suggests that the housing market is slowing down, making it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, the relatively fast pace of sales still indicates a competitive market, and businesses may need to consider the potential for housing costs to rise.

**Office Economy**
Indianapolis has a professional and office worker share ranking in the bottom tier at the 19th percentile, with a composite score of 1.29. This suggests that the city has a relatively shallow talent pool in these sectors, making it less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy workforce, such as tech or finance companies. However, the city may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-dominated economies.

The Indianapolis-Carmel-Greenwood metro area offers businesses a unique combination of a tight labor market and high costs, with a growing labor force supply and a slowing housing market. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for businesses considering this location is the high cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, and the potential for a shrinking workforce due to the contraction in labor demand.

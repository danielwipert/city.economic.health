# Pittsburgh — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Pittsburgh's economic indicators paint a nuanced picture, with both encouraging and cautionary signals. The labour market shows a mixed trend, with an unemployment rate of 3.6%, up 0.3 percentage points year-over-year. Despite this rise, average hourly earnings have grown 8.01% over the same period, indicating a robust wage environment. The civilian labour force has expanded 0.73% year-over-year, pointing to moderate workforce growth.

Business activity in Pittsburgh is characterized by a 1.07% year-over-year increase in nonfarm payroll employment and a labour demand composite score of 6.96, suggesting a relatively strong labour market. The office worker ratio composite score of 3.46 also indicates a favourable business environment. However, the housing market tells a different story, with residential building permits declining 10.55% year-over-year and median days on market rising 4.84% to 65 days, potentially signalling a slowdown.

One of Pittsburgh's notable advantages is its relatively low cost of living, with a composite score of 1.0 and a high percentile score of 93.8. Combined with strong earnings growth, this makes the city an attractive location for businesses and workers. While Pittsburgh's economy exhibits some signs of stress, particularly in the housing market, its labour market and business environment remain significant strengths.

Looking ahead, Pittsburgh's economy is expected to continue growing, driven by its strong labour market and favourable business conditions. However, the decline in residential building permits and slowing housing market warrant close attention. With its competitive cost of living and high earnings growth, Pittsburgh is well-positioned to attract businesses and talent, supporting long-term economic expansion.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Pittsburgh's economic indicators present a mixed picture, with both positive and negative signals. On the labour market front, the city's unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, with a year-over-year (YoY) increase of 0.3 percentage points. Although this rise may indicate some short-term labour market stress, the average hourly earnings have grown by 8.01% YoY, suggesting a strong wage environment. The civilian labour force has expanded by 0.73% YoY, indicating a moderate growth in workforce supply.

In terms of business activity, Pittsburgh's nonfarm payroll employment has grown by 1.07% YoY, while the labour demand composite score is 6.96, indicating a relatively strong labour market. The office worker ratio composite score of 3.46 also suggests a favourable business environment. However, the residential building permits have declined by 10.55% YoY, which may indicate some stress in the housing market. The median days on market have increased by 4.84% YoY, reaching 65 days, which could be a sign of a slowing housing market.

The cost of living composite score of 1.0 and the high percentile score of 93.8 for this metric suggest that Pittsburgh has a relatively low cost of living compared to other cities. This, combined with the strong earnings growth, may make the city an attractive location for businesses and workers. Overall, while Pittsburgh's economy shows some signs of stress, particularly in the housing market, its strong labour market and favourable business environment are notable strengths. 

Closing outlook: Pittsburgh's economy is expected to continue growing, driven by its strong labour market and favourable business environment. However, the decline in residential building permits and the slowing housing market may require close monitoring. With its relatively low cost of living and high earnings growth, Pittsburgh is well-positioned to attract businesses and workers, which could support long-term economic growth.

# Pittsburgh

**Grade: B (Average) | 51.5th percentile | May 2026**

---

Pittsburgh has an overall grade of B, ranking 51.5th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its strong wage growth and office economy. The city's economic character is defined by its top-tier wage growth of 6.81% year-over-year and its above-average share of office and professional workers, at 65th percentile. These metrics suggest a city with a strong knowledge economy and rising labor costs.

**Labor Demand**
Pittsburgh's labor demand is characterized by a -0.36% employment growth rate and a -0.841% deviation in weekly hours from its own trend, resulting in a near-median labor demand composite score of 41st percentile. This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, rather than genuine demand expansion or survivor squeeze. The city's labor market is not experiencing significant growth, which may impact businesses looking to expand their workforce.

**Unemployment**
The city's unemployment rate is 4.70%, ranking below average at the 27th percentile, indicating a relatively tight labor market. This means that businesses may face challenges in hiring, as well as upward pressure on wages. In practical terms, companies looking to staff up in Pittsburgh may need to offer competitive salaries to attract top talent.

**Wage Growth**
Pittsburgh's wage growth is a notable 6.81% year-over-year, placing it in the top tier at the 89th percentile. This rapid wage growth signals rising labor costs for employers, but also increasing purchasing power for workers. As a result, businesses may need to budget for higher labor expenses, while also benefiting from a workforce with growing disposable income.

**Cost of Living**
With a cost of living ratio of $171/sqft to $33.43/hr, Pittsburgh ranks above average at the 70th percentile in terms of affordability. This means that the city is relatively expensive compared to its peers, which could make it challenging to attract talent without offering wage premiums. However, the city's strong office economy and wage growth may help offset this disadvantage.

**Labor Force Growth**
The city's labor force is growing at a rate of 0.51% year-over-year, ranking near the median at the 52nd percentile. This modest growth indicates that the workforce supply is expanding, albeit slowly, which should support hiring capacity for businesses. However, the relatively slow growth rate may limit the city's ability to accommodate rapid expansion.

**Building Permits**
Pittsburgh's building permits are declining at a rate of -10.55% year-over-year, ranking below average at the 38th percentile. This decrease in permits suggests that housing supply is tightening, which could lead to future affordability challenges and constraints on workforce accommodation. As a result, businesses may need to consider the potential impact of limited housing options on their ability to attract and retain talent.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market in Pittsburgh is 50 days, with a year-over-year increase of 6.4%, ranking above average at the 60th percentile. This indicates a relatively slow market, which could make it more accessible for workers relocating to the city. However, the rising days on market may also signal a normalization of the housing market, rather than a decline in demand.

**Office Economy**
Pittsburgh's office economy is deep, with a professional and office worker share ranking above average at the 65th percentile. This makes the city well-suited for businesses in the tech, finance, consulting, and HQ sectors, which require a strong knowledge-economy talent pool. However, the city may be less suitable for industries with more industrial or logistics-oriented workforces.

In conclusion, Pittsburgh offers businesses a strong office economy and top-tier wage growth, but also presents challenges in terms of labor market tightness and rising labor costs. The single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the city's relatively expensive cost of living, which may require wage premiums to attract and retain talent, and the tightening housing supply, which could limit workforce accommodation and affordability.

# Pittsburgh — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

Pittsburgh's economic indicators present a mixed picture. The unemployment rate is 3.6%, placing it at the 50th percentile nationally, indicating a moderate labour market condition. Labour force participation is lower at 60.33%, ranking in the 10th percentile, which may reflect broader demographic or regional trends affecting workforce engagement.

Earnings growth year-over-year is 8.65%, scoring in the 96th percentile, indicating significant income gains for those employed. Inflation, as measured by the cost of living index, is 4.18%, and wage growth outpaces this, with a 3.39% increase in real wages over the year.

The housing market shows a complex pattern. Housing prices have increased by 17.94% year-over-year, placing Pittsburgh in the 80th percentile. However, the number of building permits has declined by 13.83% over the same period, suggesting a potential slowdown in new housing construction. The rental market also shows signs of pressure, with rents up 4.21% year-over-year, but properties are taking longer to sell, with an average of 90 days on the market.

Pittsburgh's economic performance appears to be average, with some strengths in employment earnings and housing appreciation, but also challenges in labour force participation and housing supply.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Pittsburgh's economic indicators present a mixed picture, suggesting both areas of strength and concern. The unemployment rate stands at 3.6%, placing it at the 50th percentile nationally, which indicates a moderate labor market condition. Labor force participation is notably lower at 60.33%, ranking in the 10th percentile, which may reflect broader demographic or regional trends affecting workforce engagement. On a more positive note, earnings growth year-over-year is robust at 8.65%, scoring in the 96th percentile, indicating significant income gains for those employed. Inflation, as measured by the cost of living index, is relatively contained at 4.18%, and wage growth outpaces this, with an over-the-year increase of 3.39% in real wages.

Housing market dynamics show a complex pattern. Housing prices have increased by 17.94% year-over-year, a substantial rise that places Pittsburgh in the 80th percentile. However, the number of building permits has declined by 13.83% over the same period, suggesting a potential slowdown in new housing construction. The rental market also shows signs of pressure, with rents up 4.21% year-over-year, but properties are taking longer to sell, with an average of 90 days on the market, the lowest score in the dataset.

Overall, Pittsburgh's economic performance appears to be average, with strengths in employment earnings and housing appreciation, but challenges in labor force participation and housing supply.

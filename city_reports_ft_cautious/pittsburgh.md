## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Pittsburgh's economic indicators present a mixed picture, reflecting both areas of strength and concern. The unemployment rate stands at 3.9%, placing the city slightly above the national average, which may indicate a relatively stable labor market. However, the labor force participation rate of 60.25% is notably lower than many other metropolitan areas, suggesting a potential underutilization of the workforce. On a more positive note, earnings growth year-over-year is robust at 8.36%, significantly outpacing inflation, which stands at 4.2%. This suggests that workers in Pittsburgh are experiencing real wage gains. Additionally, the cost of living appears to be manageable, with a moderate inflation rate, and weekly hours worked are average at 34.2 hours.

In the housing market, the data indicates a complex dynamic. Home prices have increased by 15.05% year-over-year, which is a strong figure and may reflect continued demand in the housing sector. However, the number of building permits has declined by 24.33% over the same period, potentially signaling a slowdown in new construction or a shift in development priorities. The days on market metric stands at 58 days, which is within a typical range and suggests a balanced market where neither buyers nor sellers have a significant advantage. The rental market also shows modest growth, with rents increasing by 4.1% year-over-year.

Overall, Pittsburgh's economic performance appears to be average, with strengths in wage growth and housing appreciation, but challenges in labor force participation and new construction activity.

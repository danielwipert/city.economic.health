## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Pittsburgh's economic indicators present a mixed picture, reflecting both areas of strength and challenges. The unemployment rate stands at 4.7%, placing it in the 24th percentile among comparable metropolitan areas, which suggests a relatively tight labor market but not one that is exceptionally robust. The labor force participation rate of 60.7% is also in the lower quartile, indicating that a significant portion of the working-age population is not actively engaged in the labor market. On a more positive note, earnings growth year-over-year is at 5.7%, ranking in the 76th percentile, which indicates a healthy increase in wages. Similarly, cost of living and wage growth relative to the national average are both in the 76th and 82nd percentiles, respectively, suggesting that Pittsburgh residents may be experiencing improved purchasing power.

Housing market dynamics show contrasting trends. The year-over-year change in housing prices is up 15.05%, placing the region in the 76th percentile, which may reflect strong demand or limited supply. However, building permits have declined by 24.33% year-over-year, ranking in the 20th percentile, which could indicate a slowdown in new construction or a shift in development priorities. The days on market metric stands at 58 days, in the 42nd percentile, suggesting a balanced market where neither buyers nor sellers have a significant advantage. Additionally, the year-over-year change in professional and business services employment is up 4.1%, ranking in the 92nd percentile, which points to a growing sector that may be contributing positively to the local economy.

Overall, Pittsburgh's economic performance appears to be average, with strengths in wage growth and certain sectors of employment, but challenges in labor force participation and housing construction.

# Houston — Economic Overview (Cautious FT Style)

## Polished, cautious FT-style column

Houston's economic indicators present a mixed picture, reflecting both areas of strength and concern. The city's unemployment rate of 4.2% places it in the 30th percentile nationally, indicating a relatively tight labor market. The labor force participation rate of 64.52% is slightly above the median, suggesting a moderate level of workforce engagement. Year-over-year earnings growth of 2.61% is modest, aligning with the 40th percentile, while the cost of living increase of 4.72% is higher, potentially affecting household budgets. The average weekly hours worked, at 36.5 hours, are among the highest in the nation, ranking in the 98th percentile.

In the housing market, the year-over-year increase in home prices of 8.71% is in line with national trends, placing Houston in the 50th percentile. However, building permits have declined by 10.61% year-over-year, suggesting a slowdown in construction activity. The average days on market for homes, at 54 days, are near the median, indicating a balanced market. The year-over-year decline in permits for single-family homes of 2.29% further indicates a cautious approach to new residential development.

Overall, Houston's economic performance, as reflected in these indicators, appears to be stable but not particularly dynamic, with some sectors showing signs of moderation.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

Houston's economic indicators present a mixed picture, reflecting both areas of strength and concern. The unemployment rate stands at 4.2%, placing it in the 30th percentile nationally, which suggests a relatively tight labor market but not one that is exceptionally robust. The labor force participation rate of 64.52% is slightly above the median, indicating a moderate level of engagement in the workforce. Year-over-year earnings growth of 2.61% is modest, aligning with the 40th percentile, while the cost of living increase of 4.72% is higher, potentially squeezing household budgets. The weekly hours worked, at 36.5 hours, are among the highest in the nation, ranking in the 98th percentile, which may reflect either strong demand for labor or limited part-time opportunities.

In the housing market, the year-over-year increase in home prices of 8.71% is in line with national trends, placing Houston in the 50th percentile. However, building permits have declined by 10.61% year-over-year, suggesting a slowdown in construction activity. The days on market for homes, at 54 days, are near the median, indicating a balanced market without significant buyer or seller pressure. The year-over-year decline in permits for single-family homes of 2.29% further indicates a cautious approach to new residential development.

Overall, Houston's economic performance, as reflected in these indicators, appears to be stable but not particularly dynamic, with some sectors showing signs of moderation.

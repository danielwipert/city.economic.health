# Houston — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Houston's labour market is experiencing moderate expansion, with non-farm employment growing 0.43% year-on-year, below the national average but stable. The unemployment rate stands at 4.2%, a 0.2 percentage point increase from a year earlier. Meanwhile, the civilian labour force has expanded 0.93% year-on-year, indicating modest workforce supply growth, while average hourly earnings have risen 4.45%, suggesting persistent wage pressure. A composite score in the 70th percentile, combined with solid employment growth and slightly above-trend weekly hours (0.64% above the 12-month baseline), points to resilient labour demand despite a marginal uptick in unemployment.

In contrast, the housing market is showing signs of emerging imbalances. Residential building permits have declined 12.89% year-on-year, ranking in the 30th percentile, which signals a contraction in new supply at a time when affordability remains relatively strong. The cost of living composite score is favourable, ranking in the 92nd percentile with a 1.08 price-to-earnings ratio. However, housing market liquidity is softening, with median days on market increasing 11.1% year-on-year to 50 days, although this does not yet indicate oversupply. The office market remains weak, with a low office worker ratio composite score in the 8th percentile, reflecting structural challenges related to hybrid work trends and elevated vacancy rates.

Looking ahead, Houston's economy is expected to remain resilient, supported by steady labour demand and strong affordability. However, near-term challenges include constrained housing construction and soft office fundamentals. While the modest rise in unemployment and slowing job growth warrant monitoring, wage growth and labour force expansion suggest underlying stability. Over the longer term, demographic inflows and energy sector investment could drive growth, provided the housing supply responds adequately.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Houston’s labour market shows moderate expansionary pressure, with nonfarm employment growing 0.43% year-on-year—below the national average but stable—while the unemployment rate stands at 4.2%, up 0.2 percentage points from a year earlier. The civilian labour force expanded 0.93% YoY, indicating modest workforce supply growth, and average hourly earnings rose 4.45%, suggesting persistent wage pressure. Labour demand, as reflected in the composite score (70th percentile), is supported by solid employment growth and slightly above-trend weekly hours (0.64% above 12-month baseline), pointing to resilient underlying demand for workers despite a marginal uptick in unemployment.

Housing market dynamics reveal emerging imbalances. Residential building permits have declined 12.89% YoY (30th percentile), signaling a contraction in new supply at a time when affordability remains relatively strong: the cost of living composite score ranks favourably at 92nd percentile (1.08 PSF/earnings ratio). However, housing market liquidity is softening, with median days on market at 50 days, up 11.1% YoY, though not yet indicating oversupply. Office absorption remains weak, as shown by the low office worker ratio composite score (8th percentile), reflecting structural challenges tied to hybrid work trends and elevated vacancy rates.

**Outlook**: Houston’s economy remains resilient, supported by steady labour demand and strong affordability, but near-term headwinds include constrained housing construction and soft office fundamentals. The modest rise in unemployment amid slowing job growth warrants monitoring, though wage growth and labour force expansion suggest underlying stability. Over the longer term, demographic inflows and energy sector investment could reinforce growth, provided housing supply responds.

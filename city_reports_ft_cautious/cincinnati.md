# Cincinnati

**Grade: A (Very Good) | 63.2th percentile | June 2026**

---

Cincinnati earns an overall grade of A, ranking in the 63.2th percentile among 50 US metros, with a composite score driven largely by its low unemployment rate of 3.10% and a strong building permits growth of +16.86% YoY. These two metrics most define the city's current economic character, suggesting a tight labor market and an expanding housing supply. The city's labor demand composite score of 4.60, although near median, is influenced by a modest employment growth rate of +0.30% YoY.

**Labor Demand**
Cincinnati's employment growth rate of +0.30% YoY, combined with a weekly hours deviation of -0.124% from its own trend, signals a moderate labor demand expansion, rather than a strong one, as hours are not running significantly above trend. This suggests that while jobs are being added, the existing workforce is not being overly stretched, indicating genuine demand rather than a survivor squeeze. The labor demand composite score of 4.60 reflects this balanced situation.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in Cincinnati is 3.10%, placing it in the top tier with a 94th percentile rank, indicating a very tight labor market with little slack. This tight market implies that businesses may face challenges in hiring, potentially leading to upward pressure on wages. For a company looking to expand or relocate, this means being prepared to offer competitive compensation packages to attract top talent.

**Wage Growth**
With a year-over-year wage growth rate of +4.25%, Cincinnati experiences moderate wage increases, ranking near the median at the 54th percentile. This rate of wage growth suggests that labor costs for employers are rising, but not at an alarming rate, while workers benefit from increasing purchasing power. This balance is crucial for businesses to manage labor costs effectively while ensuring worker satisfaction.

**Cost of Living**
Cincinnati's cost of living, with a PSF of $195/sqft and an average hourly earnings of $35.36, resulting in a ratio of 5.51, places it in the 71st percentile in terms of affordability. This means the city is more affordable than many of its peers, offering a talent attraction advantage without necessitating significant wage premiums. The modest increase in PSF of +1.0% YoY, however, suggests that affordability might slightly decrease over time.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in Cincinnati is shrinking at a rate of -1.12% YoY, indicating a contracting labor supply. This negative growth rate poses a structural headwind for hiring, as the pool of potential workers is decreasing. Businesses looking to expand in Cincinnati will need to consider strategies to attract workers from other areas or invest in workforce development programs.

**Building Permits**
With a +16.86% YoY increase in residential building permits, Cincinnati is experiencing an expansion in housing supply. This growth in permits signals developer confidence in the market and suggests that future affordability and workforce accommodation are likely to improve. As housing supply increases, it may help mitigate some of the pressures from a tight labor market by making the city more attractive to potential residents.

**Days on Market**
Homes in Cincinnati are currently sitting on the market for a median of 36 days, with a +9.1% YoY increase in days on market, placing it in the top tier with a 92nd percentile rank. This indicates a slower market that is more accessible for relocating workers, as they have more time to find suitable housing. The rising DOM suggests a healthy normalization of the housing market, making it less competitive for buyers.

**Office Economy**
Cincinnati's professional and office worker share, with a composite score of 2.38, ranks in the 40th percentile, indicating a moderately deep talent pool. This suggests that the city is suited for businesses that require a mix of professional and other skills, though it may not be as ideal for tech, finance, or consulting firms that rely heavily on a deep knowledge-economy talent pool.

In conclusion, Cincinnati offers businesses a unique blend of a tight labor market, moderate wage growth, and an expanding housing supply, making it an attractive location for companies looking to balance labor costs with access to a skilled workforce. However, the single biggest risk or constraint for decision-makers is the shrinking labor force, which may necessitate innovative recruitment and retention strategies to overcome the challenges of a contracting workforce supply.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The economic data for the Raleigh-Cary metropolitan area suggests a mixed performance across various indicators. The unemployment rate stands at 3.5%, placing it in the 86th percentile, which indicates a relatively low level of unemployment compared to other regions. The labor force participation rate of 67.45% is also above average, ranking in the 76th percentile, suggesting a robust engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Annual earnings growth of 6.01% is solid, though not exceptional, placing the region in the 72nd percentile. Inflation, as measured by the cost of living index, is at 4.77%, which is moderate and ranks in the 56th percentile.

Housing market indicators present a more nuanced picture. The year-over-year change in housing prices is 8.65%, indicating continued appreciation but at a slower pace than some other markets, placing it in the 58th percentile. However, the number of building permits has declined by 28.25% year-over-year, a significant drop that places the region in the 16th percentile, possibly reflecting a slowdown in construction activity. The days on market metric stands at 60 days, which is relatively high and ranks in the 24th percentile, suggesting a less competitive housing market compared to its peers. The weekly hours worked, at 33.4 hours, is below average, ranking in the 12th percentile, which may indicate either part-time employment trends or flexible work arrangements.

Overall, the data suggests that while Raleigh-Cary maintains a strong labor market, there are signs of moderation in both the housing sector and employment intensity.

## Original Qwen draft (for reference)

The Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metropolitan area, centered around Washington DC, exhibits a mixed economic profile based on recent data. The unemployment rate stands at 4.5%, placing it in the 28th percentile nationally, which suggests a relatively tight labor market but one that is not among the tightest in the country. Labor force participation is notably high at 67.44%, ranking in the 78th percentile, indicating a robust engagement of the working-age population in the labor market. Earnings growth over the past year has been moderate at 4.04%, situating the region in the 38th percentile, while the cost of living index, at 4.68, is slightly above the national average, placing it in the 58th percentile. These figures suggest a stable but not particularly dynamic employment environment.

In the housing sector, the annual change in home prices is modest at 0.47%, ranking in the 28th percentile, which may reflect a period of relative stability in the real estate market. However, the decline in building permits by 11.8% year-over-year, placing the region in the 30th percentile, indicates a slowdown in new construction activity. The rental market also shows signs of moderation, with a 4.38% decrease in the price-to-rent ratio year-over-year, ranking in the 4th percentile, suggesting a potential easing of rental pressures. Properties are taking an average of 58 days to sell, which is in the 68th percentile, indicating a balanced market where neither buyers nor sellers have a significant advantage.

Overall, the Washington-Arlington-Alexandria metro area appears to be experiencing a period of moderate economic stability, with some indicators pointing to a slowdown in certain sectors.

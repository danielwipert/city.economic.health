# Sacramento — Economic Overview (FT Style)

## Polished FT-style column

Sacramento's labour market is characterised by modest expansion, with the civilian labour force growing 1.2% year-on-year, a relatively strong signal that suggests continued workforce supply growth, ranking in the 68th percentile. However, this growth is offset by softening demand, as evidenced by a 0.27% contraction in nonfarm employment over the past year and a labour demand composite score in the 20th percentile. The unemployment rate stands at 4.8%, slightly above national averages, having risen 0.3 percentage points over the past year. Average hourly earnings have grown 3.56%, a mid-tier performance. Despite stagnant job growth, weekly hours worked have remained marginally above the city's 12-month baseline, with a deviation of +0.2%, indicating some near-term stability in workloads.

The housing market presents a mixed picture. Residential building permits have declined sharply, falling 15.5% year-on-year to the 26th percentile, indicating a cooling in construction activity. Homes are taking longer to sell, with an average of 39 days on the market, up 8.3% from a year ago, and a days-on-market composite score in the 64th percentile, suggesting a gradual shift towards a buyer's market. The cost of living remains high relative to incomes, with a cost-of-living composite score of 4.02, ranking in the 19th percentile, driven largely by housing affordability pressures. Furthermore, the office market is experiencing distress, reflected in a near-zero office worker ratio score, ranking in the 0th percentile, which indicates persistent underutilisation of commercial space.

Looking ahead, Sacramento's economic outlook is tempered by structural headwinds in employment growth and housing supply. While a growing labour force and stabilising housing turnover offer some positives, the absence of a rebound in business investment or public-sector job creation is likely to keep economic momentum subdued in the near term.

## Original analysis draft (for reference)

Sacramento’s labour market shows modest expansion, with the civilian labour force growing 1.2% year-on-year — a relatively strong signal (68th percentile) suggesting continued workforce supply growth. However, this is offset by softening demand: nonfarm employment contracted 0.27% over the past year, and the labour demand composite score sits in the 20th percentile. The unemployment rate of 4.8% is slightly above national averages, having risen 0.3 percentage points over the past year, while average hourly earnings grew 3.56% — a mid-tier performance. Notably, weekly hours worked are marginally above the city’s 12-month baseline (deviation: +0.2%), indicating some near-term stability in workloads despite stagnant job growth.

Housing market dynamics present a mixed picture. Residential building permits have declined sharply, falling 15.5% year-on-year (26th percentile), pointing to a cooling in construction activity. Meanwhile, homes are taking longer to sell — 39 days on average, up 8.3% from a year ago — a trend reflected in the 64th percentile days-on-market composite score, suggesting a gradual shift toward a buyer’s market. The cost of living remains high relative to incomes, with a cost-of-living composite score of 4.02 (19th percentile), driven largely by housing affordability pressures. Office market distress is evident in the near-zero office worker ratio score (0th percentile), reflecting persistent underutilisation of commercial space.

**Outlook:** Sacramento faces structural headwinds in employment growth and housing supply, tempered by a growing labour force and stabilising housing turnover. Absent a rebound in business investment or public-sector job creation, economic momentum is likely to remain subdued in the near term.

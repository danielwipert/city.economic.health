# St. Louis

**Grade: B- (Below Average) | 48.1th percentile | June 2026**

---

St. Louis has an overall grade of B- with a composite score ranking it 48.1th percentile out of 50 US metros. These metrics suggest a city with a relatively slow job market and moderate labor availability.

This combination signals a contraction in labor demand, indicating that the city is not experiencing genuine demand expansion. The negative employment growth rate and below-trend hours suggest that the job market is slowing down.

**Unemployment**
The unemployment rate in St. Louis is 3.60%, which is relatively low and ranks in the 69th percentile. This suggests a tight labor market with moderate slack, making it somewhat challenging for businesses to hire new employees. As a result, businesses may face upward pressure on wages to attract and retain talent.

The year-over-year wage growth rate in St. Louis is +1.80%, which is below average and ranks in the 24th percentile. This moderate wage growth rate implies that labor costs for employers are rising, but at a slower pace than in many other cities. Workers in St. Louis will see some improvement in their purchasing power, but it may not be as strong as in other metros.

**Labor Force Growth**
The civilian labor force in St. Louis is growing at a rate of +0.01% year-over-year, which is near the median and ranks in the 59th percentile. This slow growth rate indicates that the labor force supply is barely expanding, which may pose a structural headwind for hiring in the long term. Businesses may need to consider strategies to attract workers from other areas or invest in workforce development programs.

**Building Permits**
The number of residential building permits in St. Louis is increasing by +4.06% year-over-year, which is near the median and ranks in the 56th percentile. This moderate growth in permits suggests that housing supply is expanding, which can improve affordability and accommodate a growing workforce. However, the relatively slow pace of growth may not be enough to keep up with demand, potentially leading to future affordability challenges.

**Days on Market**
The median days on market for homes in St. Louis is 42 days, with a year-over-year increase of +7.7%. This ranks in the 82nd percentile, indicating a slower market. For workers relocating to St. Louis, this means that they may have a more accessible and less competitive housing market, with more time to find a suitable home.

**Office Economy**
St. Louis has a relatively shallow professional talent pool, with an office economy score ranking it in the 14th percentile. This suggests that the city is less suited for businesses that require a deep knowledge-economy talent pool, such as tech, finance, or consulting firms. However, it may be more suitable for businesses with industrial or logistics-dominated economies.

In conclusion, St. Louis offers a relatively affordable cost of living and a slow but still accessible housing market, which can be attractive for businesses looking to relocate or expand. However, the city's below-average labor demand and slow labor force growth pose significant risks and constraints for businesses, particularly those that require a highly skilled workforce. Decision-makers should carefully consider these factors when evaluating St. Louis as a potential business location.
